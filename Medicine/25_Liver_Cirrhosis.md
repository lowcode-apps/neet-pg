## Definition and Pathophysiology

### Definition

**Cirrhosis**:
- **End-stage chronic liver disease**
- Characterized by:
  - **Fibrosis** (bridging septa)
  - **Nodular regeneration** (hepatocyte regenerative nodules)
  - **Architectural distortion** (loss of normal lobular structure)
- **Irreversible** (advanced stages)

---

### Pathophysiology

**Progressive Fibrosis Cascade**:

```
Chronic Hepatic Injury (any cause)
         ↓
Hepatocyte death/necrosis
         ↓
Inflammatory response
         ↓
Hepatic Stellate Cell Activation
         ↓
Collagen deposition (fibrosis)
         ↓
Architectural distortion + Regenerative nodules
         ↓
CIRRHOSIS
         ↓
Portal Hypertension + Hepatocellular Dysfunction
```

**Portal Hypertension**:
- **Increased resistance** to portal blood flow
- **Hepatic venous pressure gradient (HVPG)** >5 mmHg
- **Clinically significant**: HVPG ≥10 mmHg (varices, ascites)

**Consequences**:
- **Portosystemic collaterals**: Esophageal/gastric varices, splenomegaly
- **Splanchnic vasodilation**: ↑ NO, ↑ prostaglandins
- **Hyperdynamic circulation**: ↑ cardiac output, ↓ systemic vascular resistance

---

### Classification

**Morphologic**:
- **Micronodular** (<3 mm nodules): Alcohol, hemochromatosis
- **Macronodular** (>3 mm nodules): Viral hepatitis
- **Mixed**

**Functional** (Child-Pugh Score, MELD Score):
- **Compensated**: No major complications (ascites, variceal bleeding, encephalopathy, jaundice)
- **Decompensated**: Presence of complications

## Etiology

### Common Causes (Mnemonic: "ABCDEFGH")

- **A**lcohol (40-50% in Western countries)
- **B**-Hepatitis (Hepatitis B)
- **C**-Hepatitis (Hepatitis C)
- **D**rugs, toxins
- **E**ncephalopathy (Wilson's disease - copper)
- **F**atty liver (NASH - Non-alcoholic steatohepatitis)
- **G**enetic (Hemochromatosis, Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency)
- **H**eart failure (Cardiac cirrhosis)

---

### 1. Alcohol-Related Liver Disease

**Most common cause** in Western countries

**Spectrum**:
- **Alcoholic fatty liver** → **Alcoholic hepatitis** → **Cirrhosis**

**Risk Factors**:
- **Dose-dependent**: >60-80 g/day (men), >20-40 g/day (women) × 10+ years
- **Genetics**: PNPLA3, TM6SF2 polymorphisms
- **Gender**: Women more susceptible (lower alcohol dehydrogenase)
- **Hepatitis C** coinfection

**Alcoholic Hepatitis** (acute decompensation):
- **Fever, jaundice, hepatomegaly**
- **Tender liver**
- **Leukocytosis** with neutrophilia
- **AST:ALT ratio >2:1** (AST rarely >300)
- **Maddrey Discriminant Function (DF)**:
  - DF = 4.6 × (PT patient - PT control) + Total bilirubin (mg/dL)
  - **DF ≥32**: Severe (consider corticosteroids)

**Management**:
- **Abstinence** (CRITICAL - improves survival)
- **Nutrition**: High-protein, thiamine, folate
- **Severe alcoholic hepatitis** (DF ≥32):
  - **Prednisolone 40 mg daily × 28 days** (if no infection/GI bleed)
  - **Pentoxifylline** (alternative if steroids contraindicated)

---

### 2. Chronic Viral Hepatitis

**Hepatitis C**:
- **Leading cause** of cirrhosis in many countries
- **Chronic infection**: 75-85% (after acute HCV)
- **Cirrhosis**: 20-30% after 20-30 years
- **Treatment**: Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) - >95% cure
  - May slow/halt progression if pre-cirrhotic
  - **Does NOT reverse advanced cirrhosis** (fibrosis persists)

**Hepatitis B**:
- **Chronic infection**: 90% (neonatal), 5% (adult)
- **Cirrhosis**: 20-25% of chronic carriers
- **Treatment**: Entecavir, tenofovir (suppress viral replication)
- **HCC risk**: Persists even with treatment

**Coinfections**:
- **HBV + HDV** (Hepatitis Delta): Accelerated progression
- **HCV + HIV**: Faster fibrosis progression

---

### 3. Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD) / NASH

**Increasing prevalence** (obesity epidemic)

**Spectrum**:
- **Simple steatosis** (fatty liver) → **NASH** (steatohepatitis) → **Cirrhosis**

**Risk Factors** (Metabolic Syndrome):
- **Obesity**
- **Type 2 diabetes**
- **Dyslipidemia**
- **Insulin resistance**

**Diagnosis**:
- **Diagnosis of exclusion**: Rule out alcohol, viral hepatitis, drugs
- **Imaging**: Fatty liver (ultrasound, CT, MRI)
- **Liver biopsy**: Gold standard (steatosis + inflammation + fibrosis)

**Management**:
- **Weight loss** (7-10% body weight)
- **Diabetes control**
- **Vitamin E** (800 IU daily - if non-diabetic NASH)
- **Pioglitazone** (if diabetic NASH)

---

### 4. Autoimmune Liver Diseases

**Autoimmune Hepatitis (AIH)**:
- **Young/middle-aged women**
- **Hypergammaglobulinemia** (IgG elevation)
- **Autoantibodies**:
  - **Type 1**: ANA, anti-smooth muscle (SMA)
  - **Type 2**: Anti-LKM-1 (liver-kidney microsomal)
- **Treatment**: **Prednisone + azathioprine**

**Primary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC)**:
- **Middle-aged women**
- **Cholestatic** pattern (↑ ALP, GGT)
- **Pruritis** (early symptom)
- **Anti-mitochondrial antibody (AMA)** - 95% positive
- **Treatment**: **Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA)**

**Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC)**:
- **Young men**
- **Association**: **Ulcerative colitis** (70-80%)
- **Cholestatic** pattern
- **MRCP**: **Beading** (strictures + dilations of bile ducts)
- **Complications**: **Cholangiocarcinoma** (10-20%)
- **Treatment**: UDCA (controversial), liver transplant

---

### 5. Hereditary/Metabolic

**Hemochromatosis**:
- **Hereditary**: HFE gene (C282Y mutation)
- **Iron overload** → cirrhosis, diabetes, cardiomyopathy
- **Classic triad**: Cirrhosis, Diabetes, Skin pigmentation ("bronze diabetes")
- **Labs**: ↑ Ferritin, ↑ Transferrin saturation (>45%)
- **Treatment**: **Phlebotomy** (target ferritin <50 μg/L)

**Wilson's Disease**:
- **Copper overload** (ATP7B gene mutation)
- **Neuropsychiatric symptoms** + **Liver disease**
- **Kayser-Fleischer rings** (copper deposition in cornea)
- **Labs**: ↓ Ceruloplasmin, ↑ urinary copper
- **Treatment**: **Penicillamine** (copper chelation), liver transplant

**Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency**:
- **PiZZ genotype** (severe deficiency)
- **Lung disease** (emphysema) + **Liver disease** (cirrhosis, cholestasis)
- **Diagnosis**: Low alpha-1 antitrypsin level, genotyping
- **Treatment**: Liver transplant (lung transplant if severe emphysema)

---

### 6. Vascular

**Budd-Chiari Syndrome**:
- **Hepatic vein thrombosis**
- **Causes**: Hypercoagulable states (JAK2 mutation, Factor V Leiden), oral contraceptives
- **Triad**: Abdominal pain, ascites, hepatomegaly
- **Diagnosis**: Doppler ultrasound, CT/MRI (absent hepatic vein flow)
- **Treatment**: Anticoagulation, TIPS, liver transplant

**Cardiac Cirrhosis**:
- **Chronic right heart failure** → hepatic congestion
- **"Nutmeg liver"** (gross appearance)
- **Treatment**: Treat heart failure

---

## Clinical Features and Diagnosis

### Clinical Features

**Compensated Cirrhosis**:
- Often **asymptomatic**
- Discovered incidentally (abnormal labs, imaging)

**Decompensated Cirrhosis** (MAJOR complications):
- **Ascites** (most common - 50%)
- **Variceal bleeding**
- **Hepatic encephalopathy**
- **Jaundice**

---

### Physical Examination Findings

**Stigmata of Chronic Liver Disease**:

**Skin**:
- **Jaundice** (icterus)
- **Spider angiomata** (telangiectasias - chest, face)
  - **>5** = significant
  - **Mechanism**: ↑ Estrogen (decreased hepatic clearance)
- **Palmar erythema** (thenar/hypothenar eminence redness)
- **Caput medusae** (dilated periumbilical veins - portal hypertension)

**Hands**:
- **Clubbing**
- **Dupuytren's contracture** (alcohol)
- **Terry's nails** (proximal white, distal pink - hypoalbuminemia)

**Abdomen**:
- **Ascites** (shifting dullness, fluid wave)
- **Hepatomegaly** (early) → **Small, nodular liver** (late)
- **Splenomegaly** (portal hypertension)

**Endocrine**:
- **Gynecomastia** (men - ↑ estrogen)
- **Testicular atrophy**
- **Loss of body hair**

**Neurologic**:
- **Asterixis** (hepatic encephalopathy)

**Other**:
- **Fetor hepaticus** (musty breath - methionine metabolites)
- **Cyanosis** (hepatopulmonary syndrome)

---

### Laboratory Findings

**Liver Function Tests**:
- **AST, ALT**: Mildly elevated (often <2× normal in cirrhosis)
  - **AST:ALT >2:1**: Suggests alcohol
- **ALP, GGT**: Elevated (cholestatic causes)
- **Bilirubin**: Elevated (hepatocellular dysfunction)
- **Albumin**: **Low** (decreased synthesis)
- **PT/INR**: **Prolonged** (decreased synthesis of clotting factors)

**Complete Blood Count**:
- **Thrombocytopenia** (portal hypertension → splenomegaly → platelet sequestration)
- **Anemia** (chronic disease, GI bleeding, hypersplenism)
- **Leukopenia** (hypersplenism)

**Hepatic Synthetic Function**:
- **Albumin** <3.5 g/dL
- **INR** >1.2
- Indicators of **severity**

---

### Imaging

**Ultrasound**:
- **Nodular liver surface**
- **Heterogeneous echotexture**
- **Portal vein diameter** (>13 mm suggests portal hypertension)
- **Splenomegaly**
- **Ascites**
- **Hepatocellular carcinoma** (HCC) screening

**CT/MRI**:
- Better characterization of nodules (HCC surveillance)
- **Cirrhotic morphology**: Nodularity, caudate hypertrophy, right lobe atrophy

**Transient Elastography (FibroScan)**:
- **Non-invasive** assessment of fibrosis
- **Liver stiffness measurement (LSM)**:
  - <7 kPa: No fibrosis
  - **>12.5 kPa**: Cirrhosis

---

### Liver Biopsy

**Gold Standard** for diagnosis (but often not needed)

**Indications**:
- **Uncertain etiology**
- **Assess severity** (if impacts management)
- **Rule out coexisting diseases**

**Contraindications**:
- **Coagulopathy** (INR >1.5, platelets <50,000) - relative
- **Ascites** (risk of bleeding) - use transjugular route

**Histology**:
- **Bridging fibrosis** (porto-portal, porto-central)
- **Regenerative nodules**
- **Architectural distortion**

---

## Complications

### 1. Portal Hypertension

**Definition**: Portal pressure >5 mmHg (HVPG)

**Clinically Significant**: HVPG ≥10 mmHg (varices, ascites develop)

**Consequences**:
- **Esophagogastric varices**
- **Splenomegaly** (congestive splenomegaly)
- **Ascites**
- **Hepatic encephalopathy** (portosystemic shunting)
- **Portal hypertensive gastropathy**

---

### 2. Ascites

**Most common complication** (50% at 10 years)

**Pathophysiology**:
- **Portal hypertension** (↑ hydrostatic pressure)
- **Hypoalbuminemia** (↓ oncotic pressure)
- **Splanchnic vasodilation** (NO, prostaglandins)
- **RAAS activation** (Na⁺/water retention)

**Clinical Features**:
- **Abdominal distension**
- **Weight gain**
- **Shifting dullness**, **fluid wave**
- **Respiratory compromise** (if tense)

**Diagnosis**:

**Diagnostic Paracentesis** (ALWAYS in new-onset or hospitalized patients):
- **Cell count with differential**
- **Albumin** (calculate SAAG)
- **Total protein**
- **Culture** (bedside inoculation in blood culture bottles)
- **Cytology** (if malignancy suspected)

**Serum-Ascites Albumin Gradient (SAAG)**:
```
SAAG = Serum Albumin - Ascitic Fluid Albumin

SAAG ≥1.1 g/dL = Portal Hypertension (97% accuracy)
SAAG <1.1 g/dL = Non-portal hypertension
```

**SAAG ≥1.1 g/dL** (Portal Hypertension):
- **Cirrhosis** (most common)
- Cardiac ascites (right heart failure)
- Budd-Chiari syndrome
- Massive liver metastases
- **Total protein**:
  - <2.5 g/dL: Cirrhosis (typically)
  - >2.5 g/dL: Cardiac ascites, Budd-Chiari

**SAAG <1.1 g/dL** (Non-portal hypertension):
- **Peritoneal carcinomatosis**
- **Tuberculous peritonitis** (↑ lymphocytes, ↑ ADA)
- **Pancreatic ascites** (↑↑ amylase)
- **Nephrotic syndrome**
- **Serositis** (SLE, connective tissue disease)

**Management**:

**Dietary**:
- **Sodium restriction**: **2 g/day** (88 mEq/day)
  - Most important intervention

**Diuretics**:
- **Spironolactone** (aldosterone antagonist):
  - **Starting dose**: 100 mg daily
  - **Target**: Negative sodium balance

- **Furosemide** (loop diuretic):
  - **Starting dose**: 40 mg daily
  - **Combination**: Spironolactone 100 mg + Furosemide 40 mg
  - **Ratio**: 100:40 (maintain to prevent hypokalemia/hyperkalemia)

- **Titrate** every 3-5 days (max: Spironolactone 400 mg, Furosemide 160 mg)
- **Goal**: Weight loss **0.5 kg/day** (no edema) or **1 kg/day** (with edema)

**Monitoring**:
- **Electrolytes**, **creatinine** (every 3-5 days during titration)
- **Watch for**: Hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, AKI

**Large-Volume Paracentesis (LVP)**:
- **Indications**: Tense ascites, respiratory compromise, refractory ascites
- **Procedure**: Remove **≥5 L**
- **Albumin replacement**:
  - **If >5 L removed**: **8 g albumin per liter removed**
  - Prevents post-paracentesis circulatory dysfunction

**Refractory Ascites** (10%):
- **Definition**: Unresponsive to sodium restriction + high-dose diuretics
- **Management**:
  - **Serial large-volume paracentesis** (LVP) + albumin
  - **TIPS** (Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt):
    - Creates shunt (hepatic vein ↔ portal vein) → ↓ portal pressure
    - **Improves ascites**, reduces paracentesis need
    - **Contraindications**: Severe hepatic encephalopathy, heart failure, severe liver failure (MELD >18-25)
    - **Complication**: Hepatic encephalopathy (30-50%)

  - **Liver transplantation** (definitive)

---

### 3. Spontaneous Bacterial Peritonitis (SBP)

**Infection of ascitic fluid** WITHOUT intra-abdominal source

**Pathogenesis**:
- **Bacterial translocation** from gut
- **Common organisms**: E. coli, Klebsiella, Streptococcus pneumoniae

**Clinical Features**:
- **Fever, abdominal pain**
- **Altered mental status** (encephalopathy)
- **May be SUBTLE** (only worsening encephalopathy)
- **High index of suspicion**

**Diagnosis**:

**Diagnostic Paracentesis** (in ALL hospitalized cirrhotic patients with ascites):
- **Ascitic fluid PMN count ≥250 cells/mm³** = SBP
- **Culture**: Positive in 40-60% (low yield due to low bacterial count)
  - **Inoculate blood culture bottles** at bedside (increases yield)

**Variants**:
- **Culture-negative neutrocytic ascites (CNNA)**: PMN ≥250, culture negative
  - Treat as SBP
- **Monomicrobial non-neutrocytic bacterascites (MNB)**: PMN <250, culture positive
  - If symptomatic: Treat
  - If asymptomatic: Repeat paracentesis (may be transient)

**Management**:

**Empiric Antibiotics** (start immediately - don't wait for culture):
- **Cefotaxime 2 g IV q8h** × 5 days (first-line) OR
- **Ceftriaxone 2 g IV daily** × 5 days OR
- **Fluoroquinolone** (ciprofloxacin 400 mg IV q12h) - if no prior prophylaxis

**Albumin** (reduces mortality, renal failure):
- **1.5 g/kg IV** at diagnosis
- **1 g/kg IV** on day 3
- **Especially if**: Creatinine >1 mg/dL, BUN >30 mg/dL, bilirubin >4 mg/dL

**Response**:
- **Repeat paracentesis at 48 hours**: PMN should ↓ by ≥25%
- If no response: Broaden antibiotics (consider resistant organisms, secondary peritonitis)

**Secondary Bacterial Peritonitis** (surgical abdomen):
- **Polymicrobial** culture
- **Ascitic protein >1 g/dL**, **glucose <50 mg/dL**, **LDH > serum LDH**
- **CT abdomen**: Look for perforation, abscess
- **Surgical consultation**

**Secondary Prophylaxis** (after SBP episode):
- **Norfloxacin 400 mg daily** (indefinitely) OR
- **TMP-SMX daily**
- **Decreases recurrence** from 70% to 20%
- **Liver transplant** is definitive treatment

**Primary Prophylaxis** (high-risk, never had SBP):
- **Indications**:
  - **Ascitic protein <1.5 g/dL** + **Advanced cirrhosis** (Child-Pugh ≥9 or MELD ≥15) OR
  - **Prior variceal hemorrhage**
- **Norfloxacin 400 mg daily** OR **TMP-SMX**

### 4. Hepatic Encephalopathy (HE)

**Altered mental status** from hepatic dysfunction + portosystemic shunting

**Pathophysiology**:
- **Ammonia** (from gut bacteria, protein metabolism):
  - Normally metabolized by liver (urea cycle)
  - In cirrhosis: ↓ hepatic clearance + portosystemic shunting → ↑ blood ammonia
  - Crosses blood-brain barrier → **neurotoxicity** (astrocyte swelling)
- **Other toxins**: Manganese, mercaptans, short-chain fatty acids

**Precipitating Factors** (Mnemonic: "HEPATICS"):
- **H**emorrhage (GI bleeding - protein load)
- **E**lectrolytes (hypokalemia, hyponatremia)
- **P**roteins (high-protein meal)
- **A**zotemia (renal failure)
- **T**ranquilizers, sedatives
- **I**nfection (SBP, UTI, pneumonia)
- **C**onstipation
- **S**hunts (TIPS, spontaneous portosystemic shunts)

**Clinical Features**:

**West Haven Criteria** (Grades 0-4):

| Grade | Consciousness | Neurologic | Asterixis |
|-------|---------------|------------|-----------|
| **0** | Normal | Normal | Absent |
| **1** | **Mild confusion**, sleep disturbance | Tremor, apraxia | Mild |
| **2** | **Lethargy**, disorientation | **Asterixis** (present) | Present |
| **3** | **Somnolent** but arousable | Hyperreflexia | Present |
| **4** | **Coma** | Decerebrate posturing | Absent |

**Asterixis** ("Flapping Tremor"):
- **Test**: Extend arms, dorsiflex wrists
- **Positive**: Irregular, non-rhythmic wrist flexion-extension
- **Sensitive** for Grade 2-3 HE

**Minimal Hepatic Encephalopathy (MHE)**:
- **No overt clinical symptoms**
- **Psychometric testing**: Impaired attention, reaction time
- **Impacts**: Driving, work performance, quality of life

**Diagnosis**:
- **Clinical** (based on West Haven criteria)
- **Exclude other causes**: Hypoglycemia, intracranial bleed, stroke, meningitis, Wernicke's encephalopathy
- **Ammonia level**:
  - **Elevated** in most (but NOT diagnostic)
  - **Normal ammonia** doesn't exclude HE
  - **Don't use to diagnose** - clinical diagnosis

**Management**:

**Identify and Treat Precipitant**:
- **GI bleeding**: Transfuse, treat varices
- **Infection**: Antibiotics
- **Constipation**: Lactulose
- **Discontinue sedatives**

**Lactulose** (Non-absorbable disaccharide):
- **First-line treatment**
- **Mechanism**:
  - Acidifies colon (NH₃ → NH₄⁺, trapped in colon)
  - Cathartic (↓ gut transit time, ↓ ammonia production/absorption)
- **Dosing**:
  - **Oral**: 15-30 mL (10-20 g) **2-3 times daily**
  - **Goal**: **2-3 soft stools per day**
  - **Titrate** to achieve goal
- **Rectal** (if patient can't take PO): 300 mL in 700 mL water retention enema

**Rifaximin**:
- **Non-absorbable antibiotic**
- **Mechanism**: ↓ Ammonia-producing gut bacteria
- **Dosing**: **550 mg PO BID**
- **Combination**: Lactulose + Rifaximin (better than lactulose alone)
- **Indication**: Recurrent HE (secondary prophylaxis)

**Protein Restriction**:
- **NOT recommended** (leads to malnutrition, muscle wasting)
- **Maintain normal protein** intake (1.2-1.5 g/kg/day)
- **Vegetable/dairy protein** better than meat (lower ammonia production)

**Zinc Supplementation**:
- **Zinc 220 mg PO BID** (if deficient)
- Cofactor for urea cycle enzymes

**Avoid**:
- **Sedatives**, **opioids**, **benzodiazepines**

**Refractory HE**:
- Consider **liver transplantation**

### 5. Hepatorenal Syndrome (HRS)

**Functional renal failure** in cirrhosis (kidneys structurally normal)

**Pathophysiology**:
- **Splanchnic vasodilation** → ↓ Effective arterial blood volume
- **RAAS + Sympathetic activation** → **Intense renal vasoconstriction**
- **Acute tubular necrosis** NOT present (functional)

**Types**:

**HRS Type 1** (HRS-AKI):
- **Rapid decline** in renal function
- **Doubling of serum creatinine** to >2.5 mg/dL in <2 weeks
- **Precipitants**: SBP, GI bleeding, large-volume paracentesis without albumin
- **Poor prognosis**: Median survival <2 weeks (without treatment)

**HRS Type 2** (HRS-CKD):
- **Gradual decline** in renal function
- **Moderate renal impairment** (Cr 1.5-2.5 mg/dL)
- **Refractory ascites** (main manifestation)
- **Better prognosis**: Median survival 3-6 months

**Diagnostic Criteria** (2015 ICA-AKI Criteria):
- **Cirrhosis with ascites**
- **Increase in serum creatinine** ≥0.3 mg/dL in 48 hours OR ≥50% from baseline
- **No response to diuretic withdrawal + volume expansion** (albumin 1 g/kg × 2 days)
- **Absence of shock**
- **No nephrotoxic drugs** (NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, contrast)
- **No structural kidney disease** (proteinuria <500 mg/day, normal renal ultrasound)

**Management**:

**HRS Type 1** (HRS-AKI):

**Vasoconstrictors + Albumin**:
- **Albumin**: 1 g/kg day 1 (max 100 g), then **20-40 g daily**

- **Vasoconstrictor options**:

  **Midodrine + Octreotide** (oral, outpatient):
  - **Midodrine**: 7.5-12.5 mg PO TID (α-agonist - systemic vasoconstriction)
  - **Octreotide**: 100-200 μg SC TID (splanchnic vasoconstriction)

  **Norepinephrine** (IV, ICU):
  - 0.5-3 mg/hour IV infusion
  - **More effective** than midodrine/octreotide

  **Terlipressin** (not available in US):
  - **Vasopressin analog**
  - **Most effective** pharmacologic therapy
  - 1 mg IV q4-6h

- **Goal**: ↑ Mean arterial pressure (MAP) by 10-15 mmHg → ↑ renal perfusion

**TIPS**:
- May improve renal function
- Consider if pharmacologic therapy fails

**Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT)**:
- **Bridge to transplant**
- Does NOT reverse HRS

**Liver Transplantation**:
- **Definitive treatment**
- **Simultaneous liver-kidney transplant** if prolonged AKI (>4 weeks) or on dialysis

**HRS Type 2** (HRS-CKD):
- **Midodrine + Octreotide + Albumin** (less effective than Type 1)
- **TIPS**: Improves ascites, may improve renal function
- **Liver transplantation**

### 6. Hepatopulmonary Syndrome (HPS)

**Triad**:
1. **Liver disease** (cirrhosis, portal hypertension)
2. **Intrapulmonary vascular dilations**
3. **Hypoxemia** (PaO₂ <80 mmHg or A-a gradient ≥15 mmHg on room air)

**Pathophysiology**:
- **Pulmonary vasodilation** (NO, ET-1) → **Intrapulmonary shunting** → Hypoxemia

**Clinical Features**:
- **Dyspnea** (especially **platypnea** - worse when upright)
- **Orthodeoxia** (↓ O₂ saturation when upright, improves supine)
- **Cyanosis**, **clubbing**
- **Spider angiomata** (cutaneous, pulmonary)

**Diagnosis**:
- **Arterial blood gas**: Hypoxemia (PaO₂ <80 mmHg or A-a gradient ≥15)
- **Contrast echocardiography** (agitated saline):
  - **Microbubbles appear in left heart** after 3-6 cardiac cycles (delayed)
  - **Immediate** (<3 cycles) = intracardiac shunt (ASD, VSD)
  - **Delayed** (3-6 cycles) = intrapulmonary shunt (HPS)
- **Tc-99m MAA scan**: Extrapulmonary uptake (brain, kidneys)

**Management**:
- **No effective medical therapy**
- **Oxygen supplementation**
- **Liver transplantation**: **ONLY curative treatment**
  - **MELD exception points** (prioritized for transplant)
  - **Hypoxemia may reverse** post-transplant (takes months)

---

### 7. Portopulmonary Hypertension

**Pulmonary arterial hypertension** in portal hypertension

**Definition**:
- **Mean pulmonary artery pressure (mPAP) >20 mmHg** (new definition)
- **Pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR) >3 Wood units**
- **Pulmonary capillary wedge pressure (PCWP) ≤15 mmHg** (pre-capillary)

**Clinical Features**:
- **Dyspnea** (on exertion)
- **Chest pain**
- **Syncope**
- **Right heart failure**

**Diagnosis**:
- **Echocardiography**: Elevated right ventricular systolic pressure (RVSP >40 mmHg)
- **Right heart catheterization**: Gold standard (confirms mPAP, PVR, PCWP)

**Management**:
- **Pulmonary vasodilators**:
  - **Phosphodiesterase-5 inhibitors** (sildenafil)
  - **Endothelin receptor antagonists** (bosentan, ambrisentan)
  - **Prostacyclins** (epoprostenol IV)
- **Liver transplantation**:
  - **Contraindicated** if **mPAP >45-50 mmHg** (high perioperative mortality)
  - If mPAP ↓ to <35 mmHg with treatment → consider transplant

---

### 8. Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC)

**Most common primary liver malignancy**

**Risk Factors**:
- **Cirrhosis** (80-90% of HCC)
  - **HCV, HBV** (highest risk)
  - Alcohol, NASH
- **Chronic HBV** (even without cirrhosis)

**Surveillance**:
- **ALL cirrhotic patients**
- **Ultrasound** ± **AFP** (alpha-fetoprotein)
- **Every 6 months**

**Diagnosis**:
- **Imaging** (CT/MRI with contrast):
  - **Arterial enhancement** + **Washout** on delayed phase = HCC
  - **LI-RADS** (Liver Imaging Reporting and Data System) score
- **Biopsy**: If imaging inconclusive

**Management**:
- **Resection** (if solitary, preserved liver function, no portal hypertension)
- **Liver transplantation**:
  - **Milan Criteria**:
    - Single tumor ≤5 cm OR
    - ≤3 tumors, each ≤3 cm
    - No vascular invasion, no extrahepatic disease
- **Ablation** (RFA, microwave): Small tumors (<3 cm)
- **TACE** (Transarterial chemoembolization): Intermediate-stage
- **Systemic therapy**: Sorafenib, lenvatinib (advanced HCC)

---

## Management

### General Principles

**1. Treat Underlying Cause**:
- **Alcohol**: Abstinence (improves survival, may reverse early fibrosis)
- **Hepatitis B**: Entecavir, tenofovir
- **Hepatitis C**: DAAs (cure >95%)
- **Autoimmune hepatitis**: Prednisone + azathioprine
- **Hemochromatosis**: Phlebotomy
- **Wilson's disease**: Penicillamine, trientine

**2. Prevent Progression**:
- **Avoid hepatotoxins**: Alcohol, NSAIDs, acetaminophen (>2 g/day)
- **Vaccinations**: Hepatitis A, Hepatitis B (if not immune), influenza, pneumococcal
- **Screen for HCC**: Ultrasound ± AFP every 6 months

**3. Manage Complications**:
- **Ascites**: Sodium restriction, diuretics, paracentesis
- **SBP**: Antibiotics (cefotaxime), albumin, prophylaxis
- **Hepatic encephalopathy**: Lactulose, rifaximin
- **Variceal bleeding**: Octreotide, EVL, prophylaxis (beta-blockers)
- **Hepatorenal syndrome**: Vasoconstrictors + albumin

**4. Nutrition**:
- **High-protein** diet (1.2-1.5 g/kg/day) - maintain muscle mass
- **Frequent small meals**, **late-night snack**
- **Avoid protein restriction** (worsens sarcopenia)
- **Branched-chain amino acids** (if persistent HE)

**5. Liver Transplantation**:
- **Definitive treatment** for decompensated cirrhosis
- Evaluate when **MELD ≥15** or **first decompensating event**

---

### Prognostic Scores

#### **Child-Pugh Score**

**Components** (5 parameters):

| Parameter | **1 point** | **2 points** | **3 points** |
|-----------|-------------|--------------|--------------|
| **Encephalopathy** | None | Grade 1-2 | Grade 3-4 |
| **Ascites** | None | Mild (controlled) | Moderate-severe |
| **Bilirubin** (mg/dL) | <2 | 2-3 | >3 |
| **Albumin** (g/dL) | >3.5 | 2.8-3.5 | <2.8 |
| **INR** | <1.7 | 1.7-2.3 | >2.3 |

**Classification**:
- **Class A** (5-6 points): Well-compensated, **1-year survival 100%**
- **Class B** (7-9 points): Significant functional compromise, **1-year survival 80%**
- **Class C** (10-15 points): Decompensated, **1-year survival 45%**

**Uses**:
- **Surgical risk** assessment (elective surgery)
- **Prognosis** in chronic liver disease
- **NOT used for transplant allocation** (MELD used)

---

#### **MELD Score** (Model for End-Stage Liver Disease)

**Formula**:
```
MELD = 3.78 × ln(Bilirubin mg/dL) +
       11.2 × ln(INR) +
       9.57 × ln(Creatinine mg/dL) +
       6.43

(Minimum value for each: 1.0)
```

**Range**: 6-40
- **<10**: Low risk (1-year mortality <2%)
- **10-19**: Moderate risk (6-20% mortality)
- **20-29**: High risk (20-45% mortality)
- **30-40**: Very high risk (50-70% mortality)
- **>40**: Extremely high risk (>70% mortality)

**Uses**:
- **Liver transplant allocation** (higher MELD = higher priority)
- **3-month mortality** prediction

**MELD-Na** (incorporates sodium):
- Improves prediction (hyponatremia worsens prognosis)

**Limitations**:
- Underestimates mortality in:
  - **HCC** (MELD exception points given)
  - **Hepatopulmonary syndrome**
  - **Portopulmonary hypertension**
  - **Hepatic hydrothorax**

## Liver Transplantation

### Indications

**Decompensated Cirrhosis**:
- **Ascites** (refractory)
- **Variceal bleeding** (recurrent)
- **Hepatic encephalopathy** (recurrent/refractory)
- **SBP**
- **Hepatorenal syndrome**
- **Hepatopulmonary syndrome**
- **Severe malnutrition**, **sarcopenia**

**MELD ≥15** OR **First decompensating event** → **Refer for transplant evaluation**

**Hepatocellular Carcinoma**:
- **Within Milan Criteria**:
  - Single tumor ≤5 cm OR
  - ≤3 tumors, each ≤3 cm
  - No vascular invasion, no extrahepatic disease

**Acute Liver Failure**:
- Acetaminophen overdose, viral hepatitis, drug-induced
- **King's College Criteria** (transplant vs supportive)

---

### Contraindications

**Absolute**:
- **Uncontrolled sepsis**
- **Advanced cardiopulmonary disease**
- **Extrahepatic malignancy**
- **Active alcohol/drug use**
- **Anatomic abnormalities** (impossible transplant)

**Relative**:
- **Portopulmonary hypertension** (mPAP >45-50 mmHg)
- **HIV** with detectable viral load, CD4 <100
- **Age >70-75** (depends on center)
- **Obesity** (BMI >40)
- **Poor social support**, **non-compliance**

---

### Post-Transplant Management

**Immunosuppression**:
- **Calcineurin inhibitors**: Tacrolimus (first-line) or cyclosporine
- **Antimetabolites**: Mycophenolate mofetil
- **Corticosteroids**: Prednisone (tapered)

**Complications**:
- **Rejection** (acute, chronic)
- **Infection**: CMV, fungal (early), bacterial (any time)
- **Vascular**: Hepatic artery thrombosis (most feared - graft loss)
- **Biliary**: Strictures, leaks
- **Recurrent disease**: HCV, HBV, alcohol, autoimmune
- **Malignancy**: PTLD (post-transplant lymphoproliferative disorder), skin cancer
- **Metabolic**: Diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidemia, CKD

**Survival**:
- **1-year**: 85-90%
- **5-year**: 70-75%
- **10-year**: 60%

---

## High-Yield Points

### Key Concepts

**1. Cirrhosis Pathophysiology**:
- **Fibrosis + Regenerative nodules** → Portal hypertension + Hepatocellular dysfunction

**2. Most Common Complications**:
- **Ascites** (50% at 10 years) - most common first decompensation
- **Variceal bleeding** (30%)
- **Hepatic encephalopathy** (30-40%)

**3. SAAG**:
```
SAAG = Serum Albumin - Ascitic Albumin

≥1.1 g/dL = Portal Hypertension (97% accurate)
  - Cirrhosis, cardiac ascites, Budd-Chiari

<1.1 g/dL = Non-portal hypertension
  - Peritoneal carcinomatosis, TB peritonitis, pancreatic ascites
```

**4. SBP Diagnosis**:
- **Ascitic PMN ≥250 cells/mm³**
- **Treatment**: Cefotaxime + Albumin (1.5 g/kg day 1, 1 g/kg day 3)
- **Prophylaxis**: Norfloxacin (after SBP episode, or if ascitic protein <1.5 + advanced cirrhosis)

**5. Hepatic Encephalopathy**:
- **Precipitants**: "HEPATICS" (GI bleed, infection, constipation, electrolytes, sedatives)
- **Treatment**: Lactulose (goal 2-3 soft stools/day) + Rifaximin (550 mg BID)
- **NO protein restriction** (worsens sarcopenia)

**6. Hepatorenal Syndrome**:
- **Functional renal failure** (kidneys normal)
- **Type 1** (HRS-AKI): Rapid, Cr >2.5 in <2 weeks (poor prognosis)
- **Type 2** (HRS-CKD): Gradual, refractory ascites
- **Treatment**: Albumin + Vasoconstrictors (midodrine/octreotide or terlipressin)
- **Definitive**: Liver transplant

**7. Hepatopulmonary Syndrome**:
- **Liver disease + Intrapulmonary shunting + Hypoxemia**
- **Platypnea/Orthodeoxia** (worse upright)
- **Diagnosis**: Contrast echo (delayed microbubbles in left heart, 3-6 cycles)
- **Treatment**: Liver transplant (only curative)

**8. Child-Pugh vs MELD**:
- **Child-Pugh**: Surgical risk, prognosis (Class A/B/C)
- **MELD**: **Transplant allocation** (Bili, INR, Cr)
  - **≥15**: Consider transplant evaluation
  - Exceptions: HCC, HPS, portopulmonary HTN

**9. Variceal Prophylaxis**:
- **Primary**: Beta-blocker (propranolol, carvedilol) OR EVL
- **Secondary** (after bleed): Beta-blocker + EVL (combination)

**10. HCC Surveillance**:
- **ALL cirrhotic patients**
- **Ultrasound** ± AFP every **6 months**
- **Milan Criteria** for transplant:
  - Single ≤5 cm OR ≤3 tumors each ≤3 cm

---

### Treatment Pearls

**Ascites**:
- **Sodium restriction** (2 g/day) - most important
- **Diuretics**: Spironolactone 100 mg + Furosemide 40 mg (ratio 100:40)
- **Goal**: Weight loss 0.5 kg/day (no edema) or 1 kg/day (with edema)
- **LVP >5 L**: Give albumin 8 g per liter removed

**Refractory Ascites**:
- **Serial LVP** OR **TIPS**
- **TIPS contraindications**: Severe HE, heart failure, MELD >18-25

**SBP Treatment**:
- **Cefotaxime 2 g IV q8h** × 5 days
- **Albumin 1.5 g/kg day 1, 1 g/kg day 3** (↓ mortality, ↓ HRS)

**Hepatic Encephalopathy**:
- **Find precipitant** (GI bleed, infection, constipation)
- **Lactulose**: 15-30 mL 2-3× daily (goal 2-3 soft stools/day)
- **Rifaximin**: 550 mg BID (add to lactulose for recurrent HE)

**Hepatorenal Syndrome Type 1**:
- **Albumin 1 g/kg day 1** (max 100 g), then **20-40 g daily**
- **Midodrine 7.5-12.5 mg PO TID** + **Octreotide 100-200 μg SC TID**
- **Alternative**: Norepinephrine IV (more effective)

## Practice MCQs

### Question 1
A 55-year-old man with alcoholic cirrhosis develops ascites. Diagnostic paracentesis shows: Ascitic albumin 0.8 g/dL, Serum albumin 2.5 g/dL, Ascitic protein 1.2 g/dL, PMN 80 cells/mm³. What is the SAAG and interpretation?

A) SAAG 1.7; portal hypertension; no SBP
B) SAAG 1.7; portal hypertension; SBP present
C) SAAG 0.8; non-portal hypertension; no SBP
D) SAAG 3.3; portal hypertension; no SBP

**Answer**: **A) SAAG 1.7; portal hypertension; no SBP**

**Explanation**:
- **SAAG** = Serum Albumin - Ascitic Albumin
  - = 2.5 - 0.8 = **1.7 g/dL**

- **SAAG ≥1.1 g/dL** = **Portal Hypertension** (97% accurate)
  - This patient: SAAG 1.7 → **Cirrhosis** (portal hypertension)

- **SBP Diagnosis**: Ascitic PMN **≥250 cells/mm³**
  - This patient: PMN 80 → **NO SBP**

- **SAAG <1.1 g/dL** = Non-portal hypertension (peritoneal carcinomatosis, TB peritonitis)

### Question 2
A 60-year-old woman with cirrhosis is admitted with confusion. Paracentesis shows PMN 400 cells/mm³. She is started on cefotaxime. What additional medication should be given to reduce mortality and prevent hepatorenal syndrome?

A) Lactulose
B) Albumin
C) Furosemide
D) Rifaximin

**Answer**: **B) Albumin**

**Explanation**:
- **SBP** (ascitic PMN ≥250 cells/mm³)

- **Treatment**:
  - **Antibiotics**: Cefotaxime 2 g IV q8h × 5 days
  - **Albumin**: **CRITICAL** for reducing mortality and HRS
    - **1.5 g/kg IV** at diagnosis
    - **1 g/kg IV** on day 3

- **Mechanism**: Albumin prevents circulatory dysfunction, reduces HRS incidence

- **Especially beneficial** if:
  - Creatinine >1 mg/dL
  - BUN >30 mg/dL
  - Bilirubin >4 mg/dL

- **Lactulose**: For hepatic encephalopathy (not directly for SBP)
- **Rifaximin**: For recurrent HE (not acute SBP)

### Question 3
A 50-year-old man with cirrhosis presents with altered mental status. He is oriented to person only, lethargic, and has asterixis. What is the West Haven grade of hepatic encephalopathy, and what is the first-line treatment?

A) Grade 1; rifaximin
B) Grade 2; lactulose
C) Grade 3; lactulose
D) Grade 4; liver transplant

**Answer**: **C) Grade 3; lactulose**

- **West Haven Grading**:

|-------|---------------|-----------|
| **1** | Mild confusion, sleep disturbance | Mild |
| **2** | Lethargy, disorientation | Present |
| **3** | **Somnolent but arousable** | Present |
| **4** | Coma | Absent |

  - **Somnolent** (but responsive)

  - **First-line**: **Lactulose** 15-30 mL 2-3× daily

### Question 4
A 58-year-old man with cirrhosis and ascites develops acute kidney injury. Creatinine rises from 1.0 to 2.8 mg/dL over 5 days. He has no recent diuretic use, no nephrotoxic drugs, and received albumin 1 g/kg × 2 days without improvement. Urinalysis shows no protein, no casts. What is the diagnosis?

A) Acute tubular necrosis
B) Hepatorenal syndrome Type 1
C) Pre-renal azotemia
D) Acute interstitial nephritis

**Answer**: **B) Hepatorenal syndrome Type 1**

**Explanation**:
- **Hepatorenal Syndrome (HRS) Type 1** (HRS-AKI):
  - **Rapid decline** in renal function
  - **Doubling of Cr** to >2.5 mg/dL in <2 weeks

- **Diagnostic Criteria**:
  - Cirrhosis with ascites ✓
  - ↑ Cr ≥0.3 mg/dL in 48 hrs OR ≥50% from baseline ✓
  - **No response** to volume expansion (albumin 1 g/kg × 2 days) ✓
  - No shock ✓
  - No nephrotoxic drugs ✓
  - **No structural kidney disease** (no proteinuria, no casts, normal renal US) ✓

- **Management**:
  - **Albumin** 1 g/kg day 1 (max 100 g), then 20-40 g daily
  - **Vasoconstrictors**:
    - Midodrine + Octreotide OR
    - Norepinephrine (more effective)
  - **TIPS**: Consider if pharmacologic therapy fails
  - **Liver transplant**: Definitive

- **ATN differentiation**:
  - **Muddy brown casts**, **tubular epithelial cells** on urinalysis
  - HRS has **benign urinalysis**

### Question 5
A 45-year-old man with cirrhosis presents with dyspnea that worsens when he stands up and improves when he lies down. Oxygen saturation is 88% upright, 94% supine. ABG shows PaO₂ 72 mmHg on room air. Contrast echocardiography shows microbubbles appearing in the left atrium 5 cardiac cycles after injection. What is the diagnosis?

A) Portopulmonary hypertension
B) Hepatopulmonary syndrome
C) Atrial septal defect
D) Pulmonary embolism

**Answer**: **B) Hepatopulmonary syndrome**

**Explanation**:
- **Hepatopulmonary Syndrome (HPS)** triad:
  1. **Liver disease** (cirrhosis) ✓
  2. **Intrapulmonary vascular dilations** ✓
  3. **Hypoxemia** (PaO₂ <80 mmHg) ✓

- **Clinical features**:
  - **Platypnea**: Dyspnea worse upright
  - **Orthodeoxia**: ↓ O₂ saturation when upright, improves supine
  - Cyanosis, clubbing

- **Diagnosis**:
  - **Contrast echocardiography** (agitated saline):
    - **Delayed microbubbles** in left heart (**3-6 cardiac cycles**) = Intrapulmonary shunt (HPS)
    - **Immediate** (<3 cycles) = Intracardiac shunt (ASD, VSD)

- **Management**:
  - **No effective medical therapy**
  - **Liver transplantation**: **ONLY curative treatment**
  - MELD exception points (prioritized for transplant)

- **Portopulmonary HTN**:
  - Dyspnea on exertion
  - NO platypnea/orthodeoxia
  - Echo: Elevated RVSP
  - Right heart catheterization: Elevated mPAP, PVR

### Question 6
A 62-year-old woman with cirrhosis has the following labs: Bilirubin 3.5 mg/dL, INR 2.1, Creatinine 1.8 mg/dL. What is her approximate MELD score?

A) 10-15
B) 16-20
C) 21-25
D) 26-30

**Answer**: **C) 21-25**

**Explanation**:
- **MELD Score** formula:
```
MELD = 3.78 × ln(Bili) + 11.2 × ln(INR) + 9.57 × ln(Cr) + 6.43
```

- **Calculation**:
  - ln(3.5) ≈ 1.25
  - ln(2.1) ≈ 0.74
  - ln(1.8) ≈ 0.59

  - MELD = 3.78(1.25) + 11.2(0.74) + 9.57(0.59) + 6.43
  - MELD ≈ 4.7 + 8.3 + 5.6 + 6.4
  - **MELD ≈ 25**

- **Interpretation**:
  - **<10**: Low risk
  - **10-19**: Moderate risk
  - **20-29**: **High risk** (20-45% 3-month mortality)
  - **30-40**: Very high risk
  - **>40**: Extremely high risk

- **MELD ≥15**: Consider **transplant evaluation**

### Question 7
A 52-year-old man with cirrhosis undergoes screening for esophageal varices. Endoscopy shows medium-sized varices. He has never had variceal bleeding. What is the MOST appropriate primary prophylaxis?

A) No treatment needed
B) Propranolol (non-selective beta-blocker)
C) Endoscopic variceal ligation (EVL)
D) TIPS procedure

**Answer**: **B) Propranolol (non-selective beta-blocker)** OR **C) Endoscopic variceal ligation (EVL)**

**Explanation**:
- **Primary prophylaxis** (never bled, medium-large varices):

**Options** (EITHER is acceptable):
- **Non-selective beta-blocker**:
  - **Propranolol**, **carvedilol**, or **nadolol**
  - **Target HR**: 55-60 bpm OR 25% reduction from baseline
  - **Mechanism**: ↓ Portal pressure

- **Endoscopic Variceal Ligation (EVL)**:
  - Repeat sessions every 2-4 weeks until obliteration
  - **Preferred** if beta-blocker contraindicated/intolerant

- **Both equally effective** for primary prophylaxis

- **Small varices**: No treatment (but rescreen every 1-2 years)

- **Secondary prophylaxis** (after bleed):
  - **Combination**: Beta-blocker + EVL (better than either alone)

- **TIPS**: Reserved for **refractory bleeding** (failed endoscopic + medical therapy)

### Question 8
A 48-year-old man with cirrhosis presents with ascites refractory to high-dose diuretics (spironolactone 400 mg + furosemide 160 mg daily). He requires large-volume paracentesis every 2 weeks. What is the MOST appropriate next step?

A) Increase diuretics further
B) Add midodrine and octreotide
C) Evaluate for TIPS or liver transplantation
D) Restrict fluids to 1 L/day

**Answer**: **C) Evaluate for TIPS or liver transplantation**

**Explanation**:
- **Refractory Ascites** (10% of cirrhotic patients with ascites):
  - **Definition**: Unresponsive to **sodium restriction** + **high-dose diuretics**
    - Spironolactone 400 mg + Furosemide 160 mg (maximum doses)

- **Management options**:

  **Serial Large-Volume Paracentesis (LVP)**:
  - **Every 2-4 weeks** as needed
  - **Albumin replacement** (8 g per liter removed if >5 L)
  - **Currently what patient is doing**

  **TIPS** (Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt):
  - Creates shunt → ↓ portal pressure
  - **Improves ascites**, reduces paracentesis frequency
  - **Contraindications**:
    - Severe hepatic encephalopathy
    - Heart failure
    - Severe liver failure (MELD >18-25, depends on center)
  - **Complication**: Hepatic encephalopathy (30-50%)

  **Liver Transplantation**:
  - **Definitive treatment**
  - Refractory ascites is indication for transplant evaluation

- **Midodrine + Octreotide**: For hepatorenal syndrome (NOT refractory ascites alone)

- **Fluid restriction**: NOT effective for ascites (only for hyponatremia if Na <125)

Which of the following is an ABSOLUTE contraindication to liver transplantation?

A) Age 72 years
B) Active alcohol use within last 6 months
C) Hepatitis C with detectable viral load
D) Body mass index (BMI) 42

**Answer**: **B) Active alcohol use within last 6 months**

**Explanation**:
- **Absolute Contraindications** to liver transplant:
  - **Uncontrolled sepsis**
  - **Advanced cardiopulmonary disease**
  - **Extrahepatic malignancy** (uncontrolled)
  - **Active alcohol or drug use** (most programs require ≥6 months abstinence)
  - **Anatomic abnormalities** (impossible to transplant)

- **Relative Contraindications**:
  - **Age >70-75** (depends on center, comorbidities)
  - **BMI >40** (obesity increases surgical risk)
  - **HIV** with detectable viral load, CD4 <100
  - **Portopulmonary hypertension** (mPAP >45-50 mmHg)
  - **Poor social support**, non-compliance

- **Active alcohol use**:
  - **Requires abstinence** ≥6 months (most programs)
  - **Addresses**:
    - Liver recovery (if early cirrhosis)
    - Commitment to sobriety
    - Prevents recurrent alcohol-related liver disease post-transplant

- **Hepatitis C**:
  - **NOT a contraindication** (even with detectable viral load)
  - Can treat with DAAs pre- or post-transplant

## Summary

**Liver Cirrhosis** is end-stage chronic liver disease characterized by fibrosis and nodular regeneration, leading to portal hypertension and hepatocellular dysfunction:

**Etiology**: Alcohol (most common in West), Hepatitis B/C, NASH, autoimmune, genetic

**Complications**:
- **Ascites**: SAAG ≥1.1 (portal HTN), treat with Na restriction + diuretics (spironolactone + furosemide 100:40 ratio)
- **SBP**: Ascitic PMN ≥250, treat with cefotaxime + albumin, prophylaxis with norfloxacin
- **Hepatic Encephalopathy**: Precipitants (HEPATICS), treat with lactulose (2-3 stools/day) + rifaximin
- **Hepatorenal Syndrome**: Type 1 (rapid, poor prognosis), treat with albumin + vasoconstrictors
- **Hepatopulmonary Syndrome**: Platypnea/orthodeoxia, liver transplant only curative treatment
- **Variceal Bleeding**: Octreotide + EVL, prophylaxis with beta-blockers

**Prognostic Scores**:
- **Child-Pugh**: Class A/B/C (surgical risk, prognosis)
- **MELD**: Transplant allocation (≥15 consider evaluation)

**Treatment**: Manage underlying cause, prevent progression, treat complications, liver transplantation (definitive for decompensated cirrhosis)
