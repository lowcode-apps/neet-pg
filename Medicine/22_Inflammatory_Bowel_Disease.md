## Definition and Classification

### Overview
Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD) encompasses chronic, relapsing-remitting inflammatory conditions of the gastrointestinal tract:

1. **Crohn's Disease (CD)**
   - Can affect **any part** of GI tract (mouth to anus)
   - **Transmural** inflammation
   - **Skip lesions** (discontinuous)
   - **Non-caseating granulomas** (30-50%)

2. **Ulcerative Colitis (UC)**
   - **Colon and rectum ONLY**
   - **Mucosal/submucosal** inflammation
   - **Continuous** from rectum proximally
   - **NO granulomas**

3. **IBD-Unclassified (IBD-U)**
   - Features of both CD and UC
   - ~10% of IBD cases

## Epidemiology

### Incidence and Prevalence

**Geographic Distribution**:
- **Highest**: North America, Northern Europe, UK
- **Increasing**: Asia, South America, Middle East

**Peak Age of Onset**:
- **Bimodal**: 15-30 years (primary), 50-70 years (secondary)
- CD: Slightly earlier than UC

**Gender**:
- UC: Males = Females
- CD: Females slightly > Males

### Risk Factors

**Genetic**:
- **NOD2/CARD15** (strongest CD risk gene)
- **IL23R** (protective)
- **Family History**: 10-20% have affected first-degree relative
- **Concordance**: Monozygotic twins 50% (CD), 10% (UC)

**Environmental**:
- **Smoking**:
  - **INCREASES** Crohn's disease risk (2-fold)
  - **DECREASES** UC risk (protective)
  - **WORSENS** CD course
  - **Quitting** may precipitate UC

- **Appendectomy**: Protective against UC (if done for appendicitis before age 20)
- **Antibiotics**: Early-life exposure increases risk
- **NSAIDs**: May precipitate flares
- **Oral Contraceptives**: Modest increased risk
- **Western Diet**: High fat, processed foods

**Protective Factors**:
- Breastfeeding
- Mediterranean diet
- Physical activity

## Pathophysiology

### Immune Dysregulation

**General Mechanism**:
```
Genetic Susceptibility + Environmental Triggers
              ↓
Altered Intestinal Barrier Function
              ↓
Inappropriate Immune Response to Commensal Bacteria
              ↓
Chronic Inflammation
```

### Crohn's Disease

**Th1/Th17-Mediated**:
- **IL-12, IL-23** drive Th1/Th17 differentiation
- **TNF-α, IFN-γ, IL-17** promote inflammation
- **Impaired autophagy** (NOD2 mutations)
- **Defective bacterial clearance**

**Transmural Inflammation**:
- All layers of bowel wall
- Leads to:
  - Strictures (fibrosis)
  - Fistulas
  - Abscesses

### Ulcerative Colitis

**Th2-Mediated**:
- **IL-5, IL-13** drive inflammation
- **Epithelial barrier dysfunction**
- **Mucosal ulceration**

**Mucosal Inflammation**:
- Limited to mucosa/submucosa
- Leads to:
  - Pseudopolyps
  - Toxic megacolon
  - Dysplasia/cancer

## Clinical Features

### Crohn's Disease vs Ulcerative Colitis Comparison

| Feature | **Crohn's Disease** | **Ulcerative Colitis** |
|---------|---------------------|------------------------|
| **Location** | Mouth to anus (Any part) | **Colon and rectum ONLY** |
| **Most Common Site** | **Terminal ileum + colon** (ileocolitis 50%) | **Left-sided colitis** (40-50%) |
| **Distribution** | **Skip lesions** (discontinuous) | **Continuous** from rectum |
| **Inflammation** | **Transmural** (all layers) | **Mucosal/Submucosal** only |
| **Rectal Involvement** | Often spared (50% no rectal disease) | **ALWAYS involved** (95-100%) |
| **Granulomas** | **30-50%** (non-caseating) | **NEVER** |
| **Fistulas** | **Common** (30-50%) | **Rare** |
| **Strictures** | Common | Rare (in chronic cases) |
| **Bleeding** | Less common (occult) | **Prominent** (hematochezia) |
| **Perianal Disease** | **40%** (fissures, fistulas, abscess) | Rare |
| **Smoking Effect** | **Worsens** disease | **Protective** |
| **Malignancy Risk** | Slightly increased | **HIGH** (long-standing, extensive) |
| **Surgery** | Not curative (recurs) | **Curative** (total colectomy) |

### Clinical Presentation by Location

#### **Crohn's Disease Patterns**:

**1. Ileocolitis (50%)**:
- Right lower quadrant pain
- Diarrhea (non-bloody)
- Weight loss
- May mimic appendicitis

**2. Ileitis (30%)**:
- Similar to ileocolitis
- Palpable RLQ mass
- B12 deficiency (terminal ileum)

**3. Colitis (20%)**:
- Bloody diarrhea
- Similar to UC
- May have rectal sparing

**4. Upper GI (5-15%)**:
- Nausea, vomiting
- Dysphagia
- Epigastric pain

**5. Perianal (40%)**:
- **Perianal fistulas** (most common fistula type)
- Skin tags
- Fissures, abscesses

#### **Ulcerative Colitis Extent**:

**1. Proctitis (30-40%)**:
- **Rectal bleeding** (small amounts)
- **Tenesmus** (common)
- Urgency
- Constipation (rectal inflammation)

**2. Left-sided/Distal Colitis (40-50%)**:
- Bloody diarrhea
- LLQ cramping
- Weight loss (moderate)

**3. Extensive/Pancolitis (20-30%)**:
- Severe bloody diarrhea (>6 stools/day)
- Abdominal pain
- Significant weight loss
- Fever, tachycardia
- **Highest cancer risk**

### Symptom Severity Classification

**Crohn's Disease Activity Index (CDAI)**:
- <150: Remission
- 150-220: Mild
- 220-450: Moderate
- >450: Severe

**Key Components**:
- Number of liquid stools
- Abdominal pain severity
- General well-being
- Extraintestinal manifestations
- Antidiarrheal use
- Abdominal mass
- Hematocrit
- Body weight

**Ulcerative Colitis - Truelove and Witts Severity Index**:

| Parameter | **Mild** | **Moderate** | **Severe** |
|-----------|----------|--------------|------------|
| **Stools/day** | <4 | 4-6 | **>6** |
| **Blood in stool** | Small amounts | Moderate | **Large** |
| **Fever** | None | Minimal | **>37.8°C** |
| **Heart Rate** | Normal | Moderate | **>90 bpm** |
| **Hemoglobin** | Normal | Moderate anemia | **<10.5 g/dL** |
| **ESR** | <30 | 30-60 | **>60 mm/hr** |

**Severe UC = Acute Severe UC (ASUC)**:
- **Truelove-Witts Criteria**: ≥6 bloody stools/day + systemic toxicity
- Medical emergency
- Requires hospitalization
- Risk of toxic megacolon

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Evaluation

**History**:
- Chronic diarrhea (>4 weeks)
- Rectal bleeding
- Abdominal pain
- Weight loss, fever
- Family history
- Smoking history
- Recent travel, infections

**Physical Examination**:
- Abdominal tenderness, mass
- Perianal inspection (tags, fistulas, fissures)
- Extraintestinal manifestations
- Signs of malnutrition

### Laboratory Investigations

**Inflammatory Markers**:
- **C-Reactive Protein (CRP)**:
  - Better correlates with CD than UC
  - Elevated in active disease
  - Normal CRP doesn't exclude IBD

- **Fecal Calprotectin**:
  - **>250 μg/g**: Suggests IBD (vs IBS)
  - **Sensitive** for mucosal inflammation
  - Used for:
    - Screening
    - Monitoring disease activity
    - Detecting relapse
  - NOT specific for IBD

**Stool Studies**:
- **Fecal occult blood**
- **Stool culture, C. diff toxin**: Rule out infection
- **Ova and parasites**

**Serology**:
- **Anti-Saccharomyces cerevisiae antibodies (ASCA)**: 50-70% in CD
- **Perinuclear anti-neutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies (p-ANCA)**: 50-70% in UC
- **Limited utility**: NOT diagnostic alone

**Complete Blood Count**:
- Anemia (iron deficiency, chronic disease, B12/folate)
- Leukocytosis
- Thrombocytosis (active inflammation)

**Nutritional Assessment**:
- Albumin (low)
- Iron studies
- Vitamin B12 (CD with ileal involvement)
- Vitamin D, Folate

### Endoscopic Evaluation

**Colonoscopy with Ileoscopy**:
- **Gold Standard** for diagnosis
- **Biopsies**: Multiple from all segments (even normal-appearing)
- Required for:
  - Diagnosis
  - Extent assessment
  - Surveillance for dysplasia

#### **Crohn's Disease Endoscopic Findings**:

**Macroscopic**:
- **Skip lesions** (discontinuous)
- **Aphthous ulcers** (earliest lesion)
- **Linear/serpentine ulcers**
- **"Cobblestoning"**: Deep ulcers with intervening edematous mucosa
- **Strictures**
- **Fistulous openings**
- **Rectal sparing** (common)

**Microscopic**:
- **Non-caseating granulomas** (30-50%) - HALLMARK
- Transmural inflammation
- Focal cryptitis
- Preserved goblet cells

#### **Ulcerative Colitis Endoscopic Findings**:

**Macroscopic**:
- **Continuous inflammation** from rectum
- **Loss of vascular pattern**
- **Erythema, friability**
- **Pseudopolyps** (inflammatory polyps)
- **Diffuse superficial ulceration**
- **NO skip lesions**

**Microscopic**:
- **Crypt abscesses** (neutrophils in crypts)
- **Crypt distortion**, branching
- **Goblet cell depletion**
- **Basal plasmacytosis**
- **NO granulomas**
- Mucosal/submucosal inflammation ONLY

**Mayo Endoscopic Score (UC)**:
- 0: Normal
- 1: Erythema, decreased vascular pattern
- 2: Friability, erosions
- 3: Spontaneous bleeding, ulceration

### Imaging

**Cross-Sectional Imaging** (for CD especially):

**CT/MR Enterography (CTE/MRE)**:
- **First-line** for small bowel CD
- Assess:
  - Bowel wall thickening
  - Mural enhancement
  - Strictures
  - Fistulas, abscesses
  - Mesenteric changes

**Findings**:
- **"Comb sign"**: Engorged vasa recta (mesenteric inflammation)
- **Mural stratification**: Submucosal edema
- **"Creeping fat"**: Mesenteric fat wrapping around bowel

**MR Enterography Advantages**:
- No radiation
- Preferred for young patients, surveillance
- Better soft tissue contrast

**Capsule Endoscopy**:
- **Small bowel evaluation** (if no stricture)
- **Contraindicated** if known/suspected stricture (capsule retention risk)
- Pre-screening with patency capsule or MRE

**Ultrasound**:
- Point-of-care assessment
- Bowel wall thickness
- Vascularity (Doppler)

**Plain Radiography**:
- **Acute Severe UC**: Rule out toxic megacolon, perforation
- **Toxic megacolon**: Colon diameter **>6 cm**

## Management

### Goals of Therapy
1. **Induce remission** (clinical, biochemical, endoscopic)
2. **Maintain remission**
3. **Mucosal healing** (reduces complications, hospitalization, surgery)
4. **Improve quality of life**
5. **Prevent complications**

### Treatment Approach

**Tailored Based on**:
- **Disease location** (CD: ileal, colonic, perianal; UC: extent)
- **Severity** (mild, moderate, severe)
- **Disease behavior** (CD: inflammatory, stricturing, penetrating)
- **Prior treatment response**

---

### Medical Management

#### **1. Aminosalicylates (5-ASA)**

**Mechanism**: Anti-inflammatory (↓ prostaglandins, leukotrienes, NF-κB)

**Indications**:
- **UC**: First-line for **mild-moderate disease**
  - **Proctitis**: Suppositories (Mesalamine 1 g PR daily)
  - **Left-sided**: Enema (Mesalamine 4 g PR daily) + oral
  - **Extensive**: Oral (2.4-4.8 g/day divided)

- **CD**: Limited efficacy
  - May use for **mild ileocolonic CD**
  - **NOT effective** for maintaining remission

**Formulations**:
- **Sulfasalazine**: 4-6 g/day (cheap, but side effects)
- **Mesalamine**: 2.4-4.8 g/day (better tolerated)
  - pH-dependent release
  - Time-dependent release

**Side Effects**:
- **Sulfasalazine**: Nausea, headache, rash, oligospermia (reversible), sulfa allergy
- **Mesalamine**: Generally well-tolerated
  - Rare: Interstitial nephritis, pancreatitis, paradoxical worsening

**Monitoring**:
- Baseline: CBC, renal function
- Periodic: Renal function (especially sulfasalazine)

---

#### **2. Corticosteroids**

**Mechanism**: Broad anti-inflammatory, immunosuppressive

**Indications**:
- **Moderate-severe UC or CD** (induction ONLY)
- **NOT for maintenance** (ineffective, steroid-dependent = escalate)

**Regimens**:

**Oral**:
- **Prednisone/Prednisolone**: 40-60 mg daily
  - Taper over 8-12 weeks after response
  - If no response in 2 weeks → escalate

**IV** (Severe UC):
- **Hydrocortisone**: 100 mg IV q6-8h OR
- **Methylprednisolone**: 60 mg IV daily
- Used in **Acute Severe UC (ASUC)**

**Topical** (Distal UC):
- **Budesonide foam/enema**: 2 mg PR daily

**Budesonide** (for CD):
- **Ileal/Right colonic CD**: Budesonide 9 mg daily × 8-12 weeks
- First-pass metabolism → fewer systemic side effects
- NOT for maintenance

**Side Effects**:
- Short-term: Insomnia, mood changes, hyperglycemia, hypertension
- Long-term: Osteoporosis, cataracts, weight gain, adrenal suppression, infections

**Steroid-Refractory**:
- No response after 7-10 days IV steroids (ASUC) → Consider rescue therapy or colectomy
- **Rescue**: Infliximab or Cyclosporine

**Steroid-Dependent**:
- Requires steroids to maintain remission
- **Escalate** to immunomodulators or biologics

---

#### **3. Immunomodulators**

**Thiopurines**:

**Azathioprine (AZA)**: 2-2.5 mg/kg/day
**6-Mercaptopurine (6-MP)**: 1-1.5 mg/kg/day

**Mechanism**: Purine analog → immunosuppression

**Indications**:
- **Maintain remission** (both UC and CD)
- **Steroid-sparing**
- Often combined with biologics (reduce immunogenicity)

**Onset**: **3-6 months** (slow)

**Pre-Treatment**:
- **TPMT enzyme activity**: Low = high toxicity risk
  - Low/absent: Avoid or reduce dose (0.5-1 mg/kg)
  - Heterozygous: Reduce dose
  - Normal: Standard dose

- **Hepatitis B, C, HIV, TB screening**
- **Varicella immunity**

**Monitoring**:
- **CBC, LFTs**: Every 1-2 weeks × 2 months, then every 3 months
- **6-TGN levels**: Therapeutic 230-450 pmol/8×10⁸ RBC

**Side Effects**:
- **Myelosuppression** (dose-related)
- **Hepatotoxicity**
- **Pancreatitis** (3-7%, idiosyncratic, discontinue permanently)
- **Infections**
- **Lymphoma risk**: Small increase (4/10,000 patient-years)
- **Non-melanoma skin cancer**: Increased risk

**Methotrexate**:

**Dose**:
- **Induction (CD)**: 25 mg IM/SC weekly × 16 weeks
- **Maintenance**: 15 mg IM/SC weekly

**Indications**:
- **Crohn's disease** (alternative to thiopurines)
- **NOT effective in UC**

**Contraindications**:
- **Pregnancy** (Category X - teratogenic)
- Liver disease
- Alcohol abuse

**Monitoring**:
- CBC, LFTs every 4-8 weeks
- Baseline chest X-ray

**Side Effects**:
- Nausea (give with folic acid 1 mg daily)
- Hepatotoxicity
- Myelosuppression
- Pneumonitis (rare)

---

#### **4. Biologic Therapy**

**Anti-TNF Agents**:

**Infliximab (IFX)**:
- **Chimeric** (mouse-human) monoclonal antibody
- **Indications**: Moderate-severe CD and UC
- **Dosing**:
  - **Induction**: 5 mg/kg IV at 0, 2, 6 weeks
  - **Maintenance**: 5 mg/kg IV every 8 weeks
  - May increase to 10 mg/kg or q6weeks if loss of response

- **Special Uses**:
  - **Fistulizing CD**: Higher dose often needed
  - **Acute Severe UC**: Rescue therapy (vs cyclosporine)

**Adalimumab (ADA)**:
- **Fully human** monoclonal antibody (less immunogenic)
- **SC injection** (patient-administered)
- **Dosing**:
  - **Induction**: 160 mg SC week 0, 80 mg week 2
  - **Maintenance**: 40 mg SC every 2 weeks

**Certolizumab Pegol**:
- PEGylated Fab fragment
- **CD only** (not approved for UC)
- **Safe in pregnancy** (doesn't cross placenta)

**Golimumab**:
- **UC only**
- SC injection monthly

**Pre-Treatment Screening**:
- **Tuberculosis**:
  - TST or IGRA (QuantiFERON)
  - Chest X-ray
  - If latent TB: Treat with INH × 9 months (start ≥3 weeks before anti-TNF)

- **Hepatitis B**: HBsAg, anti-HBc, anti-HBs
  - If positive: Consult hepatology, consider prophylaxis

- **Hepatitis C, HIV**
- **Varicella immunity**

**Monitoring**:
- **Infusion reactions** (infliximab)
- **Tuberculosis reactivation**
- **Opportunistic infections**
- **Therapeutic drug monitoring (TDM)**:
  - **Infliximab trough**: 5-10 μg/mL
  - **Adalimumab trough**: 8-12 μg/mL
  - **Anti-drug antibodies**: If low drug levels + high antibodies → switch

**Side Effects**:
- **Infections**: TB, fungal, viral
- **Infusion/injection reactions**
- **Hepatotoxicity**
- **Heart failure exacerbation** (contraindicated in NYHA III-IV)
- **Demyelinating disorders** (caution/contraindicated)
- **Malignancy**: Lymphoma (rare), skin cancer
- **Paradoxical psoriasis**

**Loss of Response**:
- **Optimize dosing**: Increase dose or shorten interval
- **Check drug levels**: Low levels → increase dose; antibodies → switch
- **Switch within class** (anti-TNF to anti-TNF) or **out of class**

---

**Anti-Integrin Agents**:

**Vedolizumab (VDZ)**:
- **Gut-selective** α4β7 integrin antagonist
- **Indications**: Moderate-severe CD and UC
- **Dosing**:
  - **Induction**: 300 mg IV at 0, 2, 6 weeks
  - **Maintenance**: 300 mg IV every 8 weeks

- **Advantages**:
  - **Gut-selective** (lower systemic infection risk)
  - **No TB reactivation**
  - Good for patients with contraindications to anti-TNF

- **Slower onset**: May take 12-14 weeks (vs 4-6 weeks for anti-TNF)

**Side Effects**:
- Infusion reactions (rare)
- **Progressive multifocal leukoencephalopathy (PML)**: Very rare (vs natalizumab)

---

**Anti-IL-12/23 Agents**:

**Ustekinumab (UST)**:
- **IL-12/23 inhibitor**
- **Indications**: Moderate-severe CD (also UC in some regions)
- **Dosing**:
  - **Induction**: Weight-based IV (260-520 mg at week 0)
  - **Maintenance**: 90 mg SC every 8-12 weeks

- **Good option** for anti-TNF failures

**Side Effects**:
- Infections (lower risk than anti-TNF)
- Injection site reactions

---

**JAK Inhibitors**:

**Tofacitinib**:
- **Oral** small molecule
- **UC only** (not CD)
- **Dosing**:
  - **Induction**: 10 mg PO BID × 8 weeks
  - **Maintenance**: 5 mg PO BID

**Advantages**: Oral, rapid onset

**Side Effects**:
- **Infections** (herpes zoster - vaccinate)
- **Thromboembolism** (black box warning)
- **Malignancy**
- Lipid abnormalities

**Monitoring**:
- CBC, lipids
- Hepatitis, TB screening

---

#### **5. Antibiotics**

**Limited Role in IBD**:

**Metronidazole + Ciprofloxacin**:
- **Perianal Crohn's disease**
- **Crohn's colitis** (adjunct)
- **Pouchitis** (post-colectomy UC)

**NOT for induction/maintenance** of luminal IBD

### Surgical Management

#### **Indications for Surgery**:

**General**:
- **Medical refractory disease**
- **Strictures** with obstruction
- **Fistulas, abscesses** (CD)
- **Perforation**
- **Toxic megacolon**
- **Dysplasia/Cancer**
- **Growth retardation** (pediatric)

#### **Crohn's Disease**:

**NOT Curative** (high recurrence rate):
- **70-80%** require surgery eventually
- **Recurrence**: 50% at 10 years post-resection

**Operations**:
1. **Ileocolic resection** (most common)
   - For terminal ileal disease

2. **Stricturoplasty**:
   - Preserve bowel length
   - For short strictures (<10 cm)

3. **Segmental resection**:
   - Limited resection (preserve length)

4. **Seton placement**:
   - For complex perianal fistulas

5. **Abscess drainage**:
   - Percutaneous or surgical

**Post-Op**:
- **High recurrence risk**: Endoscopic recurrence 70-90% at 1 year
- **Prophylaxis**:
  - **Anti-TNF** (most effective)
  - **Metronidazole** (3 months)
  - **Thiopurines**

- **Surveillance endoscopy**: 6-12 months post-op

#### **Ulcerative Colitis**:

**CURATIVE** (total proctocolectomy removes all diseased tissue):

**Operations**:

1. **Total Proctocolectomy with Ileal Pouch-Anal Anastomosis (IPAA)**:
   - **Most common** (young patients desiring continence)
   - **J-pouch** (most common), K-pouch, S-pouch
   - Usually 2-3 stage procedure
   - **Complications**:
     - **Pouchitis** (50% at 10 years)
     - Incontinence
     - Infertility (women)

2. **Total Proctocolectomy with End Ileostomy**:
   - Permanent ileostomy
   - For elderly, poor sphincter function

3. **Subtotal Colectomy with Ileostomy**:
   - **Emergency** for toxic megacolon, perforation
   - Rectum left in situ (definitive surgery later)

**Indications**:
- **Acute Severe UC (ASUC)** refractory to medical therapy
- **Dysplasia/Cancer**
- Chronic refractory disease
- Intolerable medication side effects

---

### Management of Acute Severe Ulcerative Colitis (ASUC)

**Truelove-Witts Criteria**:
- **≥6 bloody stools/day** + systemic toxicity (fever >37.8°C, HR >90, anemia, ESR >30)

**Hospital Admission**:

**1. Initial Assessment**:
- IV access, NPO
- **Plain abdominal X-ray**: Rule out toxic megacolon (colon >6 cm)
- **Stool culture, C. diff**: Rule out infection
- **Bloods**: CBC, CRP, albumin, electrolytes

**2. Medical Management**:

**IV Corticosteroids**:
- **Hydrocortisone 100 mg q6h** OR **Methylprednisolone 60 mg daily**
- **Assess response at Day 3**:
  - **Day 3 stool frequency** OR **Oxford Index** (stool freq + CRP)

**If No Improvement by Day 3-5**:
- **Rescue Therapy**:

  **A. Infliximab**:
  - 5 mg/kg IV at 0, 2, 6 weeks
  - **Preferred** if long-term biologic likely needed
  - Lower colectomy rate long-term

  **B. Cyclosporine**:
  - 2-4 mg/kg/day IV continuous infusion
  - **Target trough**: 200-400 ng/mL
  - **Bridge to thiopurines** (start AZA immediately)
  - **Avoid if**:
    - Cholesterol <120 mg/dL (seizure risk)
    - Concurrent use with IFX (↑ infection)
  - **Short-term only** (3-6 months)

**3. Supportive Care**:
- Fluid resuscitation, electrolyte correction
- **NG decompression** if ileus
- **Nutritional support**
- **VTE prophylaxis** (high thrombosis risk)
- **Antibiotics** ONLY if concern for infection/perforation

**4. Surgery**:
- **Indications**:
  - No response to rescue therapy by day 7
  - **Toxic megacolon** (colon >6 cm)
  - **Perforation**
  - **Massive bleeding**

- **Subtotal colectomy + end ileostomy** (emergent)

## Complications

### Crohn's Disease Complications

**1. Strictures (Fibrostenotic)**:
- **Chronic inflammation → fibrosis → narrowing**
- **Presentation**: Obstructive symptoms (pain, distension, nausea, vomiting)
- **Diagnosis**: CT/MR enterography
- **Management**:
  - **Endoscopic balloon dilation** (short strictures <4 cm, no fistula)
  - **Surgery**: Stricturoplasty or resection

**2. Fistulas (Penetrating)**:
- **Transmural inflammation → erosion into adjacent structures**
- **Types**:
  - **Perianal** (most common): Anorectal, rectovaginal
  - **Enteroenteric**: Small bowel to small bowel
  - **Enterovesical**: Bowel to bladder (pneumaturia, fecaluria, recurrent UTI)
  - **Enterocutaneous**: Bowel to skin
  - **Enterovaginal**: Bowel to vagina (feculent discharge)

- **Diagnosis**:
  - **MR Enterography/Pelvis** (best for perianal)
  - **CT Enterography**
  - **Exam under anesthesia** (perianal)

- **Management**:
  - **Medical**:
    - **Anti-TNF** (infliximab) - most effective
    - **Antibiotics** (metronidazole + ciprofloxacin) - adjunct
    - **Immunomodulators**

  - **Surgical**:
    - **Seton drainage** (perianal - non-cutting seton for drainage)
    - **Fistulotomy** (simple, low fistulas)
    - **Resection** (internal fistulas)

**3. Abscesses**:
- Intra-abdominal, perianal
- **Diagnosis**: CT/MRI
- **Management**:
  - **Drainage** (percutaneous or surgical) - MUST drain before biologics
  - **Antibiotics**
  - **After drainage**: Start/optimize medical therapy

**4. Malabsorption**:
- **Bile salt malabsorption**: Terminal ileal disease (diarrhea, steatorrhea)
  - **Tx**: Cholestyramine
- **Vitamin B12 deficiency**: Terminal ileum (megaloblastic anemia)
  - **Tx**: B12 supplementation
- **Fat-soluble vitamins** (A, D, E, K)
- **Protein-losing enteropathy**

**5. Small Bowel Obstruction**:
- Strictures
- Adhesions (post-surgical)

**6. Perianal Disease**:
- Fissures, fistulas, abscesses, skin tags
- May precede intestinal symptoms

---

### Ulcerative Colitis Complications

**1. Toxic Megacolon**:
- **Life-threatening complication**
- **Definition**: Colon diameter **>6 cm** + systemic toxicity
- **Precipitants**:
  - Severe colitis
  - **Opioids, anticholinergics, antidiarrheals** (precipitate)
  - Hypokalemia, hypocalcemia
  - Barium enema

- **Clinical**:
  - Severe abdominal pain, distension
  - Decreased bowel sounds
  - Fever, tachycardia, hypotension
  - ↓ Stool output (paradoxical)

- **Diagnosis**: **Plain abdominal X-ray** (colon >6 cm)

- **Management**:
  - **ICU admission**
  - **NPO, NG suction**
  - **IV fluids, electrolyte correction** (K⁺, Mg²⁺)
  - **IV corticosteroids** (hydrocortisone 100 mg q6h)
  - **Broad-spectrum antibiotics**
  - **NO antidiarrheals, opioids, anticholinergics**
  - **Avoid colonoscopy** (perforation risk)

  - **If no improvement in 24-48 hours OR signs of perforation**:
    - **Emergency subtotal colectomy + end ileostomy**

**2. Perforation**:
- Free perforation (peritonitis)
- Often with toxic megacolon
- **Emergency colectomy**

**3. Massive Hemorrhage**:
- **Rare** (1-4%)
- Requires transfusion >6 units/24 hrs
- If uncontrolled → **colectomy**

**4. Colorectal Cancer**:
- **Significantly increased risk**
- **Risk factors**:
  - **Duration**: 1% after 10 yrs, 2% after 20 yrs, 18% after 30 yrs
  - **Extent**: Pancolitis > left-sided > proctitis
  - **Primary sclerosing cholangitis** (5× increased risk)
  - **Family history** of CRC
  - **Severe/continuous inflammation**

- **Surveillance**:
  - **Start**: 8 years after diagnosis for pancolitis, 15 years for left-sided
  - **Frequency**:
    - **No PSC**: Every 1-3 years (depends on risk factors)
    - **PSC**: **Annually** from diagnosis

  - **Method**: Colonoscopy with **chromoendoscopy** + **random biopsies** (q10 cm × 4 quadrants)

- **Dysplasia Management**:
  - **High-grade dysplasia**: **Colectomy** (50% have cancer)
  - **Low-grade dysplasia**: Endoscopic resection (if visible) vs colectomy
  - **Indefinite for dysplasia**: Repeat in 3-6 months

**5. Strictures**:
- **Uncommon** in UC (suspect malignancy)
- **Evaluate**: Biopsy to rule out cancer

---

### Common to Both

**Venous Thromboembolism (VTE)**:
- **3× increased risk** (hypercoagulable state)
- DVT, PE, CVT (cerebral venous thrombosis)
- **Prophylaxis**:
  - Hospitalized patients: Heparin
  - Active disease: Consider prophylaxis

**Osteoporosis**:
- Chronic inflammation
- Corticosteroid use
- Malabsorption (vitamin D, calcium)
- **Screening**: DEXA scan (all patients on prolonged steroids)
- **Prevention**: Calcium + Vitamin D, bisphosphonates if indicated

## Extraintestinal Manifestations

**Prevalence**: 25-40% of IBD patients

### Classification

**1. Related to Disease Activity**:
- **Improve** with IBD treatment

**Peripheral Arthropathy (Type 1)**:
- **Large joints** (knees, ankles, hips, wrists)
- **Asymmetric, migratory**
- **Pauciarticular** (<5 joints)
- **Non-erosive** (no joint destruction)
- **Parallels IBD activity**
- **Treatment**: Treat underlying IBD

**Erythema Nodosum**:
- **Tender, raised, red nodules** on shins
- **Most common skin manifestation**
- **UC > CD**
- **Parallels IBD activity**
- **Treatment**: Treat IBD, NSAIDs (if tolerated), steroids

**Episcleritis**:
- **Red, painful eye** (less severe than uveitis)
- **Self-limited**
- **Parallels IBD activity**

**Aphthous Stomatitis**:
- Oral ulcers
- Common

---

**2. Independent of Disease Activity**:
- Do **NOT** improve with IBD treatment

**Axial Arthropathy**:
- **Ankylosing Spondylitis (AS)**:
  - **Chronic back pain** (worse at night, improves with exercise)
  - **Morning stiffness** >30 minutes
  - **Sacroiliitis** (bilateral)
  - **HLA-B27** positive (70-90%)
  - **X-ray**: Bamboo spine, syndesmophytes
  - **Does NOT parallel IBD activity**
  - **Treatment**: NSAIDs, anti-TNF (if severe)

- **Sacroiliitis**:
  - **Isolated** (without full AS)
  - **Asymptomatic** or back pain
  - **15-20%** of IBD patients

**Pyoderma Gangrenosum**:
- **Painful ulcers** with **purple, undermined borders**
- Usually **lower legs**
- **CD > UC**
- **Does NOT parallel IBD activity**
- **Associated with severe IBD**
- **Treatment**:
  - **Topical/intralesional steroids**
  - **Systemic steroids**
  - **Anti-TNF** (very effective)
  - **Avoid debridement** (worsens - pathergy)

**Uveitis**:
- **Painful, red eye with photophobia, blurred vision**
- **Medical emergency** (can cause blindness)
- **Does NOT parallel IBD**
- **Treatment**: Ophthalmology referral (topical steroids, cycloplegics)

**Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC)**:
- **Chronic cholestatic liver disease**
- **Fibrosing inflammation** of bile ducts
- **UC >> CD** (70-80% have UC)
- **5% of UC patients** have PSC
- **Does NOT improve** with colectomy

- **Presentation**:
  - Asymptomatic (incidental ↑ ALP)
  - Pruritis, jaundice (advanced)
  - Cholangitis (rare)

- **Diagnosis**:
  - **↑ ALP** (cholestatic pattern)
  - **MRCP**: **Beading** of bile ducts (strictures + dilations)
  - **ERCP**: Diagnostic (+ therapeutic if dominant stricture)

- **Complications**:
  - **Cholangiocarcinoma** (10-20% lifetime risk)
  - Cirrhosis, portal hypertension
  - **5× increased CRC risk** (even after colectomy)

- **Management**:
  - **Ursodeoxycholic acid** (UDCA): May improve LFTs (survival benefit unclear)
  - **ERCP + stenting** for dominant strictures
  - **Liver transplant** (only curative)
  - **Annual colonoscopy** (↑ CRC risk)
  - **Annual MRCP or ultrasound** + **CA 19-9** (cholangiocarcinoma surveillance)

## High-Yield Points

### Must-Know Differentiating Features: CD vs UC

1. **Crohn's**:
   - **Any GI location**, **transmural**, **skip lesions**, **granulomas** (30-50%)
   - **Perianal disease** (40%)
   - **Fistulas common**
   - **Surgery NOT curative**
   - **Smoking WORSENS**

2. **UC**:
   - **Colon/rectum ONLY**, **mucosal**, **continuous**, **NO granulomas**
   - **Rectal involvement** (95-100%)
   - **Fistulas rare**
   - **Surgery CURATIVE** (total colectomy)
   - **Smoking PROTECTIVE** (paradoxically)

### Key Endoscopic/Histologic Features

**CD**:
- **Cobblestoning**, **linear ulcers**, **skip lesions**
- **Non-caseating granulomas** (pathognomonic if present)
- Transmural inflammation

**UC**:
- **Continuous from rectum**, **loss of vascular pattern**, **pseudopolyps**
- **Crypt abscesses**, **goblet cell depletion**, **basal plasmacytosis**
- Mucosal inflammation only

### Fecal Calprotectin

- **>250 μg/g**: Suggests organic disease (IBD) vs functional (IBS)
- **Useful for**:
  - Screening
  - Monitoring activity
  - Detecting relapse
- **NOT specific** for IBD (any inflammation)

### Treatment Pearls

1. **5-ASA**:
   - **First-line mild-moderate UC** (NOT CD)
   - **Topical > oral** for distal disease

2. **Steroids**:
   - **Induction ONLY** (never maintenance)
   - **Steroid-dependent** = escalate to immunomodulators/biologics

3. **Thiopurines (AZA/6-MP)**:
   - **Check TPMT** before starting
   - **Slow onset** (3-6 months)
   - **Monitor CBC, LFTs** regularly
   - **Pancreatitis** = discontinue permanently

4. **Anti-TNF**:
   - **Screen for TB, Hep B** before starting
   - **Infliximab**: Best for fistulizing CD, acute severe UC
   - **Therapeutic drug monitoring**: Optimize dosing

5. **Vedolizumab**:
   - **Gut-selective** (safer in elderly, infection risk)
   - **Slower onset** (12-14 weeks)
   - **No TB reactivation**

### Acute Severe UC (ASUC)

- **≥6 bloody stools/day + systemic toxicity**
- **IV steroids** × 3-5 days
- **If no improvement**: Rescue (Infliximab or Cyclosporine) vs Surgery
- **Toxic megacolon** (colon >6 cm): Emergency colectomy

### Surgical Considerations

**CD**:
- **NOT curative** (70-80% require surgery)
- **High recurrence** (50% at 10 years)
- **Post-op prophylaxis**: Anti-TNF most effective

**UC**:
- **CURATIVE** (total proctocolectomy)
- **IPAA** (ileal pouch): Young patients, continent
- **Pouchitis**: 50% at 10 years

### Colorectal Cancer Surveillance (UC)

- **Start**: 8 years (pancolitis), 15 years (left-sided)
- **Frequency**: 1-3 years (higher if PSC - **annual**)
- **PSC**: **5× CRC risk** + annual colonoscopy from diagnosis
- **High-grade dysplasia**: **Colectomy** (50% have cancer)

### Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC)

- **UC >> CD** (70-80% have UC, 5% UC have PSC)
- **Does NOT improve** with IBD treatment or colectomy
- **Cholangiocarcinoma risk**: 10-20%
- **↑ CRC risk**: 5× (annual colonoscopy)
- **MRCP**: **Beading** of bile ducts
- **Treatment**: UDCA, liver transplant (curative)

### Extraintestinal Manifestations Mnemonic: "PEAS"

**Parallels IBD activity**:
- **P**eripheral arthritis (pauciarticular)
- **E**rythema nodosum
- **A**phthous ulcers
- **S**kin/eye (episcleritis)

**Does NOT parallel**:
- **Axial arthropathy** (AS, sacroiliitis)
- **Pyoderma gangrenosum**
- **Uveitis**
- **PSC**

### Perianal Crohn's Disease

- **40%** of CD patients
- **Types**: Fistulas (most common), fissures, abscesses, skin tags
- **Treatment**:
  - **Medical**: Anti-TNF + antibiotics (metro + cipro)
  - **Surgical**: Seton drainage (non-cutting), fistulotomy
  - **Drain abscesses** before starting biologics

### Fistulizing Crohn's Disease

- **Most common**: **Perianal**
- **Other types**: Enteroenteric, enterovesical (pneumaturia), enterovaginal
- **Best medical therapy**: **Infliximab**
- **Rule out abscess** (CT/MRI) before anti-TNF

### Pregnancy and IBD

- **Conception**: Best during remission
- **Medications**:
  - **Safe**: 5-ASA, steroids, AZA/6-MP, anti-TNF (except certolizumab - safest)
  - **Avoid**: Methotrexate (Category X - teratogenic), tofacitinib
- **Disease activity**: Maintain remission (active disease worsens outcomes)

## Practice MCQs

### Question 1
A 25-year-old woman presents with 6 months of abdominal pain, non-bloody diarrhea, and 5 kg weight loss. Examination reveals right lower quadrant tenderness and a palpable mass. Colonoscopy shows patchy inflammation with skip lesions, cobblestoning, and terminal ileal ulceration. Biopsy reveals non-caseating granulomas. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?

A) Ulcerative colitis
B) Crohn's disease
C) Intestinal tuberculosis
D) Colorectal cancer

**Answer**: **B) Crohn's disease**

**Explanation**:
- **Crohn's disease** is characterized by:
  - **Transmural inflammation** (can present as palpable mass)
  - **Skip lesions** (discontinuous inflammation)
  - **Terminal ileal involvement** (most common site)
  - **Cobblestoning** (deep ulcers with intervening edema)
  - **Non-caseating granulomas** (30-50% - pathognomonic if present)
  - **Non-bloody diarrhea** (ileal disease)

- **UC** would show:
  - **Continuous** inflammation from rectum
  - **Bloody diarrhea**
  - **Rectal involvement** (always)
  - **NO granulomas**

- **Intestinal TB** can mimic CD but:
  - **Caseating granulomas** (not non-caseating)
  - AFB staining positive
  - Risk factors (endemic area, immunosuppression)

### Question 2
A 30-year-old man with known Crohn's disease presents with pneumaturia and recurrent urinary tract infections. CT scan shows an abnormal connection between the sigmoid colon and bladder. What type of complication has developed?

A) Enterovaginal fistula
B) Enterocutaneous fistula
C) Enterovesical fistula
D) Perianal fistula

**Answer**: **C) Enterovesical fistula**

**Explanation**:
- **Enterovesical fistula** = Abnormal connection between bowel and bladder
- **Classic triad**:
  - **Pneumaturia** (air in urine - pathognomonic)
  - **Fecaluria** (stool in urine)
  - **Recurrent UTIs**

- **Diagnosis**: CT with oral contrast (contrast in bladder), cystoscopy
- **Treatment**:
  - Medical: Anti-TNF + antibiotics
  - Surgical: Resection of involved bowel segment + bladder repair

- **Other fistula types**:
  - **Enterovaginal**: Feculent vaginal discharge
  - **Enterocutaneous**: Bowel to skin (stool from wound)
  - **Perianal**: Most common in CD (40%)

### Question 3
A 28-year-old woman with ulcerative colitis is admitted with 8 bloody stools per day, fever 38.5°C, heart rate 110 bpm, and hemoglobin 9 g/dL. She is started on IV hydrocortisone. On day 4, her stool frequency remains 7 per day. What is the MOST appropriate next step?

A) Continue IV steroids for another week
B) Start cyclosporine or infliximab (rescue therapy)
C) Immediate colectomy
D) Add oral mesalamine

**Answer**: **B) Start cyclosporine or infliximab (rescue therapy)**

**Explanation**:
- This is **Acute Severe UC (ASUC)**:
  - **≥6 bloody stools/day** + systemic toxicity (fever, tachycardia, anemia)

- **Management**:
  - **IV steroids** (hydrocortisone 100 mg q6h or methylprednisolone 60 mg daily)
  - **Assess response at Day 3-5**:
    - If **no improvement** → **Rescue therapy** or **colectomy**

- **Rescue therapy options**:
  - **Infliximab** (5 mg/kg IV):
    - **Preferred** if long-term biologic likely
    - Lower colectomy rate

  - **Cyclosporine** (2-4 mg/kg/day IV):
    - Bridge to thiopurines
    - Avoid if cholesterol <120 (seizure risk)
    - Target trough 200-400 ng/mL

- **Surgery** (subtotal colectomy):
  - If no response to rescue therapy by day 7
  - Toxic megacolon, perforation, massive bleeding

- **Continuing steroids** beyond 5-7 days without improvement increases complications
- **5-ASA** is ineffective in severe disease

### Question 4
Which of the following extraintestinal manifestations of IBD does NOT parallel disease activity and will NOT improve with treatment of the underlying IBD?

A) Peripheral arthritis
B) Erythema nodosum
C) Pyoderma gangrenosum
D) Episcleritis

**Answer**: **C) Pyoderma gangrenosum**

**Explanation**:
- **Extraintestinal manifestations** are classified by relationship to IBD activity:

**Parallel IBD activity** (improve with IBD treatment):
- **Peripheral arthritis** (pauciarticular, large joints)
- **Erythema nodosum** (tender nodules on shins)
- **Episcleritis** (red eye, mild)
- **Aphthous stomatitis** (oral ulcers)

**Independent of IBD activity** (do NOT improve with IBD treatment):
- **Pyoderma gangrenosum**:
  - Painful ulcers with **purple, undermined borders**
  - Usually lower legs
  - **Treatment**: Topical/systemic steroids, **anti-TNF** (very effective)
  - **Avoid debridement** (pathergy - worsens with trauma)

- **Ankylosing spondylitis/Sacroiliitis**
- **Uveitis** (painful eye, photophobia - ophthalmology emergency)
- **Primary sclerosing cholangitis**

### Question 5
A 35-year-old man with ulcerative pancolitis for 12 years presents with progressive fatigue and pruritis. Labs show elevated alkaline phosphatase (450 U/L) with normal bilirubin. MRCP shows multifocal strictures and dilations of intrahepatic and extrahepatic bile ducts ("beading"). What is the diagnosis and MOST important surveillance implication?

A) Autoimmune hepatitis; monitor for cirrhosis
B) Primary biliary cholangitis; annual liver biopsy
C) Primary sclerosing cholangitis; annual colonoscopy for colorectal cancer
D) Drug-induced hepatotoxicity; stop all IBD medications

**Answer**: **C) Primary sclerosing cholangitis; annual colonoscopy for colorectal cancer**

**Explanation**:
- **Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC)**:
  - **Cholestatic liver disease** (↑ ALP >> AST/ALT)
  - **MRCP**: **"Beading"** (strictures + dilations of bile ducts) - diagnostic
  - **UC >> CD** (70-80% of PSC have UC, 5% of UC have PSC)

- **Complications**:
  - **Cholangiocarcinoma**: 10-20% lifetime risk
  - **Colorectal cancer**: **5× increased risk** (even after colectomy)
  - Cirrhosis, portal hypertension

- **Surveillance**:
  - **Annual colonoscopy** with chromoendoscopy (from diagnosis) - **CRITICAL**
  - **Annual MRCP or ultrasound** + **CA 19-9** (cholangiocarcinoma)

- **Management**:
  - **UDCA**: May improve LFTs (survival benefit unclear)
  - **Dominant stricture**: ERCP + balloon dilation/stenting
  - **Liver transplant**: Only curative option
  - **Does NOT improve** with IBD treatment or colectomy

- **PSC-specific colorectal cancer risk**:
  - **5× higher** than UC alone
  - **Annual** surveillance (not 1-3 years like UC without PSC)
  - Risk persists even after colectomy

### Question 6
A 40-year-old woman with Crohn's disease is being started on azathioprine. Before initiating therapy, which enzyme should be checked to assess for increased toxicity risk?

A) G6PD
B) TPMT (Thiopurine methyltransferase)
C) CYP2C19
D) Pseudocholinesterase

**Answer**: **B) TPMT (Thiopurine methyltransferase)**

**Explanation**:
- **Thiopurines** (azathioprine, 6-mercaptopurine):
  - Metabolized by **TPMT enzyme**
  - **Low/absent TPMT activity** → **severe myelosuppression** (life-threatening)

- **TPMT testing** (before starting):
  - **Low/absent**: Avoid or reduce dose to 0.5-1 mg/kg (↑ toxicity risk)
  - **Heterozygous** (intermediate): Reduce dose
  - **Normal**: Standard dose (AZA 2-2.5 mg/kg)

- **AZA Monitoring**:
  - **CBC, LFTs**: Every 1-2 weeks × 2 months, then every 3 months
  - **6-TGN levels**: Therapeutic 230-450 pmol/8×10⁸ RBC

- **Side Effects**:
  - **Myelosuppression** (dose-related)
  - **Pancreatitis** (3-7%, idiosyncratic - **discontinue permanently**)
  - **Hepatotoxicity**
  - **Lymphoma**: Small increased risk
  - **Non-melanoma skin cancer**: Increased risk

- **Other enzyme deficiencies**:
  - **G6PD**: Hemolysis with oxidative drugs (sulfasalazine can cause, but not as critical)
  - **CYP2C19**: Proton pump inhibitor metabolism
  - **Pseudocholinesterase**: Succinylcholine metabolism

### Question 7
A 26-year-old man with perianal Crohn's disease has persistent perianal drainage. MRI pelvis shows a complex perianal fistula with a small abscess. What is the MOST appropriate initial management?

A) Start infliximab immediately
B) Drain abscess, then start infliximab + antibiotics
C) Seton placement alone
D) Fecal diversion

**Answer**: **B) Drain abscess, then start infliximab + antibiotics**

**Explanation**:
- **Perianal Crohn's disease** (40% of CD):
  - Fistulas, abscesses, fissures, skin tags

- **Management principles**:
  1. **Rule out/drain abscess FIRST** (before biologics)
     - CT/MRI pelvic
     - **Exam under anesthesia (EUA)** - gold standard for perianal assessment
     - **Antibiotics contraindicated if undrained abscess** (can worsen)

  2. **Medical therapy**:
     - **Infliximab** (anti-TNF) - **most effective** for fistula healing
     - **Antibiotics**: Metronidazole + Ciprofloxacin (adjunct)
     - **Immunomodulators**: AZA/6-MP (maintenance)

  3. **Surgical**:
     - **Non-cutting seton**: Drainage, prevent abscess (long-term)
     - **Fistulotomy**: Simple, low fistulas
     - **Fecal diversion**: Refractory cases (stoma)

- **Starting anti-TNF with undrained abscess** = **contraindicated** (sepsis risk)

- **Perianal fistula healing**:
  - **Infliximab**: 30-50% complete healing
  - Often requires combination (anti-TNF + seton + antibiotics)
  - May need long-term maintenance

### Question 8
Which of the following is the PRIMARY advantage of vedolizumab over anti-TNF agents in treating IBD?

A) Faster onset of action
B) Oral administration
C) Gut-selective with lower systemic infection risk
D) Lower cost

**Answer**: **C) Gut-selective with lower systemic infection risk**

**Explanation**:
- **Vedolizumab** (VDZ):
  - **α4β7 integrin antagonist**
  - **Gut-selective** (blocks lymphocyte trafficking to GI tract)

- **Advantages**:
  - **Lower systemic infection risk** (vs anti-TNF)
  - **No tuberculosis reactivation** (vs anti-TNF)
  - **Good safety profile** in elderly, immunocompromised
  - Good option for **anti-TNF contraindications** (heart failure, demyelinating disorders, recurrent infections)

- **Disadvantages**:
  - **Slower onset**: 12-14 weeks (vs 4-6 weeks for anti-TNF)
  - **IV infusion** (not oral)
  - **Similar cost** to anti-TNF

- **Dosing**:
  - **Induction**: 300 mg IV at 0, 2, 6 weeks
  - **Maintenance**: 300 mg IV every 8 weeks

- **Side Effects**:
  - Generally well-tolerated
  - Infusion reactions (rare)
  - **PML**: Very rare (unlike natalizumab)

### Question 9
A 45-year-old woman with a 20-year history of ulcerative pancolitis undergoes surveillance colonoscopy. Biopsies reveal high-grade dysplasia in the ascending colon. What is the MOST appropriate management?

A) Repeat colonoscopy in 3 months
B) Endoscopic mucosal resection
C) Total proctocolectomy
D) Intensify medical therapy with biologics

**Answer**: **C) Total proctocolectomy**

**Explanation**:
- **Colorectal cancer risk in UC**:
  - Significantly increased with duration and extent
  - **Surveillance**: Start 8 years (pancolitis), 15 years (left-sided)

- **Dysplasia management**:

  **High-Grade Dysplasia (HGD)**:
  - **Total proctocolectomy** recommended
  - **50% have concurrent cancer** (may be invisible)
  - **Endoscopic resection** only if:
    - Dysplasia in discrete, completely resectable lesion
    - No flat dysplasia
    - Close surveillance acceptable

  **Low-Grade Dysplasia (LGD)**:
  - **Visible lesion**: Endoscopic resection + surveillance
  - **Flat/invisible**: Consider colectomy vs intensive surveillance (every 3-6 months)

  **Indefinite for Dysplasia**:
  - Repeat colonoscopy in 3-6 months
  - Treat inflammation (may improve grading)

- **This patient**:
  - **20-year pancolitis** (very high cancer risk)
  - **High-grade dysplasia** → **Colectomy** (curative)
  - **IPAA** (ileal pouch) if good candidate

- **UC with PSC**:
  - **Even higher CRC risk** (5×)
  - **Annual colonoscopy** from diagnosis
  - Lower threshold for colectomy

### Question 10
A 32-year-old man with Crohn's disease undergoes ileocolic resection for stricturing disease. What is the MOST effective prophylaxis to prevent post-operative recurrence?

A) Mesalamine
B) Metronidazole for 3 months
C) Anti-TNF therapy (infliximab/adalimumab)
D) No prophylaxis needed

**Answer**: **C) Anti-TNF therapy (infliximab/adalimumab)**

**Explanation**:
- **Crohn's disease recurrence after surgery**:
  - **Surgery NOT curative**
  - **Endoscopic recurrence**: 70-90% at 1 year
  - **Clinical recurrence**: 50% at 10 years
  - **Re-operation**: 30-50% at 10 years

- **Post-operative prophylaxis** (high-risk patients):

  **Risk factors for recurrence**:
  - **Smoking** (most important modifiable)
  - Prior resection
  - Penetrating behavior (fistulas)
  - Perianal disease
  - Extensive resection (>50 cm)

  **Prophylaxis efficacy**:
  - **Anti-TNF** (infliximab/adalimumab): **MOST EFFECTIVE**
    - Reduces endoscopic and clinical recurrence
    - Start within 4 weeks post-op

  - **Thiopurines** (AZA/6-MP): Moderate efficacy

  - **Metronidazole**: Modest benefit (3 months)
    - Side effects limit long-term use

  - **Mesalamine**: **NOT effective** for CD prophylaxis

- **Surveillance**:
  - **Ileocolonoscopy**: 6-12 months post-op
  - **Rutgeerts Score**: Assess endoscopic recurrence
    - i0-i1: Low risk
    - i2-i4: High risk → escalate therapy

- **Smoking cessation**: **CRITICAL** (doubles recurrence risk)

## Summary

**Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)** encompasses **Crohn's disease** and **Ulcerative colitis**, chronic relapsing-remitting inflammatory GI conditions with distinct features:

**Key Distinctions**:
- **Crohn's**: Any GI location, transmural, skip lesions, granulomas, fistulas common, NOT curative with surgery
- **UC**: Colon/rectum only, mucosal, continuous from rectum, NO granulomas, CURATIVE with colectomy

**Diagnosis**: Colonoscopy with biopsies (gold standard), fecal calprotectin (screening), CTE/MRE (small bowel CD)

**Treatment**:
- **UC mild-moderate**: 5-ASA first-line
- **Moderate-severe**: Steroids (induction) → Immunomodulators/Biologics (maintenance)
- **Anti-TNF**: Infliximab/Adalimumab (fistulizing CD, moderate-severe disease, ASUC rescue)
- **Vedolizumab**: Gut-selective (safer, slower onset)
- **ASUC**: IV steroids → Rescue (IFX/cyclosporine) vs colectomy

**Complications**:
- **CD**: Strictures, fistulas, abscesses, malabsorption
- **UC**: Toxic megacolon, perforation, massive bleeding, **colorectal cancer** (surveillance)

**Extraintestinal Manifestations**: Arthritis, erythema nodosum, pyoderma gangrenosum, uveitis, PSC (5× CRC risk)

**Surveillance**: UC pancolitis - colonoscopy every 1-3 years starting 8 years; **PSC - ANNUAL**
