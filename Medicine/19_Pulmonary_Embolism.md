## DEFINITION

**Pulmonary Embolism (PE)**: Obstruction of pulmonary arteries by thrombus (blood clot), fat, air, tumor, or amniotic fluid

**Most Common**: **Thromboembolic PE** (~95%)
- Origin: **Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)** of lower extremities (90%)
- **Venous Thromboembolism (VTE)**: DVT + PE (spectrum of same disease)

### Types by Source

```
PULMONARY EMBOLISM
│
├─ THROMBOEMBOLISM (95%) - MOST COMMON
│  • DVT (lower extremity) - 90%
│  • Pelvic veins
│  • Upper extremity DVT (central lines, pacemakers)
│
├─ FAT EMBOLISM
│  • Long bone fractures, orthopedic surgery
│  • 24-72 hours post-trauma
│  • Triad: Respiratory distress, Petechiae, CNS changes
│
├─ AIR EMBOLISM
│  • Central line insertion/removal
│  • Thoracic surgery, trauma
│
├─ AMNIOTIC FLUID EMBOLISM
│  • Childbirth, C-section
│  • DIC, shock, respiratory failure
│
└─ SEPTIC EMBOLISM
   • Tricuspid endocarditis (IV drug users)
   • Multiple peripheral infiltrates ("feeding vessel" sign)
```

## EPIDEMIOLOGY

### Incidence
- **60-70 per 100,000** population annually
- **3rd most common** acute cardiovascular syndrome (after MI, Stroke)
- Increasing incidence with age (>60 years)

### Mortality
- **Untreated**: 25-30% mortality
- **Treated**: 2-8% mortality
- **Massive PE**: 30-50% mortality (despite treatment)

### Distribution
- **Lower extremity DVT**: 90% of PE source
- **Bilateral PE**: 60%
- **Lower lobes**: More commonly affected (↑ blood flow)

## RISK FACTORS

### Virchow's Triad (Pathogenesis of Thrombosis)

```
VIRCHOW'S TRIAD
│
├─ STASIS (Decreased blood flow)
│  • Immobilization, bed rest, prolonged travel
│  • CHF, stroke with paralysis
│  • Obesity
│
├─ HYPERCOAGULABILITY
│  • Inherited thrombophilias
│  • Malignancy, pregnancy, OCP/HRT
│  • Antiphospholipid syndrome
│
└─ ENDOTHELIAL INJURY
   • Surgery, trauma, fractures
   • Central venous catheters
   • Vasculitis
```

### Risk Factors (Classified)

#### Strong Risk Factors (OR >10)

| Risk Factor | Relative Risk |
|-------------|---------------|
| **Major surgery** (especially orthopedic: Hip/Knee replacement) | 10-50x |
| **Hip/Pelvic fracture** | 10-50x |
| **Spinal cord injury** | 10-50x |
| **Prior VTE** | 10-20x |

#### Moderate Risk Factors (OR 2-10)

| Risk Factor | Relative Risk |
|-------------|---------------|
| **Immobilization** (>3 days bed rest) | 2-10x |
| **Malignancy** (active, especially pancreatic, brain, ovarian) | 5-7x |
| **Pregnancy / Postpartum** (6-fold higher risk) | 5-10x |
| **Oral contraceptives (OCP) / HRT** | 3-5x |
| **Long-distance travel** (>4-6 hours) | 2-4x |
| **CHF, COPD** | 2-3x |
| **Age** (each decade >60 years) | 1.5-2x per decade |

#### Weak Risk Factors (OR <2)

- Obesity (BMI >30)
- Varicose veins
- Smoking

### Inherited Thrombophilias

| Condition | Prevalence | VTE Risk |
|-----------|------------|----------|
| **Factor V Leiden** (Activated Protein C resistance) | **5%** (Caucasians) - MOST COMMON | 5-7x (heterozygous), 50-80x (homozygous) |
| **Prothrombin G20210A mutation** | 2-3% | 3-5x |
| **Protein C deficiency** | 0.2-0.4% | 10x |
| **Protein S deficiency** | 0.03-0.13% | 10x |
| **Antithrombin deficiency** | 0.02-0.2% | 25-50x (HIGHEST risk) |

**Screening Indications** (Thrombophilia):
- VTE at **age <50 years**
- **Unprovoked** VTE
- **Recurrent** VTE
- VTE in **unusual sites** (cerebral, mesenteric, portal vein)
- **Family history** of VTE
- VTE during pregnancy/OCP use

## PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

### Sequence of Events

```
DVT FORMATION (Lower extremity deep veins)
    ↓
EMBOLIZATION (Clot breaks off)
    ↓
MIGRATION via IVC → Right Atrium → Right Ventricle
    ↓
LODGES IN PULMONARY ARTERIES
    ↓
ACUTE EFFECTS
│
├─ RESPIRATORY
│  • ↑ Alveolar dead space (V/Q mismatch)
│  • Hypoxemia (shunt, V/Q mismatch, ↓ CO)
│  • Hyperventilation (↓ PaCO₂)
│  • Bronchoconstriction (serotonin, histamine release)
│
├─ HEMODYNAMIC
│  • ↑ Pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR)
│  • ↑ RV afterload
│  • RV dilatation, dysfunction
│  • ↓ LV preload (↓ CO)
│  • Shock (if massive PE, >50% obstruction)
│
└─ INFARCTION (10-15% of PE)
   • More common with underlying cardiopulmonary disease
   • Peripheral, wedge-shaped consolidation
   • Hemoptysis, pleuritic pain, pleural effusion
```

### Hemodynamic Classification

| Type | Definition | RV Dysfunction | Shock | Mortality |
|------|------------|----------------|-------|-----------|
| **Massive PE** | Hemodynamically unstable (SBP <90 mmHg or ↓ >40 mmHg × >15 min) | Yes | Yes | 30-50% |
| **Submassive (Intermediate)** | Hemodynamically stable + RV dysfunction/myocardial injury | Yes | No | 5-15% |
| **Low-risk (Minor)** | Hemodynamically stable, No RV dysfunction | No | No | <1% |

**RV Dysfunction Indicators**:
- **Echo**: RV dilatation (RV/LV ratio >0.9)
- **CT**: RV/LV ratio >1.0
- **BNP/NT-proBNP**: Elevated
- **Troponin**: Elevated (myocardial strain)

## CLINICAL FEATURES

### Symptoms (HIGHLY VARIABLE)

**Classic Triad** (Present in <20%):
1. **Dyspnea** (sudden onset)
2. **Pleuritic chest pain**
3. **Hemoptysis**

#### Common Symptoms

| Symptom | Frequency |
|---------|-----------|
| **Dyspnea** (sudden or progressive) | **80-90%** - MOST COMMON |
| **Pleuritic chest pain** | 40-50% |
| **Cough** | 40-50% |
| **Hemoptysis** | 10-15% (suggests infarction) |
| **Syncope** | 10-15% (suggests massive PE) |
| **Apprehension, anxiety** | 40-50% |

**Massive PE Presentation**:
- **Shock** (SBP <90 mmHg)
- **Syncope** (↓ cardiac output)
- **Cardiac arrest** (PEA - Pulseless Electrical Activity)

**Small PE**:
- May be **asymptomatic** or minimal dyspnea

### Physical Examination

#### Respiratory

| Sign | Frequency | Significance |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| **Tachypnea** (RR >20/min) | **70-90%** - MOST COMMON SIGN | Non-specific |
| **Crackles** | 50% | Infarction, atelectasis |
| **Decreased breath sounds** | 20% | Pleural effusion |
| **Wheezing** | 10-20% | Bronchoconstriction |
| **Pleural friction rub** | Rare | Infarction |

#### Cardiovascular

| Sign | Frequency | Significance |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| **Tachycardia** (HR >100/min) | 40-60% | Compensatory |
| **Hypotension** (SBP <90 mmHg) | 5-10% | **Massive PE** |
| **Loud P2** (accentuated pulmonic component) | Variable | Pulmonary hypertension |
| **RV heave** | Rare | RV strain |
| **JVP elevation** | 30% | RV failure |

#### DVT Signs

| Sign | Frequency |
|------|-----------|
| **Leg swelling, tenderness, erythema, warmth** | 30-40% |
| **Homan's sign** (dorsiflexion pain) | Unreliable, NOT recommended |

**Key Point**: **Normal physical exam does NOT exclude PE**

## DIAGNOSIS

### Diagnostic Approach

```
CLINICAL SUSPICION
    ↓
ASSESS PRE-TEST PROBABILITY (Wells Score or Revised Geneva Score)
    ↓
    ├─ LOW/INTERMEDIATE Probability
    │      ↓
    │  D-DIMER
    │      ↓
    │      ├─ NEGATIVE → PE EXCLUDED (98% NPV)
    │      └─ POSITIVE → IMAGING (CTPA or V/Q scan)
    │
    └─ HIGH Probability
           ↓
       IMAGING DIRECTLY (CTPA or V/Q scan)
       (Skip D-dimer - low specificity)
```

### 1. Clinical Probability Scores

#### Wells Score for PE

| Clinical Feature | Points |
|------------------|--------|
| **Clinical signs of DVT** (leg swelling, pain) | **3.0** |
| **PE as likely or more likely than alternative diagnosis** | **3.0** |
| **Heart rate >100/min** | 1.5 |
| **Immobilization ≥3 days or surgery in past 4 weeks** | 1.5 |
| **Previous PE or DVT** | 1.5 |
| **Hemoptysis** | 1.0 |
| **Malignancy** (treatment ongoing or within 6 months) | 1.0 |

**Interpretation**:
- **<2**: Low probability (risk ~10%)
- **2-6**: Moderate probability (risk ~30%)
- **>6**: High probability (risk ~65%)

**Alternative (Two-Level)**:
- **≤4**: PE unlikely → **D-dimer**
- **>4**: PE likely → **Imaging**

#### Revised Geneva Score

| Variable | Points |
|----------|--------|
| Age >65 years | 1 |
| Previous DVT or PE | 3 |
| Surgery or fracture within past month | 2 |
| Active malignancy | 2 |
| Unilateral lower limb pain | 3 |
| Hemoptysis | 2 |
| Heart rate 75-94/min | 3 |
| Heart rate ≥95/min | 5 |
| Pain on deep palpation and unilateral edema | 4 |

**Interpretation**:
- **0-3**: Low (8% probability)
- **4-10**: Intermediate (28%)
- **≥11**: High (74%)

### 2. D-Dimer

**What it is**: Fibrin degradation product (marker of fibrinolysis)

**Indications**:
- **Low or Intermediate** clinical probability (Wells ≤6 or Revised Geneva <11)
- **NOT indicated** in High probability (go straight to imaging)

**Cutoff**: **<500 ng/mL** (negative)

**Performance**:
- **Sensitivity**: **95-98%** (High)
- **Specificity**: **40-60%** (Low)
- **Negative Predictive Value**: **>95%** (Excellent - rules out PE)
- **Positive Predictive Value**: ~30% (Poor - many false positives)

**Interpretation**:
- **D-dimer <500** + Low/Intermediate probability → **PE EXCLUDED** (no imaging needed)
- **D-dimer ≥500** → **Proceed to imaging** (CTPA or V/Q)

**Age-Adjusted D-Dimer** (↑ specificity in elderly):
- Cutoff (age >50): **Age × 10 ng/mL**
- Example: 70-year-old → Cutoff = 700 ng/mL

**False Positives** (Elevated D-dimer WITHOUT PE):
- **Pregnancy**, postpartum
- **Malignancy**
- **Recent surgery, trauma**
- **Infection, sepsis**
- **CHF, MI**
- **DIC**
- **Liver disease**
- **Advanced age** (>70 years)

**Key Point**: D-dimer is a **rule-out test** (negative = excludes PE), NOT a rule-in test

### 3. Arterial Blood Gas (ABG)

**Classic Findings** (Present in only 50-60%):
- **Hypoxemia**: PaO₂ <80 mmHg
- **Hypocapnia**: PaCO₂ <35 mmHg (due to hyperventilation)
- **Respiratory alkalosis**: pH >7.45
- **↑ A-a gradient**: (A-a) gradient >20 mmHg

**Formula**: A-a gradient = PAO₂ - PaO₂
- PAO₂ = (FiO₂ × (Patm - PH₂O)) - (PaCO₂ / 0.8)
- Normal = 5-15 mmHg (increases with age)

**Limitations**:
- **Normal ABG does NOT exclude PE** (30-40% have normal PaO₂)
- **Non-specific** (many causes of hypoxemia)

**Utility**: Assess severity, guide oxygen therapy

### 4. Chest X-Ray

**Purpose**: **Exclude alternative diagnoses** (pneumonia, pneumothorax, rib fracture, CHF)

**Findings in PE** (Present in only 10-20%, NON-SPECIFIC):
- **Normal CXR** (50-70%) - MOST COMMON
- **Atelectasis** (band-like opacities)
- **Pleural effusion** (30-50%, usually small, unilateral)
- **Infiltrate** (consolidation, infarction)
- **Hampton's hump**: Wedge-shaped peripheral opacity (infarction) - Rare
- **Westermark sign**: Focal oligemia (↓ vascularity) distal to embolus - Rare
- **Palla's sign**: Enlarged right descending pulmonary artery - Rare

**Key Point**: CXR is usually **normal or non-specific**; **does NOT diagnose or exclude PE**

### 5. ECG

**Purpose**: Exclude MI, assess RV strain

**Findings** (Present in 70%, but NON-SPECIFIC):
- **Sinus tachycardia** (40-50%) - MOST COMMON
- **Non-specific ST-T changes** (30-40%)
- **S1Q3T3 pattern** (10-20%):
  - **S wave in lead I** (deep)
  - **Q wave in lead III** (present)
  - **T wave inversion in lead III**
  - Suggests **RV strain** (seen in massive PE)
- **Right bundle branch block (RBBB)** (6-10%)
- **Right axis deviation**
- **P pulmonale** (tall P in II, III, aVF)
- **T wave inversion V1-V4** (RV strain)

**S1Q3T3 Pattern**: **Classic** but **insensitive** (only 10-20%) and **non-specific**

**Key Point**: ECG is usually **non-specific**; **normal ECG does NOT exclude PE**

### 6. CT Pulmonary Angiography (CTPA)

**GOLD STANDARD** (First-line imaging)

**Sensitivity**: 85-95%
**Specificity**: 90-95%

**Findings**:
- **Filling defect** in pulmonary artery (partial or complete occlusion)
- **Abrupt vessel cutoff**
- **RV dilatation** (RV/LV ratio >1.0) - Indicates RV dysfunction

**Advantages**:
- **High sensitivity/specificity**
- **Fast** (minutes)
- Visualizes **alternative diagnoses** (aortic dissection, pneumonia, tumor)
- Assesses **RV/LV ratio** (prognostic)

**Disadvantages**:
- **Contrast** (allergy, renal insufficiency)
- **Radiation** (pregnancy, young patients)
- **Overdiagnosis** of small subsegmental PE (clinical significance unclear)

**Contraindications**:
- **Severe contrast allergy** (consider V/Q scan)
- **Severe renal insufficiency** (GFR <30) - Use V/Q scan or adjust contrast dose

### 7. Ventilation-Perfusion (V/Q) Scan

**Indications**:
- **Contraindication to CTPA** (renal failure, contrast allergy)
- **Pregnancy** (lower radiation to fetus than CTPA)
- **Normal CXR** (better interpretation)

**Technique**:
- **Perfusion**: IV Tc-99m macroaggregated albumin → Shows blood flow
- **Ventilation**: Inhaled Xe-133 or Tc-99m DTPA → Shows airflow

**Interpretation (PIOPED II Criteria)**:

| Result | Criteria | PE Probability |
|--------|----------|----------------|
| **Normal** | No perfusion defects | **<2%** - PE EXCLUDED |
| **Low Probability** | Small perfusion defects, matched V/Q defects | 10-20% |
| **Intermediate** | Multiple small V/Q mismatches, difficult to categorize | 20-40% |
| **High Probability** | **≥2 large segmental V/Q mismatches** OR **≥1 large + ≥2 moderate mismatches** | **85-90%** |

**Mismatch**: Perfusion defect (no blood flow) with **normal ventilation** (air flow present)
- Suggests **PE** (ventilation OK, but no perfusion due to embolus)

**Matched defect**: Both perfusion and ventilation absent (suggests parenchymal disease like pneumonia, NOT PE)

**Limitations**:
- **Non-diagnostic** in 40-50% (intermediate probability)
- Requires **normal CXR** for interpretation
- Less available than CTPA

**Key Point**: **High probability V/Q scan** = PE diagnosed, **Normal V/Q** = PE excluded, **Intermediate** = Further testing

### 8. Echocardiography

**NOT for diagnosis**, but for **risk stratification**

**Indications**:
- **Suspected massive PE** (bedside, unstable patients)
- **Risk stratification** (detect RV dysfunction)
- **Guided thrombolysis decision** (submassive PE)

**Findings Suggesting PE**:
- **RV dilatation** (RV/LV ratio >0.9)
- **RV hypokinesis** (McConnell sign: RV free wall hypokinesis with apical sparing)
- **Interventricular septal flattening** (D-shaped LV)
- **Tricuspid regurgitation** (↑ RVSP)
- **Dilated IVC** (↑ RA pressure)

**Direct Visualization of Thrombus**: Rare (<5%), only if large central clot

**Limitations**: Cannot rule out PE (50% have normal echo)

### 9. Compression Ultrasonography (Leg Veins)

**Indications**:
- Clinical suspicion of DVT
- If CTPA non-diagnostic or contraindicated

**Findings**:
- **Non-compressibility** of vein (thrombus present)
- **Absence of flow** on Doppler

**Sensitivity**: 95% for **proximal DVT** (femoral, popliteal), 50-70% for **distal DVT** (calf)

**If DVT confirmed** → Treat as VTE (same as PE), **no need for CTPA** (unless suspect PE)

### 10. Pulmonary Angiography

**Invasive, RARELY used** (replaced by CTPA)

**Gold standard** (historical), but high risk (1-2% mortality, arrhythmias, bleeding)

**Current use**: **Catheter-directed thrombolysis** in massive PE (therapeutic, not diagnostic)

## TREATMENT

### Goals
1. **Prevent death** (hemodynamic collapse)
2. **Prevent recurrence**
3. **Prevent post-thrombotic syndrome / chronic thromboembolic PH**

### Treatment Algorithm

```
PE CONFIRMED
    ↓
ASSESS HEMODYNAMIC STATUS
    ↓
    ├─ MASSIVE PE (Shock, SBP <90)
    │      ↓
    │  • Resuscitation (IV fluids, O₂, vasopressors if needed)
    │  • SYSTEMIC THROMBOLYSIS (Alteplase)
    │  • Consider: Catheter-directed thrombolysis, Embolectomy (surgical/percutaneous)
    │  • Anticoagulation (Heparin)
    │
    ├─ SUBMASSIVE PE (RV dysfunction, NO shock)
    │      ↓
    │  • Anticoagulation (Heparin → Warfarin or DOAC)
    │  • Consider: Thrombolysis (if high risk of decompensation)
    │  • Monitoring (ICU/telemetry)
    │
    └─ LOW-RISK PE (No RV dysfunction, hemodynamically stable)
           ↓
       • Anticoagulation (DOAC or LMWH → Warfarin)
       • Outpatient management (if appropriate)
```

### 1. Anticoagulation (CORNERSTONE)

**ALL patients** with confirmed PE (unless absolute contraindication)

#### A. Initial Anticoagulation (First 5-10 days)

**Options**:

| Drug | Dose | Monitoring | Notes |
|------|------|------------|-------|
| **Unfractionated Heparin (UFH)** IV | **Bolus 80 units/kg**, then **18 units/kg/hr** infusion | **aPTT** (target 1.5-2.5× control) q6h | **Preferred** if: Massive PE, renal failure (CrCl <30), high bleeding risk (reversible with protamine) |
| **Low Molecular Weight Heparin (LMWH)** | **Enoxaparin 1 mg/kg SC BID** OR **1.5 mg/kg SC OD** | NO routine monitoring (anti-Xa if obesity/renal failure) | **Preferred** for stable patients (outpatient option) |
| **Fondaparinux** | <50 kg: 5 mg, 50-100 kg: 7.5 mg, >100 kg: 10 mg SC OD | NO monitoring | Alternative if HIT |
| **Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs)** | See below | NO monitoring | **Rivaroxaban, Apixaban** can be started immediately (no parenteral bridge needed) |

**Duration**: Minimum **5 days** + INR ≥2 × 48 hours (if warfarin) OR 5-10 days (if DOAC)

#### B. Long-Term Anticoagulation

**Options**:

| Drug | Dose | Monitoring | Notes |
|------|------|------------|-------|
| **Warfarin** | Start 5-10 mg OD, adjust based on INR | **INR** (target **2-3**) | Requires parenteral bridge (UFH/LMWH × 5 days), Drug/food interactions |
| **Rivaroxaban (Xa inhibitor)** | **15 mg BID × 21 days**, then **20 mg OD** | NO monitoring | **NO parenteral bridge** needed |
| **Apixaban (Xa inhibitor)** | **10 mg BID × 7 days**, then **5 mg BID** | NO monitoring | **NO parenteral bridge** needed |
| **Edoxaban (Xa inhibitor)** | **60 mg OD** (30 mg if CrCl 30-50, weight <60 kg) | NO monitoring | **Requires parenteral bridge** (LMWH × 5 days) |
| **Dabigatran (Thrombin inhibitor)** | **150 mg BID** (110 mg if age >80, high bleeding risk) | NO monitoring | **Requires parenteral bridge** |

**Direct Oral Anticoagulants (DOACs)**:
- **Advantages**: No monitoring, rapid onset, fewer drug interactions, fixed dosing
- **Disadvantages**: Cost, adherence issues (no INR to monitor), contraindicated if CrCl <15-30 (varies by drug)
- **Preferred** in most patients (unless contraindication)

**Warfarin**:
- **Advantages**: Reversible (Vitamin K, FFP, PCC), cost, safe in severe renal failure
- **Disadvantages**: Slow onset, requires bridge, frequent monitoring (INR), drug/food interactions

**Reversal Agents**:
- **Warfarin**: Vitamin K (slow, 12-24 hr), FFP, **Prothrombin Complex Concentrate (PCC)** (rapid)
- **Dabigatran**: **Idarucizumab** (specific reversal)
- **Rivaroxaban, Apixaban, Edoxaban**: **Andexanet alfa** (specific reversal, expensive)
- **UFH**: **Protamine sulfate**
- **LMWH**: Protamine (partial reversal)

#### C. Duration of Anticoagulation

| Scenario | Duration | Rationale |
|----------|----------|-----------|
| **Provoked PE** (Surgery, trauma, temporary risk factor) | **3 months** | Low recurrence risk (<5% per year) |
| **First unprovoked PE** + Low bleeding risk | **≥3 months** (consider indefinite) | Recurrence risk 10% per year |
| **Recurrent VTE** (≥2 episodes) | **Indefinite** (lifelong) | High recurrence risk (>20% per year) |
| **Active malignancy** | **Indefinite** (as long as malignancy active) | Use **LMWH** (superior to warfarin in cancer) |
| **Thrombophilia** (e.g., Antithrombin deficiency) | **Indefinite** | High recurrence risk |

**Re-evaluation at 3 months**: Assess bleeding risk vs recurrence risk → Decide continue vs stop

**Bleeding Risk Assessment** (HAS-BLED Score):
- Hypertension, Abnormal renal/liver function, Stroke history, Bleeding history, Labile INR, Elderly (>65), Drugs/alcohol
- Score ≥3: High bleeding risk

### 2. Thrombolysis

**Indication**: **Massive PE** (hemodynamic instability, shock, cardiac arrest)
**Controversial**: Submassive PE (RV dysfunction without shock)

#### Systemic Thrombolysis

**Drugs**:
- **Alteplase (tPA)**: **100 mg IV over 2 hours** (PREFERRED)
  - Accelerated: 0.6 mg/kg over 15 min (max 50 mg) - for cardiac arrest
- **Streptokinase**: 250,000 IU bolus, then 100,000 IU/hr × 24 hr (less used)
- **Urokinase**: 4400 IU/kg bolus, then 4400 IU/kg/hr × 12-24 hr (less used)

**Mechanism**: Converts plasminogen → plasmin → Dissolves thrombus

**Efficacy**: ↓ Clot burden, ↓ Pulmonary artery pressure, ↓ RV dysfunction within **12-24 hours**

**Benefits**:
- ↓ Mortality in **massive PE** (50% → 30%)
- Rapid hemodynamic improvement

**Risks**:
- **Major bleeding**: 10-20%
- **Intracranial hemorrhage**: 1-3% (HIGHEST risk)
- Stroke, GI bleed

**Absolute Contraindications**:
- **Active bleeding**
- **Recent stroke** (<3 months)
- **Recent intracranial/spinal surgery** (<2 months)
- **Intracranial malignancy, AVM**
- **Suspected aortic dissection**

**Relative Contraindications**:
- Major surgery <10 days
- Recent trauma, CPR
- Uncontrolled hypertension (SBP >180)
- Bleeding diathesis, thrombocytopenia (<100,000)
- Pregnancy

**Contraindication to Systemic Thrombolysis** → Consider **Catheter-directed thrombolysis** or **Embolectomy**

#### Catheter-Directed Thrombolysis

**Indications**:
- Massive PE with **contraindication to systemic thrombolysis**
- Persistent hypotension despite systemic thrombolysis

**Technique**: Catheter placed into pulmonary artery → Local tPA infusion

**Advantages**: Lower tPA dose → ↓ Bleeding risk

#### Surgical/Percutaneous Embolectomy

**Indications**:
- **Massive PE** with:
  - Failed thrombolysis
  - Contraindication to thrombolysis
  - Shock requiring CPR

**Surgical Embolectomy**: Open surgery, cardiopulmonary bypass
- High mortality (20-30%), but life-saving if other options fail

**Percutaneous Embolectomy**: Catheter-based clot removal (AngioVac, FlowTriever)
- Less invasive than surgery

### 3. Supportive Care

**Oxygen**: Target SpO₂ ≥90%

**IV Fluids**: **Small boluses** (250-500 mL) if hypotension
- **Avoid large volumes** (may worsen RV failure by ↑ RV preload)

**Vasopressors** (if shock despite fluids):
- **Norepinephrine** (preferred)
- **Dobutamine** (if RV dysfunction without shock)

**Mechanical Ventilation** (if respiratory failure):
- **Avoid high PEEP** (↑ RV afterload)
- **Permissive hypercapnia** (avoid alkalosis → ↑ pulmonary vasoconstriction)

### 4. IVC Filter

**NOT routine** (does NOT prevent death, may ↑ recurrent DVT)

**Indications**:
- **Absolute contraindication to anticoagulation** (active bleeding)
- **Recurrent VTE despite adequate anticoagulation**
- **Massive PE with limited cardiopulmonary reserve** (cannot tolerate recurrent PE)

**Types**:
- **Permanent**: If lifelong contraindication
- **Retrievable**: Place during acute contraindication, remove when safe to anticoagulate (within 2-4 weeks)

**Complications**: IVC thrombosis, filter migration, perforation

## SPECIAL SITUATIONS

### 1. Pregnancy

**Risk**: 5-10x ↑ risk of VTE
- Hypercoagulable state (↑ Factors VII, VIII, X, fibrinogen, ↓ Protein S)
- Venous stasis (uterine compression of IVC)
- Highest risk: **Postpartum** (6 weeks after delivery)

**Diagnosis**:
- **D-dimer**: Not useful (↑ in normal pregnancy)
- **Compression ultrasound** (first-line): If DVT confirmed → Treat (no further imaging needed)
- **V/Q scan** (preferred over CTPA): Lower fetal radiation
- **CTPA**: If V/Q non-diagnostic (acceptable, maternal benefit > fetal risk)

**Treatment**:
- **LMWH** (Enoxaparin 1 mg/kg BID) - **PREFERRED**
  - Does not cross placenta
  - Continue throughout pregnancy + 6 weeks postpartum (minimum 3 months total)
- **UFH** (if renal failure)
- **Warfarin**: **CONTRAINDICATED** (teratogenic, especially 1st trimester)
- **DOACs**: **CONTRAINDICATED** (no safety data)

**Thrombolysis**: If massive PE (life-threatening), can be used

### 2. Malignancy

**Risk**: 5-7x ↑ risk, highest in **pancreatic, brain, ovarian, lung** cancers

**Trousseau Syndrome**: Migratory thrombophlebitis + malignancy

**Treatment**:
- **LMWH** (Enoxaparin, Dalteparin) - **PREFERRED over warfarin**
  - **CLOT Trial**: LMWH superior to warfarin in cancer-associated VTE (↓ recurrence)
- **DOACs**: **Edoxaban, Rivaroxaban** - Emerging as alternatives (HOKUSAI-VTE, SELECT-D trials)
  - Caution in GI/GU malignancies (↑ bleeding)
- **Duration**: **Indefinite** (as long as malignancy active)

### 3. Catheter-Associated Upper Extremity DVT

**Risk**: Central lines, pacemakers, PICC lines

**Treatment**:
- **Remove catheter** (if no longer needed)
- **Anticoagulation** × 3 months (same as lower extremity DVT)
- If catheter needed → Continue anticoagulation while catheter in place

### 4. Subsegmental PE

**Definition**: PE in subsegmental arteries (branches of segmental arteries)

**Management** (CONTROVERSIAL):
- **If concurrent proximal DVT** OR **High recurrence risk** → **Anticoagulate**
- **If isolated subsegmental PE** + **Low risk** + **NO DVT** → **Surveillance** (serial leg ultrasounds) vs Anticoagulation
  - AACP 2019: Suggests surveillance over anticoagulation (if low risk)

**Rationale**: Clinical significance unclear, may represent overdiagnosis with modern CTPA

### 5. Chronic Thromboembolic Pulmonary Hypertension (CTEPH)

**Definition**: Persistent PH due to organized thrombi (>3 months post-PE)

**Frequency**: 0.5-4% of PE patients

**Presentation**: Progressive dyspnea, RV failure

**Diagnosis**:
- **Echo**: Pulmonary hypertension (RVSP >40 mmHg)
- **V/Q scan**: **Mismatched perfusion defects** (more sensitive than CTPA)
- **Right heart catheterization**: mPAP ≥25 mmHg (gold standard)

**Treatment**:
- **Pulmonary Thromboendarterectomy (PTE)**: **Curative** (surgical removal of organized clots)
- **Medical**: Riociguat (if inoperable), balloon pulmonary angioplasty
- **Lifelong anticoagulation**

**Prognosis**: Poor if untreated (median survival 3-5 years); PTE improves survival

## COMPLICATIONS

### 1. Death
- **Massive PE**: 30-50% mortality (despite treatment)
- **Low-risk PE**: <1% mortality

### 2. Recurrent VTE
- **Provoked**: <5% per year (after stopping anticoagulation)
- **Unprovoked**: 10% per year (after stopping anticoagulation)
- **Recurrent VTE**: >20% per year

### 3. Chronic Thromboembolic Pulmonary Hypertension (CTEPH)
- See above

### 4. Post-Thrombotic Syndrome (from DVT)
- Chronic leg pain, swelling, skin changes (hyperpigmentation, ulcers)
- Occurs in 20-50% of DVT patients
- Prevention: **Compression stockings** (controversial), adequate anticoagulation

### 5. Bleeding (from anticoagulation)
- Major bleeding: 1-3% per year (warfarin), 1-2% (DOACs)
- ICH: 0.5% per year

## PREVENTION

### Risk Stratification

**ALL hospitalized patients** should be assessed for VTE risk

| Risk Level | Criteria | Prophylaxis |
|------------|----------|-------------|
| **Low** | Mobile, age <40, minor surgery | **Early mobilization** |
| **Moderate** | Age 40-60, major surgery, prolonged immobility | **Pharmacologic** (LMWH, UFH, Fondaparinux) OR **Mechanical** (SCDs, GCS) |
| **High** | Age >60, malignancy, prior VTE, thrombophilia, major surgery | **Pharmacologic** (preferred) |
| **Very High** | Hip/knee replacement, hip fracture, spinal cord injury | **Pharmacologic** (extended duration, 10-35 days) + **Mechanical** |

### Pharmacologic Prophylaxis

| Drug | Dose | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| **Enoxaparin (LMWH)** | **40 mg SC OD** (general surgery), **30 mg SC BID** (orthopedic) | PREFERRED |
| **UFH** | **5000 units SC TID** | If renal failure (CrCl <30) |
| **Fondaparinux** | **2.5 mg SC OD** | Alternative |

**Duration**:
- **General surgery**: Until discharge (7-10 days)
- **Hip/Knee replacement**: **10-35 days** (extended prophylaxis)
- **Medical patients**: Duration of hospitalization + 7-14 days (if high risk)

**Contraindications**: Active bleeding, severe thrombocytopenia (<50,000), recent neurosurgery/spinal anesthesia

### Mechanical Prophylaxis

**Indications**: **Contraindication to anticoagulation**, or **adjunct to pharmacologic** (very high risk)

**Methods**:
- **Graduated Compression Stockings (GCS)**: 15-30 mmHg
- **Intermittent Pneumatic Compression (IPC)** / **Sequential Compression Devices (SCDs)**: Inflate/deflate → ↑ venous flow

**Efficacy**: ↓ VTE by 50-60% (less effective than pharmacologic alone)

### Long-Distance Travel

**Risk**: Flights >4-6 hours → 2-4x ↑ risk

**Prevention** (General population):
- **Calf exercises**, walk every 1-2 hours
- **Adequate hydration**
- **Avoid excessive alcohol**

**High-risk travelers** (prior VTE, thrombophilia, obesity, pregnancy):
- **Compression stockings** (knee-high, 15-30 mmHg)
- **Prophylactic LMWH** (consider if very high risk)

## NEET PG HIGH-YIELD POINTS

### Must-Know Facts

1. **Most Common Source**: **DVT of lower extremity** (90%)

2. **Risk Factors**:
   - **Strong** (OR >10): Major surgery (hip/knee), fractures, spinal cord injury, prior VTE
   - **Moderate**: Immobilization, malignancy, pregnancy, OCP/HRT
   - **Inherited**: **Factor V Leiden** (most common), Prothrombin G20210A, Protein C/S deficiency, Antithrombin deficiency

3. **Clinical Features**:
   - **MOST COMMON SYMPTOM**: **Dyspnea** (80-90%)
   - **MOST COMMON SIGN**: **Tachypnea** (70-90%)
   - Classic triad (dyspnea, pleuritic pain, hemoptysis): <20%
   - **Normal exam does NOT exclude PE**

4. **Wells Score for PE**:
   - Clinical DVT signs: 3 points
   - PE as likely as alternative: 3 points
   - **≤4**: PE unlikely → **D-dimer**
   - **>4**: PE likely → **Imaging**

5. **D-Dimer**:
   - **High sensitivity (95-98%)**, Low specificity (40-60%)
   - **<500 ng/mL**: **EXCLUDES PE** (if low/intermediate probability)
   - Use for **LOW/INTERMEDIATE** probability ONLY (NOT high probability)
   - **Age-adjusted**: Age × 10 ng/mL (if >50 years)

6. **Diagnostic Imaging**:
   - **CTPA**: **GOLD STANDARD** (85-95% sensitivity/specificity)
   - **V/Q scan**: If CTPA contraindicated (renal failure, contrast allergy, pregnancy)
     - **High probability**: ≥2 large segmental mismatches → PE confirmed
     - **Normal**: PE excluded

7. **ABG Findings**:
   - Hypoxemia (PaO₂ <80), Hypocapnia (PaCO₂ <35), ↑ A-a gradient
   - **Normal ABG does NOT exclude PE** (30-40% have normal PaO₂)

8. **ECG**:
   - **Sinus tachycardia** (most common)
   - **S1Q3T3**: Deep S in I, Q in III, Inverted T in III (RV strain, only 10-20%)
   - **Normal ECG does NOT exclude PE**

9. **Classification**:
   - **Massive**: Shock (SBP <90), high mortality (30-50%)
   - **Submassive**: RV dysfunction WITHOUT shock
   - **Low-risk**: No RV dysfunction, hemodynamically stable

10. **Anticoagulation**:
    - **Initial**: UFH (if massive PE, renal failure) OR LMWH (if stable) OR DOAC (Rivaroxaban/Apixaban - no bridge needed)
    - **Long-term**: **DOAC** (preferred) OR Warfarin (INR 2-3)
    - **Duration**:
      - **Provoked**: **3 months**
      - **Unprovoked**: **≥3 months**, consider indefinite
      - **Recurrent VTE, Active malignancy, Thrombophilia**: **Indefinite**

11. **Thrombolysis**:
    - **Indication**: **Massive PE** (shock)
    - **Drug**: **Alteplase (tPA) 100 mg IV over 2 hours**
    - **Contraindication**: Active bleeding, recent stroke (<3 mo), recent surgery, ICH

12. **IVC Filter**:
    - **NOT routine**
    - **Indication**: **Absolute contraindication to anticoagulation** OR Recurrent VTE despite adequate anticoagulation

13. **Pregnancy**:
    - **LMWH** throughout pregnancy + 6 weeks postpartum (minimum 3 months)
    - **Warfarin, DOACs CONTRAINDICATED**

14. **Malignancy**:
    - **LMWH preferred over warfarin** (↓ recurrence)
    - **Duration**: **Indefinite** (as long as cancer active)

### High-Yield Diagnostic Clues

| Scenario | Diagnosis/Action |
|----------|------------------|
| **Sudden dyspnea + pleuritic pain + recent surgery** | High suspicion PE → Imaging (CTPA) |
| **Wells ≤4 + D-dimer <500** | **PE EXCLUDED** (no imaging needed) |
| **Wells >4** | **Imaging** directly (skip D-dimer) |
| **V/Q: ≥2 large segmental mismatches** | **High probability → PE confirmed** |
| **S1Q3T3 on ECG** | RV strain (suggests massive PE, but only 10-20% sensitive) |
| **RV/LV ratio >1 on CTPA** | RV dysfunction (submassive/massive PE) |
| **SBP <90 mmHg** | **Massive PE** → Consider **thrombolysis** |
| **Recurrent VTE despite warfarin INR 2-3** | Consider IVC filter, workup for malignancy/thrombophilia |

### Important Cutoffs

| Parameter | Cutoff | Significance |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| **D-dimer** | <500 ng/mL | Excludes PE (if low/intermediate probability) |
| **Wells Score** | ≤4 vs >4 | PE unlikely vs PE likely |
| **SBP** | <90 mmHg | Massive PE (thrombolysis indication) |
| **INR** (Warfarin) | **2-3** | Therapeutic target |
| **RV/LV ratio** (CTPA) | >1.0 | RV dysfunction |
| **RV/LV ratio** (Echo) | >0.9 | RV dysfunction |

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Using D-dimer in high-probability patients** ❌
   - D-dimer only for **low/intermediate** probability
   - High probability → **Imaging directly**

2. **Excluding PE based on normal CXR or ECG** ❌
   - **Normal CXR/ECG does NOT exclude PE**
   - Need CTPA or V/Q scan

3. **Excluding PE based on normal PaO₂** ❌
   - **30-40% of PE have normal PaO₂**

4. **Thrombolysis in submassive PE (RV dysfunction without shock)** ❌
   - **Controversial**, not routine
   - Only if high risk of decompensation
   - **Massive PE (shock)** → Clear indication

5. **Warfarin without parenteral bridge** ❌
   - Warfarin takes **5-7 days** to reach therapeutic level
   - **MUST bridge** with UFH/LMWH × 5 days + INR ≥2 × 48 hours

6. **Routine IVC filter placement** ❌
   - **NOT routine** (does NOT prevent death, may ↑ DVT)
   - Only if **absolute contraindication to anticoagulation**

7. **DOACs in severe renal failure (CrCl <30)** ❌
   - **Contraindicated** (accumulation, ↑ bleeding)
   - Use **Warfarin** or **UFH**

### Mnemonics

1. **"PEP"** - PE Presentation:
   - **P**leuritic pain, **E**xertional dyspnea (sudden), hemo**P**tysis

2. **"PERC" Rule** (PE Rule-out Criteria) - LOW RISK if ALL negative:
   - Age <50, HR <100, SpO₂ ≥95%, No hemoptysis, No estrogen, No prior VTE, No leg swelling, No surgery/trauma
   - If ALL negative + Clinical suspicion low → **No testing needed**

3. **"S1Q3T3"** - ECG RV strain:
   - **S** in lead I, **Q** in lead III, inverted **T** in lead III

## PRACTICE MCQs

### Question 1
A 55-year-old woman presents 5 days after hip replacement surgery with sudden dyspnea. Wells score is 7 (high probability). What is the most appropriate next step?

A. D-dimer
B. Chest X-ray
C. CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA)
D. V/Q scan

**Answer**: **C. CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA)**

**Explanation**:
- **Wells Score 7** = **High probability** (>6 points)

**High Probability** → **Imaging DIRECTLY** (skip D-dimer)

**Why CTPA**:
- **GOLD STANDARD** for PE diagnosis
- Fast, high sensitivity/specificity
- No contraindications mentioned

**Why NOT D-dimer**:
- D-dimer is **ONLY for low/intermediate probability** (Wells ≤6)
- In **high probability**, D-dimer has **poor specificity** (many false positives)
- Would delay diagnosis

**Algorithm**:
- **Wells ≤4** (PE unlikely) → **D-dimer** first
- **Wells >4** (PE likely) → **Imaging** directly

**Key Point**: High clinical probability (Wells >6) → CTPA directly (do NOT check D-dimer)

### Question 2
A 40-year-old man with calf pain has Wells score of 3 (low probability). D-dimer is 350 ng/mL. What is the most appropriate next step?

A. CT pulmonary angiography
B. V/Q scan
C. Start anticoagulation
D. No further testing, PE excluded

**Answer**: **D. No further testing, PE excluded**

**Explanation**:
- **Wells ≤4** (Low/Intermediate probability) + **D-dimer <500 ng/mL** → **PE EXCLUDED**

**D-dimer Performance**:
- **Negative Predictive Value >95%**: D-dimer <500 + Low/Intermediate probability → **Safely excludes PE**
- No imaging needed

**Algorithm**:
```
Wells ≤4 → D-dimer
    ├─ <500 ng/mL → **PE EXCLUDED** (stop)
    └─ ≥500 ng/mL → Imaging (CTPA or V/Q)
```

**Why NOT Imaging**:
- **Unnecessary** (D-dimer <500 rules out PE)
- Would expose patient to radiation, contrast, cost

**Key Point**: Wells ≤4 + D-dimer <500 → PE EXCLUDED (no further testing)

### Question 3
A patient with confirmed massive PE (SBP 80 mmHg) has no contraindications to thrombolysis. What is the most appropriate treatment?

A. Warfarin
B. Rivaroxaban
C. Alteplase (tPA)
D. IVC filter

**Answer**: **C. Alteplase (tPA)**

**Explanation**:
- **Massive PE** (SBP <90 mmHg) → **SYSTEMIC THROMBOLYSIS**

**Thrombolysis Indication**:
- **Massive PE** (hemodynamic instability, shock, cardiac arrest)
- **Alteplase (tPA) 100 mg IV over 2 hours** - PREFERRED

**Benefits**:
- Rapid clot dissolution (12-24 hours)
- ↓ Mortality (50% → 30%)
- Improves hemodynamics

**Why NOT Anticoagulation Alone** (A, B):
- **Warfarin/Rivaroxaban** are for **stable patients**
- **Massive PE requires urgent intervention** (anticoagulation too slow)
- **Start heparin + thrombolysis** together

**Why NOT IVC Filter** (D):
- **Does NOT treat acute PE** (prevents recurrence only)
- **NOT indicated** unless contraindication to anticoagulation

**Contraindications to Thrombolysis**:
- Active bleeding, recent stroke, recent surgery, ICH
- If contraindicated → **Embolectomy** (surgical or catheter-directed)

**Key Point**: Massive PE (shock) → Systemic thrombolysis (Alteplase) + Anticoagulation

### Question 4
A 60-year-old woman with unprovoked PE is treated with anticoagulation. After 3 months, she has recovered fully with low bleeding risk. What is the most appropriate duration of anticoagulation?

A. Stop now (completed 3 months)
B. Continue for 6 months total, then stop
C. Continue indefinitely (lifelong)
D. Continue for 1 year total, then stop

**Answer**: **C. Continue indefinitely (lifelong)**

**Explanation**:
- **First unprovoked PE** + **Low bleeding risk** → Consider **INDEFINITE anticoagulation**

**Anticoagulation Duration**:

| Scenario | Duration | Recurrence Risk (off anticoagulation) |
|----------|----------|---------------------------------------|
| **Provoked** (surgery, trauma) | **3 months** | <5% per year |
| **First unprovoked** | **≥3 months**, consider **indefinite** | **10% per year** |
| **Recurrent VTE** (≥2 episodes) | **Indefinite** | >20% per year |

**Rationale**:
- **Unprovoked PE** = High recurrence risk (10% per year)
- **Benefit of continued anticoagulation** > **Bleeding risk** (if low bleeding risk)

**Re-evaluation at 3 months**:
- Assess **bleeding risk** (HAS-BLED score)
- Assess **recurrence risk**
- **Shared decision-making** with patient

**If High Bleeding Risk**: May stop after 3 months (accept recurrence risk)

**Key Point**: Unprovoked PE + Low bleeding risk → Consider indefinite anticoagulation (recurrence risk 10% per year)

### Question 5
A pregnant woman at 28 weeks gestation presents with suspected PE. Compression ultrasound of legs is negative. What is the most appropriate next imaging test?

A. CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA)
B. V/Q scan
C. Pulmonary angiography
D. No imaging, start anticoagulation empirically

**CTPA**:

- **Lower maternal breast radiation** than V/Q
- Use if high suspicion and need definitive diagnosis

**Algorithm in Pregnancy**:
1. **Compression ultrasound** (legs) first
   - If **positive** (DVT) → **Treat** (no further imaging needed)
2. If **negative** → **V/Q scan** (preferred) or CTPA

**Treatment if PE Confirmed**:
- **LMWH** (Enoxaparin 1 mg/kg BID)
- Continue throughout pregnancy + 6 weeks postpartum (minimum 3 months total)
- **Warfarin, DOACs CONTRAINDICATED**

**Key Point**: Pregnancy + Suspected PE → V/Q scan preferred (lower fetal radiation)

### Question 6
Which of the following is an absolute contraindication to thrombolysis in massive PE?

A. Age >75 years
B. Recent stroke 2 months ago
C. Uncontrolled hypertension (BP 190/110)
D. Thrombocytopenia (platelets 80,000/μL)

- **Active bleeding**
- **Recent stroke** (<3 months)

- Major surgery <10 days
- Recent trauma, CPR
- Pregnancy

**If Absolute Contraindication**:

- **Embolectomy** (surgical or percutaneous)

**Key Point**: Recent stroke (<3 months) = Absolute contraindication to thrombolysis

### Question 7
A patient with PE is started on warfarin and LMWH. INR is 2.3 on day 3. When can LMWH be stopped?

A. Now (INR >2)
B. After INR >2 for 24 hours
C. After INR >2 for 48 hours
D. After 5 days minimum, AND INR >2 for 48 hours

**Answer**: **D. After 5 days minimum, AND INR >2 for 48 hours**

**Explanation**:
- **LMWH bridge** with warfarin: Continue until **BOTH** criteria met:
  1. **Minimum 5 days** of overlap, **AND**
  2. **INR ≥2 for 48 hours** (2 consecutive days)

**Rationale**:
- **Warfarin** takes **5-7 days** to deplete Vitamin K-dependent clotting factors (especially Factor II, which has longest half-life ~60 hours)
- **INR reflects Factor VII** (shortest half-life ~6 hours) → INR may become therapeutic before full anticoagulation
- Need **48 hours** of INR ≥2 to ensure **all factors depleted**

**This Patient**:
- Day 3, INR 2.3
- **Continue LMWH** until day 5 + INR ≥2 × 48 hours
- **Cannot stop yet** (only day 3)

**Key Point**: LMWH bridge → Stop after ≥5 days AND INR ≥2 × 48 hours (NOT just INR ≥2 alone)

### Question 8
A 50-year-old man with PE has creatinine clearance of 20 mL/min. Which anticoagulant is most appropriate for long-term treatment?

A. Rivaroxaban
B. Apixaban
C. Dabigatran
D. Warfarin

**Answer**: **D. Warfarin**

**Explanation**:
- **Severe renal failure (CrCl <30 mL/min)** → **Warfarin** or **UFH** (NOT DOACs)

**DOACs in Renal Failure**:

| Drug | Contraindication (CrCl) |
|------|-------------------------|
| **Rivaroxaban** | <30 mL/min |
| **Apixaban** | <25 mL/min |
| **Edoxaban** | <30 mL/min |
| **Dabigatran** | <30 mL/min |

**Rationale**: DOACs are **renally excreted** → Accumulation → ↑ Bleeding risk

**Safe Options in Severe Renal Failure**:
- **Warfarin** (hepatic metabolism, no renal excretion)
- **UFH** (can use, adjust based on aPTT)

**LMWH**: Avoid in CrCl <30 (accumulation, ↑ bleeding)

**Key Point**: Severe renal failure (CrCl <30) → Warfarin (DOACs contraindicated)

### Question 9
V/Q scan shows 2 large segmental mismatched perfusion defects with normal ventilation. What is the interpretation?

A. Low probability
B. Intermediate probability
C. High probability
D. Normal

**Answer**: **C. High probability**

**Explanation**:
- **≥2 large segmental V/Q mismatches** = **High probability** for PE

**V/Q Scan Interpretation**:

| Result | Criteria | PE Probability |
|--------|----------|----------------|
| **Normal** | No perfusion defects | <2% - **PE excluded** |
| **Low** | Small defects, matched V/Q | 10-20% |
| **Intermediate** | Multiple small mismatches, difficult to categorize | 20-40% |
| **High** | **≥2 large segmental mismatches** OR **1 large + ≥2 moderate mismatches** | **85-90%** - **PE confirmed** |

**This Patient**:
- **2 large segmental mismatches** → **High probability** → **PE diagnosis confirmed**

**Mismatch**: Perfusion defect + **Normal ventilation**
- Suggests **PE** (ventilation OK, but no perfusion due to embolus)

**Matched defect**: Both perfusion + ventilation absent → Parenchymal disease (NOT PE)

**Next Step**: **Start anticoagulation** (high probability V/Q = PE confirmed)

**Key Point**: ≥2 large segmental mismatches on V/Q → High probability → PE confirmed

### Question 10
A patient with active pancreatic cancer develops PE. What is the most appropriate long-term anticoagulation?

A. Warfarin
B. LMWH (Enoxaparin)
C. Rivaroxaban
D. IVC filter

**Answer**: **B. LMWH (Enoxaparin)**

**Explanation**:
- **Cancer-associated VTE** → **LMWH preferred over warfarin**

**Evidence**:
- **CLOT Trial**: LMWH vs Warfarin in cancer patients with VTE
  - **LMWH superior**: ↓ Recurrent VTE (9% vs 17%), no ↑ bleeding
- **Mechanism**: LMWH has anti-inflammatory, anti-angiogenic effects

**Regimen**:
- **Enoxaparin 1 mg/kg BID** OR **1.5 mg/kg OD**
- **Duration**: **Indefinite** (as long as malignancy active)

**DOACs** (C):
- **Edoxaban, Rivaroxaban**: Emerging alternatives (HOKUSAI-VTE, SELECT-D trials)
- **Caution** in GI/GU malignancies (↑ bleeding risk)
- Can be used if patient prefers (oral), but LMWH still preferred

**Warfarin** (A):
- **Less effective than LMWH** in cancer patients
- Drug/food interactions, difficult to maintain INR

**IVC Filter** (D):
- **NOT routine** (only if contraindication to anticoagulation)

**Key Point**: Cancer-associated VTE → LMWH preferred over warfarin (↓ recurrent VTE)

## SUMMARY

**Pulmonary Embolism (PE)** is obstruction of pulmonary arteries, most commonly by thrombus from DVT:
- **Spectrum of VTE**: DVT → PE
- **Risk**: Virchow's Triad (Stasis, Hypercoagulability, Endothelial injury)

**Diagnosis**:
- **Pre-test probability**: Wells Score (≤4 → D-dimer, >4 → Imaging)
- **D-dimer**: <500 → Excludes PE (if low/intermediate probability)
- **CTPA**: GOLD STANDARD (85-95% sensitivity/specificity)
- **V/Q scan**: If CTPA contraindicated (pregnancy, renal failure)

**Treatment**:
- **Anticoagulation**: ALL patients (DOAC preferred, or Warfarin)
  - Duration: **Provoked (3 mo)**, **Unprovoked (≥3 mo, consider indefinite)**
- **Thrombolysis**: **Massive PE** (shock) - Alteplase 100 mg IV over 2 hr
- **Special**: Pregnancy (LMWH), Cancer (LMWH > Warfarin), Severe renal failure (Warfarin)

**Prevention**: Risk stratification + Pharmacologic (LMWH 40 mg OD) or Mechanical (SCDs)

**Prognosis**: Untreated (25-30% mortality), Treated (2-8%), Massive (30-50%)
