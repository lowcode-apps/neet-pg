## Definition and Staging

### Definition (KDOQI 2012)

**Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)**: Abnormalities of kidney structure or function for **>3 months**

**Criteria** (≥1 for >3 months):
1. **GFR <60 mL/min/1.73 m²** OR
2. **Markers of kidney damage** (≥1):
   - **Albuminuria** (≥30 mg/24 hrs or ACR ≥30 mg/g)
   - **Urine sediment abnormalities** (RBCs, casts)
   - **Electrolyte abnormalities** (renal tubular disorders)
   - **Structural abnormalities** on imaging (polycystic kidneys, hydronephrosis, small kidneys)
   - **History of kidney transplantation**

---

### GFR Estimation

**Equations**:

**1. CKD-EPI (Chronic Kidney Disease Epidemiology Collaboration)**:
- **Most accurate**
- Uses: Serum creatinine, age, sex, race
- **Preferred** for clinical use

**2. MDRD (Modification of Diet in Renal Disease)**:
- Older, less accurate
- Underestimates GFR >60

**3. Cockcroft-Gault**:
- Uses: Serum creatinine, age, weight, sex
- **Estimates creatinine clearance** (not GFR)
- Used for **drug dosing**

**Limitations**:
- **Inaccurate** in:
  - Extremes of body size (very obese, amputees)
  - Severe malnutrition, muscle wasting
  - Pregnancy
  - Rapidly changing kidney function (AKI)

**Gold Standard**:
- **Inulin clearance** (research only)
- **Iothalamate clearance**, **Iohexol clearance** (specialized centers)

---

### CKD Staging (by GFR)

| Stage | **GFR (mL/min/1.73 m²)** | **Description** | **Action** |
|-------|--------------------------|-----------------|------------|
| **G1** | **≥90** | Normal/high GFR + kidney damage | Diagnose/treat, slow progression |
| **G2** | **60-89** | Mildly ↓ GFR + kidney damage | Estimate progression |
| **G3a** | **45-59** | Mild-moderate ↓ GFR | Evaluate/treat complications |
| **G3b** | **30-44** | Moderate-severe ↓ GFR | Evaluate/treat complications |
| **G4** | **15-29** | Severely ↓ GFR | Prepare for RRT |
| **G5** | **<15** | **Kidney failure** | **RRT** (dialysis or transplant) |

**Note**: Stages G1-G2 require **evidence of kidney damage** (not just GFR)

---

### Albuminuria Staging

| Stage | **ACR (mg/g)** | **24-hr Urine (mg/day)** | **Description** |
|-------|----------------|--------------------------|-----------------|
| **A1** | **<30** | <30 | Normal to mildly increased |
| **A2** | **30-300** | 30-300 | **Moderately increased (Microalbuminuria)** |
| **A3** | **>300** | >300 | **Severely increased (Macroalbuminuria)** |

**ACR** = Albumin-to-Creatinine Ratio (spot urine)

**Higher albuminuria** → Worse prognosis (↑ progression, ↑ cardiovascular risk)

## Etiology

### Leading Causes (US)

**1. Diabetes Mellitus (40%)**:
- **Type 2 > Type 1**
- **Diabetic nephropathy** (nodular glomerulosclerosis)

**2. Hypertension (30%)**:
- **Hypertensive nephrosclerosis** (arteriolar sclerosis)

**3. Glomerulonephritis (10-15%)**:
- **IgA nephropathy** (most common primary GN worldwide)
- **Focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)**
- **Membranous nephropathy**

**4. Polycystic Kidney Disease**:
- **Autosomal dominant** (ADPKD)
- Leading **genetic cause**

**5. Chronic Interstitial Nephritis**:
- **Analgesic nephropathy** (chronic NSAIDs)
- **Chronic pyelonephritis**
- **Aristolochic acid** (Chinese herbs)

**6. Obstructive Uropathy**:
- **BPH**, stones, tumors

**7. Other**:
- **HIV-associated nephropathy (HIVAN)**
- **Lupus nephritis**
- **Amyloidosis**
- **Multiple myeloma** (cast nephropathy)

---

### Risk Factors

**Non-modifiable**:
- **Age** >60
- **Family history** (CKD, diabetes, hypertension)
- **Race**: African American, Hispanic, Native American (↑ risk)

**Modifiable**:
- **Diabetes mellitus**
- **Hypertension**
- **Obesity**
- **Smoking**
- **Nephrotoxins**: NSAIDs, aminoglycosides, contrast

## Pathophysiology and Progression

### Progression Mechanisms

**Hyperfiltration Theory**:
```
Nephron loss (any cause)
      ↓
Remaining nephrons hyperfiltrate (↑ GFR per nephron)
      ↓
↑ Glomerular capillary pressure
      ↓
Glomerular injury (proteinuria, sclerosis)
      ↓
Further nephron loss
      ↓
Vicious cycle → Progressive CKD
```

**Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS)**:
- **Angiotensin II**: Efferent arteriole constriction → ↑ intraglomerular pressure
- **Aldosterone**: Fibrosis

**Proteinuria**:
- **Toxic to tubules** → tubulointerstitial inflammation, fibrosis

**Other Factors**:
- **Hypertension** (systemic and glomerular)
- **Dyslipidemia**
- **Hyperglycemia** (diabetes)
- **Smoking**

---

### Factors Accelerating Progression

**Mnemonic: "DASH"**
- **D**iabetes (poor control)
- **A**lbuminuria (>300 mg/day)
- **S**moking
- **H**ypertension (uncontrolled)

**Others**:
- **Obesity**
- **Acute kidney injury** (recurrent AKI → CKD)
- **Nephrotoxins** (NSAIDs, contrast)
- **Obstructive uropathy**

## Clinical Features

### Early CKD (Stages 1-3)

**Often asymptomatic** (discovered incidentally on labs)

**May have**:
- **Hypertension**
- **Mild anemia**
- **Proteinuria** (if glomerular disease)
- **Nocturia** (loss of concentrating ability)

---

### Advanced CKD (Stages 4-5)

**Uremia** (accumulation of waste products):

**Constitutional**:
- **Fatigue**, weakness
- **Anorexia**, nausea, vomiting
- **Weight loss**

**Cardiovascular**:
- **Hypertension** (volume overload, RAAS activation)
- **Left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH)**
- **Pericarditis** (uremic pericarditis - "friction rub")
- **Cardiomyopathy**

**Neurologic**:
- **Uremic encephalopathy**: Confusion, asterixis, seizures, coma
- **Peripheral neuropathy**: "Restless legs syndrome", sensory loss
- **Autonomic neuropathy**

**Hematologic**:
- **Anemia** (↓ erythropoietin)
- **Bleeding** (platelet dysfunction, despite normal platelet count)

**Gastrointestinal**:
- **Nausea, vomiting**
- **Anorexia**
- **Gastritis**, **peptic ulcers**
- **Uremic fetor** (ammonia breath)

**Musculoskeletal**:
- **Renal osteodystrophy** (CKD-MBD)
- **Bone pain**, fractures
- **Muscle weakness**

**Dermatologic**:
- **Pruritus** (uremic pruritus)
- **Uremic frost** (urea crystals on skin - rare, severe)
- **Pallor** (anemia)

**Metabolic**:
- **Metabolic acidosis** (↓ H⁺ excretion)
- **Hyperkalemia** (↓ K⁺ excretion)
- **Hyperphosphatemia** (↓ phosphate excretion)
- **Hypocalcemia** (↓ vitamin D activation)
- **Hyperparathyroidism** (secondary)

---

## Complications

### 1. Cardiovascular Disease (Leading Cause of Death)

**Mechanisms**:
- **Accelerated atherosclerosis**: Dyslipidemia, inflammation, oxidative stress
- **Left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH)**: Hypertension, volume overload, anemia
- **Vascular calcification**: CKD-MBD (high phosphate, Ca-P product)
- **Uremia**: Direct cardiotoxicity

**Manifestations**:
- **Coronary artery disease** (MI)
- **Heart failure**
- **Arrhythmias** (hyperkalemia)
- **Sudden cardiac death**

**Risk**:
- **CKD = CAD risk equivalent**
- **GFR <60**: 2-3× ↑ cardiovascular mortality

---

### 2. Anemia

**Etiology**:

**Primary**: **↓ Erythropoietin (EPO)** production by kidneys
- **EPO** normally stimulates RBC production
- **CKD** → ↓ EPO → ↓ RBC production

**Secondary**:
- **Iron deficiency**: Bleeding (uremic platelet dysfunction, anticoagulation on dialysis)
- **Inflammation** (↑ hepcidin → ↓ iron availability)
- **Shortened RBC lifespan** (uremic toxins)
- **Bone marrow suppression** (aluminum toxicity - from phosphate binders, rare now)

**Clinical Features**:
- **Fatigue**, weakness
- **Dyspnea** on exertion
- **Tachycardia**
- **Exacerbates LVH**

**Diagnosis**:
- **Normocytic, normochromic anemia**
- **Typically starts** at **GFR <60** (Stage 3)
- **Worsens** as GFR declines

**Workup**:
- **CBC**: Hemoglobin, MCV
- **Iron studies**:
  - **Serum iron**, **TIBC**, **Ferritin**, **Transferrin saturation (TSAT)**
  - **Goal**: Ferritin >100 ng/mL (dialysis >200), TSAT >20%
- **Reticulocyte count**
- **Rule out other causes**: B12, folate, hemolysis

**Management**:

**Iron Replacement** (if deficient):
- **Oral iron**: Ferrous sulfate 325 mg TID
  - **Less effective** in CKD (inflammation, hepcidin)
- **IV iron**: More effective (especially on dialysis)
  - **Iron sucrose**, **ferric gluconate**, **iron dextran**

**Erythropoiesis-Stimulating Agents (ESAs)**:
- **Epoetin alfa**, **Darbepoetin alfa**
- **Indications**: Hgb <10 g/dL (symptomatic anemia) + adequate iron stores
- **Dosing**: Titrate to **Hgb goal 10-11.5 g/dL**
  - **NOT >11.5** (↑ cardiovascular risk, stroke, thrombosis)
- **Monitor**: Hgb monthly, adjust dose

**Blood Transfusion**:
- **Avoid if possible** (risk of allosensitization → affects transplant)
- **Only if**: Severe anemia (Hgb <7) with symptoms, ESA failure

---

### 3. CKD-Mineral and Bone Disorder (CKD-MBD)

**Definition**: Abnormalities in calcium, phosphate, PTH, vitamin D, and bone in CKD

**Pathophysiology**:
```
↓ GFR
  ↓
↓ Phosphate excretion → Hyperphosphatemia
  ↓
↓ Calcium (binds phosphate)
  ↓
↓ Vitamin D activation (kidneys convert 25-OH-D → 1,25-OH-D)
  ↓
↓ Calcium absorption (intestine)
  ↓
Hypocalcemia + Hyperphosphatemia
  ↓
↑ PTH (secondary hyperparathyroidism)
  ↓
Bone resorption (↑ calcium, ↑ phosphate from bone)
  ↓
Renal Osteodystrophy + Vascular Calcification
```

**Components**:

**A. Secondary Hyperparathyroidism**:
- **Elevated PTH** (normal response to ↓ calcium, ↑ phosphate)
- **Bone resorption** → osteitis fibrosa cystica (high-turnover bone disease)

**B. Adynamic Bone Disease**:
- **Suppressed PTH** (over-treatment with vitamin D, calcium)
- **Low bone turnover**

**C. Vascular Calcification**:
- **Calcium-phosphate deposition** in vessels
- **↑ Cardiovascular mortality**

---

**Laboratory Findings**:

| Parameter | **Finding** |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Phosphate** | **Elevated** (>4.5 mg/dL) |
| **Calcium** | Low-normal or low |
| **PTH** | **Elevated** (secondary hyperparathyroidism) |
| **25-OH Vitamin D** | Low (nutritional deficiency) |
| **1,25-OH Vitamin D** | **Low** (↓ renal activation) |
| **Alkaline phosphatase** | Elevated (bone turnover) |

---

**Clinical Features**:

**Bone Disease** (Renal Osteodystrophy):
- **Bone pain**
- **Fractures** (pathologic)
- **Skeletal deformities** (children)
- **Muscle weakness**

**Vascular Calcification**:
- **Coronary artery calcification**
- **Valvular calcification** (aortic, mitral)
- **Medial arterial calcification** (Mönckeberg sclerosis)
- **Calciphylaxis** (rare, severe - calcification of small vessels → skin necrosis)

**Soft Tissue Calcification**:
- **Metastatic calcification**: Eyes (red eye), joints (pseudogout), periarticular

---

**Management**:

**1. Phosphate Control** (MOST IMPORTANT):

**Dietary Restriction**:
- **<800-1000 mg/day** phosphate
- Limit: Dairy, nuts, processed foods, sodas

**Phosphate Binders**:
- **Taken with meals** (bind dietary phosphate)

**Calcium-based**:
- **Calcium carbonate**, **calcium acetate**
- **Caution**: Hypercalcemia, vascular calcification (if Ca-P product >55)

**Non-calcium-based** (Preferred):
- **Sevelamer** (Renagel, Renvela)
- **Lanthanum carbonate**
- **Advantages**: No hypercalcemia, less vascular calcification

**Aluminum-based** (Avoid):
- **Aluminum hydroxide**
- **Risk**: Aluminum toxicity (bone disease, encephalopathy)

**Goal**: Phosphate **3.5-5.5 mg/dL** (Stage 3-4), **<5.5 mg/dL** (Stage 5)

---

**2. Calcium**:
- **Monitor** calcium levels
- **Goal**: Normal range (8.5-10.5 mg/dL)
- **Supplement** if low (with vitamin D)
- **Avoid** if high Ca-P product (>55 mg²/dL²)

---

**3. Vitamin D**:

**Nutritional Vitamin D** (if deficient):
- **Ergocalciferol (Vitamin D2)** or **Cholecalciferol (Vitamin D3)**
- **Goal**: 25-OH Vitamin D **>30 ng/mL**

**Active Vitamin D** (Calcitriol):
- **Calcitriol** (1,25-OH Vitamin D) or **Alfacalcidol**
- **Indications**: Secondary hyperparathyroidism with low vitamin D
- **Mechanism**: ↑ Calcium absorption, ↓ PTH
- **Monitor**: Calcium, phosphate (risk of hypercalcemia, hyperphosphatemia)

**Vitamin D Analogs** (Selective):
- **Paricalcitol**, **Doxercalciferol**
- **Less hypercalcemia** than calcitriol

---

**4. PTH Management**:

**Goal PTH** (KDIGO):
- **Stage 3-4**: Maintain in normal range (if elevated, investigate/treat)
- **Stage 5 (dialysis)**: **2-9× upper limit of normal** (150-600 pg/mL)
  - **Overly suppressed** → adynamic bone disease
  - **Very high** → high-turnover bone disease

**Treatment** (if PTH elevated):
- **Phosphate binders** (↓ phosphate → ↓ PTH stimulus)
- **Vitamin D analogs** (↓ PTH)
- **Calcimimetics**:
  - **Cinacalcet** (Sensipar): ↑ Calcium-sensing receptor sensitivity → ↓ PTH
  - **Indications**: Secondary hyperparathyroidism on dialysis (PTH >800 pg/mL)
  - **Side effects**: Hypocalcemia, nausea

**Parathyroidectomy**:
- **Indications**: Severe, refractory hyperparathyroidism (PTH >800-1000 despite medical therapy), calciphylaxis, bone pain/fractures
- **Subtotal** or **total parathyroidectomy + autotransplantation**

---

### 4. Metabolic Acidosis

**Pathophysiology**:
- **↓ GFR** → ↓ H⁺ excretion (↓ ammonia production, ↓ bicarbonate reabsorption)
- **Anion gap metabolic acidosis** (uremic acids accumulate)

**Consequences**:
- **Muscle wasting** (protein catabolism)
- **Bone disease** (bone buffers acid → calcium loss)
- **Hyperkalemia** (H⁺/K⁺ exchange)

**Management**:
- **Sodium bicarbonate** (oral):
  - **Goal**: Serum bicarbonate **22-26 mEq/L**
  - **Dosing**: 650-1300 mg (1-2 tabs) PO TID
- **Dietary**: Low protein (↓ acid load) - but must maintain adequate nutrition

---

### 5. Hyperkalemia

**Pathophysiology**:
- **↓ GFR** → ↓ K⁺ excretion
- **Medications**: ACE inhibitors, ARBs, aldosterone antagonists (spironolactone), NSAIDs
- **Metabolic acidosis** (K⁺ shifts out of cells)

**Clinical Features**:
- **Often asymptomatic** (until severe)
- **ECG changes**: Peaked T waves → widened QRS → sine wave → cardiac arrest
- **Muscle weakness**

**Management**:

**Acute** (K⁺ >6.5 or ECG changes):
- **Calcium gluconate** (membrane stabilization)
- **Insulin + glucose** (shift K⁺ into cells)
- **Sodium bicarbonate** (if acidotic)
- **Dialysis** (if refractory)

**Chronic**:
- **Dietary restriction** (<2 g/day K⁺)
- **Discontinue K⁺-sparing agents** (ACE/ARBs - if possible)
- **Loop diuretics** (if residual kidney function)
- **K⁺ binders**:
  - **Sodium polystyrene sulfonate** (Kayexalate) - GI exchange resin
  - **Patiromer** (Veltassa), **Sodium zirconium cyclosilicate** (Lokelma) - newer, better tolerated

---

### 6. Hypertension

**Prevalence**: 80-90% of CKD patients

**Mechanisms**:
- **Volume overload** (↓ Na⁺ excretion)
- **RAAS activation**
- **Sympathetic activation**
- **Endothelial dysfunction**

**Management**:
- **Goal BP**: **<130/80 mmHg** (KDIGO 2021)
- **<120/80 mmHg** (if tolerated, albuminuria >300 mg/day)

**First-Line**:
- **ACE inhibitors** or **ARBs** (especially if albuminuria, diabetes)
  - **Renoprotective** (↓ proteinuria, slow progression)
  - **Monitor**: Creatinine (may ↑ 30% - acceptable), potassium

**Additional Agents**:
- **Diuretics**: Loop (if GFR <30), thiazides (if GFR >30)
- **Calcium channel blockers**
- **Beta-blockers** (if heart failure, CAD)

---

### 7. Uremic Bleeding

**Mechanism**:
- **Platelet dysfunction** (uremic toxins impair platelet adhesion/aggregation)
- **Normal platelet count**, **normal PT/aPTT**, **prolonged bleeding time**

**Clinical Features**:
- **Bruising**, **mucosal bleeding** (epistaxis, GI bleeding)

**Management**:
- **Dialysis** (removes uremic toxins - most effective)
- **DDAVP** (Desmopressin): Releases vWF → ↑ platelet function (temporary)
- **Conjugated estrogens**: ↑ Factor VIII, vWF (longer-lasting)
- **Cryoprecipitate**: vWF, Factor VIII
- **Platelet transfusion**: Rarely effective (platelets also become dysfunctional)

---

## Management

### Goals
1. **Slow progression** of CKD
2. **Treat reversible causes**
3. **Manage complications**
4. **Prepare for RRT** (if Stage 4-5)

---

### Slow Progression

**1. Blood Pressure Control** (MOST IMPORTANT):
- **Goal**: <130/80 mmHg
- **ACE inhibitors / ARBs**:
  - **Renoprotective** (beyond BP lowering)
  - **↓ Proteinuria**, ↓ intraglomerular pressure
  - **Preferred** in CKD with albuminuria, diabetes

**2. Glycemic Control** (if diabetic):
- **HbA1c goal**: **<7%** (or <6.5% if tolerated without hypoglycemia)
- **Tight control** slows diabetic nephropathy progression

**3. Proteinuria Reduction**:
- **ACE inhibitors / ARBs** (first-line)
- **SGLT2 inhibitors** (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin):
  - **Renoprotective** in diabetic and non-diabetic CKD
  - **↓ Albuminuria**, slow GFR decline, ↓ cardiovascular events
- **GLP-1 agonists**: Cardiovascular + renal benefits (diabetic patients)

**4. Lifestyle Modifications**:
- **Smoking cessation** (CRITICAL - smoking accelerates CKD)
- **Weight loss** (if obese)
- **Exercise**

**5. Dietary**:
- **Low-protein diet**: **0.6-0.8 g/kg/day** (Stage 4-5)
  - **Slows progression** (↓ hyperfiltration, ↓ uremic toxins)
  - **Risk**: Malnutrition (close monitoring)
- **Low sodium**: <2 g/day (BP control)
- **Low phosphate**: <800-1000 mg/day

**6. Avoid Nephrotoxins**:
- **NSAIDs** (except low-dose aspirin for CAD)
- **Aminoglycosides**
- **Contrast** (if unavoidable: pre-hydrate)
- **Herbal supplements** (aristolochic acid)

---

### Treat Reversible Causes

**1. Obstruction**: Relieve (BPH, stones)
**2. Volume depletion**: Restore volume
**3. Medications**: Stop nephrotoxins (NSAIDs, ACE/ARBs if AKI)
**4. Infections**: Treat (pyelonephritis)

---

### Manage Complications

**Anemia**: ESAs (target Hgb 10-11.5), iron supplementation
**CKD-MBD**: Phosphate binders, vitamin D, PTH control
**Metabolic acidosis**: Sodium bicarbonate (goal HCO₃ 22-26)
**Hyperkalemia**: Diet, K⁺ binders, adjust medications
**Hypertension**: ACE/ARBs, diuretics, CCBs
**Dyslipidemia**: Statins (↓ cardiovascular risk)
**Cardiovascular disease**: Aspirin, statins, BP/DM control

---

### Vaccinations

**Recommended**:
- **Influenza** (annual)
- **Pneumococcal** (PCV13, PPSV23)
- **Hepatitis B** (especially pre-dialysis) - check anti-HBs titers
- **COVID-19**

---

### Referral to Nephrology

**Indications**:
- **GFR <30** (Stage 4) - prepare for RRT
- **Rapidly declining GFR** (>5 mL/min/year)
- **Persistent proteinuria** (ACR >300 mg/g)
- **Difficult-to-control hypertension** or complications
- **Uncertain etiology** (may need biopsy)

---

## Renal Replacement Therapy

### Indications (Stage 5 - GFR <15)

**Absolute**:
- **Uremic symptoms**: Pericarditis, encephalopathy, neuropathy, bleeding
- **Refractory volume overload** (pulmonary edema)
- **Refractory hyperkalemia**
- **Severe metabolic acidosis**
- **Malnutrition** despite adequate intake (uremia)

**Relative**:
- **GFR <10-15** (even if asymptomatic - prevent complications)

---

### Modalities

#### **1. Hemodialysis (HD)**

**Mechanism**:
- **Blood** pumped through **semipermeable membrane** (dialyzer)
- **Diffusion** (solute removal) + **Ultrafiltration** (volume removal)

**Access**:
- **Arteriovenous fistula (AVF)** - **PREFERRED**:
  - **Artery-vein anastomosis** (usually radial artery - cephalic vein)
  - **Matures** in 2-4 months
  - **Lowest infection rate**, longest survival
- **Arteriovenous graft (AVG)**:
  - **Synthetic conduit** (if poor veins)
  - Matures faster (2-4 weeks)
  - Higher infection, thrombosis rate than AVF
- **Tunneled central venous catheter (CVC)**:
  - **Temporary** (until AVF/AVG matures) or **permanent** (if no other access)
  - **Highest infection rate**

**Frequency**:
- **3× per week**, 3-4 hours/session (in-center)
- **Home HD**: More frequent, shorter sessions (daily)

**Advantages**:
- **Efficient** (rapid solute/volume removal)
- **3× per week** (vs daily PD)

**Disadvantages**:
- **Requires vascular access**
- **Hemodynamic stress** (hypotension during treatment)
- **Dietary restrictions** (K⁺, phosphate, fluid)
- **Lifestyle**: In-center 3× per week

**Complications**:
- **Hypotension** (during dialysis)
- **Muscle cramps**
- **Infection** (catheter, AVF/AVG)
- **Thrombosis** (access)
- **Dialysis disequilibrium syndrome** (cerebral edema - rare, first few sessions)

---

#### **2. Peritoneal Dialysis (PD)**

**Mechanism**:
- **Peritoneum** as semipermeable membrane
- **Dialysate** instilled into peritoneal cavity → diffusion/osmosis → drain

**Types**:

**Continuous Ambulatory PD (CAPD)**:
- **Manual exchanges** 4-5× per day (patient-performed)
- **Dwell time** 4-6 hours

**Automated PD (APD / CCPD)**:
- **Cycler machine** performs exchanges at night (while sleeping)
- **More convenient**

**Access**:
- **Peritoneal catheter** (Tenckhoff catheter) - tunneled into peritoneal cavity

**Advantages**:
- **Home-based** (independence)
- **Continuous** (more physiologic, better fluid/electrolyte control)
- **No vascular access** needed
- **Fewer dietary restrictions**
- **Better preservation** of residual kidney function

**Disadvantages**:
- **Less efficient** than HD (slower solute removal)
- **Daily** (vs 3× week HD)
- **Catheter** (body image, infection risk)
- **Not suitable** if:
  - Abdominal surgery (adhesions)
  - Abdominal wall hernias
  - Obesity (inadequate clearance)

**Complications**:
- **Peritonitis** (most common):
  - **Cloudy dialysate**, abdominal pain, fever
  - **Diagnosis**: Dialysate WBC >100/μL with >50% PMNs
  - **Treatment**: Intraperitoneal antibiotics (vancomycin + ceftazidime or aminoglycoside)
- **Catheter infection** (exit-site, tunnel)
- **Ultrafiltration failure** (over time - peritoneal membrane sclerosis)
- **Hernias** (↑ intra-abdominal pressure)
- **Hyperglycemia** (glucose in dialysate absorbed)
- **Protein loss** (into dialysate)

---

#### **3. Kidney Transplantation**

**Best long-term outcome** (vs dialysis):
- **Longer survival**
- **Better quality of life**
- **Fewer dietary restrictions**

**Types**:
- **Deceased donor** (most common)
- **Living donor** (better outcomes - shorter cold ischemia time, planned)

**Evaluation**:
- **Recipient**: Cardiac clearance, malignancy screening, infections
- **Donor**: ABO compatibility, HLA matching (better match = less rejection)

**Immunosuppression**:
- **Induction**: Anti-thymocyte globulin (ATG) or Basiliximab
- **Maintenance** (triple therapy):
  - **Calcineurin inhibitor**: Tacrolimus (preferred) or Cyclosporine
  - **Antimetabolite**: Mycophenolate mofetil
  - **Corticosteroid**: Prednisone (often tapered/withdrawn)

**Complications**:
- **Rejection**:
  - **Acute**: Days to months (T-cell mediated, antibody-mediated)
  - **Chronic**: Months to years (gradual graft decline)
- **Infection**: CMV, BK virus, fungal (immunosuppression)
- **Malignancy**: PTLD (post-transplant lymphoproliferative disorder), skin cancer
- **Calcineurin inhibitor toxicity**: Nephrotoxicity, hypertension, diabetes
- **Cardiovascular disease**
- **Recurrent disease** (original disease in transplant)

**Contraindications**:
- **Active infection**
- **Active malignancy** (wait 2-5 years after treatment)
- **Severe cardiac/pulmonary disease**
- **Non-adherence**

## High-Yield Points

### CKD Definition
- **GFR <60** for **>3 months** OR
- **Markers of kidney damage** (albuminuria, abnormal sediment, structural abnormality) for >3 months

### Staging

| Stage | **GFR** | **Key Points** |
|-------|---------|----------------|
| **G3a** | 45-59 | Start evaluating complications |
| **G3b** | 30-44 | Evaluate/treat complications |
| **G4** | 15-29 | **Prepare for RRT** |
| **G5** | <15 | **Kidney failure - RRT** |

### Leading Causes
- **Diabetes** (40%) - #1
- **Hypertension** (30%) - #2
- **Glomerulonephritis** (10-15%)

### Slowing Progression
- **BP control**: <130/80 (ACE/ARBs preferred)
- **Glycemic control**: HbA1c <7%
- **Proteinuria reduction**: ACE/ARBs, SGLT2 inhibitors
- **Smoking cessation**
- **Avoid nephrotoxins** (NSAIDs)

### Complications

**Anemia**:
- **↓ Erythropoietin** (primary cause)
- **Starts at GFR <60**
- **ESAs** (target Hgb 10-11.5, NOT >11.5)
- **Iron supplementation** (goal ferritin >100, TSAT >20%)

**CKD-MBD**:
```
↓ GFR → ↑ Phosphate → ↓ Calcium → ↓ Vit D activation
   ↓
↑ PTH (secondary hyperparathyroidism)
   ↓
Bone disease + Vascular calcification
```
- **Phosphate binders** (sevelamer preferred over calcium-based)
- **Vitamin D** (calcitriol)
- **PTH goal**: 2-9× ULN (dialysis)

**Metabolic Acidosis**:
- **Sodium bicarbonate** (goal HCO₃ 22-26)

**Hyperkalemia**:
- **Dietary restriction**, K⁺ binders
- **Acute**: Calcium gluconate + insulin/glucose + dialysis (if refractory)

### Renal Replacement Therapy

**Hemodialysis**:
- **3× per week**, 3-4 hours
- **Access**: **AVF preferred** > AVG > Catheter
- **Complication**: Infection, hypotension

**Peritoneal Dialysis**:
- **Daily**, home-based
- **Complication**: **Peritonitis** (cloudy dialysate, abdominal pain)
  - **Dialysate WBC >100/μL with >50% PMNs**
  - **Treatment**: Intraperitoneal antibiotics

**Transplant**:
- **Best long-term outcome**
- **Immunosuppression**: Tacrolimus + mycophenolate + prednisone
- **Complication**: Rejection, infection (CMV, BK virus), malignancy (PTLD)

### Question 8
A 60-year-old man with CKD Stage 5 is preparing for hemodialysis. Which vascular access has the LONGEST survival and LOWEST infection rate?

- **Very temporary** (ICU, urgent dialysis)

### Question 10
A 68-year-old man with CKD Stage 4 is started on an ACE inhibitor for blood pressure control. One week later, his creatinine rises from 2.5 to 3.8 mg/dL. What is the MOST likely explanation?

A) Acute tubular necrosis
B) Bilateral renal artery stenosis
C) Acute interstitial nephritis
D) Disease progression

**Answer**: **B) Bilateral renal artery stenosis**

**Explanation**:
- **ACE Inhibitor-Induced AKI**:
  - **Creatinine ↑ from 2.5 to 3.8** (↑ 52% - significant) ✓
  - **Shortly after starting ACE inhibitor** (1 week) ✓

**Mechanism**:
- **Normal**: Angiotensin II constricts **efferent arteriole** → maintains GFR
- **ACE inhibitor**: Blocks Ang II → **Efferent dilation** → ↓ intraglomerular pressure → ↓ GFR

**Severe GFR drop suggests**:
- **Bilateral renal artery stenosis** (or single kidney):
  - **Kidneys depend on efferent constriction** to maintain GFR
  - **ACE inhibitor** → loss of compensation → **severe GFR drop**

**Management**:
- **Stop ACE inhibitor**
- **Workup**: Renal artery Doppler ultrasound, CT/MR angiography
- **If bilateral RAS confirmed**: Revascularization (angioplasty, stenting, surgery)

**Acceptable Cr rise**:
- **Up to 30%** is acceptable when starting ACE/ARB (hemodynamic effect)
- **This patient**: 52% rise → **excessive** → suspect RAS

**AIN**: Would have fever, rash, eosinophilia (not just Cr rise)

**Disease progression**: Too rapid for natural progression (1 week)

## Summary

**Chronic Kidney Disease (CKD)**:
- **Definition**: GFR <60 OR kidney damage markers for >3 months
- **Staging**: G1-G5 (by GFR), A1-A3 (by albuminuria)

**Causes**: Diabetes (40%), Hypertension (30%), Glomerulonephritis (10-15%)

**Slowing Progression**:
- **BP control** <130/80 (ACE/ARBs preferred)
- **Glycemic control** (HbA1c <7%)
- **Proteinuria reduction** (ACE/ARBs, SGLT2 inhibitors)
- **Smoking cessation**, avoid nephrotoxins

**Complications**:
- **Anemia**: ↓ EPO (ESAs target Hgb 10-11.5, iron supplementation)
- **CKD-MBD**: ↑ Phosphate → ↑ PTH → Bone disease + vascular calcification (phosphate binders, vitamin D)
- **Metabolic acidosis**: Sodium bicarbonate (goal HCO₃ 22-26)
- **Hyperkalemia**: Dietary restriction, K⁺ binders
- **Cardiovascular disease**: Leading cause of death

**RRT** (Stage 5 - GFR <15):
- **Hemodialysis**: 3× per week, AVF preferred (lowest infection)
- **Peritoneal Dialysis**: Daily, home-based; peritonitis (cloudy dialysate, WBC >100)
- **Transplant**: Best long-term outcome; immunosuppression (tacrolimus + mycophenolate + prednisone)
