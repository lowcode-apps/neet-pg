## DEFINITION & CLASSIFICATION

### Definition

**Respiratory Failure**: Inability of the respiratory system to maintain adequate gas exchange

**Arterial Blood Gas Criteria** (at Sea Level, Room Air):
- **PaO₂ <60 mmHg** (Hypoxemia), OR
- **PaCO₂ >45 mmHg** (Hypercapnia)

### Classification

```
RESPIRATORY FAILURE
│
├─ BY MECHANISM
│  ├─ TYPE I (Hypoxemic) - PaO₂ <60, PaCO₂ normal/low
│  │  • Pneumonia, Pulmonary edema, PE, ILD, ARDS
│  │  • V/Q mismatch, Shunt
│  │
│  └─ TYPE II (Hypercapnic) - PaCO₂ >45 (± Hypoxemia)
│     • COPD exacerbation, Neuromuscular disease, CNS depression
│     • Hypoventilation
│
├─ BY ONSET
│  ├─ ACUTE (Minutes to hours)
│  │  • ARDS, Pneumonia, PE, Pneumothorax
│  │
│  ├─ CHRONIC (Weeks to months)
│  │  • COPD, ILD, Neuromuscular disease
│  │
│  └─ ACUTE-ON-CHRONIC
│     • COPD exacerbation, Neuromuscular crisis
│
└─ BY LOCATION OF DEFECT
   ├─ PUMP FAILURE (Type II)
   │  • CNS: Drug overdose, stroke
   │  • Neuromuscular: MG, GBS, ALS
   │  • Chest wall: Kyphoscoliosis, flail chest
   │
   └─ LUNG PARENCHYMAL (Type I)
      • Alveolar: Pneumonia, pulmonary edema
      • Interstitial: ILD, ARDS
      • Vascular: PE, Pulmonary hypertension
```

### Mnemonic: "VENT" for Causes of Respiratory Failure
- **V** - Ventilation/Perfusion mismatch (Type I)
- **E** - (hypo)vEntilation (Type II)
- **N** - Neuromuscular/CNS (Type II)
- **T** - parenchymal/inTerstitial disease (Type I)

## PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

### Normal Gas Exchange

**Oxygen Transport**:
1. **Ventilation**: Air movement in/out of alveoli
2. **Diffusion**: O₂ crosses alveolar-capillary membrane
3. **Perfusion**: Blood flow through pulmonary capillaries
4. **V/Q Matching**: Ventilation matches perfusion (V/Q ratio ~0.8-1.0)

**Carbon Dioxide Elimination**:
- Depends primarily on **alveolar ventilation**
- PaCO₂ ∝ CO₂ production / Alveolar ventilation

### Mechanisms of Hypoxemia

| Mechanism | PaO₂ Response to 100% O₂ | A-a Gradient | Example |
|-----------|--------------------------|--------------|---------|
| **1. Low FiO₂** | ↑ Corrects | Normal | High altitude |
| **2. Hypoventilation** | ↑ Corrects | **Normal** | Drug overdose, neuromuscular disease |
| **3. V/Q Mismatch** | ↑ Partially corrects | **↑ Increased** | **COPD, Asthma, PE** (MOST COMMON) |
| **4. Shunt** | **No correction** | **↑↑ Markedly increased** | **ARDS**, Pneumonia, Pulmonary edema |
| **5. Diffusion Limitation** | ↑ Corrects | ↑ Increased | **ILD** (exercise), High altitude |

**A-a Gradient** = PAO₂ - PaO₂
- **Normal**: 5-15 mmHg (↑ with age: Age/4 + 4)
- PAO₂ = (FiO₂ × [Patm - PH₂O]) - (PaCO₂ / 0.8)
- At room air (FiO₂ 0.21): PAO₂ ≈ 150 - (PaCO₂ × 1.25)

**Key Point**: **Normal A-a gradient** = **Hypoventilation** (extra-pulmonary), **↑ A-a gradient** = **Lung parenchymal disease**

### Mechanisms of Hypercapnia

**PaCO₂ = (VCO₂ × 0.863) / V̇A**

Where:
- VCO₂ = CO₂ production
- V̇A = Alveolar ventilation

**Causes**:
1. **↑ CO₂ Production** (rare alone, compensate by ↑ ventilation)
   - Fever, sepsis, overfeeding
2. **↓ Alveolar Ventilation** (MOST COMMON)
   - CNS depression, neuromuscular disease, airway obstruction

## TYPE I RESPIRATORY FAILURE (HYPOXEMIC)

### Definition
- **PaO₂ <60 mmHg** on room air
- **PaCO₂ Normal or ↓** (hyperventilation due to hypoxemia)

### Mechanisms
- **V/Q Mismatch** (MOST COMMON)
- **Shunt** (intrapulmonary or intracardiac)
- **Diffusion limitation** (exercise, ILD)

### Common Causes

| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Alveolar Filling** | **Pneumonia** (MOST COMMON), Pulmonary edema (cardiogenic, non-cardiogenic), Hemorrhage |
| **Interstitial/Alveolar Injury** | **ARDS**, ILD, Radiation pneumonitis |
| **Vascular** | **Pulmonary embolism**, Pulmonary hypertension |
| **Airway/Alveolar Collapse** | Atelectasis, Bronchiectasis |

### Clinical Features

**Symptoms**:
- **Dyspnea** (MOST COMMON)
- Tachypnea (RR >20/min)
- Cyanosis (PaO₂ <55 mmHg)
- Confusion, agitation (severe hypoxemia)

**Signs**:
- Tachycardia
- Accessory muscle use
- Nasal flaring
- Cyanosis (central: Tongue, lips)

**ABG**:
- **PaO₂ <60 mmHg**
- **PaCO₂ Normal (<45 mmHg) or ↓** (hyperventilation)
- **pH Normal or ↑** (respiratory alkalosis if hyperventilating)

### Management

1. **Oxygen Therapy** (Correct hypoxemia)
   - Target SpO₂ **90-96%** (94-98% if no COPD risk)
   - Methods: Nasal cannula, Face mask, High-flow nasal oxygen

2. **Treat Underlying Cause**
   - Pneumonia: Antibiotics
   - Pulmonary edema: Diuretics, Nitrates
   - PE: Anticoagulation
   - ARDS: Lung-protective ventilation

3. **Mechanical Ventilation** (If severe)
   - Indications: PaO₂ <60 despite O₂, Impending respiratory arrest, Work of breathing

## TYPE II RESPIRATORY FAILURE (HYPERCAPNIC)

### Definition
- **PaCO₂ >45 mmHg**
- **± Hypoxemia** (PaO₂ <60 mmHg)
- **Alveolar Hypoventilation**

### Mechanisms
- **↓ Respiratory Drive** (CNS depression)
- **↓ Neuromuscular Function**
- **↑ Airway Resistance** / **↓ Lung Compliance**
- **↑ Work of Breathing** → Respiratory muscle fatigue

### Common Causes ("Can't Breathe")

```
TYPE II RESPIRATORY FAILURE CAUSES
│
├─ CNS DEPRESSION (↓ Respiratory drive)
│  • Drug overdose (Opioids, Benzodiazepines)
│  • Brainstem stroke, trauma
│  • Hypothyroidism (severe)
│  • Central sleep apnea
│
├─ NEUROMUSCULAR (↓ Respiratory muscle function)
│  • Myasthenia Gravis, GBS, ALS
│  • Phrenic nerve injury
│  • Cervical spinal cord injury (C3-C5)
│  • Muscular dystrophy, Polio
│
├─ CHEST WALL / PLEURA
│  • Kyphoscoliosis, Ankylosing spondylitis
│  • Flail chest, Rib fractures
│  • Massive pleural effusion, Pneumothorax
│  • Obesity hypoventilation syndrome
│
└─ AIRWAY OBSTRUCTION / PARENCHYMAL
   • **COPD exacerbation** (MOST COMMON)
   • Severe asthma (status asthmaticus)
   • Upper airway obstruction (foreign body, tumor)
   • End-stage ILD (rare)
```

### Mnemonic: "DOPE" for Type II Causes
- **D** - Drugs (CNS depression)
- **O** - Obstruction (COPD, asthma)
- **P** - Pump failure (neuromuscular, chest wall)
- **E** - End-stage lung disease

### Clinical Features

**Symptoms**:
- **Dyspnea**
- **Confusion, Somnolence** (CO₂ narcosis)
- Headache (vasodilation from hypercapnia)
- Asterixis (flapping tremor)

**Signs**:
- **↓ Respiratory rate** (if CNS/neuromuscular) OR Tachypnea (if COPD)
- **Shallow breathing**
- Paradoxical breathing (diaphragmatic paralysis)
- **Bounding pulse** (hypercapnia → vasodilation)
- **Papilledema** (↑ ICP from CO₂ retention)

**ABG**:
- **PaCO₂ >45 mmHg** (↑↑ if acute)
- **pH <7.35** (Acute respiratory acidosis) OR **pH normal** (Chronic, compensated)
- **HCO₃⁻**: Normal (Acute) OR ↑ (Chronic compensation)

**Acute vs Chronic**:
- **Acute**: pH ↓ 0.08 for every 10 mmHg ↑ in PaCO₂
- **Chronic**: pH ↓ 0.03 for every 10 mmHg ↑ in PaCO₂ (renal compensation → ↑ HCO₃⁻)

### Management

1. **Non-Invasive Ventilation (NIV)** - **FIRST-LINE for COPD, Neuromuscular**
   - BiPAP (Bilevel Positive Airway Pressure)
   - Reduces need for intubation, mortality

2. **Treat Underlying Cause**
   - **COPD exacerbation**: Bronchodilators, Steroids, Antibiotics (if infection)
   - **Drug overdose**: Naloxone (opioids), Flumazenil (benzodiazepines)
   - **Myasthenia Gravis**: Pyridostigmine, IVIG, Plasmapheresis
   - **GBS**: IVIG, Plasmapheresis

3. **Oxygen Therapy** (CAUTION in COPD)
   - **Controlled oxygen**: Target SpO₂ **88-92%** (avoid hyperoxia → ↑ CO₂)
   - **Venturi mask** (delivers precise FiO₂)

4. **Mechanical Ventilation** (If NIV fails or contraindicated)
   - Indications: pH <7.25, Severe acidosis, Hemodynamic instability, Altered mental status

## ACUTE RESPIRATORY DISTRESS SYNDROME (ARDS)

### Definition (Berlin Criteria, 2012)

**ARDS**: Acute diffuse inflammatory lung injury leading to **↑ pulmonary vascular permeability, ↑ lung weight, ↓ aerated lung tissue**

**Criteria** (ALL must be present):

| Component | Criterion |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Timing** | Within **1 week** of known clinical insult OR new/worsening respiratory symptoms |
| **Chest Imaging** | **Bilateral opacities** (CXR or CT) - NOT fully explained by effusions, collapse, nodules |
| **Origin of Edema** | **NOT fully explained by cardiac failure** or fluid overload (need objective assessment - Echo if doubt) |
| **Oxygenation Defect** | **PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio ≤300 mmHg** (with PEEP or CPAP ≥5 cm H₂O) |

### Severity Classification

| Severity | PaO₂/FiO₂ Ratio | Mortality |
|----------|-----------------|-----------|
| **Mild** | 200-300 mmHg | 27% |
| **Moderate** | 100-200 mmHg | 32% |
| **Severe** | **<100 mmHg** | **45%** |

**PEEP ≥5 cm H₂O** required for diagnosis

### Etiology

#### Direct Lung Injury (Pulmonary ARDS)

| Cause | Frequency |
|-------|-----------|
| **Pneumonia** (bacterial, viral, fungal) | **MOST COMMON** (40-50%) |
| **Aspiration** (gastric contents) | 10-15% |
| **Inhalation injury** (smoke, toxins) | 5% |
| **Pulmonary contusion** (chest trauma) | 5% |
| **Near-drowning** | Rare |

#### Indirect Lung Injury (Extrapulmonary ARDS)

| Cause | Frequency |
|-------|-----------|
| **Sepsis** | **MOST COMMON** (30-40%) |
| **Severe trauma / Polytrauma** | 10% |
| **Pancreatitis** | 5% |
| **Multiple transfusions** (TRALI) | 5% |
| **Drug overdose** (opioids, cocaine) | Rare |
| **Burns** | Rare |

**Mnemonic: "SPARTAN" for ARDS Causes**:
- **S** - Sepsis
- **P** - Pneumonia, Pancreatitis
- **A** - Aspiration
- **R** - tRauma
- **T** - Transfusions (TRALI)
- **A** - inhalAtion injury
- **N** - Near-drowning

### Pathophysiology

```
INITIATING INJURY (Sepsis, Pneumonia, Trauma)
    ↓
INFLAMMATORY RESPONSE
• Cytokine release (TNF-α, IL-1, IL-6, IL-8)
• Neutrophil recruitment and activation
    ↓
ENDOTHELIAL & EPITHELIAL INJURY
    ↓
↑ ALVEOLAR-CAPILLARY PERMEABILITY
    ↓
PULMONARY EDEMA (Protein-rich fluid in alveoli)
    ↓
HYALINE MEMBRANE FORMATION
    ↓
IMPAIRED GAS EXCHANGE
• Shunt (blood bypasses non-ventilated alveoli)
• V/Q mismatch
    ↓
SEVERE HYPOXEMIA (Refractory to oxygen)
    ↓
FIBROSIS (if prolonged, organizing phase)
```

**Three Phases**:
1. **Exudative** (0-7 days): Alveolar edema, hyaline membranes, neutrophil infiltration
2. **Proliferative** (7-21 days): Type II pneumocyte proliferation, fibroblast activity
3. **Fibrotic** (>21 days): Collagen deposition, fibrosis (if survives)

### Clinical Features

**Symptoms** (Develop over hours-days):
- **Severe dyspnea**
- Tachypnea (RR >30/min)
- Hypoxemia refractory to oxygen

**Signs**:
- **Diffuse crackles** (bilateral)
- Tachycardia
- **Cyanosis**
- Accessory muscle use

**Chest X-Ray**:
- **Bilateral diffuse infiltrates** ("white-out lung")
- **Normal heart size** (distinguishes from cardiogenic pulmonary edema)

**ABG**:
- **Severe hypoxemia** (PaO₂ <60 mmHg despite high FiO₂)
- **Respiratory alkalosis** (early, hyperventilation)
- **Respiratory acidosis** (late, fatigue)

**Pulmonary Artery Catheter** (if performed):
- **PCWP ≤18 mmHg** (normal LA pressure → excludes cardiogenic edema)

### Differential Diagnosis

**ARDS vs Cardiogenic Pulmonary Edema**:

| Feature | ARDS | Cardiogenic Edema |
|---------|------|-------------------|
| **Mechanism** | ↑ Permeability (non-cardiogenic) | ↑ Hydrostatic pressure |
| **CXR** | Bilateral infiltrates, **Normal heart size** | Infiltrates, **Enlarged heart**, Kerley B lines, Cephalization |
| **PCWP** | **≤18 mmHg** | **>18 mmHg** |
| **BNP** | Normal or mildly ↑ | **Markedly ↑** (>400-500 pg/mL) |
| **Response to diuretics** | **Poor** | **Good** |
| **Protein** (pleural fluid/edema) | **High** (>0.5) | **Low** (<0.5) |

### Management

#### 1. Lung-Protective Ventilation Strategy (CORNERSTONE)

**Evidence**: **ARDSNet Trial** (2000) - ↓ Mortality from 40% → 31%

**Principles**:

| Parameter | Target | Rationale |
|-----------|--------|-----------|
| **Tidal Volume (Vt)** | **6 mL/kg IBW** (Ideal Body Weight) | ↓ Volutrauma, barotrauma |
| **Plateau Pressure (Pplat)** | **≤30 cm H₂O** | ↓ Alveolar overdistension |
| **PEEP** | **5-15 cm H₂O** (titrated to FiO₂) | Recruit collapsed alveoli, ↓ FiO₂ requirement |
| **FiO₂** | Lowest to maintain **SpO₂ 88-95%** (PaO₂ 55-80 mmHg) | Avoid oxygen toxicity |
| **Respiratory Rate** | Adjust to maintain pH >7.20 | Permissive hypercapnia acceptable |

**Ideal Body Weight (IBW)**:
- **Male**: 50 + 0.91 × (Height in cm - 152.4)
- **Female**: 45.5 + 0.91 × (Height in cm - 152.4)

**Example**: 170 cm tall male → IBW = 50 + 0.91 × (170-152.4) = **66 kg** → Vt = 6 × 66 = **396 mL** (~400 mL)

**Permissive Hypercapnia**: Accept PaCO₂ >45 mmHg (even 60-80 mmHg) to avoid high tidal volumes
- **Contraindication**: ↑ ICP, severe acidosis (pH <7.15)

#### 2. PEEP (Positive End-Expiratory Pressure)

**Purpose**: Recruit collapsed alveoli, improve oxygenation, ↓ FiO₂ requirement

**Titration**:
- **ARDSNet PEEP/FiO₂ table** (higher PEEP for higher FiO₂)
- Typical: **10-15 cm H₂O** (severe ARDS may need higher)

**Risks**: Barotrauma, ↓ Cardiac output (↑ intrathoracic pressure → ↓ venous return)

#### 3. Prone Positioning

**Indication**: **Severe ARDS** (PaO₂/FiO₂ <150 mmHg)

**Mechanism**:
- Improves V/Q matching (dorsal lung recruitment)
- ↓ Shunt

**Protocol**: **≥16 hours/day** prone positioning

**Evidence**: **PROSEVA Trial** - ↓ Mortality (32% → 16%)

**Contraindications**: Spinal instability, open chest/abdomen, pregnancy, severe hemodynamic instability

#### 4. Neuromuscular Blockade

**Indication**: **Severe ARDS** (PaO₂/FiO₂ <150 mmHg) with patient-ventilator dyssynchrony

**Drug**: **Cisatracurium** infusion × **48 hours**

**Evidence**: **ACURASYS Trial** - ↓ Mortality in severe ARDS (if used early)

**Recent**: **ROSE Trial** (2019) - NO mortality benefit with routine paralysis (conflicting)

**Current Recommendation**: Use if patient-ventilator dyssynchrony despite sedation, NOT routine

#### 5. Fluid Management

**Strategy**: **Conservative fluid management** (negative fluid balance)

**Evidence**: **FACTT Trial** - Conservative strategy:
- ↓ Ventilator days
- ↓ ICU stay
- **NO ↑ mortality or renal failure**

**Goal**: CVP 4-6 mmHg (or lowest tolerated without hypotension)

**Caution**: Avoid hypovolemia (↓ organ perfusion)

#### 6. Extracorporeal Membrane Oxygenation (ECMO)

**Indication**: **Refractory hypoxemia** despite maximal ventilatory support
- PaO₂/FiO₂ <50-80 mmHg on FiO₂ 1.0 + PEEP 15-20
- Plateau pressure >30 despite lung-protective ventilation

**Mechanism**: Bypass lungs for gas exchange → Allow lung rest

**Evidence**: **EOLIA Trial** (2018) - Trend toward ↓ mortality (not significant, but crossover)

**Availability**: Specialized centers only

#### 7. Supportive Care

- **Treat underlying cause**: Antibiotics (pneumonia), source control (sepsis)
- **Sedation**: Minimize (daily awakening trials), avoid over-sedation
- **Nutrition**: Early enteral nutrition (within 24-48 hours)
- **DVT prophylaxis**: LMWH or UFH
- **Stress ulcer prophylaxis**: H2-blockers or PPIs
- **Avoid**: Steroids (NO benefit, may harm), Nitric oxide (NO mortality benefit)

### Prognosis

**Mortality**: **30-45%** overall (varies by severity)
- Mild: 27%
- Moderate: 32%
- Severe: 45%

**Survivors**: Most recover lung function within 6-12 months, but many have persistent:
- Reduced exercise capacity
- Pulmonary fibrosis (if prolonged ARDS)
- Neurocognitive impairment, PTSD (ICU-related)

**Predictors of Mortality**:
- Advanced age, immunosuppression
- Sepsis, multi-organ failure
- Severity (PaO₂/FiO₂ <100)
- Non-pulmonary organ dysfunction

## OXYGEN THERAPY

### Indications

**Acute Hypoxemia**:
- **PaO₂ <60 mmHg** OR
- **SpO₂ <90%** (88-92% if COPD risk)

**Goal**: SpO₂ **94-98%** (90-96% if COPD/chronic hypoxemia)

### Oxygen Delivery Systems

#### 1. Low-Flow Systems (Variable FiO₂)

**FiO₂ depends on patient's inspiratory flow and respiratory pattern**

| Device | Flow Rate | FiO₂ Delivered | When to Use |
|--------|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| **Nasal Cannula** | 1-6 L/min | **24-44%** (~4% per L/min) | Mild hypoxemia, stable patients |
| **Simple Face Mask** | 5-10 L/min | **40-60%** | Moderate hypoxemia |
| **Non-Rebreather Mask** (with reservoir) | 10-15 L/min | **60-90%** (up to 100% if tight seal) | Severe hypoxemia |

**Nasal Cannula**:
- **Rule**: Each 1 L/min ↑ FiO₂ by ~4%
- 1 L/min → 24%, 2 L → 28%, 3 L → 32%, 4 L → 36%, 5 L → 40%, 6 L → 44%

#### 2. High-Flow Systems (Fixed FiO₂)

**FiO₂ independent of patient's respiratory pattern**

| Device | Flow Rate | FiO₂ Delivered | When to Use |
|--------|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| **Venturi Mask** | Variable (depends on adapter) | **24%, 28%, 31%, 35%, 40%, 50%** (precise) | **COPD** (need controlled FiO₂), Type II RF |
| **High-Flow Nasal Cannula (HFNC)** | **30-60 L/min** | **21-100%** (adjustable) | Hypoxemic respiratory failure, post-extubation |

**Venturi Mask**:
- **Uses Venturi principle** (fixed oxygen dilution with room air)
- **Most precise FiO₂** delivery
- **IDEAL for COPD** (avoid CO₂ retention from excess O₂)

**High-Flow Nasal Cannula (HFNC)**:
- Heated, humidified, high-flow oxygen
- **Advantages**: Comfort, washout of dead space, PEEP effect, better oxygenation
- **Indications**: Hypoxemic RF (Type I), immunocompromised, post-extubation

#### 3. Reservoir Systems

**Non-Rebreather Mask**:
- **Reservoir bag** fills with 100% O₂ during exhalation
- Patient breathes from reservoir → Higher FiO₂
- One-way valves prevent exhaled air entering reservoir

### Special Considerations

#### COPD Patients (Risk of CO₂ Retention)

**Mechanism**: Chronic hypercapnia → Blunted central chemoreceptor response → Hypoxemia drives ventilation
- **High FiO₂** → ↑ PaO₂ → **Loss of hypoxic drive** → **↓ Ventilation** → **↑ PaCO₂**

**Target**: **SpO₂ 88-92%** (NOT 94-98%)

**Devices**: **Venturi mask** (28-31%) preferred

**Monitoring**: Serial ABG (watch for rising PaCO₂)

**Myth**: "Oxygen causes CO₂ retention" is partially true but **NEVER withhold O₂** if hypoxemic
- **Give oxygen**, monitor ABG, use NIV if CO₂ rises

#### Long-Term Oxygen Therapy (LTOT)

**Indications** (COPD, ILD):
- **PaO₂ ≤55 mmHg**, OR
- **PaO₂ 56-59 mmHg** + Evidence of end-organ hypoxia:
  - Cor pulmonale (RV failure)
  - Polycythemia (Hct >55%)
  - Pulmonary hypertension

**Duration**: **≥15 hours/day** (preferably 24 hours)

**Evidence**: **NOTT Trial** - LTOT ↑ survival in COPD with chronic hypoxemia

## MECHANICAL VENTILATION

### Indications

**Absolute**:
- **Apnea**, Cardiac arrest
- **Severe hypoxemia** (PaO₂ <60 despite high FiO₂)
- **Severe acidosis** (pH <7.25 with hypercapnia)
- **Impending respiratory arrest** (exhaustion, altered mental status)

**Relative**:
- Excessive work of breathing (accessory muscle use, diaphoresis)
- Hemodynamic instability
- Need for airway protection (↓ GCS, vomiting)

### Modes of Ventilation

#### 1. Volume-Controlled (VC)

**Set Parameter**: **Tidal Volume (Vt)**, Respiratory Rate, FiO₂, PEEP
**Variable**: Pressure (Pplat varies with lung compliance)

**Advantage**: Guaranteed minute ventilation
**Disadvantage**: Risk of high pressures (barotrauma) if lung compliance ↓

**Use**: ARDS (with **lung-protective strategy**: Vt 6 mL/kg, Pplat ≤30)

#### 2. Pressure-Controlled (PC)

**Set Parameter**: **Inspiratory Pressure**, Respiratory Rate, FiO₂, PEEP
**Variable**: Tidal Volume (varies with lung compliance)

**Advantage**: Pressure-limited (↓ barotrauma risk)
**Disadvantage**: Vt may vary (if compliance changes)

**Use**: ARDS, asthma (pressure-limited)

#### 3. Assist-Control (AC)

**Concept**: Ventilator delivers full support for EVERY breath (patient-triggered or machine-triggered)

**Use**: Acute respiratory failure, full support needed

**Risk**: Respiratory alkalosis (if patient tachypneic → excessive minute ventilation)

#### 4. Synchronized Intermittent Mandatory Ventilation (SIMV)

**Concept**: Ventilator delivers SET number of breaths; patient can take additional breaths (unsupported or with Pressure Support)

**Use**: Weaning

#### 5. Pressure Support Ventilation (PSV)

**Concept**: Patient triggers ALL breaths, ventilator provides PRESET pressure support

**Use**: **Weaning**, Spontaneous breathing trial

**Typical Setting**: PS 5-20 cm H₂O (enough to overcome endotracheal tube resistance)

### Ventilator Settings (Initial)

| Parameter | Typical Setting | Notes |
|-----------|-----------------|-------|
| **Mode** | AC (Volume or Pressure Control) | Full support initially |
| **Tidal Volume (Vt)** | **6 mL/kg IBW** (ARDS), 8-10 mL/kg (others) | Lung-protective if ARDS |
| **Respiratory Rate (RR)** | **12-20/min** | Adjust for pH/PaCO₂ target |
| **FiO₂** | **Start 100%**, titrate down | Target SpO₂ 88-95% (ARDS), 94-98% (others) |
| **PEEP** | **5-10 cm H₂O** | Higher (10-20) in ARDS |
| **I:E Ratio** | **1:2 to 1:3** (inspiration:expiration) | Allow adequate exhalation (avoid air-trapping) |
| **Plateau Pressure** (Pplat) | **≤30 cm H₂O** (ARDS) | Pause at end-inspiration to measure |

### Monitoring

**Hourly**:
- **Tidal Volume**, Peak Inspiratory Pressure (PIP), Plateau Pressure (Pplat)
- **SpO₂**

**Daily**:
- **ABG** (assess oxygenation, ventilation, acid-base)
- **CXR** (tube position, pneumothorax, infiltrates)

**Pressure Monitoring**:
- **Peak Inspiratory Pressure (PIP)**: Total pressure (airway + alveolar + PEEP)
  - ↑ PIP: Airway obstruction (secretions, bronchospasm, kinked tube)
- **Plateau Pressure (Pplat)**: Alveolar pressure (measured with end-inspiratory hold)
  - ↑ Pplat: ↓ Lung compliance (pneumonia, ARDS, pneumothorax, pulmonary edema)
- **Driving Pressure** = Pplat - PEEP
  - Should be **<15 cm H₂O** (predictor of mortality in ARDS)

### Weaning from Mechanical Ventilation

**When to Consider Weaning**:
- Underlying cause improved/resolved
- Adequate oxygenation (PaO₂/FiO₂ >150-200, PEEP ≤8, FiO₂ ≤0.5)
- Hemodynamically stable (no vasopressors or low-dose)
- Awake, able to protect airway

**Weaning Process**:

1. **Daily Spontaneous Awakening Trial (SAT)**: ↓ Sedation → Assess readiness

2. **Spontaneous Breathing Trial (SBT)**: 30-120 minutes
   - **T-piece** (no support) OR
   - **Pressure Support 5-7 cm H₂O** + PEEP 5 cm H₂O

3. **Assess SBT Success**:
   - **Passed**: RR <30-35/min, SpO₂ >90%, HR change <20%, SBP change <20%, no distress
   - **Failed**: Tachypnea, hypoxemia, tachycardia, hypertension, diaphoresis, agitation

4. **If SBT Passed → EXTUBATE**

5. **If SBT Failed → Continue ventilation**, reassess daily

**Predictors of Successful Extubation**:
- **Rapid Shallow Breathing Index (RSBI)** = RR / Vt (in liters)
  - **<105**: High likelihood of success
  - **>105**: High risk of failure

**Post-Extubation**:
- High-flow nasal cannula or non-invasive ventilation (especially high-risk patients)
- Monitor closely × 24-48 hours

## NON-INVASIVE VENTILATION (NIV)

### Definition
Positive pressure ventilation delivered via **face mask** or **nasal mask** (NO endotracheal intubation)

### Types

#### 1. CPAP (Continuous Positive Airway Pressure)
- **Single pressure** throughout respiratory cycle
- Improves oxygenation (alveolar recruitment)
- **Use**: Cardiogenic pulmonary edema, OSA

#### 2. BiPAP (Bilevel Positive Airway Pressure)
- **Two pressures**: IPAP (Inspiratory) > EPAP (Expiratory)
- IPAP: Augments tidal volume (↓ work of breathing, ↓ PaCO₂)
- EPAP: Like PEEP (recruits alveoli, ↑ PaO₂)
- **Use**: **Type II respiratory failure** (COPD, neuromuscular), Cardiogenic pulmonary edema

**Typical Settings**:
- **IPAP**: 10-20 cm H₂O
- **EPAP**: 4-10 cm H₂O
- **FiO₂**: Titrate to SpO₂ goal

### Indications

**Strong Evidence** (↓ Intubation, ↓ Mortality):
1. **COPD Exacerbation** (MOST COMMON)
   - pH 7.25-7.35, PaCO₂ >45 mmHg
2. **Cardiogenic Pulmonary Edema**
3. **Immunocompromised patients** with acute respiratory failure
4. **Post-extubation respiratory failure** (prevention)

**Moderate Evidence**:
- Obesity hypoventilation syndrome
- Neuromuscular disease (chronic Type II RF)
- Post-operative respiratory failure
- Palliative care (avoid intubation)

### Contraindications

**Absolute**:
- **Cardiac/respiratory arrest**
- **Inability to protect airway** (↓ GCS, excessive secretions, vomiting)
- **Severe hemodynamic instability**
- **Severe upper GI bleed**
- **Facial trauma/surgery** (mask cannot be fit)

**Relative**:
- Uncooperative patient
- High aspiration risk
- Excessive secretions
- Recent upper GI surgery

### Monitoring & Success Criteria

**Monitor**:
- **ABG** (1-2 hours after starting): ↓ PaCO₂, ↑ pH, ↑ PaO₂
- **Clinical improvement**: ↓ RR, ↓ accessory muscle use, ↑ alertness

**Success** (Continue NIV):
- ↓ PaCO₂ by 5-10 mmHg
- ↑ pH toward normal
- ↓ RR <25-30/min
- Improved mental status

**Failure** (Intubate):
- **Worsening acidosis** (pH <7.25)
- **↑ PaCO₂** despite NIV
- **Persistent tachypnea** (RR >35/min)
- **Hemodynamic instability**
- **Unable to tolerate mask**

**Trial Duration**: 1-2 hours → Reassess → Continue vs Intubate

## COMPLICATIONS

### Mechanical Ventilation Complications

#### 1. Barotrauma / Volutrauma

**Definition**: Lung injury from high pressure (barotrauma) or high volume (volutrauma)

**Manifestations**:
- **Pneumothorax** (MOST COMMON) - Sudden ↑ PIP, hypotension, unilateral ↓ breath sounds
- Pneumomediastinum
- Subcutaneous emphysema

**Prevention**: Lung-protective ventilation (Vt 6 mL/kg, Pplat ≤30 cm H₂O)

#### 2. Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia (VAP)

**Definition**: Pneumonia developing **>48 hours** after intubation

**Risk Factors**: Prolonged intubation, re-intubation, supine position, aspiration

**Diagnosis**: New infiltrate + fever + purulent secretions + ↑ WBC

**Prevention**:
- **Elevate head of bed 30-45°**
- **Daily sedation vacation** (assess extubation readiness)
- **Oral care** with chlorhexidine
- **Subglottic suctioning** (special ET tubes)

**Treatment**: Broad-spectrum antibiotics (cover Pseudomonas, MRSA)

#### 3. Ventilator-Induced Lung Injury (VILI)

**Mechanism**: Overdistension (high Vt/pressure), cyclic atelectasis, inflammation

**Prevention**: Lung-protective ventilation

#### 4. Hemodynamic Effects

**Positive Pressure** → ↑ Intrathoracic pressure → ↓ Venous return → ↓ Cardiac output

**Manifestations**: Hypotension (especially with high PEEP)

**Management**: IV fluids, ↓ PEEP if tolerated

#### 5. Oxygen Toxicity

**Mechanism**: **High FiO₂ (>0.6) for prolonged periods** → Free radical damage

**Manifestations**: Worsening ARDS, tracheobronchitis

**Prevention**: Use **lowest FiO₂** to maintain SpO₂ 88-95%

#### 6. Auto-PEEP (Intrinsic PEEP)

**Definition**: Incomplete exhalation → Air-trapping → Positive alveolar pressure at end-expiration

**Causes**: **Short expiratory time** (high RR, long I-time), **Airflow obstruction** (COPD, asthma)

**Effects**: ↓ Venous return → ↓ CO, barotrauma, trigger difficulty

**Detection**: Expiratory flow doesn't return to zero before next breath

**Management**: ↑ Expiratory time (↓ RR, ↓ I:E ratio), bronchodilators, ↓ Vt

### Non-Invasive Ventilation Complications

- **Mask discomfort**, skin breakdown (pressure ulcers)
- **Gastric distension** (air swallowing)
- **Aspiration** (if vomiting occurs)
- **Eye irritation** (air leak)

## NEET PG HIGH-YIELD POINTS

### Must-Know Facts

1. **Respiratory Failure Definition**:
   - **PaO₂ <60 mmHg** OR **PaCO₂ >45 mmHg**

2. **Type I vs Type II**:

| Feature | Type I (Hypoxemic) | Type II (Hypercapnic) |
|---------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| **PaO₂** | **<60 mmHg** | May be normal or ↓ |
| **PaCO₂** | Normal or ↓ | **>45 mmHg** |
| **A-a gradient** | **↑ Increased** | Normal (if pure hypoventilation) |
| **Mechanism** | **V/Q mismatch, Shunt** | **Hypoventilation** |
| **Common causes** | **Pneumonia, ARDS, PE** | **COPD, Neuromuscular, CNS depression** |
| **Treatment** | **Oxygen** | **NIV** (BiPAP), Treat cause |

3. **Mechanisms of Hypoxemia**:
   - **V/Q Mismatch**: MOST COMMON (COPD, Pneumonia) - Partially corrects with O₂
   - **Shunt**: ARDS, Pneumonia - **Does NOT correct** with 100% O₂
   - **Hypoventilation**: Drug overdose, neuromuscular - Corrects with O₂, **NORMAL A-a gradient**

4. **ARDS (Berlin Criteria)**:
   - Timing: **Within 1 week** of insult
   - Imaging: **Bilateral opacities**
   - Edema: **NOT cardiac** (PCWP ≤18 mmHg, BNP normal)
   - Oxygenation: **PaO₂/FiO₂ ≤300 mmHg** (with PEEP ≥5)
   - **Severity**: Mild (200-300), Moderate (100-200), Severe (<100)

5. **ARDS Causes**:
   - **MOST COMMON**: **Pneumonia** (pulmonary), **Sepsis** (extrapulmonary)
   - **Mnemonic**: "**SPARTAN**" - Sepsis, Pneumonia/Pancreatitis, Aspiration, tRauma, Transfusions, inhalAtion, Near-drowning

6. **ARDS Treatment** (Lung-Protective Ventilation):
   - **Tidal Volume**: **6 mL/kg IBW** (NOT 8-10)
   - **Plateau Pressure**: **≤30 cm H₂O**
   - **PEEP**: 5-15 cm H₂O (titrate to FiO₂)
   - **Prone positioning**: ≥16 hrs/day (if PaO₂/FiO₂ <150) - ↓ Mortality
   - **Conservative fluid strategy**: ↓ Ventilator days

7. **Oxygen Delivery**:
   - **Nasal cannula**: 1-6 L/min → 24-44% FiO₂ (4% per L/min)
   - **Non-rebreather**: 10-15 L/min → 60-90% FiO₂
   - **Venturi mask**: **MOST PRECISE FiO₂** → Use in **COPD**

8. **COPD Oxygen Target**: **SpO₂ 88-92%** (avoid hyperoxia → CO₂ retention)

9. **NIV Indications** (↓ Intubation, ↓ Mortality):
   - **COPD exacerbation** (pH 7.25-7.35, PaCO₂ >45)
   - **Cardiogenic pulmonary edema**
   - **Immunocompromised** patients

10. **NIV Contraindications**:
    - **Cardiac/respiratory arrest**
    - **Unable to protect airway** (↓ GCS, vomiting, excessive secretions)
    - Severe hemodynamic instability

11. **Mechanical Ventilation Indications**:
    - **Severe hypoxemia** (PaO₂ <60 despite O₂)
    - **Severe acidosis** (pH <7.25 with hypercapnia)
    - **Impending arrest** (exhaustion, ↓ GCS)

12. **Ventilator Complications**:
    - **Barotrauma**: Pneumothorax (sudden ↑ PIP, hypotension)
    - **VAP**: Pneumonia >48 hours after intubation
    - **Auto-PEEP**: Air-trapping (COPD, asthma) → ↓ CO, barotrauma

### High-Yield Formulas

**1. A-a Gradient**:
- A-a gradient = PAO₂ - PaO₂
- PAO₂ = 150 - (PaCO₂ × 1.25) [simplified, at room air]
- **Normal**: 5-15 mmHg (or Age/4 + 4)
- **↑ A-a gradient** → Lung parenchymal disease

**2. PaO₂/FiO₂ Ratio**:
- Normal: 400-500 mmHg
- ARDS: ≤300 mmHg

**3. Ideal Body Weight (IBW)**:
- Male: 50 + 0.91 × (Height in cm - 152.4)
- Female: 45.5 + 0.91 × (Height in cm - 152.4)

**4. Tidal Volume (ARDS)**:
- **6 mL/kg IBW**

**5. Rapid Shallow Breathing Index (RSBI)**:
- RSBI = RR / Vt (in liters)
- **<105**: Likely successful extubation
- **>105**: High risk of failure

### Important Cutoffs

| Parameter | Cutoff | Significance |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| **PaO₂** | <60 mmHg | Respiratory failure |
| **PaCO₂** | >45 mmHg | Hypercapnia (Type II RF) |
| **pH** | <7.25 | Severe acidosis (intubation threshold) |
| **PaO₂/FiO₂** | ≤300 mmHg | ARDS |
| **PaO₂/FiO₂** | <100 mmHg | Severe ARDS |
| **PCWP** | ≤18 mmHg | Non-cardiogenic edema (ARDS) |
| **Plateau Pressure** | ≤30 cm H₂O | Target in ARDS |
| **SpO₂ (COPD)** | 88-92% | Target oxygen saturation |
| **SpO₂ (Others)** | 94-98% | Target oxygen saturation |

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Using high tidal volume (8-10 mL/kg) in ARDS** ❌
   - Use **6 mL/kg IBW** (lung-protective ventilation)

2. **Targeting SpO₂ 94-98% in COPD patients** ❌
   - Target **88-92%** (avoid CO₂ retention)

3. **Giving high FiO₂ (100%) for prolonged periods** ❌
   - **Oxygen toxicity** (FiO₂ >0.6 × >24-48 hours)
   - Titrate to **lowest FiO₂** for SpO₂ goal

4. **Diagnosing ARDS without PEEP ≥5 cm H₂O** ❌
   - PaO₂/FiO₂ must be measured **with PEEP ≥5**

5. **Using NIV in cardiac arrest or unable to protect airway** ❌
   - **Contraindication** → Intubate

6. **Forgetting prone positioning in severe ARDS** ❌
   - **PaO₂/FiO₂ <150** → Prone ≥16 hours/day (↓ mortality)

7. **Not checking for auto-PEEP in COPD/asthma on ventilator** ❌
   - Can cause **barotrauma, ↓ CO**
   - Manage: ↑ Expiratory time, ↓ RR, bronchodilators

### Question 1
A 70-year-old man with COPD exacerbation has ABG: pH 7.28, PaCO₂ 68 mmHg, PaO₂ 55 mmHg, HCO₃⁻ 30 mEq/L on room air. What is the most appropriate initial management?

A. Intubation and mechanical ventilation
B. High-flow oxygen (Non-rebreather mask)
C. BiPAP (Non-invasive ventilation)
D. Venturi mask 24% oxygen

**Answer**: **C. BiPAP (Non-invasive ventilation)**

**Explanation**:
- **Diagnosis**: **Acute-on-chronic Type II respiratory failure** (COPD exacerbation)

**ABG Analysis**:
- **pH 7.28** (Acidemia)
- **PaCO₂ 68 mmHg** (Hypercapnia)
- **HCO₃⁻ 30 mEq/L** (Baseline compensation for chronic CO₂ retention)
- **Acute respiratory acidosis** (pH <7.35, ↑ PaCO₂)

**NIV (BiPAP) Indications**:
- **COPD exacerbation** with **pH 7.25-7.35**, PaCO₂ >45 mmHg
- **First-line treatment** (↓ intubation rate, ↓ mortality)

**This Patient**: pH 7.28 (>7.25) → **Trial of NIV** appropriate

**BiPAP Benefits**:
- **↓ PaCO₂** (IPAP augments ventilation)
- **↑ PaO₂** (EPAP recruits alveoli)
- **↓ Work of breathing**

**Monitoring**: ABG in 1-2 hours → If improving (↑ pH, ↓ PaCO₂) → Continue NIV
**If pH <7.25 or worsening** → **Intubation**

**Why NOT Other Options**:
- **Intubation** (A): NOT first-line if pH >7.25 (try NIV first)
- **Non-rebreather** (B): High FiO₂ → Risk of **worsening CO₂ retention** in COPD (loss of hypoxic drive)
- **Venturi 24%** (D): **TOO LOW** for PaO₂ 55 mmHg (need higher FiO₂, but controlled → BiPAP allows this)

**Key Point**: COPD exacerbation + pH 7.25-7.35 + PaCO₂ >45 → NIV (BiPAP) first-line

### Question 2
A patient on mechanical ventilation for ARDS has the following settings: Vt 400 mL, RR 20/min, PEEP 10, FiO₂ 0.6. The patient weighs 100 kg and is 180 cm tall. Plateau pressure is 35 cm H₂O. What is the most appropriate change?

A. Increase PEEP to 15 cm H₂O
B. Decrease tidal volume to 450 mL
C. Decrease tidal volume to 330 mL
D. Increase FiO₂ to 1.0

**Answer**: **C. Decrease tidal volume to 330 mL**

**Explanation**:
- **Problem**: **Plateau pressure >30 cm H₂O** (35 cm H₂O) → Risk of barotrauma/VILI

**ARDS Lung-Protective Ventilation**:
- **Tidal Volume**: **6 mL/kg IBW**
- **Plateau Pressure**: **≤30 cm H₂O**

**Calculate IBW** (Male, 180 cm):
- IBW = 50 + 0.91 × (180 - 152.4) = 50 + 25.1 = **75 kg**

**Target Tidal Volume**:
- 6 mL/kg × 75 kg = **450 mL**

**Current Tidal Volume**: 400 mL (already low, but Pplat still >30)

**Action**: **Further ↓ Tidal Volume** to achieve Pplat ≤30 cm H₂O
- Try **330 mL** (4.4 mL/kg) → Recheck Pplat
- **Permissive hypercapnia** acceptable (allow PaCO₂ to rise to protect lung)

**Why NOT Other Options**:
- **↑ PEEP** (A): May ↑ Pplat further (PEEP adds to total pressure)
- **↓ Vt to 450 mL** (B): Would be appropriate initial target, but **Pplat already >30 at 400 mL** → Need LOWER Vt
- **↑ FiO₂** (D): Doesn't address high Pplat, ↑ oxygen toxicity risk

**Key Point**: ARDS ventilation → Vt 6 mL/kg IBW, Pplat ≤30 cm H₂O (may need Vt <6 if Pplat still high)

### Question 3
A 45-year-old man with severe pneumonia develops ARDS. ABG on FiO₂ 1.0 and PEEP 15 cm H₂O shows PaO₂ 60 mmHg. What is the PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio and ARDS severity?

A. 60, Mild ARDS
B. 60, Moderate ARDS
C. 60, Severe ARDS

| Mild | 200-300 | ❌ |
| Moderate | 100-200 | ❌ |

A patient on nasal cannula at 3 L/min has SpO₂ 85%. The approximate FiO₂ being delivered is:

### Question 6
A patient with ARDS on mechanical ventilation (Vt 6 mL/kg, PEEP 10, FiO₂ 0.8) has PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio of 80 mmHg. What is the most appropriate next step to improve oxygenation?

A. Increase tidal volume to 8 mL/kg
B. Increase FiO₂ to 1.0
C. Prone positioning
D. Decrease PEEP to 5 cm H₂O

**Answer**: **C. Prone positioning**

**Explanation**:
- **Severe ARDS** (PaO₂/FiO₂ <100 mmHg)

- **Indication**: **Severe ARDS** (PaO₂/FiO₂ <150 mmHg, especially <100)

### Question 8
What is the most important intervention to prevent ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP)?

A. Prophylactic antibiotics
B. Daily sedation vacation and assessment for extubation
C. Supine positioning
D. Nasogastric tube placement

**Answer**: **B. Daily sedation vacation and assessment for extubation**

**Explanation**:
- **VAP Prevention**:

| Intervention | Evidence | Impact |
|--------------|----------|--------|
| **Elevate head of bed 30-45°** | Strong | ↓ Aspiration |
| **Daily sedation vacation + SBT** | **Strong** | **↓ Duration of ventilation** → ↓ VAP |
| **Oral care with chlorhexidine** | Moderate | ↓ Oropharyngeal colonization |
| **Subglottic suctioning** | Moderate | Remove secretions above cuff |
| **Avoid unnecessary re-intubation** | Strong | ↓ Trauma, aspiration |

**Daily Sedation Vacation + Spontaneous Breathing Trial (SBT)**:
- **Most important** → **Shortens duration of mechanical ventilation**
- Shorter ventilation → **↓ VAP risk** (VAP incidence ~1-3% per day of ventilation)

**Why NOT Other Options**:
- **Prophylactic antibiotics** (A): **NOT recommended** (↑ resistance, no benefit)
- **Supine positioning** (C): **Increases VAP risk** (should elevate head 30-45°)
- **NGT** (D): May ↑ aspiration risk (if not needed, avoid)

**Key Point**: Daily sedation vacation + SBT → Shortest duration of ventilation → ↓ VAP

### Question 9
A mechanically ventilated patient suddenly develops hypotension, tachycardia, and unilateral decreased breath sounds. Peak inspiratory pressure has increased from 25 to 40 cm H₂O. What is the most likely complication?

A. Pneumothorax
B. Mucus plugging
C. Ventilator-associated pneumonia
D. Auto-PEEP

**Answer**: **A. Pneumothorax**

**Explanation**:
- **Classic Presentation of Tension Pneumothorax**:
  - **Sudden** hypotension, tachycardia
  - **Unilateral ↓ breath sounds**
  - **↑ Peak inspiratory pressure (PIP)**

**Mechanism**: Air accumulates in pleural space → Lung collapse → ↑ Airway resistance → ↑ PIP → Mediastinal shift → ↓ Venous return → **Hypotension**

**Diagnosis**:
- **Clinical** (don't wait for CXR if unstable)
- **CXR**: Absent lung markings, mediastinal shift

**Management**:
- **Emergency needle decompression** (2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line)
- **Chest tube insertion**

**Why NOT Other Options**:
- **Mucus plugging** (B): **↑ PIP**, but **bilateral** ↓ breath sounds, no hypotension
- **VAP** (C): Gradual onset (>48 hours), fever, infiltrate, NOT sudden
- **Auto-PEEP** (D): ↓ CO possible, but **NOT unilateral** ↓ breath sounds

**Differentiating High PIP Causes**:

| Cause | PIP | Plateau Pressure | Unilateral Signs |
|-------|-----|------------------|------------------|
| **Pneumothorax** | **↑** | **↑** | **Yes** (↓ breath sounds on affected side) |
| **Mucus plug / bronchospasm** | **↑** | Normal/mildly ↑ | No (bilateral) |
| **↓ Lung compliance** (ARDS, edema) | **↑** | **↑** | No (bilateral) |
| **Auto-PEEP** | **↑** | **↑** | No (bilateral) |

**Key Point**: Sudden hypotension + ↑ PIP + Unilateral ↓ breath sounds on ventilator = Pneumothorax (emergency)

### Question 10
A 60-year-old man with COPD is on home oxygen. Which of the following indicates he needs long-term oxygen therapy (LTOT)?

A. PaO₂ 60 mmHg
B. PaO₂ 57 mmHg with cor pulmonale
C. SpO₂ 94% at rest
D. PaO₂ 70 mmHg during exercise

**Answer**: **B. PaO₂ 57 mmHg with cor pulmonale**

**Explanation**:
- **Long-Term Oxygen Therapy (LTOT) Indications**:

| Criterion | Evidence |
|-----------|----------|
| **PaO₂ ≤55 mmHg** at rest | Absolute indication |
| **PaO₂ 56-59 mmHg** + **Cor pulmonale** OR **Polycythemia (Hct >55%)** | Absolute indication |
| **SpO₂ ≤88%** at rest | Corresponds to PaO₂ ~55 mmHg |

**Evidence**: **NOTT Trial** (Nocturnal Oxygen Therapy Trial)
- LTOT (≥15 hrs/day) ↑ survival in COPD with chronic hypoxemia
- **ONLY intervention** (besides smoking cessation) that ↑ survival in COPD

**Duration**: **≥15 hours/day** (preferably 24 hours)

**This Patient**:
- **PaO₂ 57 mmHg** (56-59 range) + **Cor pulmonale** → **Indication for LTOT** ✓

**Why NOT Other Options**:
- **PaO₂ 60 mmHg** (A): Above threshold (>59), no indication unless cor pulmonale/polycythemia
- **SpO₂ 94%** (C): Normal, no indication
- **PaO₂ 70 mmHg during exercise** (D): **Exercise desaturation alone** NOT indication for continuous LTOT (may use PRN during exercise)

**Key Point**: LTOT indicated if PaO₂ ≤55 mmHg OR PaO₂ 56-59 + Cor pulmonale/Polycythemia

## SUMMARY

**Respiratory Failure** is inability to maintain adequate gas exchange:
- **Type I (Hypoxemic)**: PaO₂ <60, PaCO₂ normal/low → V/Q mismatch, Shunt
- **Type II (Hypercapnic)**: PaCO₂ >45 → Hypoventilation

**ARDS** (Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome):
- **Berlin Criteria**: Bilateral opacities, within 1 week, PaO₂/FiO₂ ≤300, NOT cardiac
- **Causes**: Pneumonia (pulmonary), Sepsis (extrapulmonary)
- **Treatment**: **Lung-protective ventilation** (Vt 6 mL/kg, Pplat ≤30, PEEP 5-15), **Prone positioning** (if severe), Conservative fluids

**Oxygen Therapy**:
- Target SpO₂ **94-98%** (88-92% if COPD)
- Devices: Nasal cannula (24-44%), Venturi (precise, for COPD), Non-rebreather (60-90%)

**NIV (BiPAP)**:
- Indications: **COPD exacerbation**, Cardiogenic pulmonary edema
- Contraindications: Cardiac arrest, unable to protect airway

**Mechanical Ventilation**:
- Indications: Severe hypoxemia, severe acidosis, impending arrest
- Complications: Pneumothorax, VAP, VILI, oxygen toxicity

**Prognosis**: Varies by etiology (ARDS 30-45% mortality)
