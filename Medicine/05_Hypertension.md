## Definition & Classification

### Definition
**Persistent elevation of blood pressure ≥140/90 mmHg** (based on average of ≥2 readings on ≥2 occasions)

### Classification (JNC 8 / ACC/AHA 2017)

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│           BLOOD PRESSURE CLASSIFICATION                     │
├────────────────┬──────────────────┬────────────────────────┤
│   CATEGORY     │  SYSTOLIC (mmHg) │  DIASTOLIC (mmHg)      │
├────────────────┼──────────────────┼────────────────────────┤
│ Normal         │     <120         │   AND <80              │
├────────────────┼──────────────────┼────────────────────────┤
│ Elevated       │   120-129        │   AND <80              │
├────────────────┼──────────────────┼────────────────────────┤
│ Stage 1 HTN    │   130-139        │   OR 80-89             │
├────────────────┼──────────────────┼────────────────────────┤
│ Stage 2 HTN    │    ≥140          │   OR ≥90               │
├────────────────┼──────────────────┼────────────────────────┤
│ Hypertensive   │    ≥180          │   AND/OR ≥120          │
│ Crisis         │                  │  + End-organ damage    │
└────────────────┴──────────────────┴────────────────────────┘
```

**Note:** **WHO/ISH** still uses **≥140/90 mmHg** as threshold for hypertension

### Special Definitions

**Isolated Systolic Hypertension:**
- SBP ≥140 mmHg with DBP <90 mmHg
- Common in **elderly** (loss of arterial compliance)
- **Strong predictor** of cardiovascular risk

**White Coat Hypertension:**
- Elevated BP in clinic but normal at home
- **Confirm with ABPM** (Ambulatory BP Monitoring)
- Lower cardiovascular risk than sustained HTN

**Masked Hypertension:**
- Normal BP in clinic but elevated at home
- **Higher cardiovascular risk**
- Confirm with home BP monitoring

## Etiology

### Primary (Essential) Hypertension - **90-95%**

**Definition:** Hypertension without identifiable cause

**Risk Factors - "ABCDEFGH"**
```
A - Age (>55 men, >65 women)
B - Black race
C - Cigarette smoking
D - Diabetes mellitus
E - Elevated cholesterol
F - Family history
G - Gender (Males > Females before menopause)
H - High sodium intake, High BMI
```

**Pathophysiology:**
- Multifactorial
- Genetic predisposition + Environmental factors
- ↑ Sympathetic activity
- ↑ Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS)
- ↑ Sodium retention
- Endothelial dysfunction

### Secondary Hypertension - **5-10%**

**Suspect if:**
- Age <30 or >55 years at onset
- **Resistant hypertension** (uncontrolled on 3 drugs including diuretic)
- **Sudden onset** or sudden worsening
- Severe hypertension (>180/110)
- Hypokalemia (unprovoked)

**Causes - "ABCDE"**

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│         SECONDARY HYPERTENSION CAUSES                    │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ A - ADRENAL                                              │
│     • Primary aldosteronism (Conn's syndrome) - MC       │
│     • Pheochromocytoma                                   │
│     • Cushing's syndrome                                 │
│     • Congenital adrenal hyperplasia                     │
│                                                          │
│ B - BAD KIDNEYS (Renal) - Most common overall           │
│     • Chronic kidney disease                             │
│     • Renovascular disease (Renal artery stenosis)       │
│     • Polycystic kidney disease                          │
│     • Glomerulonephritis                                 │
│                                                          │
│ C - COARCTATION of Aorta                                │
│                                                          │
│ D - DRUGS                                                │
│     • NSAIDs, Steroids                                   │
│     • OCP, Decongestants                                 │
│     • Cocaine, Amphetamines                              │
│     • Erythropoietin, Cyclosporine                       │
│                                                          │
│ E - ENDOCRINE                                            │
│     • Hyperthyroidism, Hypothyroidism                    │
│     • Hyperparathyroidism                                │
│     • Acromegaly                                         │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

#### **Key Secondary Causes:**

**1. Renal Artery Stenosis**
- **Most common:** Atherosclerosis (older, males, smokers)
- **Young females:** Fibromuscular dysplasia
- **Clue:** Abdominal bruit, ↑ Creatinine with ACE inhibitors
- **Investigation:** Renal Doppler, CT/MR angiography
- **Treatment:** Angioplasty ± stenting (if bilateral or solitary kidney with worsening renal function)

**2. Primary Aldosteronism (Conn's Syndrome)**
- **Most common** endocrine cause of secondary HTN
- **Causes:**
  - Bilateral adrenal hyperplasia (60%) - **Most common**
  - Aldosterone-producing adenoma (35%)
- **Clue:** **Hypertension + Hypokalemia** (unprovoked)
- **Screening:** Aldosterone-to-Renin Ratio (ARR)
  - ARR >20-30 suggests primary aldosteronism
- **Confirmation:** Saline suppression test, Oral sodium loading
- **Localization:** Adrenal CT, Adrenal vein sampling
- **Treatment:**
  - Adenoma → **Adrenalectomy** (curative)
  - Bilateral hyperplasia → **Spironolactone** or Eplerenone

**3. Pheochromocytoma**
- **"10% tumor":** 10% bilateral, 10% extra-adrenal, 10% malignant, 10% familial
- **Clue:** **Episodic headaches, sweating, palpitations** (paroxysmal HTN)
- **Investigation:**
  - **24-hour urinary metanephrines** (best screening test)
  - **Plasma free metanephrines**
  - Imaging: Adrenal CT/MRI, MIBG scan
- **Treatment:**
  - **Preoperative:** α-blocker (Phenoxybenzamine) **FIRST**, then β-blocker
  - **Surgical resection** (curative)
- **Never give β-blocker first** (unopposed α-stimulation → hypertensive crisis)

**4. Cushing's Syndrome**
- **Clue:** Central obesity, moon face, purple striae, proximal myopathy
- **Screening:** 24-hour urinary cortisol, overnight dexamethasone suppression test
- **Treatment:** Treat underlying cause

**5. Coarctation of Aorta**
- **Clue:** **Upper limb HTN + Lower limb hypotension**, Radio-femoral delay
- **Investigation:** Echocardiography, CT/MR angiography
- **Treatment:** Balloon angioplasty ± stenting, surgical repair

## Pathophysiology

### Hemodynamics
**BP = Cardiac Output (CO) × Systemic Vascular Resistance (SVR)**

**Mechanisms:**
1. **↑ Cardiac Output:**
   - ↑ Sodium retention → ↑ Blood volume
   - ↑ Sympathetic activity

2. **↑ SVR:**
   - Vasoconstriction (Angiotensin II, Catecholamines)
   - Vascular remodeling (thickening)

### Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS)

```
        Renin (Kidney)
             ↓
     Angiotensinogen → Angiotensin I
                            ↓ ACE
                      Angiotensin II
                       ↙         ↘
              Vasoconstriction   Aldosterone
              (↑ SVR)            (↑ Na+ retention)
                                 (↑ CO)
```

### End-Organ Damage

**Target Organs - "HBKRE"**
```
H - Heart (LVH, CHF, IHD)
B - Brain (Stroke, Encephalopathy)
K - Kidneys (CKD, Proteinuria)
R - Retina (Hypertensive retinopathy)
E - Eyes (Vision loss)
```

**Chronic Hypertension Effects:**
- **Heart:** LVH (most common), Diastolic dysfunction, HFpEF, IHD
- **Brain:** Stroke (hemorrhagic/ischemic), Vascular dementia
- **Kidneys:** Hypertensive nephrosclerosis, CKD
- **Eyes:** Hypertensive retinopathy, Vision loss
- **Vessels:** Atherosclerosis, AAA, Peripheral arterial disease

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms

**Essential Hypertension:**
- **Usually ASYMPTOMATIC** ("Silent killer")
- Detected incidentally on routine check-up

**Symptoms (if present):**
- **Headache** (occipital, early morning)
- Dizziness
- Blurred vision
- Epistaxis (nosebleeds)
- Tinnitus

**Symptoms of Complications:**
- **Chest pain** (IHD, MI)
- **Dyspnea** (Heart failure)
- **Hemiparesis** (Stroke)
- **Altered consciousness** (Hypertensive encephalopathy)

### Physical Examination

**General:**
- **Measure BP properly:**
  - Rest 5 minutes
  - Appropriate cuff size (bladder covers 80% of arm)
  - Arm at heart level
  - Average of 2-3 readings

**Cardiovascular:**
- **S4 gallop** (LVH with diastolic dysfunction)
- **Displaced apex** (LVH)
- **Loud A2** (Hypertension)
- **Murmurs** (Aortic stenosis, regurgitation)

**Signs of Secondary Causes:**
- **Abdominal bruit** (Renal artery stenosis)
- **Radio-femoral delay** (Coarctation)
- **Cushingoid features** (Cushing's)
- **Enlarged kidneys** (Polycystic kidney disease)

**Signs of End-Organ Damage:**
- **Fundoscopy:** Hypertensive retinopathy
- **Neurological deficits:** Previous stroke
- **Peripheral pulses:** Peripheral arterial disease

## Investigations

### Initial Evaluation

**Objectives:**
1. Assess for **secondary causes**
2. Evaluate **end-organ damage**
3. Identify **cardiovascular risk factors**

### Basic Investigations

**Mandatory:**
1. **ECG:**
   - LVH (Sokolow-Lyon criteria: SV1 + RV5/V6 >35 mm)
   - Ischemic changes
   - Arrhythmias (AF)

2. **Urinalysis:**
   - Proteinuria (renal damage)
   - Hematuria (glomerulonephritis)

3. **Serum Creatinine & eGFR:**
   - Assess renal function
   - CKD staging

4. **Serum Electrolytes:**
   - **Hypokalemia:** Primary aldosteronism, diuretic use
   - Hyperkalemia: CKD, RAS inhibitors

5. **Fasting Blood Glucose & HbA1c:**
   - Diabetes screening

6. **Lipid Profile:**
   - Cardiovascular risk assessment

7. **Complete Blood Count:**
   - Anemia (CKD)
   - Polycythemia (secondary causes)

**Additional Investigations (if indicated):**

8. **Echocardiography:**
   - LVH (more sensitive than ECG)
   - LV systolic/diastolic function
   - Valvular disease

9. **Fundoscopy:**
   - Hypertensive retinopathy grading

10. **24-hour ABPM or Home BP monitoring:**
    - Confirm diagnosis
    - White coat/Masked hypertension

**Grade III-IV:** Requires urgent treatment

### Screening for Secondary Hypertension

**When to Screen:**
- Age <30 or >55 years
- Resistant hypertension (uncontrolled on 3 drugs including diuretic)
- Sudden onset/worsening
- Severe HTN (>180/110)
- Unprovoked hypokalemia

**Investigations:**
- **Renal artery stenosis:** Renal Doppler, CT/MR angiography
- **Primary aldosteronism:** Aldosterone-Renin ratio, Saline suppression test
- **Pheochromocytoma:** 24-hour urinary metanephrines
- **Cushing's:** 24-hour urinary cortisol, Dexamethasone suppression test
- **Coarctation:** Echocardiography, CT/MR angiography
- **Thyroid:** TSH, Free T4

## Management

### Goals of Treatment

**Target BP:**
- **General population:** <140/90 mmHg
- **Diabetes/CKD:** <130/80 mmHg
- **Elderly (>65 years):** <150/90 mmHg (more lenient)

**2017 ACC/AHA Guidelines:**
- **General:** <130/80 mmHg (more aggressive)

### Non-Pharmacological (Lifestyle Modifications)

**"LEAP DS"**
```
L - Limit alcohol (<2 drinks/day men, <1 drink/day women)
E - Exercise (30 min/day, 5 days/week)
A - Avoid smoking
P - Potassium-rich diet (fruits, vegetables)

D - DASH diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension)
    • ↑ Fruits, vegetables, low-fat dairy
    • ↓ Saturated fat, cholesterol
    • Effect: ↓ 8-14 mmHg

S - Sodium restriction (<2.4 g/day)
    • Effect: ↓ 5-6 mmHg
    • Weight loss (if overweight)
```

**Weight Loss:**
- **Most effective** non-pharmacological measure
- Every 1 kg loss → ↓ 1-2 mmHg BP

### Pharmacological Management

#### **When to Start Drugs:**

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│     WHEN TO START ANTI-HYPERTENSIVE DRUGS             │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Stage 1 HTN (130-139/80-89):                           │
│   • With CVD/10-year ASCVD risk ≥10% → Start drugs    │
│   • No CVD/low risk → Lifestyle modification           │
│                                                        │
│ Stage 2 HTN (≥140/90):                                 │
│   • Start drugs + lifestyle modification               │
│                                                        │
│ Hypertensive crisis (≥180/120):                        │
│   • Immediate treatment (may need IV)                  │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

#### **First-Line Anti-Hypertensive Drugs - "ABCD"**

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              FIRST-LINE DRUGS ("ABCD")                       │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ A - ACE inhibitors (or ARBs)                                 │
│ B - Beta-blockers                                            │
│ C - Calcium channel blockers                                 │
│ D - Diuretics (Thiazides)                                    │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

#### **1. ACE Inhibitors (ACEIs)**

**Examples:** Ramipril, Enalapril, Lisinopril, Perindopril

**Mechanism:** Inhibit ACE → ↓ Angiotensin II → ↓ Vasoconstriction, ↓ Aldosterone

**Indications (Compelling):**
- **Diabetes** with proteinuria
- **CKD** with proteinuria
- **Heart failure** (HFrEF)
- **Post-MI**
- **LVH**

**Side Effects:**
- **Dry cough** (10-15%) - due to ↑ Bradykinin (most common)
- **Hyperkalemia**
- **Angioedema** (rare but serious)
- ↑ Creatinine (acceptable if <30% increase)
- **Teratogenic** (Contraindicated in pregnancy)

**Contraindications:**
- **Bilateral renal artery stenosis** (may cause acute renal failure)
- **Pregnancy**
- **History of angioedema**
- Hyperkalemia (K+ >5.5)

#### **2. Angiotensin Receptor Blockers (ARBs)**

**Examples:** Losartan, Valsartan, Telmisartan, Irbesartan

**Mechanism:** Block AT1 receptors → ↓ Effects of Angiotensin II

**Indications:**
- **Same as ACEIs**
- **Alternative if ACEI causes cough** (ARBs don't increase bradykinin)

**Side Effects:**
- Similar to ACEIs **BUT NO COUGH**
- Hyperkalemia
- ↑ Creatinine
- Teratogenic

**Contraindications:** Same as ACEIs

**ACEI + ARB Combination:**
- **NOT recommended** (↑ Risk of hyperkalemia, AKI, hypotension)
- Exception: Nephrotic syndrome (under specialist guidance)

#### **3. Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs)**

**Types:**
- **Dihydropyridines (DHP):** Amlodipine, Nifedipine, Felodipine
- **Non-Dihydropyridines:** Verapamil, Diltiazem

**Mechanism:** Block Ca2+ channels → Vasodilation (DHP) or ↓ HR + ↓ Contractility (Non-DHP)

**Indications:**
- **Elderly** (Isolated systolic HTN)
- **Black patients**
- **Angina**
- **Raynaud's phenomenon**

**Side Effects:**
- **Peripheral edema** (Amlodipine) - most common
- **Flushing, headache**
- **Gingival hyperplasia**
- **Constipation** (Verapamil)
- **Bradycardia, AV block** (Non-DHP)

**Contraindications:**
- **Non-DHP:** Heart failure (HFrEF), AV block, Sick sinus syndrome
- **DHP:** Severe aortic stenosis

**Note:** Verapamil/Diltiazem **NOT** with β-blockers (risk of bradycardia/heart block)

#### **4. Diuretics**

**Thiazides:** Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ), Chlorthalidone, Indapamide

**Mechanism:** ↓ Na+ reabsorption in DCT → Natriuresis → ↓ Blood volume

**Indications:**
- **Elderly**
- **Black patients**
- **Heart failure**
- **Osteoporosis** (↓ Ca2+ excretion)

**Side Effects - "HYPO GLUC"**
```
HYPO:
  • Hyponatremia
  • Hypokalemia (most common)
  • Hypocalcemia (rare - thiazides ↓ Ca excretion)
  • Hypomagnesemia

GLUC:
  • Glucose elevation (hyperglycemia)
  • Lipid elevation (hyperlipidemia)
  • Uric acid elevation (hyperuricemia → Gout)
  • Calcium retention
```

**Contraindications:**
- Gout (relative)
- Severe renal impairment (ineffective if GFR <30)

**Loop Diuretics** (Furosemide, Torsemide):
- **Not first-line** for HTN
- Use if **CKD** (GFR <30) or **Heart failure**

**Potassium-Sparing Diuretics:**
- **Spironolactone:** Aldosterone antagonist
  - **Use:** Resistant HTN, Primary aldosteronism
  - **Side effects:** Hyperkalemia, Gynecomastia
- **Amiloride:** ENaC blocker
  - No anti-androgen effects (unlike Spironolactone)

#### **5. Beta-Blockers**

**Examples:** Metoprolol, Atenolol, Bisoprolol, Carvedilol

**Mechanism:** Block β-receptors → ↓ HR, ↓ Contractility, ↓ Renin

**Indications (Compelling):**
- **Post-MI**
- **Heart failure** (Bisoprolol, Carvedilol, Metoprolol succinate)
- **Angina**
- **Arrhythmias** (AF, SVT)
- **Young patients** with hyperkinetic circulation

**Side Effects:**
- **Bradycardia**
- **Fatigue, Depression**
- **Bronchospasm** (Non-selective)
- **Mask hypoglycemia** (in diabetics)
- **Cold extremities**
- **Erectile dysfunction**
- **Worsen lipid profile**

**Contraindications:**
- **Asthma/COPD** (relative - use cardioselective β1)
- **Heart block** (2°/3°)
- **Severe peripheral vascular disease**
- **Cocaine use** (unopposed α → vasoconstriction)

**Note:** **No longer first-line** for uncomplicated hypertension (less effective in preventing stroke)

#### **Combination Therapy**

**Preferred Combinations:**
```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      PREFERRED DRUG COMBINATIONS                       │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ 1. ACEI/ARB + CCB                                      │
│ 2. ACEI/ARB + Diuretic                                 │
│ 3. CCB + Diuretic                                      │
│                                                        │
│ Triple therapy: ACEI/ARB + CCB + Diuretic              │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**AVOID:**
- ACEI + ARB (↑ Adverse effects, no benefit)
- β-blocker + Non-DHP CCB (↑ Bradycardia, AV block)

#### **Algorithm for Hypertension Treatment**

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│         HYPERTENSION TREATMENT ALGORITHM                 │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                          │
│  Stage 1 HTN (130-139/80-89)                             │
│         ↓                                                │
│  Lifestyle modification                                  │
│  + Drug if CVD/High risk                                 │
│         ↓                                                │
│  ACEI/ARB or CCB or Thiazide                             │
│                                                          │
│  ────────────────────────────────────────────────        │
│                                                          │
│  Stage 2 HTN (≥140/90)                                   │
│         ↓                                                │
│  Start 2 drugs (combination pill if possible)            │
│         ↓                                                │
│  ACEI/ARB + CCB  OR  ACEI/ARB + Thiazide                 │
│         ↓                                                │
│  If not controlled:                                      │
│  Add 3rd drug (Triple: ACEI/ARB + CCB + Thiazide)        │
│         ↓                                                │
│  If still not controlled (Resistant HTN):                │
│  Add Spironolactone (25-50 mg)                           │
│  Or consider other causes                                │
│                                                          │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Race-Based Approach:**
- **Black patients:** Start with **CCB or Thiazide** (less responsive to ACEI/ARB monotherapy)
- **White/Asian patients:** Start with **ACEI/ARB**

**Age-Based Approach:**
- **<55 years:** **ACEI/ARB** (higher renin)
- **>55 years or Black:** **CCB or Thiazide** (lower renin, salt-sensitive)

### Resistant Hypertension

**Definition:** BP uncontrolled on **3 drugs** (including diuretic) at optimal doses

**Causes - "ABCDEFGH"**
```
A - Adherence (Poor compliance) - Most common
B - Blood pressure measurement error (White coat)
C - Chronic kidney disease
D - Drugs (NSAIDs, Steroids, OCP, Decongestants)
E - Excessive alcohol
F - Fluid overload (inadequate diuretic)
G - "G"Secondary causes (Aldosteronism, RAS, Pheo)
H - High sodium intake
```

**Management:**
1. **Confirm true resistance:**
   - Check adherence
   - ABPM (rule out white coat)
   - Proper BP technique

2. **Optimize therapy:**
   - Ensure diuretic is adequate
   - Switch HCTZ to Chlorthalidone (more potent)
   - Add **Spironolactone 25-50 mg** (most effective 4th agent)

3. **Screen for secondary causes**

4. **Consider:**
   - α-blockers (Doxazosin)
   - Direct vasodilators (Hydralazine, Minoxidil)
   - Central α-agonists (Clonidine, Methyldopa)
   - Referral to hypertension specialist

## Hypertensive Emergencies

### Definition

**Hypertensive Crisis:** Severe elevation of BP (>180/120 mmHg)

**Two Types:**

1. **Hypertensive Emergency:**
   - BP >180/120 mmHg **WITH acute end-organ damage**
   - Requires **immediate IV therapy** (within 1 hour)
   - Target: Reduce BP by **25% in first hour**, then to 160/100 over next 2-6 hours

2. **Hypertensive Urgency:**
   - BP >180/120 mmHg **WITHOUT acute end-organ damage**
   - Can use **oral agents**
   - Reduce BP over **24-48 hours**

### Hypertensive Emergency - End-Organ Damage

**Manifestations:**
- **Hypertensive encephalopathy** (Headache, confusion, seizures, vision changes)
- **Acute stroke** (Ischemic or hemorrhagic)
- **Acute MI**
- **Acute pulmonary edema**
- **Acute kidney injury**
- **Aortic dissection**
- **Eclampsia**
- **Retinopathy Grade IV** (Papilledema)

### Management of Hypertensive Emergency

**General Principles:**
1. **IV anti-hypertensives**
2. **ICU monitoring**
3. **Gradual BP reduction** (too rapid → ischemia)
   - **First hour:** Reduce MAP by 25%
   - **Next 2-6 hours:** Reduce to 160/100
   - **Next 24-48 hours:** Normalize

**NEVER reduce BP too rapidly** (Risk of stroke, MI, renal failure)

**IV Anti-Hypertensive Drugs:**

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      IV ANTI-HYPERTENSIVES FOR EMERGENCIES                  │
├──────────────────┬──────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ DRUG             │  INDICATION                              │
├──────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Labetalol        │ Most situations (α+β blocker)            │
│                  │ Pregnancy (DOC)                          │
├──────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Nicardipine      │ Most situations (CCB)                    │
│                  │ Stroke                                   │
├──────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Nitroprusside    │ Aortic dissection (with β-blocker)       │
│                  │ Acute pulmonary edema                    │
│                  │ (Caution: Cyanide toxicity if >48-72 h)  │
├──────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Nitroglycerin    │ Acute coronary syndrome                  │
│                  │ Acute pulmonary edema                    │
├──────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Esmolol          │ Aortic dissection (DOC)                  │
│                  │ Perioperative HTN                        │
├──────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Hydralazine      │ Eclampsia/Pre-eclampsia                  │
│                  │ (Avoid if aortic dissection)             │
├──────────────────┼──────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Enalaprilat      │ Acute LV failure                         │
│ (IV ACEI)        │ (Avoid in ACS)                           │
└──────────────────┴──────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Special Situations:**

**Aortic Dissection:**
- **Target:** SBP <120 mmHg, HR <60 bpm (within 20 min)
- **Drugs:** **β-blocker (Esmolol) FIRST**, then Nitroprusside
- **Never** vasodilate before β-blockade (↑ dP/dt → propagate dissection)

**Acute Ischemic Stroke:**
- **Permissive hypertension** (Allow BP to remain elevated initially)
- **Only lower if BP >220/120 mmHg** (or >185/110 if thrombolysis candidate)
- Use **Labetalol or Nicardipine**

**Intracerebral Hemorrhage:**
- **Target:** SBP 140-160 mmHg (balance perfusion vs bleeding)

**Acute Coronary Syndrome:**
- **Nitroglycerin** (↓ Preload, ↓ Myocardial O₂ demand)
- Avoid pure vasodilators (reflex tachycardia)

**Acute Pulmonary Edema:**
- **Nitroglycerin or Nitroprusside** (↓ Preload + afterload)
- **Loop diuretics** (Furosemide)

**Pregnancy (Eclampsia/Severe Pre-eclampsia):**
- **Labetalol IV** (DOC) or **Hydralazine**
- **Magnesium sulfate** for seizure prophylaxis
- **Avoid:** ACEIs, ARBs (teratogenic)

### Hypertensive Urgency

**No acute end-organ damage**

**Management:**
- **Oral agents** (Resume/Intensify home medications)
- **Outpatient management** (unless very high risk)
- Reduce BP over **24-48 hours**
- **Do NOT use short-acting nifedipine** (risk of precipitous drop)

**Oral Drugs:**
- **Captopril 25 mg** (Oral, fast-acting)
- **Labetalol 200-400 mg PO**
- **Clonidine 0.1-0.2 mg PO**
- **Amlodipine 5-10 mg PO**

## Special Situations

### Hypertension in Pregnancy

**Classification:**
1. **Chronic HTN:** Pre-existing (before 20 weeks)
2. **Gestational HTN:** New-onset after 20 weeks (no proteinuria)
3. **Pre-eclampsia:** HTN + Proteinuria after 20 weeks
4. **Eclampsia:** Pre-eclampsia + Seizures

**Treatment:**
- **Target:** <140/90 mmHg
- **Safe drugs:** Methyldopa (DOC), Labetalol, Nifedipine
- **AVOID:** ACEIs, ARBs (teratogenic), Atenolol (IUGR)

**Detailed in:** ObsGyn/01_Hypertensive_Disorders_of_Pregnancy.md

### Hypertension in Diabetes

**Target:** <130/80 mmHg

**First-line:** **ACEI or ARB** (renoprotective)

**Benefits:**
- ↓ Proteinuria
- ↓ Progression to ESRD
- ↓ Cardiovascular events

### Hypertension in CKD

**Target:** <130/80 mmHg (if proteinuria >1 g/day)

**First-line:** **ACEI or ARB**

**Monitoring:**
- Check K+ and Creatinine after starting ACEI/ARB
- ↑ Creatinine <30% acceptable
- If ↑ Creatinine >30% → Suspect bilateral RAS

### Hypertension Post-MI

**Preferred:**
- **β-blocker** (↓ Recurrent MI, mortality)
- **ACEI** (↓ Remodeling, mortality)

### Hypertension with Heart Failure

**HFrEF:**
- **ACEI/ARB + β-blocker + Aldosterone antagonist + Loop diuretic**
- (See Heart Failure topic for details)

**HFpEF:**
- **Diuretics** for volume overload
- Treat underlying HTN

## NEET PG High-Yield Points

### 🔥 MUST KNOW

1. **Definition:** BP ≥140/90 mmHg (WHO/ISH), ≥130/80 (ACC/AHA 2017)

2. **Essential (Primary) HTN:** 90-95% of cases

3. **Most common cause of secondary HTN:**
   - **Overall:** Renal disease (CKD, Renovascular)
   - **Endocrine:** Primary aldosteronism

4. **Primary aldosteronism clue:** HTN + **Unprovoked hypokalemia**
   - **Most common cause:** Bilateral adrenal hyperplasia
   - **Screening:** Aldosterone-Renin Ratio (ARR)
   - **Treatment:** Adenoma → Surgery; Hyperplasia → Spironolactone

5. **Pheochromocytoma clue:** Episodic **headaches, sweating, palpitations**
   - **Screening:** 24-hour urinary metanephrines
   - **Treatment:** α-blocker **FIRST**, then β-blocker, then surgery

6. **Renal artery stenosis:**
   - **Elderly males:** Atherosclerosis
   - **Young females:** Fibromuscular dysplasia
   - **Clue:** ↑ Creatinine with ACEIs

7. **First-line drugs - "ABCD":**
   - **A:** ACEIs/ARBs (Diabetes, CKD, HF, Post-MI, LVH)
   - **B:** β-blockers (Post-MI, HF, Angina, Arrhythmias)
   - **C:** CCBs (Elderly, Black, ISH, Angina)
   - **D:** Diuretics (Thiazides - Elderly, Black, HF)

8. **ACEI side effects:**
   - **Cough** (10-15%) - most common
   - **Angioedema** (rare)
   - Hyperkalemia
   - **Teratogenic**

9. **ARBs:** Same as ACEIs but **NO COUGH**

10. **ACEI + ARB:** **NOT recommended** (↑ Adverse effects)

11. **CCB side effects:** **Peripheral edema** (most common - Amlodipine)

12. **Thiazide side effects - "HYPO GLUC":**
    - Hypokalemia, Hyponatremia
    - Hyperglycemia, Hyperlipidemia, Hyperuricemia (Gout)

13. **Race/Age approach:**
    - **<55 years or White:** ACEI/ARB
    - **>55 years or Black:** CCB or Thiazide

14. **Resistant HTN:** Uncontrolled on **3 drugs** (including diuretic)
    - **Add:** Spironolactone (most effective 4th agent)

15. **Hypertensive Emergency:**
    - BP >180/120 **WITH end-organ damage**
    - **IV drugs, ICU**
    - Reduce MAP by **25% in 1st hour**

16. **Hypertensive Urgency:**
    - BP >180/120 **WITHOUT end-organ damage**
    - **Oral drugs, outpatient**

17. **Aortic dissection:**
    - **Target:** SBP <120 mmHg, HR <60 bpm
    - **Drugs:** **β-blocker (Esmolol) FIRST**, then Nitroprusside

18. **Pregnancy HTN:**
    - **Safe:** Methyldopa, Labetalol, Nifedipine
    - **AVOID:** ACEIs, ARBs (teratogenic)

19. **Diabetes/CKD:** **ACEI/ARB** first-line (renoprotective)

20. **Post-MI:** **β-blocker + ACEI** (↓ Mortality)

### Clinical Scenarios

**Scenario 1:** Young female with HTN, hypokalemia, low renin → **Primary aldosteronism**

**Scenario 2:** Episodic HTN with headaches, sweating, palpitations → **Pheochromocytoma** (24-hr urine metanephrines)

**Scenario 3:** HTN + Abdominal bruit + ↑ Creatinine with ACEI → **Renal artery stenosis**

**Scenario 4:** HTN + Upper limb BP > Lower limb BP + Radio-femoral delay → **Coarctation of aorta**

**Scenario 5:** Patient on ACEI develops dry cough → Switch to **ARB**

**Scenario 6:** Black patient with HTN, no comorbidities → Start **CCB or Thiazide**

**Scenario 7:** Diabetic with HTN and microalbuminuria → Start **ACEI or ARB**

**Scenario 8:** HTN uncontrolled on ACEI + CCB + Thiazide → Add **Spironolactone**

**Scenario 9:** Pregnant woman with HTN → Use **Methyldopa or Labetalol**, AVOID ACEIs

**Scenario 10:** Aortic dissection with BP 200/110 → Give **β-blocker (Esmolol) FIRST**, then Nitroprusside

### Quick Revision Checklist

- [ ] BP classification: Normal, Stage 1, Stage 2
- [ ] Essential vs Secondary HTN (90-95% vs 5-10%)
- [ ] Secondary causes: Renal (most common), Aldosteronism, Pheo, Cushing's, Coarctation
- [ ] Primary aldosteronism: HTN + Hypokalemia, ARR, Spironolactone
- [ ] Pheochromocytoma: Triad (Headache, Sweating, Palpitations), α-blocker first
- [ ] First-line drugs: ACEI/ARB, CCB, Thiazide, β-blocker (with indications)
- [ ] ACEI: Cough, Angioedema, Hyperkalemia, Teratogenic
- [ ] ARB: No cough
- [ ] CCB: Peripheral edema
- [ ] Thiazide: HYPO GLUC
- [ ] Combination therapy: ACEI+CCB, ACEI+Thiazide, CCB+Thiazide
- [ ] Resistant HTN: 3 drugs, add Spironolactone
- [ ] Hypertensive emergency: IV drugs, reduce MAP 25% in 1 hour
- [ ] Aortic dissection: β-blocker FIRST
- [ ] Pregnancy: Methyldopa, Labetalol, avoid ACEIs/ARBs

## Practice Questions

### Question 1
A 35-year-old female presents with hypertension. Labs show K+ 2.8 mEq/L, HCO3- 30 mEq/L. Plasma aldosterone is elevated and renin is suppressed. What is the most likely diagnosis?

**A)** Pheochromocytoma
**B)** Primary aldosteronism
**C)** Cushing's syndrome
**D)** Renal artery stenosis

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: B) Primary aldosteronism**

**Explanation:**
- **Hypokalemia + Metabolic alkalosis** (↑ HCO3-)
- **Elevated aldosterone + Suppressed renin** → Primary (autonomous) aldosterone secretion
- Classic presentation of **Conn's syndrome**
- Screen with **Aldosterone-Renin Ratio** (elevated)
- **Most common cause:** Bilateral adrenal hyperplasia (60%)
- **Treatment:** Adenoma → Surgery; Hyperplasia → Spironolactone

**Why not others:**
- Pheo: Episodic symptoms, no typical electrolyte changes
- Cushing's: Cushingoid features, ↑ cortisol
- RAS: ↑ Renin (not suppressed)
</details>

### Question 2
A 45-year-old male with hypertension is started on an ACEI. After 2 weeks, he develops a persistent dry cough. What is the most appropriate next step?

**A)** Continue ACEI and add cough suppressant
**B)** Switch to ARB
**C)** Switch to CCB
**D)** Discontinue and observe

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: B) Switch to ARB**

**Explanation:**
- **Dry cough** is a common side effect of ACEIs (10-15%)
- Mechanism: ↑ Bradykinin (ACE normally breaks down bradykinin)
- **ARBs** do NOT cause cough (don't affect bradykinin)
- ARBs have **same indications and benefits** as ACEIs
- **Switch to ARB** if patient has compelling indication for RAAS blockade (e.g., diabetes, CKD, HF)
- CCB is alternative but loses renoprotective benefit if diabetic/CKD
</details>

### Question 3
A 60-year-old male with HTN on ACEI, amlodipine, and hydrochlorothiazide has BP 160/95 mmHg. What is the best 4th agent to add?

**A)** Atenolol
**B)** Spironolactone
**C)** Doxazosin
**D)** Hydralazine

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: B) Spironolactone**

**Explanation:**
- Patient has **resistant hypertension** (uncontrolled on 3 drugs including diuretic)
- **Spironolactone 25-50 mg** is the **most effective 4th-line agent**
- Blocks aldosterone → Addresses aldosterone breakthrough
- **PATHWAY trial** showed Spironolactone superior to Doxazosin and Bisoprolol
- Monitor for **hyperkalemia** (especially with ACEI)

**Why not others:**
- β-blockers: Less effective, not recommended as routine add-on
- Doxazosin: Less effective than Spironolactone
- Hydralazine: Side effects, less effective
</details>

### Question 4
A patient presents with severe headache and BP 210/130 mmHg. CT shows intracerebral hemorrhage. What is the target BP?

**A)** <120/80 mmHg immediately
**B)** <140-160/90-100 mmHg
**C)** <180/105 mmHg
**D)** No BP lowering (permissive hypertension)

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: B) <140-160/90-100 mmHg**

**Explanation:**
- **Intracerebral hemorrhage** with severe HTN
- **Target SBP:** 140-160 mmHg (balance between preventing hematoma expansion and maintaining cerebral perfusion)
- **INTERACT-2 and ATACH-2 trials:** Intensive BP lowering (SBP <140) may be safe and prevent hematoma growth
- **Avoid excessive lowering** (risk of perihematomal ischemia)
- Use **IV Labetalol or Nicardipine**

**Why not others:**
- Too aggressive lowering (option A) may worsen ischemia
- Too high (option C) risks hematoma expansion
- Permissive hypertension (option D) used in ischemic stroke, NOT hemorrhagic
</details>

### Question 5
A pregnant woman at 32 weeks gestation has BP 165/105 mmHg with 2+ proteinuria. Which drug is contraindicated?

**A)** Methyldopa
**B)** Labetalol
**C)** Nifedipine
**D)** Enalapril

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: D) Enalapril**

**Explanation:**
- Diagnosis: **Severe pre-eclampsia** (BP ≥160/110 + Proteinuria at >20 weeks)
- **ACEIs (Enalapril) and ARBs are CONTRAINDICATED** in pregnancy
  - **Teratogenic** effects:
    - 1st trimester: Cardiac/CNS malformations
    - 2nd/3rd trimester: Renal dysgenesis, Oligohydramnios, IUGR, Fetal death
- **Safe anti-hypertensives in pregnancy:**
  - **Methyldopa** (DOC for chronic HTN)
  - **Labetalol** (DOC for acute severe HTN/IV use)
  - **Nifedipine** (alternative)
  - **Hydralazine** (IV for emergencies)

**Management of severe pre-eclampsia:**
- IV Labetalol or Hydralazine
- Magnesium sulfate (seizure prophylaxis)
- Plan delivery (definitive treatment)
</details>

### Question 6
A patient with aortic dissection presents with BP 200/110 mmHg and HR 110 bpm. What is the first drug to give?

**A)** Nitroprusside
**B)** Esmolol (β-blocker)
**C)** Hydralazine
**D)** Nitroglycerin

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: B) Esmolol (β-blocker)**

**Explanation:**
- **Aortic dissection:** Medical emergency
- **Goals:**
  - **HR <60 bpm** (↓ dP/dt - force of LV contraction)
  - **SBP <120 mmHg**
  - Achieve within **20 minutes**

- **Treatment sequence:**
  1. **β-blocker FIRST** (Esmolol IV) - ↓ HR, ↓ dP/dt
  2. **Then** add Nitroprusside (if BP still high)

- **NEVER give vasodilator first** (Nitroprusside, Hydralazine, Nitroglycerin)
  - Causes reflex tachycardia → ↑ dP/dt → Propagates dissection

**Why Esmolol:**
- **Ultra-short acting** IV β-blocker
- Easy to titrate
- Rapid onset and offset
</details>

### Question 7
A 28-year-old male has HTN with BP in upper limbs 170/90 mmHg and lower limbs 110/70 mmHg. Femoral pulses are delayed compared to radial. Most likely diagnosis?

**A)** Aortic regurgitation
**B)** Coarctation of aorta
**C)** Pheochromocytoma
**D)** Renal artery stenosis

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: B) Coarctation of aorta**

**Explanation:**
- **Classic triad:**
  1. **Upper limb HTN + Lower limb hypotension**
  2. **Radio-femoral delay**
  3. **Weak/absent femoral pulses**

- **Coarctation:** Narrowing of aorta (usually at ductus arteriosus insertion - distal to left subclavian artery)
- **Associated findings:**
  - **Rib notching** on CXR (collateral vessels erode ribs)
  - **Bicuspid aortic valve** (50%)
  - **Berry aneurysms** (CNS)
  - **Turner syndrome** (15%)

- **Investigation:** Echocardiography, CT/MR angiography
- **Treatment:** Balloon angioplasty ± stenting or surgical repair

**Why not others:**
- AR: Wide pulse pressure, water-hammer pulse, no differential BP
- Pheo: Episodic symptoms
- RAS: No differential BP between limbs
</details>

### Question 8
A diabetic patient with HTN and microalbuminuria (urine albumin 100 mg/day) should be started on which drug class?

**A)** Thiazide diuretic
**B)** CCB
**C)** ACEI or ARB
**D)** Beta-blocker

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: C) ACEI or ARB**

**Explanation:**
- **Diabetes + Microalbuminuria** → Strong indication for RAAS blockade
- **ACEIs/ARBs are FIRST-LINE** in diabetic nephropathy

**Benefits:**
- **Renoprotection:**
  - ↓ Intraglomerular pressure (dilate efferent arteriole)
  - ↓ Proteinuria

- **Cardiovascular protection:**
  - ↓ MI, Stroke, CV death

**Landmark Trials:**
- **HOPE trial:** Ramipril ↓ CV events in diabetics
- **RENAAL, IDNT trials:** ARBs slow diabetic nephropathy

**Target BP in diabetes:** <130/80 mmHg

**Monitoring:**
- Check K+ and Creatinine after starting
- ↑ Creatinine <30% acceptable
</details>
