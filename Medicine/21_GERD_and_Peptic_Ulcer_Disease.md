## GASTROESOPHAGEAL REFLUX DISEASE (GERD)

### Definition

**GERD**: Chronic condition where gastric contents reflux into the esophagus, causing symptoms and/or complications

**Physiologic Reflux**: Brief episodes (<1 hour/day total), no symptoms
**Pathologic Reflux (GERD)**: Frequent/prolonged episodes causing symptoms/mucosal injury

### Epidemiology

- **Prevalence**: 10-20% of Western populations
- **Age**: Can occur at any age, increases with age
- **Sex**: Male = Female (esophagitis more common in men)

### Pathophysiology

**Normal Antireflux Mechanisms**:
1. **Lower Esophageal Sphincter (LES)**: Tonically contracted (10-30 mmHg)
2. **Diaphragmatic crura**: External compression
3. **Angle of His**: Acute angle at gastroesophageal junction
4. **Esophageal clearance**: Peristalsis, salivary bicarbonate
5. **Mucosal resistance**: Mucus, bicarbonate secretion, blood flow

**Mechanisms of GERD**:

```
PRIMARY MECHANISM
    ↓
TRANSIENT LES RELAXATIONS (TLESRs) - MOST COMMON (60-70%)
• Inappropriate relaxation NOT triggered by swallowing
• Vagally mediated
• Triggered by: Gastric distension, fatty meals
    ↓
REFLUX OF GASTRIC CONTENTS
• Acid, Pepsin, Bile (if duodenogastric reflux)
    ↓
ESOPHAGEAL MUCOSAL INJURY
• ↓ if: Prolonged contact time, ↓ esophageal clearance
    ↓
SYMPTOMS & COMPLICATIONS
```

**Contributing Factors**:
- **↓ LES pressure**: Smoking, alcohol, caffeine, chocolate, peppermint, calcium channel blockers, nitrates
- **Delayed gastric emptying**: Gastroparesis
- **Hiatal hernia**: Disrupts LES function
- **↑ Intra-abdominal pressure**: Obesity, pregnancy, tight clothing
- **Esophageal clearance defect**: Scleroderma

### Clinical Features

#### Typical Symptoms

| Symptom | Frequency | Characteristics |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Heartburn** | **75-90%** - MOST COMMON | Burning retrosternal discomfort, worse after meals, lying down, bending |
| **Regurgitation** | 60% | Effortless return of gastric contents to mouth (sour/bitter taste) |
| **Dysphagia** | 30% | Suggests stricture or severe esophagitis |

**Heartburn**:
- **Postprandial** (30 min - 2 hours after meals)
- **Worse**: Lying down, bending forward, large meals, fatty/spicy foods
- **Relief**: Antacids, sitting upright

#### Atypical/Extraesophageal Symptoms

| Symptom | Mechanism |
|---------|-----------|
| **Chronic cough** | Microaspiration, vagal reflex |
| **Asthma** (Reflux-induced) | Microaspiration → bronchospasm |
| **Laryngitis** (Hoarseness) | Acid contact with larynx |
| **Dental erosions** | Acid exposure |
| **Globus sensation** | "Lump in throat" feeling |
| **Chest pain** (non-cardiac) | Esophageal spasm, acid sensitivity |

**Alarm Features** (Suggest complications or malignancy):
- **Dysphagia** (especially progressive)
- **Odynophagia** (painful swallowing)
- **Unintentional weight loss** (>5% body weight)
- **GI bleeding** (hematemesis, melena)
- **Anemia**
- **Persistent vomiting**
- **Age >50 years** with new-onset symptoms

### Diagnosis

#### Clinical Diagnosis

**Typical GERD** (Heartburn + Regurgitation):
- **Empiric PPI trial** (4-8 weeks) → If symptoms resolve → Diagnosis confirmed
- **NO need for endoscopy** if:
  - Age <50 years
  - No alarm features
  - Typical symptoms

#### Endoscopy (Upper GI Endoscopy/EGD)

**Indications**:
- **Alarm features** (dysphagia, weight loss, bleeding, anemia)
- **Age ≥50 years** with new-onset GERD
- **Refractory symptoms** (not responding to PPI)
- **Long-standing GERD** (>5-10 years) → Screen for Barrett's esophagus
- **Severe symptoms** suggesting erosive esophagitis

**Findings**:
- **Normal** (60-70%) = **Non-Erosive Reflux Disease (NERD)**
- **Erosive esophagitis** (30-40%)
- **Barrett's esophagus** (10-15% of chronic GERD)
- **Stricture** (<5%)

**Los Angeles Classification** (Erosive Esophagitis):

| Grade | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **A** | ≥1 mucosal break **≤5 mm**, not extending between mucosal folds |
| **B** | ≥1 mucosal break **>5 mm**, not extending between mucosal folds |
| **C** | Mucosal breaks **continuous between ≥2 mucosal folds**, involving **<75%** of circumference |
| **D** | Mucosal breaks involving **≥75% of circumference** |

#### 24-Hour pH Monitoring

**Indications**:
- **Diagnosis unclear** (atypical symptoms, normal endoscopy)
- **PPI-refractory** symptoms (to confirm acid reflux)
- **Pre-operative evaluation** (before antireflux surgery)

**Technique**:
- **Wireless Bravo capsule** (preferred, more comfortable)
- **Transnasal catheter** (conventional)

**Performed OFF PPI** (stop 7 days before)

**Interpretation**:
- **DeMeester Score >14.7** OR
- **pH <4 for >4% of time** → Abnormal reflux

#### Impedance-pH Monitoring

**Advantage**: Detects **non-acid reflux** (weakly acidic, alkaline)
- Useful if patient on PPI (pH monitoring alone may miss non-acid reflux)

#### Esophageal Manometry

**NOT for GERD diagnosis** (reflux is NOT a motility disorder)

**Indications**:
- **Pre-operative** (before fundoplication) → Rule out achalasia, scleroderma
- **Dysphagia** with normal endoscopy → Rule out motility disorders

### Management

#### Lifestyle Modifications

**Dietary**:
- **Avoid**: Fatty foods, chocolate, peppermint, caffeine, alcohol, citrus, tomatoes, spicy foods
- **Small, frequent meals** (avoid large meals)
- **Avoid eating 2-3 hours before bedtime**

**Positional**:
- **Elevate head of bed** (6-8 inches) - Use blocks, NOT extra pillows
- **Left lateral sleeping position**

**Other**:
- **Weight loss** (if BMI >25) - **Most effective**
- **Smoking cessation**
- **Avoid tight clothing**

**Medications to Avoid** (↓ LES pressure):
- Calcium channel blockers, Nitrates, Theophylline, Anticholinergics, Benzodiazepines

#### Pharmacologic Treatment

##### 1. Antacids

**Mechanism**: Neutralize gastric acid

**Examples**: Calcium carbonate, Magnesium hydroxide, Aluminum hydroxide

**Use**: **On-demand** for mild/intermittent symptoms

**Limitations**: Short duration (1-2 hours), not for chronic GERD

##### 2. H2-Receptor Antagonists (H2RAs)

**Mechanism**: Block histamine-stimulated acid secretion

**Examples**:
- **Ranitidine** (withdrawn 2020 due to NDMA contamination)
- **Famotidine** 20-40 mg BID
- Cimetidine, Nizatidine

**Efficacy**: ↓ Symptoms in mild GERD, **inferior to PPIs**

**Use**: Mild/intermittent GERD, on-demand, or **add to PPI** (bedtime dose if nocturnal symptoms)

**Tachyphylaxis**: Develops within 2-4 weeks (↓ efficacy)

##### 3. Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs) - **FIRST-LINE**

**Mechanism**: Irreversibly inhibit H⁺/K⁺-ATPase (proton pump) in parietal cells

**Examples**:

| Drug | Dose | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| **Omeprazole** | 20-40 mg OD | First PPI |
| **Esomeprazole** | 20-40 mg OD | S-isomer of omeprazole |
| **Pantoprazole** | 40 mg OD | IV formulation available |
| **Lansoprazole** | 30 mg OD | |
| **Rabeprazole** | 20 mg OD | Faster onset |

**Dosing**:
- **Standard**: Once daily, **30 minutes before breakfast**
- **Severe**: BID dosing (before breakfast and dinner)

**Efficacy**:
- **Symptom relief**: 80-90%
- **Erosive esophagitis healing**: 80-90% at 8 weeks

**Duration**:
- **Mild-Moderate**: 4-8 weeks
- **Erosive esophagitis**: 8-12 weeks
- **Maintenance**: Lowest effective dose (on-demand or daily)

**PPI-Refractory GERD** (15-30% of patients):
- **Optimize**: Take 30 min before meals, ensure compliance
- **Increase dose**: BID dosing
- **Switch PPI** (different PPI may work)
- **Add H2RA** at bedtime (nocturnal acid breakthrough)
- **Re-evaluate**: 24-hr pH monitoring (confirm acid reflux), endoscopy (rule out other causes)

**Side Effects** (Long-term use):
- **Common**: Headache, diarrhea, nausea
- **Rare but important**:
  - **↑ Risk of fractures** (hip, spine, wrist) - ↓ Calcium absorption
  - **Hypomagnesemia** (chronic use >1 year)
  - **↑ Risk of *C. difficile* infection**
  - **Interstitial nephritis**
  - **Fundic gland polyps** (benign)
  - **↑ Risk of community-acquired pneumonia** (controversial)

**Contraindication**: Co-administration with clopidogrel (↓ clopidogrel activation, especially omeprazole/esomeprazole)

##### 4. Prokinetics

**Mechanism**: ↑ LES pressure, ↑ esophageal clearance, ↑ gastric emptying

**Examples**:
- **Metoclopramide** 10 mg TID (before meals)
- Domperidone (not available in US)

**Efficacy**: **Limited** (minimal benefit over PPI alone)

**Use**: **Adjunct** if gastroparesis component

**Side Effects**:
- **Metoclopramide**: **Tardive dyskinesia** (limit use to <12 weeks), drowsiness, dystonia

**Not Recommended** as monotherapy

##### 5. Baclofen

**Mechanism**: GABA-B agonist → ↓ TLESRs

**Use**: **Refractory GERD**, especially with regurgitation

**Dose**: 5-20 mg TID

**Side Effects**: Drowsiness, dizziness, weakness

#### Surgical Treatment (Fundoplication)

**Indications**:
- **Severe symptoms** despite maximal medical therapy
- **Patient preference** (young patients not wanting lifelong PPI)
- **Large hiatal hernia** with symptoms
- **Complications** (stricture, Barrett's with dysplasia)

**Contraindications**:
- **Achalasia**, scleroderma (esophageal dysmotility)
- **Poor surgical candidate**

**Procedure**:

**Nissen Fundoplication** (360° wrap):
- Gastric fundus wrapped completely around lower esophagus
- **Laparoscopic** (preferred) or open

**Efficacy**: 85-90% symptom control at 5 years

**Complications**:
- **Dysphagia** (10-20%, usually temporary)
- **Gas-bloat syndrome** (difficulty belching/vomiting)
- **Diarrhea** (vagal injury)
- **Wrap failure** (5-10% long-term)

**Alternatives**: Partial fundoplication (Toupet 270°, Dor 180°) - ↓ Dysphagia, but ↑ recurrence

#### Endoscopic Therapies

**Stretta Procedure**: Radiofrequency ablation of LES

**Transoral Incisionless Fundoplication (TIF)**: Endoscopic fundoplication

**Efficacy**: Less than surgical fundoplication, NOT widely adopted

### Complications of GERD

#### 1. Esophageal Stricture

**Pathophysiology**: Chronic inflammation → Fibrosis → Narrowing

**Clinical**: **Progressive dysphagia** (solids → liquids)

**Diagnosis**: Endoscopy (smooth, tapered narrowing)

**Treatment**:
- **Endoscopic dilation** (bougie or balloon)
- **High-dose PPI** (to prevent recurrence)
- May need repeated dilations

#### 2. Barrett's Esophagus

**Definition**: Replacement of normal **squamous epithelium** with **intestinal metaplasia** (columnar epithelium with goblet cells)

**Epidemiology**:
- **10-15% of chronic GERD patients**
- **Male > Female** (3:1)
- Caucasian, obesity, smoking

**Diagnosis**: **Endoscopy + Biopsy**
- **Endoscopy**: Salmon-pink mucosa extending ≥1 cm above gastroesophageal junction
- **Biopsy**: Intestinal metaplasia (goblet cells on histology) - **REQUIRED for diagnosis**

**Seattle Protocol**: Biopsies every 1-2 cm in 4 quadrants

**Cancer Risk**: **0.5% per year** progression to esophageal adenocarcinoma

**Surveillance**:

| Dysplasia Grade | Surveillance Interval |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| **No dysplasia** | Every **3-5 years** |
| **Low-grade dysplasia (LGD)** | Every **6-12 months** (or ablation) |
| **High-grade dysplasia (HGD)** | **Endoscopic ablation** OR Surveillance every 3 months |

**Treatment**:
- **PPI therapy** (does NOT reverse Barrett's, but may ↓ progression)
- **HGD/Early cancer**: **Endoscopic ablation** (Radiofrequency ablation - RFA) ± Endoscopic mucosal resection (EMR)

#### 3. Esophagitis

See Los Angeles classification above

**Treatment**: PPI × 8-12 weeks

#### 4. Respiratory Complications

- **Chronic cough**
- **Asthma exacerbation**
- **Recurrent pneumonia** (aspiration)
- **Laryngitis**

## PEPTIC ULCER DISEASE (PUD)

### Definition

**Peptic Ulcer**: Mucosal break (≥5 mm) extending through muscularis mucosae

**Locations**:
- **Duodenal ulcer** (DU): **Most common** (70%)
- **Gastric ulcer** (GU): 25%
- **Both**: 5%

### Epidemiology

- **Prevalence**: 5-10% lifetime risk
- **Declining** due to ↓ *H. pylori* prevalence, ↑ PPI use
- **Age**: Can occur at any age, peak 55-65 years

### Etiology

```
PEPTIC ULCER DISEASE CAUSES
│
├─ H. PYLORI INFECTION (50-70% of DU, 30-50% of GU)
│  • MOST COMMON cause overall
│  • Prevalence: 50% in developing countries, 20% in developed
│
├─ NSAIDs (20-30%)
│  • Aspirin, Ibuprofen, Naproxen, COX-2 inhibitors
│  • Dose-dependent risk
│  • Risk factors: Age >65, history of PUD/GI bleed, steroids, anticoagulants
│
├─ STRESS ULCERS (ICU patients)
│  • Mechanical ventilation >48 hours
│  • Coagulopathy
│  • Burns (Curling ulcer), Head trauma (Cushing ulcer)
│
├─ ZOLLINGER-ELLISON SYNDROME (Gastrinoma) - Rare (<1%)
│  • Multiple/refractory ulcers, diarrhea
│
└─ OTHER (Rare)
   • Crohn's disease
   • Infection (CMV, HSV - immunocompromised)
   • Malignancy (lymphoma, adenocarcinoma)
   • Cocaine use
```

**Mnemonic: "HANZ" for PUD Causes**:
- **H** - *H. pylori*
- **A** - Aspirin/NSAIDs
- **N** - ↑ (N) acid states (Zollinger-Ellison)
- **Z** - Zollinger-Ellison syndrome

**Idiopathic** (10-20%): No *H. pylori*, no NSAIDs (likely undetected NSAID use or false-negative *H. pylori* tests)

### Pathophysiology

**Balance**: Aggressive factors (Acid, Pepsin, *H. pylori*, NSAIDs) vs Protective factors (Mucus, Bicarbonate, Blood flow, Prostaglandins)

**Ulcer Formation**: Imbalance → Mucosal injury

#### H. pylori Mechanism

```
H. PYLORI COLONIZATION (Gastric antrum, duodenum)
    ↓
VIRULENCE FACTORS
• Urease (produces NH₃ → ↑ pH, allows survival)
• CagA, VacA toxins (mucosal damage)
    ↓
INFLAMMATORY RESPONSE
• Neutrophil infiltration, cytokines (IL-8, TNF-α)
    ↓
MUCOSAL INJURY
    ↓
DUODENAL ULCER (70% have H. pylori)
• ↑ Gastric acid secretion (antral gastritis → ↑ Gastrin)
• Gastric metaplasia in duodenum → H. pylori colonizes → ulcer
    ↓
GASTRIC ULCER (50% have H. pylori)
• Corpus gastritis → ↓ Acid secretion (but still enough for ulcer)
• Direct mucosal injury
```

#### NSAID Mechanism

**NSAIDs → Inhibit COX-1 → ↓ Prostaglandins → ↓ Mucus, ↓ Bicarbonate, ↓ Blood flow → Mucosal injury**

**COX-2 Selective Inhibitors** (Celecoxib): ↓ GI risk vs non-selective NSAIDs, but NOT zero risk

### Clinical Features

#### Symptoms

| Feature | Duodenal Ulcer | Gastric Ulcer |
|---------|----------------|---------------|
| **Epigastric pain** | **MOST COMMON** (80-90%) | **MOST COMMON** (70-80%) |
| **Timing** | **2-5 hours AFTER meals**, **Nocturnal** (midnight-3 AM) | **Within 1 hour AFTER meals** (food worsens) |
| **Relief** | **Food, Antacids** | Variable (may worsen with food) |
| **Weight** | Unchanged or ↑ (eat more to relieve pain) | **↓ Weight loss** (avoid food due to pain) |

**Classic DU Pain Pattern**: "**Burning hunger pain**" 2-5 hours after meals → Relieved by food → Recurs

**Classic GU Pain Pattern**: Pain **shortly after eating** → Fear of eating → Weight loss

**Asymptomatic**: 20-30% (especially elderly, NSAID users)

#### Physical Examination

- **Epigastric tenderness** (50-70%)
- Usually **non-specific**
- **Peritoneal signs** (rigidity, rebound) → Perforation

### Diagnosis

#### Upper GI Endoscopy (EGD)

**GOLD STANDARD**

**Indications**:
- **Suspected PUD**
- **Alarm features** (age >50, weight loss, dysphagia, bleeding, anemia)
- **Persistent symptoms** despite treatment

**Findings**:
- **Duodenal ulcer**: Usually D1 (bulb), round/oval, clean base (if healed)
- **Gastric ulcer**: Lesser curvature (most common), biopsy **REQUIRED** (rule out malignancy)

**Gastric Ulcer Biopsy**:
- **ALWAYS biopsy** gastric ulcers (5% are malignant - adenocarcinoma)
- **Repeat endoscopy** at 8-12 weeks after treatment (confirm healing, rule out malignancy)

**Duodenal Ulcer Biopsy**: NOT required (malignancy rare)

#### H. pylori Testing

**Test for ALL patients with PUD**

**Methods**:

| Test | Sensitivity | Specificity | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------------|-------|
| **Rapid Urease Test (RUT)** (Biopsy-based) | 90-95% | 95-100% | **During endoscopy**, results in minutes, cheap |
| **Histology** (Biopsy-based) | 95-99% | **95-99%** | **GOLD STANDARD**, H&E stain or special stains |
| **Urea Breath Test (UBT)** (Non-invasive) | 95-98% | 95-98% | **Best non-invasive**, check eradication |
| **Stool Antigen Test** (Non-invasive) | 90-95% | 90-95% | Alternative to UBT |
| **Serology** (IgG antibody) (Non-invasive) | 85-92% | 79-90% | **NOT for active infection** (stays + after eradication), NOT for test-of-cure |

**False Negatives** (all except serology):
- **PPI use** (stop 2 weeks before), **Antibiotics** (stop 4 weeks), **Bismuth** (stop 4 weeks)
- **Recent bleeding** (RUT may be false negative)

**Test-of-Cure** (Confirm eradication):
- **When**: **≥4 weeks after treatment**
- **Which test**: **Urea Breath Test** OR **Stool Antigen** (NOT serology)
- **Indications**: **ALL patients** (especially if bleeding ulcer, persistent symptoms)

#### Barium Upper GI Series

**NOT first-line** (replaced by endoscopy)

**Finding**: "**Crater**" or "**Niche**" sign (barium-filled ulcer)

**Limitation**: Cannot biopsy, less sensitive than endoscopy

### Management

#### General Principles

1. **Eradicate *H. pylori*** (if positive)
2. **Stop NSAIDs** (if possible)
3. **Acid suppression** (PPI)
4. **Lifestyle modifications** (avoid smoking, alcohol, NSAIDs)

#### H. pylori Eradication

**Goal**: Cure ulcer (↓ recurrence from 60-70% → <5%), ↓ bleeding risk, ↓ cancer risk

##### First-Line Regimens

**1. Clarithromycin-Based Triple Therapy** (Standard, but ↓ efficacy due to resistance):

**PPI + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin × 14 days**
- PPI (Omeprazole 20 mg or equivalent) BID
- Clarithromycin 500 mg BID
- Amoxicillin 1 g BID

**Efficacy**: **70-85%** (↓ if clarithromycin resistance >15%)

**Use ONLY if**: Low clarithromycin resistance area (<15%) AND no prior macrolide exposure

**2. Bismuth Quadruple Therapy** (Preferred if high clarithromycin resistance):

**PPI + Bismuth + Tetracycline + Metronidazole × 10-14 days**
- PPI BID
- Bismuth subsalicylate 525 mg QID OR Bismuth subcitrate 120 mg QID
- Tetracycline 500 mg QID
- Metronidazole 500 mg TID (or 250 mg QID)

**Efficacy**: **90-95%** (High, NOT affected by clarithromycin resistance)

**Single-capsule formulation**: **Pylera** (Bismuth + Metronidazole + Tetracycline) - 3 capsules QID × 10 days + PPI

**3. Concomitant Therapy** (Alternative):

**PPI + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin + Metronidazole × 10-14 days**

**Efficacy**: **85-90%**

##### Second-Line Regimens (If first-line fails)

**Levofloxacin-Based Triple Therapy**:

**PPI + Levofloxacin + Amoxicillin × 10-14 days**
- PPI BID
- Levofloxacin 500 mg OD
- Amoxicillin 1 g BID

**Efficacy**: **75-90%**

**Alternative**: Bismuth Quadruple (if not used first-line)

##### Test-of-Cure

**ALWAYS perform** ≥4 weeks after treatment completion

**Method**: **Urea Breath Test** OR **Stool Antigen**

**If Still Positive** → Repeat treatment with different regimen + Check compliance

#### Acid Suppression (PPI)

**Duration**:

| Indication | Duration |
|------------|----------|
| **Duodenal ulcer** (uncomplicated) | **4 weeks** |
| **Gastric ulcer** (uncomplicated) | **8 weeks** |
| **Complicated ulcer** (bleeding, perforation) | **8-12 weeks** |
| **NSAID-associated** (if continuing NSAID) | **Chronic PPI** (as long as NSAID continued) |
| **Giant ulcer** (>2 cm) | **12 weeks** |

**Dose**: Standard (Omeprazole 20 mg OD or equivalent)

**Follow-up Endoscopy**:
- **Gastric ulcer**: **ALWAYS repeat at 8-12 weeks** (confirm healing, rule out malignancy)
- **Duodenal ulcer**: NOT needed (unless persistent symptoms)

#### NSAID Management

**If NSAID-induced ulcer**:

1. **Stop NSAID** (if possible) - **MOST IMPORTANT**
   - Switch to acetaminophen, topical NSAIDs
   - If pain control needed: COX-2 inhibitor (celecoxib) + PPI

2. **If NSAID must continue**:
   - **PPI** (standard dose) - **MOST EFFECTIVE prophylaxis**
   - Alternative: **Misoprostol** 200 mcg QID (prostaglandin analog) - Side effects: Diarrhea, cramping

**Prevention** (High-risk patients needing chronic NSAID):
- **High risk**: Age >65, prior PUD/GI bleed, concurrent steroids/anticoagulants
- **Strategy**: **COX-2 inhibitor + PPI** (best protection)

#### Refractory Ulcers

**Definition**: Ulcer not healed after 8-12 weeks of PPI

**Causes**:
- **Non-compliance** (MOST COMMON)
- **Persistent *H. pylori*** (inadequate eradication)
- **Continued NSAID use** (often surreptitious)
- **Smoking**
- **Zollinger-Ellison syndrome** (if multiple/refractory ulcers)
- **Gastric cancer** (if gastric ulcer)
- **Crohn's disease**

**Workup**:
- **Repeat endoscopy** with biopsy (rule out malignancy)
- **Test *H. pylori* status** (urea breath test or biopsy)
- **Assess compliance**, NSAID use, smoking
- **Fasting gastrin level** (if ZES suspected)

## HELICOBACTER PYLORI INFECTION

### Microbiology

- **Gram-negative**, spiral, flagellated bacterium
- **Urease-positive** (key for survival in acidic environment)

### Epidemiology

- **Global prevalence**: 50% (higher in developing countries ~70-90%, lower in developed ~20-30%)
- **Acquisition**: Childhood (fecal-oral, oral-oral transmission)
- **Persistence**: Lifelong if untreated

### Associated Diseases

| Disease | Association |
|---------|-------------|
| **Chronic gastritis** | **100%** of infected |
| **Duodenal ulcer** | **70%** have *H. pylori* |
| **Gastric ulcer** | **50%** have *H. pylori* |
| **Gastric adenocarcinoma** | **70-90%** have *H. pylori* (WHO Class I carcinogen) |
| **MALT lymphoma** | **>90%** have *H. pylori* (eradication can cure early-stage) |
| **Functional dyspepsia** | 50% (controversial benefit of eradication) |

### Diagnosis

See table above (Rapid urease, Histology, Urea breath test, Stool antigen, Serology)

### Treatment

See *H. pylori* eradication section above

### Indications for *H. pylori* Testing and Treatment

**Test and Treat**:
- **Peptic ulcer disease** (active or history)
- **Gastric MALT lymphoma**
- **Early gastric cancer** (post-resection)
- **Unexplained iron deficiency anemia**
- **Chronic idiopathic thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP)**
- **First-degree relatives** of gastric cancer patients
- **Uninvestigated dyspepsia** (age <60, no alarm features) - "Test and treat" strategy

**Do NOT Test** (No benefit):
- **Asymptomatic** individuals (unless high gastric cancer risk - family history)
- **GERD** (no clear benefit, may worsen symptoms in some)

## COMPLICATIONS

### 1. Upper GI Bleeding

**MOST COMMON complication** (15-20% of PUD)

See **GI Bleeding** chapter for detailed management

**Presentation**:
- **Hematemesis** (fresh blood or "coffee-ground")
- **Melena** (black, tarry stools)
- **Hematochezia** (bright red blood per rectum - if massive bleeding)

**Management**:
1. **Resuscitation**: IV fluids, blood transfusion (target Hgb 7-9 g/dL)
2. **PPI**: **High-dose IV** (Pantoprazole 80 mg bolus → 8 mg/hr infusion × 72 hours)
3. **Endoscopy**: Within **24 hours** (preferably 12-24 hours)
   - **Forrest Classification** (predicts rebleeding risk)
   - **Endoscopic therapy**: Injection (epinephrine), thermal (heater probe, APC), clips
4. **Post-endoscopy**: PPI BID × 2 weeks → Daily
5. ***H. pylori* eradication** (if positive)

**Forrest Classification**:

| Class | Description | Rebleeding Risk |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Ia** | Spurting arterial bleeding | **90%** |
| **Ib** | Oozing bleeding | **50%** |
| **IIa** | **Visible vessel** (non-bleeding) | **50%** |
| **IIb** | **Adherent clot** | **25-30%** |
| **IIc** | Flat pigmented spot | **10%** |
| **III** | Clean base (no stigmata) | **5%** |

**Endoscopic Therapy**: Ia, Ib, IIa, IIb

**Rebleeding**: 10-20% (risk factors: age >60, shock, Hgb <8, Forrest Ia/Ib/IIa)

**Angiography**: If rebleeding despite endoscopic therapy (embolization)

**Surgery**: If failed endoscopic + angiographic therapy (oversewing ulcer, vagotomy/pyloroplasty)

### 2. Perforation

**2nd most common complication** (5-10%)

**Presentation**:
- **Sudden, severe abdominal pain**
- **Peritoneal signs** (board-like rigidity, rebound tenderness)
- **Free air under diaphragm** on upright CXR (70-80%)

**Anterior duodenal ulcers** perforate more commonly (free perforation)
**Posterior duodenal ulcers** bleed more commonly (erode into gastroduodenal artery)

**Diagnosis**:
- **Upright CXR**: **Pneumoperitoneum** (free air under diaphragm)
- **CT abdomen**: More sensitive (if CXR negative)

**Management**:
1. **Resuscitation**: IV fluids, NPO, NG tube decompression
2. **Antibiotics**: Broad-spectrum (Pip-Tazo or Ceftriaxone + Metronidazole)
3. **Surgery**: **Definitive treatment**
   - **Graham patch** (omental patch closure)
   - Biopsy ulcer edge (rule out malignancy)
   - **Vagotomy** rarely done now (PPI era)
4. **Post-op**: **High-dose PPI** × 8-12 weeks, *H. pylori* eradication

**Non-operative management** (selected cases):
- Small perforation, hemodynamically stable, no peritonitis
- IV antibiotics, PPI, NPO
- Close monitoring (if deteriorates → surgery)

### 3. Gastric Outlet Obstruction (GOO)

**Rare** (<5%)

**Causes**:
- **Pyloric channel/duodenal ulcer** → Edema, scarring, fibrosis
- Gastric cancer (must rule out)

**Presentation**:
- **Nausea, vomiting** (large volume, food from prior day)
- **Early satiety**, bloating
- **Weight loss**
- **Succussion splash** (sloshing sound on shaking abdomen)

**Diagnosis**:
- **Endoscopy**: Narrowing at pylorus/duodenum, cannot pass scope
- **Barium study**: Dilated stomach, delayed gastric emptying
- **Saline load test**: Give 750 mL saline via NG → Aspirate after 30 min → >400 mL retained = GOO

**Management**:
1. **Acute edema** (recent ulcer):
   - **NG decompression**
   - **High-dose PPI** IV (Pantoprazole 80 mg BID)
   - **Hydration, Electrolyte correction** (hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis)
   - **Trial 72-96 hours** → If improves, continue medical Rx
2. **Chronic scarring** (fibrosis):
   - **Endoscopic balloon dilation** (may need repeated)
   - **Surgery**: Vagotomy + Antrectomy (with Billroth I or II), or Vagotomy + Gastrojejunostomy

**Complications** (Post-surgery):
- **Dumping syndrome**: Rapid gastric emptying → Hypoglycemia, diarrhea
- **Afferent loop syndrome**: Bile reflux
- **Alkaline reflux gastritis**

### 4. Penetration

**Ulcer erodes into adjacent organ** (pancreas most common)

**Presentation**:
- **Change in pain pattern**: Constant, radiates to back (pancreatic penetration)
- **↑ Amylase/Lipase** (pancreatitis)

**Diagnosis**: CT/MRI (ulcer extending beyond gastric/duodenal wall)

**Management**: High-dose PPI; Surgery if medical failure

## ZOLLINGER-ELLISON SYNDROME

### Definition

**Gastrinoma**: Gastrin-secreting tumor (usually in **gastrinoma triangle**: Duodenum > Pancreas > Lymph nodes)

**Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome (ZES)**: Gastrinoma → **Hypergastrinemia** → **↑↑ Gastric acid** → **Severe/multiple PUD**, diarrhea

### Epidemiology

- **Rare**: <1% of PUD cases
- **Age**: 30-50 years
- **MEN-1**: 20-30% have gastrinoma

### Clinical Features

**Classic Triad** (not always present):
1. **Severe, refractory, or recurrent peptic ulcers**
2. **Diarrhea** (50-70%) - Acid hypersecretion → Fat malabsorption
3. **Gastrin-secreting tumor**

**Suggestive Features**:
- **Multiple ulcers** (especially **distal duodenum, jejunum** - unusual locations)
- **Giant ulcers** (>2 cm)
- **Refractory to standard PPI therapy**
- **Ulcers + Diarrhea**
- **Family history of MEN-1** (hyperparathyroidism, pituitary adenoma, pancreatic tumor)

### Diagnosis

#### 1. Fasting Serum Gastrin

**When to Test**:
- Refractory/recurrent ulcers
- Multiple ulcers
- Ulcer + Diarrhea
- Family history of MEN-1

**Procedure**: **STOP PPI ≥1 week** before test (PPIs ↑ gastrin)

**Interpretation**:

| Gastrin Level | Interpretation |
|---------------|----------------|
| <100 pg/mL | Normal |
| 100-500 pg/mL | Indeterminate (many causes) |
| **>1000 pg/mL** + **Gastric pH <2** | **Highly suggestive of ZES** |

**Other Causes of Hypergastrinemia**:
- **PPI use** (MOST COMMON) - Stop PPI × 1 week
- **Atrophic gastritis** (achlorhydria → ↑ gastrin, but pH >4)
- **H. pylori** gastritis
- Chronic kidney disease
- Retained gastric antrum (post-surgery)

**Key**: **Gastrin >1000** + **Gastric pH <2** (↑ acid) → ZES

#### 2. Gastric pH

**Measure during endoscopy**

**ZES**: pH **<2** (hyperacidity)
**Atrophic gastritis**: pH **>4** (achlorhydria)

#### 3. Secretin Stimulation Test

**GOLD STANDARD** (if gastrin 100-1000, diagnosis unclear)

**Procedure**:
- Give **secretin 2 units/kg IV**
- Measure gastrin at 0, 2, 5, 10, 15, 20 minutes

**Interpretation**:
- **↑ Gastrin >200 pg/mL above baseline** → **ZES**
- **No change or ↓ gastrin** → Not ZES

**Mechanism**: Secretin stimulates gastrin release in gastrinoma, but inhibits in normal tissue

#### 4. Localization (Once diagnosis confirmed)

**Imaging**:
- **CT/MRI abdomen**: Detect tumor (sensitivity 50-70%)
- **Endoscopic ultrasound (EUS)**: More sensitive for small tumors
- **Somatostatin receptor scintigraphy** (Octreotide scan): Detect primary and metastases (sensitivity 80-90%)
- **Selective arterial secretin injection** (SASI): Provocative test + angiography

### Treatment

#### Medical (Acid Suppression)

**High-dose PPI**:
- **Omeprazole 60-120 mg/day** (divided doses)
- Goal: Basal acid output <10 mEq/hr (<5 mEq/hr if prior gastric surgery)

**Lifelong therapy** if tumor unresectable

#### Surgical

**Curative**: If **localized**, **non-metastatic**

**Procedure**: **Tumor resection** (enucleation or pancreaticoduodenectomy)

**MEN-1 patients**: Often multiple tumors → Surgery more complex, may not be curative

**Metastatic**: **Chemotherapy** (streptozocin + 5-FU), **Somatostatin analogs** (octreotide) - ↓ Gastrin, ↓ Tumor growth

### Prognosis

- **Survival**: 90% at 5 years (if localized), 50% at 5 years (if metastatic)
- **Malignant**: 60-90% (slow-growing)
- **Metastases**: Liver (most common)

## NEET PG HIGH-YIELD POINTS

### Must-Know Facts

#### GERD

1. **MOST COMMON Cause**: **Transient LES relaxations (TLESRs)** (60-70%)

2. **Symptoms**:
   - **Heartburn** (MOST COMMON) - Burning retrosternal, worse after meals/lying down
   - **Regurgitation** - Effortless return of gastric contents

3. **Diagnosis**:
   - **Empiric PPI trial** (no endoscopy if <50 years, no alarm features)
   - **Endoscopy**: If alarm features (dysphagia, weight loss, age ≥50, bleeding)

4. **Treatment**:
   - **First-line**: **PPI** (Omeprazole 20 mg OD × 4-8 weeks)
   - **Lifestyle**: Elevate head of bed, weight loss, avoid triggers
   - **Surgery (Fundoplication)**: If refractory or patient preference

5. **Barrett's Esophagus**:
   - **Squamous → Intestinal metaplasia** (goblet cells on biopsy)
   - **Cancer risk**: 0.5% per year → Esophageal adenocarcinoma
   - **Surveillance**: No dysplasia (q3-5 years), LGD (q6-12 months), HGD (ablation or q3 months)

#### Peptic Ulcer Disease

6. **MOST COMMON Cause**: ***H. pylori*** (50-70% DU, 30-50% GU)

7. **Duodenal vs Gastric Ulcer**:

| Feature | Duodenal Ulcer | Gastric Ulcer |
|---------|----------------|---------------|
| **Pain timing** | **2-5 hours AFTER meals** | **Within 1 hour** |
| **Relief** | **Food, Antacids** | Variable |
| **Weight** | Unchanged | **↓ Weight loss** |
| **Malignancy** | **Rare** (no biopsy needed) | **5% malignant** (ALWAYS biopsy) |

8. **H. pylori Testing**:
   - **Invasive**: Rapid urease test (RUT), Histology (GOLD STANDARD)
   - **Non-invasive**: **Urea breath test** (BEST non-invasive), Stool antigen
   - **NOT for test-of-cure**: Serology (IgG stays positive after eradication)
   - **Test-of-cure**: ≥4 weeks after treatment (Urea breath test or Stool antigen)

9. **H. pylori Eradication**:
   - **First-line**: **Bismuth Quadruple** (PPI + Bismuth + Tetracycline + Metronidazole × 10-14 days) - 90-95% efficacy
   - **Alternative**: Clarithromycin triple (if low resistance area) - 70-85% efficacy
   - **Test-of-cure**: **ALWAYS** (≥4 weeks after treatment)

10. **PPI Duration**:
    - **DU**: 4 weeks
    - **GU**: 8 weeks (+ **repeat endoscopy** to confirm healing, rule out malignancy)

11. **Complications**:
    - **Bleeding** (MOST COMMON, 15-20%): Hematemesis, melena
      - **Forrest IIa (visible vessel)**: 50% rebleeding risk → Endoscopic therapy
    - **Perforation**: **Free air under diaphragm** on upright CXR → Surgery (Graham patch)
    - **GOO**: Succussion splash, vomiting food from prior day

#### Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome

12. **Triad** (not always present):
    - Severe/multiple/refractory ulcers
    - **Diarrhea**
    - Gastrinoma

13. **Diagnosis**:
    - **Fasting gastrin >1000 pg/mL** + **Gastric pH <2** → Highly suggestive
    - **Secretin stimulation test**: ↑ Gastrin >200 pg/mL (GOLD STANDARD)

14. **Treatment**: **High-dose PPI** (Omeprazole 60-120 mg/day), Surgery if localized

### Comparison Tables

#### GERD vs PUD

| Feature | GERD | PUD |
|---------|------|-----|
| **Primary symptom** | **Heartburn** (retrosternal burning) | **Epigastric pain** |
| **Timing** | Postprandial, lying down | DU: 2-5 hrs post-meal; GU: 0-1 hr |
| **Relief** | Antacids, sitting up | DU: Food; GU: Variable |
| **Diagnosis** | Empiric PPI trial, Endoscopy if alarm features | **Endoscopy** (GOLD STANDARD) |
| **Treatment** | **PPI** × 4-8 weeks | PPI × 4-8 weeks + **H. pylori eradication** |

#### Duodenal vs Gastric Ulcer

| Feature | Duodenal Ulcer | Gastric Ulcer |
|---------|----------------|---------------|
| **Frequency** | **70%** (MOST COMMON) | 25% |
| **H. pylori** association | **70%** | 50% |
| **Pain timing** | **2-5 hours after meals**, **Nocturnal** | **Within 1 hour** after meals |
| **Relief** | **Food, Antacids** | Variable (may worsen) |
| **Weight** | Unchanged or ↑ | **↓ Weight loss** |
| **Location** | **D1 (bulb)** | **Lesser curvature** |
| **Biopsy** | **NOT needed** (malignancy rare) | **ALWAYS biopsy** (5% malignant) |
| **Repeat endoscopy** | NOT needed | **ALWAYS at 8-12 weeks** (confirm healing) |

#### H. pylori Tests

| Test | Type | Sensitivity | Specificity | Use | Notes |
|------|------|-------------|-------------|-----|-------|
| **Rapid Urease (RUT)** | Invasive | 90-95% | 95-100% | During endoscopy | Fast, cheap |
| **Histology** | Invasive | 95-99% | **95-99%** | **GOLD STANDARD** | Requires biopsy |
| **Urea Breath Test** | Non-invasive | 95-98% | 95-98% | **Best non-invasive**, Test-of-cure | Stop PPI × 2 weeks |
| **Stool Antigen** | Non-invasive | 90-95% | 90-95% | Alternative to UBT | |
| **Serology (IgG)** | Non-invasive | 85-92% | 79-90% | **NOT for test-of-cure** | Stays + after eradication |

### High-Yield Drugs

#### PPI

| Drug | Dose | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| **Omeprazole** | 20-40 mg OD | First PPI |
| **Esomeprazole** | 20-40 mg OD | S-isomer |
| **Pantoprazole** | 40 mg OD | IV available (bleeding) |

**Dosing**: **30 minutes before breakfast**

**Side Effects** (long-term):
- **↑ Fracture risk** (hip, spine), **Hypomagnesemia**, **C. diff** infection
- **Contraindication**: Clopidogrel (especially omeprazole/esomeprazole)

### Important Cutoffs

| Parameter | Cutoff | Significance |
|-----------|--------|--------------|
| **Gastrin** | >1000 pg/mL + pH <2 | Highly suggestive of ZES |
| **Secretin stimulation** | ↑ Gastrin >200 pg/mL | ZES (GOLD STANDARD test) |
| **Barrett's progression** | 0.5% per year | Risk of adenocarcinoma |
| **Forrest IIa** | 50% rebleeding | Endoscopic therapy needed |

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Not biopsying gastric ulcers** ❌
   - **ALWAYS biopsy** gastric ulcers (5% malignant)
   - **Repeat endoscopy** at 8-12 weeks (confirm healing)

2. **Using serology for test-of-cure** ❌
   - Serology stays positive after eradication
   - Use **Urea breath test** or **Stool antigen** (≥4 weeks after Rx)

3. **Not stopping PPI before H. pylori testing** ❌
   - **False negative** if on PPI
   - Stop **2 weeks** before (except serology)

4. **Short antibiotic duration for H. pylori** ❌
   - Need **10-14 days** (NOT 7 days)

5. **Not checking gastric pH in hypergastrinemia workup** ❌
   - **ZES**: Gastrin ↑ + pH <2 (↑ acid)
   - **Atrophic gastritis**: Gastrin ↑ + pH >4 (↓ acid)

6. **Starting PPI in young GERD patient without trial** ❌
   - Empiric PPI trial is **diagnostic AND therapeutic** (no endoscopy if <50, no alarm features)

### Mnemonics

1. **"HANZ"** - PUD Causes:
   - **H** - *H. pylori*
   - **A** - Aspirin/NSAIDs
   - **N** - ↑ (N) acid states
   - **Z** - Zollinger-Ellison

2. **"Triple Therapy"** - H. pylori (Clarithromycin-based):
   - **P**PI + **C**larithromycin + **A**moxicillin ("**PCA**")

3. **"Quadruple Therapy"** - H. pylori (Bismuth-based):
   - **P**PI + **B**ismuth + **T**etracycline + **M**etronidazole ("**PBTM**")

4. **Forrest Classification** (Bleeding ulcer):
   - **I**: Active bleeding (Ia = spurting, Ib = oozing)
   - **II**: Recent bleeding (IIa = vessel, IIb = clot, IIc = spot)
   - **III**: Clean base

### Question 1
A 35-year-old man with epigastric pain has endoscopy showing a duodenal ulcer. Biopsy is positive for *H. pylori*. What is the most appropriate treatment?

A. PPI alone for 4 weeks
B. PPI + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin for 14 days
C. PPI + H2RA for 8 weeks
D. Surgery (vagotomy)

**Answer**: **B. PPI + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin for 14 days**

**Explanation**:
- **Diagnosis**: *H. pylori*-positive duodenal ulcer

**Treatment**:
- **H. pylori eradication** + **Acid suppression**

**H. pylori Eradication Regimens**:
1. **Clarithromycin Triple Therapy** (if low resistance):
   - **PPI + Clarithromycin + Amoxicillin × 14 days** ✓
2. **Bismuth Quadruple** (preferred if high resistance):
   - PPI + Bismuth + Tetracycline + Metronidazole × 10-14 days

**This Patient**: First-line treatment → Triple or Quadruple therapy acceptable (option B is correct)

**Why NOT Other Options**:
- **PPI alone** (A): Does NOT eradicate *H. pylori* → Ulcer recurrence 60-70%
- **PPI + H2RA** (C): No antibiotics for *H. pylori*
- **Surgery** (D): NOT indicated (medical therapy is effective)

**Additional**:
- **PPI duration after eradication**: Continue × 4 weeks (DU) or 8 weeks (GU)
- **Test-of-cure**: Urea breath test ≥4 weeks after treatment

**Key Point**: *H. pylori*-positive PUD → Eradication (antibiotics) + PPI

### Question 2
A 60-year-old man on chronic NSAIDs for arthritis develops a gastric ulcer. He needs to continue NSAIDs. After ulcer healing, what is the best strategy to prevent recurrence?

A. PPI alone
B. H2RA alone
C. COX-2 inhibitor + PPI
D. Stop NSAIDs permanently

**Answer**: **C. COX-2 inhibitor + PPI**

**Explanation**:
- **Problem**: High-risk patient (age >60, prior ulcer) needing chronic NSAIDs

**Prevention Strategy**:

| Option | GI Risk Reduction |
|--------|-------------------|
| COX-2 inhibitor alone | 50% (vs non-selective NSAID) |
| PPI alone (with non-selective NSAID) | 50-70% |
| **COX-2 inhibitor + PPI** | **>80%** (BEST) |

**This Patient**: **COX-2 inhibitor (Celecoxib) + PPI** → **Maximum protection**

**Alternatives** (if COX-2 not available):
- **Non-selective NSAID + PPI** (next best)
- **Misoprostol 200 mcg QID** (effective but poorly tolerated - diarrhea)

**Why NOT Other Options**:
- **PPI alone** (A): Good, but **combination better** (especially high-risk)
- **H2RA** (B): **Inferior to PPI** for ulcer prevention
- **Stop NSAIDs** (D): Ideal, but patient needs NSAIDs for arthritis

**High-Risk Factors**:
- Age >65
- **Prior PUD or GI bleed**
- Concurrent steroids, anticoagulants, SSRIs
- High-dose or multiple NSAIDs

**Key Point**: High-risk patient needing NSAIDs → COX-2 inhibitor + PPI (best protection)

### Question 3
A patient with GERD has failed 8 weeks of PPI therapy. What is the most appropriate next step?

A. Switch to a different PPI
B. Increase PPI to BID dosing
C. Add H2RA at bedtime
D. Repeat endoscopy

**Answer**: **B. Increase PPI to BID dosing**

**Explanation**:
- **PPI-Refractory GERD** (15-30% of patients)

**Stepwise Approach**:

1. **Optimize current PPI**:
   - **Take 30 min before meals** (not at bedtime)
   - **Ensure compliance**
   - **Increase to BID dosing** (before breakfast and dinner) ✓

2. **Switch PPI** (if no response to BID):
   - Different PPI may work (individual variation in metabolism)

3. **Add H2RA at bedtime**:
   - For **nocturnal acid breakthrough**
   - (Note: Tachyphylaxis develops in 2-4 weeks)

4. **Re-evaluate diagnosis**:
   - **24-hour pH monitoring** (confirm acid reflux)
   - **Endoscopy** (if not done, or rule out complications)
   - **Manometry** (if dysphagia, rule out achalasia/motility disorder)

**This Patient**: First step → **Increase PPI to BID** (before switching or adding drugs)

**Why NOT D (Endoscopy) First**:
- If GERD confirmed and no alarm features → Optimize medical therapy first
- Endoscopy if: Alarm features, diagnostic uncertainty, or persistent symptoms despite BID PPI

**Other Causes of PPI-Refractory Symptoms**:
- **Non-compliance** (MOST COMMON)
- **Functional heartburn** (no acid reflux on pH monitoring)
- **Eosinophilic esophagitis** (dysphagia, food impaction, normal pH monitoring)
- **Gastroparesis** (delayed gastric emptying)

**Key Point**: PPI-refractory GERD → First step: Optimize (BID dosing), then consider alternatives

### Question 5
A patient with *H. pylori*-positive duodenal ulcer completed eradication therapy 6 weeks ago. Which test should be used to confirm eradication?

A. Serology (IgG antibody)
B. Urea breath test
C. Rapid urease test (endoscopic biopsy)
D. Stool antigen test

**Answer**: **B. Urea breath test** (or D. Stool antigen test - both acceptable)

**Explanation**:
- **Test-of-Cure**: Confirm *H. pylori* eradication **≥4 weeks after treatment**

**Which Test**:

| Test | Use for Test-of-Cure? | Reason |
|------|------------------------|--------|
| **Urea Breath Test** | **YES** (BEST non-invasive) | Detects active infection |

| **Serology (IgG)** | **NO** | Stays positive after eradication (takes months-years to ↓) |
| **Rapid Urease (RUT)** | **YES**, but invasive | Requires endoscopy (NOT needed if asymptomatic) |

**This Patient**:
- **Urea breath test** OR **Stool antigen** (both non-invasive, detect active infection)
- **Preferred**: **Urea breath test** (slightly better sensitivity/specificity)

**Timing**: **≥4 weeks** after completion of antibiotics (and ≥2 weeks off PPI)

**Why NOT Serology (A)**:
- **IgG antibody** remains positive for **months to years** after eradication
- **Cannot distinguish** active infection from past infection

**Indications for Test-of-Cure**:
- **ALL patients** (especially if bleeding ulcer, persistent symptoms, or first eradication attempt)

**If Still Positive**:
- **Repeat treatment** with **different regimen** (e.g., if used triple, switch to quadruple)
- Check **compliance** (did patient take full course?)

**Key Point**: Test-of-cure → Urea breath test or Stool antigen (NOT serology) ≥4 weeks after Rx

### Question 8
A 50-year-old man with long-standing GERD (15 years) has endoscopy showing salmon-pink mucosa extending 3 cm above the gastroesophageal junction. Biopsy shows intestinal metaplasia with no dysplasia. What is the most appropriate surveillance interval?

A. No surveillance needed
B. Repeat endoscopy in 1 year
C. Repeat endoscopy in 3-5 years
D. Repeat endoscopy in 6 months

**Answer**: **C. Repeat endoscopy in 3-5 years**

**Explanation**:
- **Diagnosis**: **Barrett's Esophagus** (intestinal metaplasia on biopsy, **NO dysplasia**)

**Barrett's Esophagus**:
- Replacement of squamous epithelium with **intestinal metaplasia** (goblet cells)
- **Risk**: **0.5% per year** progression to esophageal adenocarcinoma

**Surveillance**:

| Dysplasia Grade | Surveillance Interval |
|-----------------|----------------------|
| **No dysplasia** | Every **3-5 years** ✓ |
| **Low-grade dysplasia (LGD)** | Every **6-12 months** (or ablation) |
| **High-grade dysplasia (HGD)** | **Endoscopic ablation** (RFA) OR Every 3 months |

**This Patient**: **No dysplasia** → **Surveillance every 3-5 years**

**Biopsy Protocol**: **Seattle Protocol** (4-quadrant biopsies every 1-2 cm)

**Treatment**:
- **PPI therapy** (high-dose) - Does NOT reverse Barrett's, but may ↓ progression
- **Surveillance** (as above)
- **HGD**: **Radiofrequency ablation (RFA)** ± Endoscopic mucosal resection (EMR)

**Key Point**: Barrett's esophagus with NO dysplasia → Surveillance every 3-5 years

### Question 9
A patient with bleeding duodenal ulcer undergoes endoscopy showing a visible vessel (Forrest IIa). After endoscopic therapy, what is the estimated rebleeding risk?

A. 5%
B. 25%
C. 50%
D. 90%

**Answer**: **C. 50%**

**Explanation**:
- **Forrest Classification** (Predicts rebleeding risk)

**Forrest IIa**: **Visible vessel** (non-bleeding, protuberant vessel in ulcer base)

**Rebleeding Risk**: **50%** (HIGH) ✓

**Forrest Classification**:

| Class | Description | Rebleeding Risk | Endoscopic Therapy? |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **Ia** | **Spurting** arterial bleeding | **90%** | **YES** |
| **Ib** | **Oozing** bleeding | **50%** | **YES** |
| **IIa** | **Visible vessel** (non-bleeding) | **50%** | **YES** ✓ |
| **IIb** | **Adherent clot** | **25-30%** | **YES** |
| **IIc** | Flat pigmented spot | **10%** | NO (low risk) |
| **III** | Clean base | **5%** | NO (low risk) |

**Endoscopic Therapy**:
- **Indications**: Forrest **Ia, Ib, IIa, IIb** (active bleeding or high-risk stigmata)
- **Methods**:
  - **Injection**: Epinephrine (1:10,000)
  - **Thermal**: Heater probe, Argon plasma coagulation (APC), Bipolar cautery
  - **Mechanical**: Clips
  - **Combination**: Epinephrine + Thermal/Clips (most effective)

**Management** (Forrest IIa):
1. **Endoscopic therapy** (injection + thermal/clips)
2. **High-dose PPI IV**: Pantoprazole 80 mg bolus → 8 mg/hr infusion × 72 hours
3. **PPI BID** (after IV) × 2 weeks
4. ***H. pylori* eradication** (if positive)

**Key Point**: Forrest IIa (visible vessel) → 50% rebleeding risk → Endoscopic therapy needed

### Question 10
Which of the following is an indication for endoscopy in a patient with GERD?

A. Heartburn for 2 weeks
B. Age 30 years with typical symptoms
C. Progressive dysphagia
D. Symptoms responsive to PPI

**Answer**: **C. Progressive dysphagia**

**Explanation**:
- **Alarm Features** → **Endoscopy indicated** (rule out complications, malignancy)

**Alarm Features** ("RED FLAGS"):
- **Dysphagia** (especially **progressive**) ✓ - Suggests stricture or malignancy
- **Odynophagia** (painful swallowing)
- **Unintentional weight loss** (>5% body weight)
- **GI bleeding** (hematemesis, melena, anemia)
- **Persistent vomiting**
- **Age ≥50 years** with **new-onset** GERD

**This Patient**: **Progressive dysphagia** → **Endoscopy MANDATORY** (rule out stricture, cancer)

**When Endoscopy NOT Needed** (Empiric PPI trial):
- **Age <50 years**
- **NO alarm features**
- **Typical symptoms** (heartburn, regurgitation)

**Why NOT Other Options**:
- **Heartburn × 2 weeks** (A): Short duration, trial PPI first
- **Age 30 with typical symptoms** (B): Empiric PPI trial (no endoscopy needed)
- **Responsive to PPI** (D): Good response confirms GERD (no endoscopy needed unless long-standing GERD >5-10 years → screen for Barrett's)

**Long-Standing GERD (>5-10 years)**: Consider endoscopy to screen for **Barrett's esophagus** (even without alarm features)

**Key Point**: GERD with alarm features (dysphagia, weight loss, bleeding, age ≥50) → Endoscopy

## SUMMARY

**GERD** (Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease):
- **Mechanism**: Transient LES relaxations (TLESRs) - MOST COMMON
- **Symptoms**: Heartburn (postprandial, lying down), Regurgitation
- **Diagnosis**: Empiric PPI trial (if <50, no alarm features); Endoscopy if alarm features
- **Treatment**: **PPI** (Omeprazole 20 mg OD × 4-8 weeks), Lifestyle modifications
- **Complication**: **Barrett's esophagus** (0.5%/year → adenocarcinoma) → Surveillance

**Peptic Ulcer Disease** (PUD):
- **Causes**: ***H. pylori*** (50-70% DU, 30-50% GU) - MOST COMMON, NSAIDs (20-30%)
- **DU vs GU**: DU pain 2-5 hrs post-meal (food relieves), GU pain within 1 hr (food worsens, weight loss)
- **Diagnosis**: **Endoscopy** (GOLD STANDARD), *H. pylori* testing (RUT, Histology, UBT)
- **Treatment**:
  - ***H. pylori* eradication**: **Bismuth Quadruple** (90-95%) or Clarithromycin Triple (70-85%)
  - **PPI** × 4 weeks (DU) or 8 weeks (GU)
  - **ALWAYS biopsy gastric ulcers** (5% malignant), Repeat endoscopy at 8-12 weeks
- **Complications**: Bleeding (Forrest IIa = 50% rebleeding), Perforation (free air), GOO

**Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome** (ZES):
- **Gastrinoma** → ↑ Gastrin → ↑ Acid → Multiple/refractory ulcers + Diarrhea
- **Diagnosis**: Gastrin >1000 + pH <2 → Secretin stimulation test (GOLD STANDARD)
- **Treatment**: **High-dose PPI** (Omeprazole 60-120 mg/day), Surgery if localized
