    POSTPARTUM HEMORRHAGE (PPH) - NEET PG Study Material¶

    Definition¶

    Primary PPH: Blood loss ≥500 mL (vaginal delivery) or ≥1000 mL (cesarean section) within 24 hours of delivery

    Secondary PPH: Excessive bleeding >24 hours to 12 weeks postpartum

    Severe PPH: Blood loss ≥1000 mL OR signs of hemodynamic instability

    NEET PG: PPH = Blood loss ≥500 mL within 24 hours (vaginal delivery)

    Epidemiology¶

    • Incidence: 5-10% of deliveries
    • Leading cause of maternal mortality worldwide (25% of maternal deaths)
    • Most common in first 24 hours (primary PPH)

    Etiology - "4 Ts"¶

    Cause Percentage Mnemonic
    **T**one (Uterine atony) 70% MOST COMMON
    **T**rauma (Genital tract) 20%
    **T**issue (Retained products) 10%
    **T**hrombin (Coagulopathy) 1%

    NEET PG: Uterine atony = Most common cause of PPH (70%)

    1. UTERINE ATONY (Tone) - 70%¶

    Definition¶

    Failure of uterus to contract after delivery of placenta → Bleeding from placental site

    Risk Factors - "ATONY"¶

    **A**bruptio placentae, Anemia **T**wins/Multiple gestation **O**verdistended uterus (Polyhydramnios, Macrosomia) **N**ultiparous (Grand multiparity >5) **Y**oung maternal age, prolonged **Y**ears (labor)

    Others: - Prolonged labor (>12 hours) - Precipitous labor (<3 hours) - Chorioamnionitis - Uterine fibroids - Previous PPH - Magnesium sulfate (tocolytic) - General anesthesia - Halogenated agents (relax uterus)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Grand multiparity: Uterine muscle fatigue - Overdistended uterus: Twins, polyhydramnios, macrosomia - Prolonged labor: Uterine exhaustion

    Clinical Features¶

    • Uterus: Soft, boggy, poorly contracted
    • Continuous bleeding (no clots initially)
    • Per vaginal bleeding with blood clots

    Diagnosis¶

    • Clinical: Soft, non-contracted uterus
    • Bimanual examination: Uterus does not contract with massage

    2. TRAUMA (Genital Tract Injuries) - 20%¶

    Types¶

    A. Cervical Laceration - Cause: Rapid/precipitous delivery, forceps, large baby - Signs: Bright red bleeding despite contracted uterus - Diagnosis: Speculum examination (visualize cervix) - Treatment: Suturing under anesthesia

    B. Vaginal Laceration - Cause: Operative delivery (forceps, vacuum) - Types: - Sulcus tears: Lateral vaginal wall - Periurethral tears - Treatment: Suturing

    C. Perineal Tears (see classification below)

    D. Uterine Rupture - Cause: Obstructed labor, previous cesarean scar, trauma - Signs: Sudden severe pain, shock, fetal distress - Diagnosis: Clinical + imaging - Treatment: Emergency laparotomy, repair/hysterectomy

    E. Uterine Inversion - Definition: Uterus turns inside-out - Cause: Excessive cord traction, fundal pressure - Signs: Sudden shock, mass at introitus, severe pain - Treatment: Immediate manual replacement (Johnson's maneuver) + tocolysis

    NEET PG: Bleeding despite contracted uterus = Genital tract trauma (do speculum exam)

    Perineal Tear Classification¶

    Degree Involvement Management
    First Vaginal mucosa, perineal skin May not need suturing (minor)
    Second Perineal muscles (NOT anal sphincter) Suture in layers
    Third Anal sphincter involved Repair in OR by experienced surgeon
    Fourth Anal sphincter + Rectal mucosa Repair in OR, broad-spectrum antibiotics

    NEET PG: 3rd & 4th degree tears involve anal sphincter (repair in OR)

    3. TISSUE (Retained Products) - 10%¶

    Causes¶

    A. Retained Placenta - Definition: Placenta not delivered within 30 minutes of baby - Types: - Adherent placenta: No plane of cleavage - Trapped placenta: Cervix closes before delivery - Placenta accreta/increta/percreta: Abnormal placental invasion

    B. Retained Placental Fragments - Incomplete placental delivery - Cotyledon missing on inspection

    C. Retained Blood Clots - Large clots prevent uterine contraction

    D. Retained Membranes

    Clinical Features¶

    • Boggy uterus despite massage
    • Intermittent bleeding

    Diagnosis¶

    • Placental inspection: Missing cotyledon/vessels going to edge
    • Bimanual examination: Uterus larger than expected
    • Ultrasound: Echogenic material in uterine cavity

    Management¶

    • Manual removal of placenta (under anesthesia)
    • Uterine curettage (if fragments)
    • Oxytocics

    NEET PG: Retained placenta = Not delivered within 30 minutes → Manual removal

    Abnormal Placentation¶

    Type Definition Incidence
    Placenta accreta Adherent to myometrium (no decidua basalis) 80%
    Placenta increta Invades into myometrium 15%
    Placenta percreta Penetrates through serosa (bladder, bowel) 5%

    Risk Factors: - Previous cesarean section (MOST IMPORTANT) - Placenta previa - Multiparity - Previous uterine surgery

    Diagnosis: - Antenatal: USG, MRI (loss of retroplacental clear zone) - Delivery: Placenta does not separate, bleeding

    Management: - Cesarean hysterectomy (placenta left in situ) - Avoid forceful placental removal (massive hemorrhage)

    NEET PG: Placenta accreta = Previous cesarean + Placenta previa (high risk)

    4. THROMBIN (Coagulopathy) - 1%¶

    Causes¶

    Pre-existing: - Von Willebrand disease - Hemophilia carrier - ITP - Anticoagulant therapy

    Acquired: - DIC (most common in obstetrics) - HELLP syndrome - Massive transfusion (dilutional coagulopathy)

    DIC in Obstetrics - Causes¶

    "ASAP" - **A**bruption (placental abruption) - MOST COMMON - **S**epsis - **A**mniotic fluid embolism - **P**re-eclampsia/HELLP

    Clinical Features: - Generalized oozing (IV sites, gums) - No clot formation - Petechiae, ecchymoses

    Diagnosis: - ↑ PT, ↑ aPTT - ↓ Platelets (<100,000) - ↓ Fibrinogen (<200 mg/dL) - ↑ D-dimer, ↑ FDP

    Management: - Treat underlying cause - FFP (fresh frozen plasma) - Cryoprecipitate (if fibrinogen <100 mg/dL) - Platelet transfusion (if <50,000)

    NEET PG: Placental abruption = Most common obstetric cause of DIC

    Clinical Assessment¶

    Signs of Shock¶

    • Tachycardia (>100 bpm)
    • Hypotension (SBP <90 mmHg)
    • Tachypnea
    • Oliguria (<30 mL/hr)
    • Altered consciousness
    • Cold, clammy skin

    Blood Loss Estimation (often underestimated)¶

    Blood Loss Clinical Features
    500-1000 mL Tachycardia (100-110), normal BP
    1000-1500 mL Tachycardia (110-120), mild hypotension, anxiety
    1500-2000 mL Tachycardia (>120), hypotension, oliguria, confusion
    >2000 mL Severe shock, collapse, anuria

    NEET PG: Tachycardia is earliest sign of hypovolemia

    Prevention of PPH (ALL DELIVERIES)¶

    Active Management of Third Stage of Labor (AMTSL)¶

    Components (after delivery of baby): 1. Oxytocin 10 units IM/IV immediately after anterior shoulder 2. Controlled cord traction (after signs of placental separation) 3. Uterine massage after placental delivery

    Benefits: ↓ PPH by 60%, ↓ severe PPH, ↓ need for transfusion

    NEET PG: AMTSL = Oxytocin + Controlled cord traction + Uterine massage (reduces PPH)

    Management of PPH¶

    Initial Resuscitation (ABCD)¶

    A**irway: Maintain patency **B**reathing: O2 15 L/min **C**irculation: - **2 large-bore IV cannulae (14-16G) - Rapid IV fluids (crystalloids: NS/RL) - Blood sampling: CBC, coagulation profile, cross-match **D**rugs: Uterotonics

    Identify & Treat Cause ("4 Ts")¶

    1. TONE (Uterine Atony) - MOST COMMON¶

    Step-by-step approach:

    Step 1: Uterine Massage - Bimanual uterine compression - Rub fundus to stimulate contraction

    Step 2: Uterotonics (in order of use)

    A. Oxytocin (First-line): - 10 units IM/IV bolus - 20-40 units in 1L NS IV infusion - Mechanism: Contracts uterus - Side effects: Hypotension (if rapid IV), water intoxication

    B. Methylergometrine (Second-line): - 0.2 mg IM (can repeat after 15 min) - Mechanism: Sustained uterine contraction - Contraindications: Hypertension, pre-eclampsia, heart disease - Side effects: HTN, nausea, vomiting

    C. Carboprost (15-methyl PGF2α) (Third-line): - 250 μg IM (can repeat every 15 min, max 8 doses) - Mechanism: Uterine contraction - Contraindications: Asthma (bronchospasm) - Side effects: Diarrhea, fever, bronchospasm

    D. Misoprostol (PGE1): - 800-1000 μg (PR/sublingual) - Mechanism: Uterine contraction - Advantage: Stable at room temperature, cheap - Side effects: Fever, shivering

    NEET PG High-Yield: - First-line: Oxytocin - Methylergometrine: Contraindicated in hypertension - Carboprost: Contraindicated in asthma

    Step 3: Uterine Tamponade - Intrauterine balloon (Bakri balloon, Foley catheter) - Fill with 300-500 mL saline - Provides tamponade effect

    Step 4: Conservative Surgical

    Uterine compression sutures: - B-Lynch suture: Brace suture (most common) - Cho suture

    Pelvic vessel ligation: - Uterine artery ligation - Internal iliac (hypogastric) artery ligation

    Step 5: Radiological - Uterine artery embolization (if available, hemodynamically stable)

    Step 6: Hysterectomy (Last Resort) - Subtotal hysterectomy preferred (faster, less blood loss) - Total hysterectomy if cervical involvement

    NEET PG: Management ladder = Massage → Oxytocin → Methylergometrine → Carboprost → Balloon → Surgery

    2. TRAUMA¶

    Cervical/Vaginal Lacerations: - Speculum examination (good lighting, anesthesia) - Suturing (absorbable sutures)

    Uterine Rupture: - Emergency laparotomy - Repair (if feasible) or Hysterectomy

    Uterine Inversion: - Manual replacement (Johnson's maneuver): - Cup inverted fundus in palm - Push upward through cervix - Do NOT remove placenta if still attached (causes bleeding) - Tocolytics (relax uterus): Terbutaline, nitroglycerin, halothane - After replacement: Oxytocics

    NEET PG: Uterine inversion = Manual replacement BEFORE removing placenta

    3. TISSUE¶

    Retained Placenta: - Manual removal (under anesthesia) - Controlled cord traction - Oxytocics after removal

    Retained Placental Fragments: - Uterine curettage (gentle) - Ultrasound guidance

    4. THROMBIN¶

    DIC Management: - Treat underlying cause (deliver baby, treat sepsis) - Component therapy: - FFP: 15-20 mL/kg (if PT/aPTT >1.5× normal) - Cryoprecipitate: If fibrinogen <100 mg/dL (10 units) - Platelets: If <50,000 (10 units) - PRBC: Maintain Hb >7 g/dL

    Blood Transfusion¶

    Indications: - Hb <7 g/dL - Ongoing bleeding - Hemodynamic instability

    Massive Transfusion Protocol: - PRBC : FFP : Platelets = 1 : 1 : 1 ratio

    NEET PG: Massive transfusion = 1:1:1 ratio (PRBC:FFP:Platelets)

    Complications of PPH¶

    Immediate¶

    1. Hypovolemic shock
    2. DIC
    3. Renal failure (acute tubular necrosis)
    4. ARDS
    5. Multi-organ failure
    6. Death

    Long-term¶

    1. Sheehan syndrome (Pituitary necrosis):
    2. Failure of lactation
    3. Amenorrhea
    4. Hypothyroidism

    5. Adrenal insufficiency

    6. Asherman syndrome: Intrauterine adhesions (if aggressive curettage)

    7. Anemia

    NEET PG: Sheehan syndrome = Postpartum pituitary necrosis (failure of lactation, amenorrhea)

    Secondary PPH (>24 hours to 12 weeks)¶

    Causes¶

    1. Retained products of conception (most common)
    2. Endometritis
    3. Subinvolution of uterus
    4. Gestational trophoblastic disease

    Clinical Features¶

    • Delayed bleeding (>24 hours)
    • Foul-smelling lochia (if infection)
    • Fever (endometritis)
    • Open cervical os

    Diagnosis¶

    • Ultrasound: Retained products (echogenic material)
    • Speculum exam: Visualize cervix, rule out trauma

    Management¶

    • Antibiotics (if infection): Broad-spectrum
    • Uterine curettage (if retained products)
    • Oxytocics

    NEET PG: Secondary PPH = Usually retained products → Curettage

    Practice MCQs¶

    1. Most common cause of PPH? Answer: Uterine atony (70%)

    2. Definition of PPH? Answer: Blood loss ≥500 mL (vaginal) or ≥1000 mL (cesarean) within 24 hours

    3. Contraindication to methylergometrine? Answer: Hypertension, pre-eclampsia

    4. Contraindication to carboprost? Answer: Asthma

    5. First-line uterotonic? Answer: Oxytocin

    6. Bleeding despite contracted uterus suggests? Answer: Genital tract trauma (do speculum exam)

    7. Most common obstetric cause of DIC? Answer: Placental abruption

    8. Uterine inversion management? Answer: Manual replacement (Johnson's maneuver) + Tocolytics

    Memory Aids¶

    Causes of PPH - "4 Ts"¶

    • **T**one (Atony) - 70%
    • **T**rauma
    • **T**issue (Retained)
    • **T**hrombin (Coagulopathy)

    Uterotonics (Order of Use)¶

    "**O**xytocin **M**ary **C**arried **M**isoprostol" - **O**xytocin (1st line) - **M**ethylergometrine (2nd line) - **C**arboprost (3rd line) - **M**isoprostol

    Contraindications¶

    "Methyl is Hyper, Carbo makes you Wheeze" - Methylergometrine: Hypertension - Carboprost: Asthma (wheeze)

    AMTSL¶

    "OCM" - **O**xytocin - **C**ontrolled cord traction - **M**assage

    • Know definition of PPH (primary vs secondary)
    • Learn "4 Ts" causes with percentages
    • Understand risk factors for uterine atony
    • Know perineal tear classification
    • Learn AMTSL components
    • Memorize uterotonics (order, contraindications)
    • Understand management ladder for atonic PPH
    • Know management of uterine inversion
    • Learn complications (Sheehan syndrome)
    • Practice 40+ MCQs on PPH
