# LOCAL ANESTHETICS - NEET PG Study Material

## DEFINITION

**Local anesthetics**: Drugs that reversibly block nerve conduction when applied locally, producing loss of sensation **without loss of consciousness**.

**Site of action**: Peripheral nerves (NOT CNS)

**NEET PG**: Local anesthetics block nerve conduction, patient remains conscious

---

## MECHANISM OF ACTION

### Nerve Conduction - Normal

```
Resting state: Inside negative (-70 mV)
         ↓
   Stimulus → Na+ channels open
         ↓
   Na+ influx → Depolarization
         ↓
   Action potential propagation
```

### Local Anesthetic Action

**Mechanism**:
1. **Block voltage-gated Na⁺ channels** (from inside of nerve membrane)
2. Prevent Na⁺ influx
3. **Prevent depolarization** → No action potential
4. Reversible blockade

**Form**: Must be in **uncharged (base) form** to cross nerve membrane
- Inside nerve: Becomes **charged (ionized) form** → Active
- **pKa** important (determines ionization)

**NEET PG High-Yield**: Local anesthetics block Na⁺ channels (reversible)

---

## CHEMISTRY

### Structure

**Basic structure**:
```
Lipophilic     Intermediate     Hydrophilic
(Aromatic ring) — (Ester/Amide) — (Amine group)
```

### Classification by Linkage

**A. Esters** (COO):
- Procaine
- Cocaine
- Tetracaine
- Benzocaine
- Chloroprocaine

**B. Amides** (NHCO):
- Lidocaine (Lignocaine)
- Bupivacaine
- Ropivacaine
- Prilocaine
- Mepivacaine
- Etidocaine

**NEET PG High-Yield**: Esters vs Amides (different metabolism, allergy)

---

## PHARMACOKINETICS

### Absorption

**Factors affecting absorption**:
1. **Vascularity** of site (↑ vascularity → ↑ absorption → ↓ duration)
2. **Vasoconstrictor** (epinephrine) → ↓ absorption → ↑ duration
3. Dose & concentration

**Order of vascularity** (fastest to slowest absorption):
```
IV > Tracheal > Intercostal > Caudal > Epidural > Brachial plexus > Subcutaneous
```

**NEET PG**: Adding epinephrine ↑ duration, ↓ systemic toxicity

---

### Metabolism

**Esters**:
- Hydrolyzed by **plasma cholinesterase** (pseudocholinesterase)
- Rapid metabolism
- **PABA** (para-aminobenzoic acid) formed → **Allergic reactions**
- Prolonged in cholinesterase deficiency

**Amides**:
- Metabolized in **liver** (by microsomal enzymes)
- Slower metabolism
- **Less allergic** (no PABA)
- Prolonged in liver disease

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Esters → Plasma cholinesterase → PABA → Allergic
- Amides → Liver → Less allergic

---

### Distribution

**Factors**:
- **Protein binding**: ↑ Binding → ↑ duration (bupivacaine > lidocaine)
- **Lipid solubility**: ↑ Lipid solubility → ↑ potency (bupivacaine > lidocaine)

**NEET PG**: Bupivacaine more potent & longer duration than lidocaine

---

## ORDER OF NERVE BLOCKADE

**Small diameter, myelinated fibers blocked FIRST**

**Sequence**:
```
Autonomic (B fibers)
    ↓
Pain, temperature (C fibers, Aδ)
    ↓
Touch, pressure (Aβ)
    ↓
Motor (Aα) - LAST
```

**Clinical**: **Sympathetic block > Sensory block > Motor block**

**NEET PG**: Pain blocked before motor function (can move but no pain)

---

## INDIVIDUAL DRUGS

### LIDOCAINE (Lignocaine) - Prototype

**Classification**: Amide

**Properties**:
- **Most widely used**
- Moderate potency, duration
- **Rapid onset**

**Potency**: Moderate (1x)
**Duration**: 1-2 hours (without epinephrine), 2-4 hours (with epinephrine)
**Maximum dose**:
- **Without epinephrine**: 4-5 mg/kg (max 300 mg)
- **With epinephrine**: 7 mg/kg (max 500 mg)

---

#### Uses

**1. Local infiltration anesthesia**

**2. Nerve blocks** (dental, peripheral)

**3. Epidural/Spinal anesthesia**

**4. Topical** (mucous membranes)

**5. IV - Antiarrhythmic** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Class IB antiarrhythmic**
- Ventricular arrhythmias (VT, VF)
- Digoxin-induced arrhythmias
- **Post-MI arrhythmias**

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Lidocaine max dose: 4-5 mg/kg (plain), 7 mg/kg (with epi)
- IV lidocaine for ventricular arrhythmias

---

### BUPIVACAINE

**Classification**: Amide

**Properties**:
- **High potency** (4x lidocaine)
- **Long duration** (4-8 hours)
- **High protein binding** (95%)
- **High lipid solubility**
- **Slow onset**

**Maximum dose**: 2-3 mg/kg (max 175 mg without epi, 225 mg with epi)

---

#### Uses

**1. Epidural anesthesia** (labor, surgery)

**2. Spinal anesthesia**

**3. Peripheral nerve blocks**

**NEET PG**: Bupivacaine = Long-acting, high potency

---

#### Cardiotoxicity ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Most cardiotoxic** local anesthetic:
- Binds to cardiac Na⁺ channels
- **Difficult to resuscitate** if overdose
- Arrhythmias, cardiac arrest

**Treatment**:
- **Lipid emulsion** (20% intralipid) - specific antidote
- CPR

**NEET PG High-Yield**: Bupivacaine cardiotoxicity treated with lipid emulsion

---

### ROPIVACAINE

**Classification**: Amide

**Properties**:
- Similar to bupivacaine
- **Less cardiotoxic** (safer)
- Pure S-enantiomer

**Uses**: Epidural, nerve blocks (preferred over bupivacaine)

**NEET PG**: Ropivacaine less cardiotoxic than bupivacaine

---

### PROCAINE

**Classification**: Ester

**Properties**:
- **First synthetic** local anesthetic
- Low potency
- Short duration
- **Rapid hydrolysis** (plasma cholinesterase)
- **Allergenic** (PABA formation)

**Uses**:
- Spinal anesthesia (now rarely used)
- Infiltration

**NEET PG**: Procaine = First synthetic LA, ester, allergenic

---

### COCAINE

**Classification**: Ester

**Properties**:
- **Natural** (only naturally occurring LA)
- **Vasoconstrictor** (blocks NE reuptake) - UNIQUE
- **No need for epinephrine**
- **CNS stimulant** (euphoria, addiction)
- High abuse potential

**Uses**:
- **Topical** (ENT procedures - nose, throat)
- Vasoconstriction + anesthesia

**Adverse effects**:
- **Addiction**
- Myocardial infarction, stroke
- Hypertension, arrhythmias

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Cocaine = ONLY LA with vasoconstrictor property
- Natural LA, high abuse potential

---

### TETRACAINE

**Classification**: Ester

**Properties**:
- **High potency** (10x procaine)
- **Long duration**
- Slow onset

**Uses**:
- **Spinal anesthesia** (preferred ester)
- Topical (eye drops)

**NEET PG**: Tetracaine = Spinal anesthesia (ester)

---

### BENZOCAINE

**Classification**: Ester

**Properties**:
- **NOT ionizable** (poor water solubility)
- **Topical use ONLY**
- Cannot be injected

**Uses**:
- Topical (mucous membranes, skin)
- Lozenges (sore throat)

**Adverse effect**:
- **Methemoglobinemia** (especially in infants)

**NEET PG**: Benzocaine = Topical only, methemoglobinemia risk

---

### PRILOCAINE

**Classification**: Amide

**Properties**:
- Similar to lidocaine
- Least toxic systemically

**Adverse effect**:
- **Methemoglobinemia** (metabolite: o-toluidine)
- Treatment: **Methylene blue**

**Uses**:
- **EMLA cream** (Eutectic Mixture of Local Anesthetics: Prilocaine + Lidocaine)
- Topical anesthesia before IV cannulation

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Prilocaine → Methemoglobinemia
- EMLA cream = Prilocaine + Lidocaine

---

## ADJUVANTS TO LOCAL ANESTHETICS

### EPINEPHRINE (Adrenaline)

**Concentration**: 1:200,000 (5 mcg/ml)

**Actions**:
- **Vasoconstriction** (α1)
- ↓ Systemic absorption
- ↑ Duration of action
- ↓ Systemic toxicity
- ↓ Bleeding

**Contraindications**:
- **Digits** (fingers, toes)
- **Penis**
- **Ear, nose tip**
- Risk of **ischemic necrosis** (end-arteries)

**NEET PG**: Don't use epinephrine in digits (risk of gangrene)

---

### SODIUM BICARBONATE

**Action**:
- ↑ pH → ↑ uncharged form
- **Faster onset**

---

### HYALURONIDASE

**Action**:
- Breaks down connective tissue
- ↑ Diffusion
- Larger area of anesthesia

**Use**: Ophthalmic blocks

---

## METHODS OF ADMINISTRATION

### 1. Topical (Surface) Anesthesia

**Drugs**: Lidocaine, benzocaine, tetracaine, cocaine

**Sites**: Mucous membranes (eye, nose, throat, urethra)

**Advantage**: Painless application

---

### 2. Infiltration Anesthesia

**Method**: Direct injection into tissue

**Uses**: Minor surgery, suturing, dental

**Drug**: Lidocaine (most common)

---

### 3. Nerve Block (Regional)

**Method**: Injection near specific nerve/plexus

**Types**:
- **Peripheral nerve blocks**: Brachial plexus, femoral, sciatic
- **Field block**: Block multiple nerves around surgical site

**Uses**: Limb surgery, dental

---

### 4. Spinal (Subarachnoid) Anesthesia

**Method**: Injection into **CSF** (subarachnoid space)
- Between L3-L4 or L4-L5

**Drugs**: Bupivacaine (heavy/hyperbaric), lidocaine, tetracaine

**Spread**: Depends on:
- **Baricity** (specific gravity relative to CSF)
  - **Hyperbaric** (heavy): Spreads downward (gravity)
  - **Isobaric**: No preferential spread
  - **Hypobaric** (light): Spreads upward
- Patient position
- Dose, volume

**Advantages**:
- Profound anesthesia
- Muscle relaxation
- Small dose required

**Uses**: Lower abdominal, pelvic, lower limb surgery

**NEET PG**: Spinal anesthesia = Subarachnoid, hyperbaric spreads downward

---

**Complications**:

**1. Hypotension** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Most common**
- Sympathetic blockade → Vasodilation
- Treatment: IV fluids, vasopressors (ephedrine, phenylephrine)

**2. Post-dural puncture headache** (PDPH):
- CSF leak
- **Worse when upright**, better lying down
- Treatment: Bed rest, hydration, **epidural blood patch**

**3. Total spinal** (too high level):
- Respiratory paralysis
- Hypotension
- Loss of consciousness
- Treatment: Ventilation, vasopressors

**4. Neurological** (rare):
- Cauda equina syndrome
- Arachnoiditis

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Spinal anesthesia most common complication = Hypotension
- PDPH = CSF leak, worse upright, epidural blood patch

---

### 5. Epidural Anesthesia

**Method**: Injection into **epidural space** (outside dura)
- Usually lumbar region

**Drugs**: Bupivacaine, ropivacaine, lidocaine (larger doses than spinal)

**Advantages**:
- **Catheter** can be placed → continuous infusion
- **Segmental block** possible
- Less hypotension than spinal

**Uses**:
- **Labor analgesia** (most common)
- Thoracic, abdominal, pelvic surgery
- Chronic pain management

**NEET PG**: Epidural = Outside dura, catheter possible, less hypotension

---

**Complications**:
- Hypotension (less than spinal)
- Dural puncture (wet tap) → PDPH
- Intravascular injection → Systemic toxicity
- Total spinal (if dural puncture unrecognized)

---

### Spinal vs Epidural

| Feature | Spinal | Epidural |
|---------|--------|----------|
| **Site** | Subarachnoid (in CSF) | Epidural space (outside dura) |
| **Dose** | Small | Large |
| **Onset** | Rapid (2-5 min) | Slower (15-20 min) |
| **Duration** | Fixed | Variable (catheter) |
| **Hypotension** | More common | Less common |
| **Headache** | More common | Less (unless dural puncture) |
| **Motor block** | Complete | Variable (can be segmental) |

**NEET PG**: Spinal faster onset, smaller dose; Epidural catheter possible

---

### 6. IV Regional Anesthesia (Bier's Block)

**Method**:
1. Exsanguinate limb (Esmarch bandage)
2. Inflate tourniquet (above systolic BP)
3. Inject LA IV (distal to tourniquet)
4. LA diffuses into tissues

**Drug**: Lidocaine (prilocaine safer)

**Uses**: Upper limb surgery (short procedures)

**Complications**:
- **Tourniquet pain** (limits duration to ~1 hour)
- Systemic toxicity (if tourniquet deflated too early)

**NEET PG**: Bier's block = IV regional, tourniquet essential

---

## SYSTEMIC TOXICITY

### CNS Toxicity (Occurs FIRST)

**Low blood levels**:
- Circumoral numbness, tingling
- Lightheadedness, tinnitus
- Visual disturbances

**Higher levels**:
- **Seizures** (cortical excitation)
- CNS depression (coma, respiratory arrest)

**NEET PG**: CNS toxicity occurs before cardiac toxicity

---

### Cardiovascular Toxicity

**Higher blood levels**:
- **Myocardial depression**
- **Arrhythmias** (VT, VF)
- **Hypotension**
- **Cardiac arrest**

**NEET PG**: Bupivacaine most cardiotoxic

---

### Treatment of Systemic Toxicity

**1. Stop injection**

**2. Airway management**:
- 100% O₂
- Ventilation

**3. Seizures**:
- **Benzodiazepines** (diazepam, midazolam)
- NOT thiopental (↑ cardiotoxicity)

**4. Cardiovascular support**:
- IV fluids
- Vasopressors

**5. Specific antidote** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **20% Lipid emulsion** (Intralipid)
- Especially for **bupivacaine toxicity**

**6. CPR** (prolonged may be needed)

**NEET PG High-Yield**: Lipid emulsion for local anesthetic toxicity (especially bupivacaine)

---

## ALLERGIC REACTIONS

**Esters** > Amides (PABA formation)

**Treatment**:
- Antihistamines
- Corticosteroids
- Epinephrine (if anaphylaxis)

**NEET PG**: Esters more allergenic than amides

---

## HIGH-YIELD DRUG COMPARISON

| Drug | Class | Potency | Duration | Key Feature |
|------|-------|---------|----------|-------------|
| **Lidocaine** | Amide | 1x | 1-2 hr | Most widely used, IV antiarrhythmic |
| **Bupivacaine** | Amide | 4x | 4-8 hr | Long-acting, cardiotoxic |
| **Ropivacaine** | Amide | 4x | 4-8 hr | Less cardiotoxic than bupivacaine |
| **Procaine** | Ester | 0.25x | 0.5-1 hr | First synthetic, allergenic |
| **Cocaine** | Ester | 1x | 0.5-1 hr | Natural, vasoconstrictor, abuse |
| **Tetracaine** | Ester | 10x | 3-4 hr | Spinal anesthesia |
| **Benzocaine** | Ester | - | - | Topical only, methemoglobinemia |
| **Prilocaine** | Amide | 1x | 1-2 hr | Methemoglobinemia, EMLA cream |

## Practice MCQs

1. **Mechanism of action of local anesthetics?**
   Answer: Block voltage-gated Na⁺ channels

2. **Why add epinephrine to local anesthetics?**
   Answer: Prolong duration, reduce systemic toxicity

3. **Where is epinephrine contraindicated?**
   Answer: Digits (fingers, toes, penis, ear, nose)

4. **Max dose of lidocaine with epinephrine?**
   Answer: 7 mg/kg (max 500 mg)

5. **Most cardiotoxic local anesthetic?**
   Answer: Bupivacaine

6. **Antidote for bupivacaine toxicity?**
   Answer: 20% Lipid emulsion

7. **Local anesthetic with intrinsic vasoconstrictor property?**
   Answer: Cocaine

8. **Drug causing methemoglobinemia?**
   Answer: Prilocaine, Benzocaine

9. **Treatment for methemoglobinemia?**
   Answer: Methylene blue

10. **Most common complication of spinal anesthesia?**
    Answer: Hypotension

11. **Treatment for post-dural puncture headache?**
    Answer: Epidural blood patch

12. **First sign of local anesthetic systemic toxicity?**
    Answer: CNS symptoms (circumoral numbness, tinnitus, seizures)
