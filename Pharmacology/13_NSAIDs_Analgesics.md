# NSAIDs & ANALGESICS - NEET PG Study Material

## CLASSIFICATION OF ANALGESICS

### Non-Opioid Analgesics
**1. NSAIDs (Non-Selective COX Inhibitors)**:
- Aspirin
- Ibuprofen, Naproxen, Diclofenac, Indomethacin
- Ketorolac, Piroxicam, Meloxicam

**2. Selective COX-2 Inhibitors (Coxibs)**:
- Celecoxib, Etoricoxib, Parecoxib

**3. Para-aminophenol Derivative**:
- Paracetamol (Acetaminophen)

### Opioid Analgesics
- Morphine, Codeine, Fentanyl, Tramadol, Pethidine (Meperidine)

### Adjuvant Analgesics
- Antidepressants (Amitriptyline)
- Anticonvulsants (Gabapentin, Pregabalin)

## PROSTAGLANDIN SYNTHESIS

### Arachidonic Acid Pathway

```
Membrane Phospholipids
        ↓ [Phospholipase A2]
Arachidonic Acid
        ↓ [Cyclooxygenase (COX)]
Prostaglandin H2 (PGH2)
        ↓
    ↙       ↓       ↘
PGE2    PGI2      TXA2
(Pain,  (Vasodilation, (Platelet
Fever,  Gastric     aggregation,
Inflam) protection) Vasoconstriction)
```

**COX-1**: Constitutive (always present)
- **Gastric protection** (mucus, bicarbonate)
- **Platelet function** (TXA2)
- Renal blood flow

**COX-2**: Inducible (inflammation, pain)
- **Inflammation, pain, fever**
- Also: Renal function, vascular homeostasis

**NEET PG High-Yield**: COX-1 = Housekeeping (gastric protection), COX-2 = Inflammation

---

## MECHANISM OF NSAIDs

**Inhibit cyclooxygenase (COX)** → ↓ Prostaglandin synthesis

**Effects**:
1. **Anti-inflammatory** (↓ PGE2)
2. **Analgesic** (↓ PGE2 sensitization of nociceptors)
3. **Antipyretic** (↓ PGE2 in hypothalamus)
4. **Antiplatelet** (aspirin - ↓ TXA2)

**NEET PG**: NSAIDs inhibit COX → ↓ prostaglandins

---

## ASPIRIN (Acetylsalicylic Acid)

### Mechanism

**Irreversible COX inhibitor** (acetylates enzyme):
- **Non-selective** (COX-1 & COX-2)
- **Antiplatelet effect** (↓ TXA2 in platelets)
- Platelets cannot synthesize new COX (no nucleus) → **Effect lasts 7-10 days** (platelet lifespan)

**NEET PG**: Aspirin = Irreversible COX inhibitor, antiplatelet effect lasts 7-10 days

---

### Dose-Dependent Effects

**Low dose (75-325 mg/day)**:
- **Antiplatelet** effect
- Preferentially inhibits COX-1 in platelets

**Intermediate dose (500-1000 mg)**:
- **Analgesic, antipyretic**

**High dose (4-6 g/day)**:
- **Anti-inflammatory**

**Very high dose (>6 g/day)**:
- **Toxic** (salicylism)

**NEET PG**: Low-dose aspirin = Antiplatelet, high-dose = Anti-inflammatory

---

### Clinical Uses

**1. Antiplatelet** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Acute MI** (chew 325 mg immediately)
- **Unstable angina, ACS**
- **Secondary prevention** (post-MI, post-stroke)
- **Primary prevention** (high CV risk)
- **Stent thrombosis prevention** (with clopidogrel)

**2. Acute pain, fever** (headache, myalgia)

**3. Rheumatic fever**:
- Anti-inflammatory (high-dose)

**4. Kawasaki disease** ⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- High-dose (anti-inflammatory) → Low-dose (antiplatelet)

**5. Pericarditis, pleuritis**

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Aspirin = DOC for acute MI (antiplatelet)
- Kawasaki disease (high→low dose)

---

### Adverse Effects

**1. GI toxicity** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Gastritis, peptic ulcer, bleeding**
- **Most common serious effect**
- Mechanism: ↓ PGE2/PGI2 → ↓ mucus, ↓ bicarbonate, ↓ blood flow
- **Direct mucosal irritation**
- Prevention: Enteric-coated, with food, PPI

**2. Bleeding** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- Antiplatelet effect
- **↑ Bleeding time**
- **Stop 7-10 days before surgery**
- Contraindicated: Hemophilia, active bleeding, peptic ulcer

**3. Reye's syndrome** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Acute encephalopathy + hepatotoxicity**
- **Children with viral illness** (influenza, varicella)
- **Contraindicated in children <12 years** (especially with fever)

**4. Hypersensitivity reactions** ⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Aspirin-induced asthma** (bronchospasm)
- **Aspirin triad**: Asthma + Nasal polyps + Aspirin sensitivity
- Urticaria, angioedema

**5. Salicylism** (chronic toxicity):
- Tinnitus, vertigo, headache
- Nausea, vomiting
- **Tinnitus = Early sign**

**6. Acute salicylate poisoning** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Respiratory alkalosis** (initial - direct CNS stimulation)
- **Metabolic acidosis** (later - accumulation of acids)
- Hyperthermia (↑ metabolism)
- CNS: Confusion, seizures, coma
- **Treatment**:
  - **Gastric lavage** (if early)
  - **Activated charcoal**
  - **Alkaline diuresis** (sodium bicarbonate - ↑ renal excretion)
  - **Hemodialysis** (severe)

**7. Renal toxicity**:
- Acute interstitial nephritis
- Analgesic nephropathy (chronic use)

**8. Uric acid**:
- **Low dose**: ↓ Uric acid excretion (↑ uric acid)
- **High dose**: ↑ Uric acid excretion (↓ uric acid)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Aspirin most common = GI toxicity
- Reye's syndrome in children (contraindicated <12 years)
- Aspirin triad = Asthma + polyps + aspirin sensitivity
- Salicylate poisoning = Respiratory alkalosis → metabolic acidosis
- Treatment: Alkaline diuresis (sodium bicarbonate)

---

### Contraindications

**Absolute**:
- **Children <12 years** (with viral illness - Reye's syndrome)
- **Active peptic ulcer**
- **Hemophilia, bleeding disorders**
- **Severe hepatic/renal impairment**
- **Aspirin-induced asthma**
- **Third trimester pregnancy** (premature ductus closure)

**NEET PG**: Aspirin contraindicated in children (Reye's), peptic ulcer, bleeding disorders

---

### Drug Interactions

**1. Warfarin**:
- **↑ Bleeding risk** (antiplatelet + anticoagulant)
- ↑ Warfarin effect (displaces from protein)

**2. Methotrexate**:
- ↑ Methotrexate toxicity (↓ renal excretion)

**3. Antihypertensives**:
- ↓ Antihypertensive effect (↓ prostaglandin-mediated vasodilation)

## NON-SELECTIVE NSAIDs

### IBUPROFEN

**Properties**:
- **Safest NSAID** (GI, CV)
- Reversible COX inhibitor
- Short half-life

**Uses**:
- Mild-moderate pain, fever
- Dysmenorrhea
- OTC (over-the-counter)

**Adverse effects**:
- Similar to aspirin but **less GI toxicity**
- **No Reye's syndrome** (safer in children >6 months)

**NEET PG**: Ibuprofen = Safest NSAID

---

### NAPROXEN

**Properties**:
- Longer half-life (twice daily)
- **Better GI tolerability** than most NSAIDs

**Uses**:
- Arthritis, gout, dysmenorrhea

---

### DICLOFENAC

**Properties**:
- Potent anti-inflammatory
- **Topical** preparations available

**Uses**:
- Arthritis, acute pain
- **Topical**: Musculoskeletal pain

**Adverse effects**:
- **Higher CV risk** than ibuprofen
- Hepatotoxicity

**NEET PG**: Diclofenac available as topical gel

---

### INDOMETHACIN

**Properties**:
- **Very potent** anti-inflammatory
- **Highest CNS side effects**

**Uses**:
- **Patent ductus arteriosus (PDA)** closure ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - In neonates
  - ↓ PGE2 → ductus constriction
- **Gout** (acute attack)
- Ankylosing spondylitis

**Adverse effects**:
- **High GI toxicity**
- **CNS**: Headache, dizziness (common)

**NEET PG High-Yield**: Indomethacin = DOC for PDA closure (neonates)

---

### KETOROLAC

**Properties**:
- **Potent analgesic** (comparable to morphine)
- **Minimal anti-inflammatory**
- **Parenteral** (IV/IM) available

**Uses**:
- **Postoperative pain** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - Opioid-sparing
- Acute moderate-severe pain
- Renal colic

**Duration**: **≤5 days** (↑ GI/bleeding risk if longer)

**Adverse effects**:
- **High bleeding risk**
- GI toxicity
- Renal toxicity

**NEET PG**: Ketorolac = Potent parenteral NSAID for postoperative pain (max 5 days)

---

### PIROXICAM

**Properties**:
- **Very long half-life** (45 hours) - once daily

**Uses**:
- Chronic arthritis

**Adverse effects**:
- **Higher GI toxicity** (long half-life)

---

## SELECTIVE COX-2 INHIBITORS (Coxibs)

**Examples**: **Celecoxib**, Etoricoxib, Parecoxib

### Mechanism

**Selective COX-2 inhibitors**:
- Spare COX-1 (gastric protection, platelets)
- **↓ GI toxicity**
- **No antiplatelet effect**

**NEET PG**: COX-2 inhibitors spare COX-1 → less GI toxicity, no antiplatelet effect

---

### Uses

**1. Arthritis** (osteoarthritis, rheumatoid):
- Especially if **high GI risk**

**2. Acute pain**:
- Postoperative (parecoxib - IV)
- Dysmenorrhea

**Advantage**:
- **↓ GI toxicity** (vs non-selective NSAIDs)
- **No effect on platelet function** (safe with anticoagulants)

---

### Adverse Effects

**1. Cardiovascular risk** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **↑ MI, stroke, thrombosis**
- Mechanism: ↓ PGI2 (vasodilation, antiplatelet) but NOT TXA2 (prothrombotic)
- **Rofecoxib withdrawn** (2004 - ↑ CV events)
- **Black box warning**

**2. Renal toxicity**:
- Similar to non-selective NSAIDs
- COX-2 important for renal function

**3. Sulfonamide allergy** (celecoxib):
- Contains sulfonamide group

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- COX-2 inhibitors ↑ cardiovascular risk (MI, stroke)
- Less GI toxicity than non-selective NSAIDs

---

### Contraindications

- **Post-CABG surgery** (↑ CV events)
- **Severe cardiovascular disease**
- **Sulfonamide allergy** (celecoxib)

---

## PARACETAMOL (Acetaminophen)

### Mechanism

**Weak COX inhibitor** (mainly in CNS):
- **Antipyretic, analgesic**
- **NO anti-inflammatory** (no peripheral COX inhibition)
- Exact mechanism unclear

**NEET PG**: Paracetamol = Analgesic, antipyretic, NOT anti-inflammatory

---

### Uses

**1. Mild-moderate pain, fever** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **First-line** for mild pain
- **Safest** analgesic-antipyretic

**2. Osteoarthritis**:
- First-line (better than NSAIDs for chronic use)

**Advantages**:
- **No GI toxicity**
- **No platelet effect**
- **No Reye's syndrome** (safe in children)
- **Safe in asthma**
- **Safe in pregnancy** (Category B)

**NEET PG**: Paracetamol = Safest, first-line for pain/fever

---

### Adverse Effects

**Therapeutic doses**:
- **Very safe**
- Rare: Rash, blood dyscrasias

**Overdose** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Hepatotoxicity** (potentially fatal)
- **Toxic dose**: >10-15 g (adults), >150 mg/kg (children)

**Mechanism of hepatotoxicity**:
```
Normal dose:
Paracetamol → Glucuronidation/Sulfation (90%)
           → CYP2E1 → NAPQI (toxic) → Glutathione conjugation (safe)

Overdose:
↑ NAPQI formation (saturated conjugation pathways)
↓ Glutathione depletion
→ NAPQI accumulates → Hepatic necrosis
```

**Phases of paracetamol poisoning**:
1. **0-24 hours**: Nausea, vomiting (mild)
2. **24-72 hours**: ↑ AST, ALT, PT (hepatotoxicity)
3. **72-96 hours**: **Hepatic necrosis, failure** (jaundice, encephalopathy)
4. **>4 days**: Recovery or death

**Treatment** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
1. **Gastric lavage** (if <1 hour)
2. **Activated charcoal** (if <4 hours)
3. **N-acetylcysteine (NAC)** - **Antidote**
   - **Most effective if given within 8 hours**
   - Still beneficial up to 24 hours
   - **Mechanism**: ↑ Glutathione synthesis
   - **IV or oral**
4. Supportive care
5. **Liver transplant** (if fulminant hepatic failure)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Paracetamol overdose = Hepatotoxicity
- Antidote = N-acetylcysteine (NAC) - best within 8 hours
- NAC increases glutathione

## COMMON NSAID ADVERSE EFFECTS

### 1. GI Toxicity ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Spectrum**: Dyspepsia → Gastritis → Peptic ulcer → Bleeding/perforation

**Risk factors**:
- Age >60
- Previous peptic ulcer
- High-dose/long-duration
- Concurrent steroids, anticoagulants
- H. pylori infection

**Prevention**:
- **PPI** (omeprazole) - most effective
- Misoprostol (PGE1 analog) - less tolerated
- COX-2 inhibitors (if low CV risk)
- Take with food

**NEET PG**: NSAIDs cause peptic ulcer; prevent with PPI

---

### 2. Renal Toxicity ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Mechanisms**:
- **Acute kidney injury** (↓ renal blood flow - ↓ PGE2)
- **Acute interstitial nephritis** (hypersensitivity)
- **Analgesic nephropathy** (chronic use - papillary necrosis)
- **Hyperkalemia** (↓ renin, ↓ aldosterone)
- **Hyponatremia** (water retention)

**Risk factors**:
- Volume depletion, heart failure
- Pre-existing renal disease
- Elderly
- Concurrent ACE-I, ARBs, diuretics

**NEET PG**: NSAIDs cause AKI (↓ renal blood flow), hyperkalemia

---

### 3. Cardiovascular Toxicity ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Effects**:
- **Hypertension** (↓ prostaglandin-mediated vasodilation)
- **Edema** (Na⁺/water retention)
- **↓ Antihypertensive efficacy**
- **MI, stroke** (especially COX-2 inhibitors)
- **Heart failure exacerbation**

**NEET PG**: NSAIDs ↑ BP, ↓ antihypertensive effect

---

### 4. Hypersensitivity ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Manifestations**:
- **Bronchospasm** (aspirin-induced asthma)
- Urticaria, angioedema
- Anaphylaxis (rare)

**Cross-reactivity**: Common among NSAIDs

**NEET PG**: NSAID hypersensitivity = Bronchospasm, urticaria

---

### 5. Hematological

- **↑ Bleeding time** (antiplatelet effect)
- Aplastic anemia (rare)

---

### 6. Hepatotoxicity

- ↑ Transaminases
- Rare: Hepatitis

---

## OPIOID ANALGESICS - Brief Overview

### MORPHINE - Prototype

**Mechanism**: **μ-opioid receptor agonist** (CNS, periphery)

**Effects**:
- **Analgesia** (strong)
- **Sedation, euphoria**
- **Respiratory depression**
- **Cough suppression**
- **GI**: ↓ Motility (constipation), ↑ sphincter tone
- **Miosis** (pinpoint pupils)
- **Histamine release** (itching, hypotension)

**NEET PG**: Morphine = μ-opioid agonist, respiratory depression, miosis, constipation

---

### Uses

**1. Severe pain** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- Cancer pain
- Postoperative pain
- Acute MI (↓ pain, anxiety, preload)

**2. Acute pulmonary edema**:
- ↓ Preload, anxiety

**3. Diarrhea** (codeine, loperamide)

**4. Cough** (codeine, dextromethorphan)

---

### Adverse Effects

**1. Respiratory depression** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Most serious**
- **Antidote**: **Naloxone** (opioid antagonist)

**2. Constipation** ⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Most common**
- **No tolerance** (persists with chronic use)
- Prophylaxis: Laxatives

**3. Nausea, vomiting**:
- CTZ stimulation

**4. Addiction, dependence**:
- Physical & psychological

**5. Tolerance**:
- Develops to analgesia, sedation, respiratory depression
- **NOT to miosis, constipation**

**6. Withdrawal syndrome**:
- Flu-like symptoms, craving, anxiety
- **NOT life-threatening** (vs alcohol, benzodiazepines)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Morphine most serious = Respiratory depression (antidote: naloxone)
- Most common = Constipation (no tolerance)

---

### TRAMADOL

**Mechanism**: **Dual action**
- Weak μ-opioid agonist
- ↑ Serotonin & norepinephrine (reuptake inhibition)

**Uses**:
- Moderate pain
- Neuropathic pain

**Advantages**:
- **Less respiratory depression** than morphine
- **Less addiction potential**

**Adverse effects**:
- Seizures (lowers seizure threshold)
- Serotonin syndrome (with SSRIs, MAOIs)
- Nausea

**NEET PG**: Tramadol = Dual mechanism, seizures, serotonin syndrome

---

### NALOXONE

**Mechanism**: **Pure opioid antagonist** (competitive)

**Uses**:
- **Opioid overdose** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - Respiratory depression
  - **IV/IM/Intranasal**
  - **Short half-life** (1-2 hours) - may need repeated doses

**NEET PG**: Naloxone = Opioid antagonist for overdose

## Practice MCQs

1. **Drug of choice for acute MI (antiplatelet)?**
   Answer: Aspirin (chew 325 mg)

2. **Aspirin contraindicated in children because?**
   Answer: Reye's syndrome risk

3. **Antidote for paracetamol overdose?**
   Answer: N-acetylcysteine (NAC)

4. **NSAID for PDA closure in neonates?**
   Answer: Indomethacin

5. **Safest NSAID for GI?**
   Answer: Ibuprofen (or COX-2 inhibitors)

6. **NSAID safe in pregnancy?**
   Answer: Paracetamol (Category B)

7. **Treatment for aspirin overdose?**
   Answer: Alkaline diuresis (sodium bicarbonate), hemodialysis

8. **Most common adverse effect of morphine?**
   Answer: Constipation

9. **Antidote for opioid overdose?**
   Answer: Naloxone

10. **COX-2 inhibitor main disadvantage?**
    Answer: ↑ Cardiovascular risk (MI, stroke)
