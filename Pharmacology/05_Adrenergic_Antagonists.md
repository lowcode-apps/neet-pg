# ADRENERGIC ANTAGONISTS (Sympatholytics) - NEET PG Study Material

## CLASSIFICATION

### By Receptor Selectivity

**A. α-Blockers**:
1. **Non-selective**: Phentolamine, Phenoxybenzamine, Tolazoline
2. **Selective α1**: Prazosin, Terazosin, Doxazosin, Tamsulosin, Alfuzosin
3. **Selective α2**: Yohimbine, Mirtazapine

**B. β-Blockers**:
1. **Non-selective**: Propranolol, Nadolol, Timolol
2. **Selective β1**: Atenolol, Metoprolol, Bisoprolol, Esmolol
3. **Mixed (α + β)**: Labetalol, Carvedilol

**C. α & β Blockers**: Labetalol, Carvedilol

---

## α-ADRENERGIC BLOCKERS

### NON-SELECTIVE α-BLOCKERS

#### PHENTOLAMINE

**Mechanism**: **Competitive** α1 & α2 antagonist

**Actions**:
- **α1 blockade**: Vasodilation → ↓ BP
- **α2 blockade**: ↑ NE release → **reflex tachycardia**
- ↑ Cardiac output

**NEET PG**: α2 blockade prevents negative feedback → ↑ NE release → tachycardia

---

**Clinical Uses**:

**1. Pheochromocytoma**:
- **Diagnosis**: Phentolamine test (↓ BP if positive)
- **Preoperative management** (before surgery)
- **Hypertensive crisis**

**2. Norepinephrine extravasation**:
- **Local infiltration** to prevent tissue necrosis

**3. Clonidine overdose** (reverses α2 effects)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Phentolamine = Pheochromocytoma, NE extravasation
- Given BEFORE β-blocker in pheochromocytoma

---

**Adverse Effects**:
- **Reflex tachycardia** (α2 blockade)
- Orthostatic hypotension
- Nasal congestion

---

#### PHENOXYBENZAMINE

**Mechanism**: **Irreversible (non-competitive)** α1 & α2 antagonist

**Actions**:
- Long duration (24-48 hours)
- Similar to phentolamine but longer acting

---

**Clinical Uses**:

**1. Pheochromocytoma** (preoperative preparation):
- **Drug of choice** for medical management
- Start 1-2 weeks before surgery
- Allows volume expansion

**NEET PG**: Phenoxybenzamine = DOC for pheochromocytoma (preop)

---

**Adverse Effects**:
- Postural hypotension
- Reflex tachycardia
- Ejaculation failure

---

### SELECTIVE α1-BLOCKERS

**PRAZOSIN, TERAZOSIN, DOXAZOSIN**

**Mechanism**: **Selective competitive α1 antagonist**

**Actions**:
- **α1 blockade**: Vasodilation (arteriolar + venous)
- ↓ Peripheral resistance
- ↓ BP
- **No reflex tachycardia** (α2 intact - negative feedback preserved)
- Relaxes bladder neck & prostate smooth muscle

**NEET PG**: α1-selective blockers don't cause tachycardia (α2 intact)

---

#### Clinical Uses

**1. Hypertension**:
- Effective but NOT first-line
- Useful in combination therapy
- **First-dose hypotension** (common - dose at bedtime)

**2. Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)**:
- ↓ Bladder outlet obstruction
- Improves urine flow

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Prazosin = Hypertension + BPH
- **First-dose phenomenon** (severe hypotension after first dose)

---

#### Adverse Effects

- **First-dose hypotension** (give at bedtime)
- Orthostatic hypotension
- Dizziness, headache
- Nasal congestion
- **NO reflex tachycardia**

**NEET PG**: First-dose hypotension with α1-blockers (prazosin)

---

#### TAMSULOSIN, ALFUZOSIN

**Properties**:
- **Selective for α1A** (prostate > vascular)
- Less orthostatic hypotension than prazosin

**Uses**:
- **BPH** (preferred over prazosin)
- Less BP effect

**NEET PG**: Tamsulosin = BPH (uroselective)

---

### SELECTIVE α2-BLOCKERS

**YOHIMBINE**

**Mechanism**: α2 antagonist

**Uses**:
- **Erectile dysfunction** (↑ sympathetic flow)
- Research purposes

**NEET PG**: Yohimbine = α2 blocker (limited use)

---

## β-ADRENERGIC BLOCKERS

### NON-SELECTIVE β-BLOCKERS

#### PROPRANOLOL - Prototype

**Mechanism**: **Non-selective** β1 & β2 antagonist

**Pharmacological Actions**:

**1. Cardiovascular** (β1):
- ↓ Heart rate (negative chronotropy)
- ↓ Contractility (negative inotropy)
- ↓ AV conduction
- ↓ Cardiac output
- ↓ BP

**2. Renin-angiotensin** (β1):
- ↓ Renin release → ↓ BP

**3. Respiratory** (β2):
- **Bronchoconstriction** (↑ airway resistance)
- **Contraindicated in asthma/COPD**

**4. Metabolic** (β2):
- ↓ Glycogenolysis → **masks hypoglycemia symptoms**
- ↓ Lipolysis
- **Caution in diabetes**

**5. Eye**:
- ↓ Intraocular pressure (↓ aqueous humor production)

**6. CNS**:
- Crosses BBB (lipophilic)
- Sedation, depression, nightmares

**NEET PG**: Non-selective β-blockers contraindicated in asthma

---

#### Clinical Uses of Propranolol

**1. Hypertension**:
- Effective, but NOT first-line alone
- Useful in combination

**2. Angina pectoris**:
- ↓ Myocardial O2 demand (↓ HR, ↓ contractility)
- ↑ Diastolic filling time

**3. Myocardial infarction**:
- ↓ Mortality (especially post-MI)
- ↓ Arrhythmias, ↓ reinfarction

**4. Arrhythmias**:
- Supraventricular tachycardia (SVT)
- Atrial fibrillation (rate control)
- Ventricular arrhythmias

**5. Heart failure** (chronic, stable):
- **Paradoxically beneficial** (long-term)
- Carvedilol, metoprolol, bisoprolol preferred

**6. Hyperthyroidism** (thyroid storm):
- Controls sympathetic symptoms (tremor, tachycardia, anxiety)
- **Does NOT affect thyroid hormone levels**
- **Blocks T4 → T3 conversion** (peripheral)

**7. Migraine prophylaxis**

**8. Essential tremor**

**9. Performance anxiety**

**10. Portal hypertension**:
- ↓ Portal pressure
- Prevents variceal bleeding

**11. Glaucoma** (timolol - topical)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Propranolol = Thyroid storm (symptomatic), migraine prophylaxis
- β-blockers ↓ mortality in MI
- Portal hypertension prophylaxis

---

#### Adverse Effects

**1. Cardiovascular**:
- **Bradycardia, heart block**
- **Worsening heart failure** (acute use)
- Hypotension

**2. Respiratory**:
- **Bronchospasm** (β2 blockade)

**3. Metabolic**:
- **Masks hypoglycemia symptoms** (tachycardia, tremor)
- **Doesn't mask sweating** (cholinergic)
- Hyperglycemia (↓ insulin release)
- Dyslipidemia (↑ TG, ↓ HDL)

**4. CNS**:
- Fatigue, depression
- Nightmares, insomnia

**5. Peripheral**:
- Cold extremities (vasoconstriction)
- Raynaud's phenomenon

**6. Sexual**:
- Erectile dysfunction

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- β-blockers mask hypoglycemia symptoms (except sweating)
- Contraindicated in asthma, COPD

---

#### Contraindications

**Absolute**:
- **Asthma, COPD** (bronchospasm)
- **Heart block** (2nd/3rd degree)
- **Severe bradycardia** (<60 bpm)
- **Acute heart failure** (decompensated)
- **Cardiogenic shock**

**Relative**:
- Diabetes (especially on insulin)
- Peripheral vascular disease
- Depression

**NEET PG**: β-blockers absolutely contraindicated in asthma

---

#### β-Blocker Withdrawal

**NEVER stop abruptly** → **Rebound phenomena**:
- ↑ Angina attacks
- ↑ Arrhythmias
- Hypertensive crisis
- MI

**Mechanism**: Upregulation of β-receptors

**NEET PG**: β-blocker withdrawal → rebound angina, MI

---

#### Other Non-Selective β-Blockers

**NADOLOL**:
- Long-acting (once daily)
- Doesn't cross BBB (hydrophilic)
- Less CNS effects

**TIMOLOL**:
- Used **topically for glaucoma**

**NEET PG**: Timolol = Topical for glaucoma

---

### SELECTIVE β1-BLOCKERS (Cardioselective)

**ATENOLOL, METOPROLOL, BISOPROLOL, ESMOLOL**

**Advantages over non-selective**:
- **Safer in asthma/COPD** (relatively - still caution)
- **Safer in diabetes** (less metabolic effects)
- **Safer in peripheral vascular disease**

**Note**: Selectivity is **dose-dependent** (lost at high doses)

**NEET PG**: β1-selective safer (but not absolutely safe) in asthma

---

#### ATENOLOL

**Properties**:
- Selective β1
- **Hydrophilic** (doesn't cross BBB)
- Long-acting (once daily)
- **Renal excretion** (dose adjustment in renal failure)

**Uses**:
- Hypertension
- Angina
- Post-MI

**NEET PG**: Atenolol = Hydrophilic, once daily, renal excretion

---

#### METOPROLOL

**Properties**:
- Selective β1
- **Lipophilic** (crosses BBB)
- Hepatic metabolism

**Uses**:
- Hypertension
- Angina
- Heart failure (chronic)
- Post-MI

**NEET PG**: Metoprolol = One of 3 β-blockers proven in heart failure

---

#### BISOPROLOL

**Uses**:
- Heart failure (chronic) - proven benefit
- Hypertension

**NEET PG**: Bisoprolol = Heart failure (along with carvedilol, metoprolol)

---

#### ESMOLOL

**Properties**:
- **Very short-acting** (t½ = 9 minutes)
- Metabolized by **RBC esterases**
- **IV only**

**Uses**:
- **Perioperative hypertension/tachycardia**
- **Supraventricular tachycardia** (acute)
- Thyroid storm

**NEET PG**: Esmolol = Ultra-short acting, IV, perioperative use

---

## MIXED α & β BLOCKERS

### LABETALOL

**Mechanism**: **α1 + β (non-selective)** antagonist
- **Ratio**: β:α = 3:1 (oral), 7:1 (IV)

**Actions**:
- ↓ BP (α1 blockade - vasodilation)
- ↓ Heart rate (β1 blockade)
- **No reflex tachycardia** (α blockade prevents)

---

#### Clinical Uses

**1. Hypertensive emergencies**:
- **Pregnancy** (pre-eclampsia, eclampsia) - **safe in pregnancy**
- Pheochromocytoma crisis (after α-blockade)

**2. Chronic hypertension in pregnancy**

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Labetalol = Hypertensive emergency in pregnancy (safe)
- Pheochromocytoma (after phenoxybenzamine)

---

#### Adverse Effects

- Postural hypotension
- Fatigue
- Bronchospasm (less than pure β-blockers)
- Hepatotoxicity

---

### CARVEDILOL

**Mechanism**: **α1 + β (non-selective)** antagonist + **antioxidant**

**Actions**:
- Similar to labetalol
- Additional antioxidant properties
- ↓ Vascular remodeling

---

#### Clinical Uses

**1. Heart failure** (chronic):
- **Proven mortality benefit**
- One of 3 β-blockers for heart failure

**2. Hypertension**

**3. Post-MI**

**NEET PG**: Carvedilol = Heart failure (proven benefit)

---

## β-BLOCKERS IN SPECIAL CONDITIONS

### Heart Failure

**Proven beneficial** (chronic, stable HF):
- **Carvedilol**
- **Metoprolol** (succinate)
- **Bisoprolol**

**Mechanism**: ↓ Cardiac remodeling, ↓ apoptosis

**NEET PG**: Only 3 β-blockers proven in HF (Carvedilol, Metoprolol, Bisoprolol)

---

### Thyroid Storm

**β-blockers** (propranolol preferred):
- Controls sympathetic symptoms
- **Blocks T4 → T3 conversion** (peripheral)
- Does NOT affect thyroid hormone production

**NEET PG**: Propranolol in thyroid storm (symptomatic + ↓ T4→T3)

---

### Migraine Prophylaxis

**Propranolol, metoprolol, timolol**:
- Mechanism unclear
- Prevents attacks (not acute treatment)

---

### Glaucoma

**Timolol** (topical):
- ↓ Aqueous humor production
- ↓ IOP

**Caution**: Systemic absorption → bradycardia, bronchospasm

**NEET PG**: Timolol eye drops for glaucoma

---

## Practice MCQs

1. **Drug of choice for pheochromocytoma preoperative management?**
   Answer: Phenoxybenzamine

2. **Antidote for norepinephrine extravasation?**
   Answer: Phentolamine (local infiltration)

3. **α1-blocker causing first-dose hypotension?**
   Answer: Prazosin

4. **Drug of choice for BPH with minimal BP effects?**
   Answer: Tamsulosin

5. **β-blocker contraindicated in asthma?**
   Answer: Propranolol (non-selective)

6. **β-blocker safest in asthma?**
   Answer: β1-selective (atenolol, metoprolol) - still use caution

7. **Ultra-short acting β-blocker for perioperative use?**
   Answer: Esmolol

8. **Drug of choice for hypertensive emergency in pregnancy?**
   Answer: Labetalol

9. **Three β-blockers proven in heart failure?**
   Answer: Carvedilol, Metoprolol (succinate), Bisoprolol

10. **Topical β-blocker for glaucoma?**
    Answer: Timolol
