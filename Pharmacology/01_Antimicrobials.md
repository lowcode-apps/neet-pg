# ANTIMICROBIAL AGENTS - NEET PG Study Material

## Classification of Antibiotics

### By Mechanism of Action
1. **Cell Wall Synthesis Inhibitors**: Beta-lactams, Glycopeptides
2. **Protein Synthesis Inhibitors**: Aminoglycosides, Macrolides, Tetracyclines
3. **DNA Synthesis Inhibitors**: Fluoroquinolones
4. **RNA Synthesis Inhibitors**: Rifampicin
5. **Folate Synthesis Inhibitors**: Sulfonamides, Trimethoprim
6. **Cell Membrane Disruptors**: Polymyxins, Daptomycin

---

## BETA-LACTAM ANTIBIOTICS

### Mechanism of Action
- Inhibit **transpeptidase** (penicillin-binding proteins)
- Prevent **peptidoglycan cross-linking**
- Activate autolysins
- **Bactericidal**

**NEET PG**: Beta-lactams are **bactericidal** (kill bacteria)

---

### 1. PENICILLINS

#### Natural Penicillins
**Penicillin G (Benzylpenicillin)**
- IV/IM route (acid-labile)
- **Spectrum**: Gram-positive cocci, Treponema, Actinomyces
- **Uses**: Streptococcal pharyngitis, syphilis, gas gangrene
- **DOC for**: Syphilis, actinomycosis

**Penicillin V (Phenoxymethyl penicillin)**
- Oral (acid-stable)
- Streptococcal pharyngitis

**NEET PG**: Benzathine penicillin G - Long-acting (IM, for syphilis, RF prophylaxis)

#### Anti-Staphylococcal Penicillins
**Cloxacillin, Dicloxacillin, Nafcillin, Oxacillin**
- **β-lactamase resistant** (bulky side chain)
- **Uses**: MSSA (Methicillin-sensitive Staph aureus)

**NEET PG**: Cloxacillin = DOC for MSSA

#### Broad-Spectrum Penicillins (Aminopenicillins)
**Ampicillin, Amoxicillin**
- **Spectrum**: Gram-positive + some Gram-negative (H. influenzae, E. coli, Proteus)
- **Uses**: UTI, respiratory infections, endocarditis prophylaxis, Listeria
- **Amoxicillin**: Better oral absorption than ampicillin

**NEET PG**:
- **Ampicillin**: DOC for Listeria monocytogenes
- **Amoxicillin**: First-line for H. pylori (with clarithromycin + PPI)

#### Anti-Pseudomonal Penicillins
**Piperacillin, Ticarcillin**
- **Spectrum**: Pseudomonas, broad Gram-negative
- **Always combined with β-lactamase inhibitor** (Piperacillin-Tazobactam)

**NEET PG**: Piperacillin-Tazobactam = Broad-spectrum, covers Pseudomonas

---

### β-Lactamase Inhibitors
**Clavulanic acid, Sulbactam, Tazobactam**
- **No intrinsic antimicrobial activity**
- Irreversibly inhibit β-lactamases

**Combinations**:
- **Amoxicillin-Clavulanate** (Augmentin)
- **Ampicillin-Sulbactam**
- **Piperacillin-Tazobactam**

**NEET PG**: β-lactamase inhibitors restore activity against β-lactamase-producing bacteria

---

### 2. CEPHALOSPORINS

**Mechanism**: Same as penicillins (inhibit cell wall synthesis)
**Structure**: β-lactam ring + dihydrothiazine ring

#### 1st Generation
**Cefazolin, Cephalexin, Cefadroxil**
- **Spectrum**: Gram-positive cocci, some Gram-negative
- **Uses**: Skin infections, surgical prophylaxis (cefazolin)

**NEET PG**: Cefazolin = Surgical prophylaxis

#### 2nd Generation
**Cefuroxime, Cefoxitin, Cefaclor**
- **Spectrum**: ↑ Gram-negative coverage (H. influenzae, Moraxella)
- **Cefoxitin**: Anaerobic coverage (B. fragilis)

#### 3rd Generation
**Ceftriaxone, Cefotaxime, Ceftazidime**
- **Spectrum**: Excellent Gram-negative, crosses BBB
- **Ceftriaxone/Cefotaxime**: Meningitis, gonorrhea, typhoid
- **Ceftazidime**: Pseudomonas coverage

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- **Ceftriaxone**: DOC for meningitis (Neisseria, Pneumococcus, H. influenzae), gonorrhea, typhoid
- Crosses BBB
- Long half-life (once-daily dosing)

#### 4th Generation
**Cefepime**
- **Spectrum**: Gram-positive + Gram-negative + Pseudomonas
- **Use**: Febrile neutropenia

#### 5th Generation
**Ceftaroline**
- **Spectrum**: MRSA coverage + Gram-negatives

**NEET PG**: Only cephalosporin covering MRSA

---

### 3. CARBAPENEMS
**Imipenem, Meropenem, Ertapenem, Doripenem**

**Spectrum**: **Broadest-spectrum** antibiotics
- Gram-positive + Gram-negative + Anaerobes + Pseudomonas
- **Resistant to most β-lactamases**

**Uses**: Severe hospital-acquired infections, multidrug-resistant organisms

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- **Imipenem**: Always given with **cilastatin** (inhibits renal dehydropeptidase)
- **Meropenem**: Preferred for CNS infections (lower seizure risk)
- **Ertapenem**: NO Pseudomonas coverage

**Adverse Effects**: Seizures (Imipenem > Meropenem)

---

### 4. MONOBACTAMS
**Aztreonam**
- **Spectrum**: Gram-negative **ONLY** (including Pseudomonas)
- **No cross-reactivity** with other β-lactams
- **Use**: Penicillin-allergic patients with Gram-negative infections

**NEET PG**: Safe in penicillin allergy (no cross-reactivity)

---

### Adverse Effects of β-Lactams
1. **Hypersensitivity** (most common): Rash, anaphylaxis
   - **Cross-reactivity**: 10% between penicillins & cephalosporins
   - **No cross-reactivity**: Aztreonam
2. **Diarrhea**: C. difficile colitis
3. **Interstitial nephritis**: Methicillin
4. **Hemolytic anemia**: High-dose penicillin
5. **Seizures**: Imipenem (high doses)
6. **Disulfiram-like reaction**: Cefoperazone (has methylthiotetrazole side chain)

**NEET PG**: Cefoperazone & Cefotetan → Disulfiram-like reaction with alcohol

---

## GLYCOPEPTIDES

### VANCOMYCIN
**Mechanism**: Inhibits cell wall synthesis (binds D-Ala-D-Ala of peptidoglycan)

**Spectrum**: Gram-positive **ONLY** (cannot cross Gram-negative outer membrane)

**Uses**:
- **MRSA** infections (DOC)
- **C. difficile** colitis (oral route)
- Endocarditis (with gentamicin)
- Penicillin-allergic patients

**Route**: **IV** (poorly absorbed orally) - except for C. difficile (oral)

**Adverse Effects**:
1. **Red man syndrome**: Histamine release (NOT allergy)
   - Prevention: Slow infusion, antihistamines
2. **Nephrotoxicity**: Especially with aminoglycosides
3. **Ototoxicity**
4. **Thrombophlebitis**

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- **DOC for MRSA**
- **Oral vancomycin** = DOC for C. difficile colitis
- **Red man syndrome** ≠ Allergy (slow infusion prevents it)

---

## AMINOGLYCOSIDES

**Gentamicin, Tobramycin, Amikacin, Streptomycin, Neomycin**

**Mechanism**:
- Inhibit **30S ribosomal subunit**
- Cause misreading of mRNA
- **Bactericidal** (concentration-dependent)

**Spectrum**: Aerobic Gram-negative (E. coli, Pseudomonas, Klebsiella)

**Uses**:
- **Severe Gram-negative infections** (sepsis, UTI)
- **Synergy with β-lactams**: Endocarditis, enterococcal infections
- **Gentamicin**: Most commonly used
- **Amikacin**: Resistant Gram-negatives
- **Streptomycin**: TB (second-line), plague, tularemia
- **Neomycin**: Topical use only (too toxic systemically), hepatic encephalopathy (oral)

**NEET PG**: Aminoglycosides require **O2** to enter bacteria (ineffective against anaerobes)

**Adverse Effects**:
1. **Nephrotoxicity**: Acute tubular necrosis (dose-related, reversible)
2. **Ototoxicity**: Irreversible (vestibular > cochlear)
3. **Neuromuscular blockade**: Worsens myasthenia gravis
4. **Teratogenic**: Fetal ototoxicity

**NEET PG**:
- **Once-daily dosing** (concentration-dependent killing, long PAE)
- **Monitor**: Trough levels (nephrotoxicity), peak levels (efficacy)
- **Contraindicated**: Pregnancy, myasthenia gravis

---

## MACROLIDES

**Erythromycin, Azithromycin, Clarithromycin**

**Mechanism**: Inhibit **50S ribosomal subunit** (block translocation)
**Effect**: **Bacteriostatic** (bactericidal at high concentrations)

**Spectrum**: Gram-positive, atypicals (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia, Legionella)

**Uses**:
- **Erythromycin**: Atypical pneumonia, Campylobacter enteritis, acne (topical), prokinetic
- **Azithromycin**: CAP, STDs (Chlamydia, gonorrhea), traveler's diarrhea, MAC prophylaxis (HIV)
- **Clarithromycin**: H. pylori, MAC, atypicals

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- **Azithromycin**: Long half-life, good tissue penetration, once-daily dosing
- **Alternative to penicillin** in allergic patients
- **Erythromycin**: Motilin agonist (prokinetic effect)

**Adverse Effects**:
1. **GI upset**: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea (erythromycin > azithromycin)
2. **QT prolongation**: Torsades de pointes
3. **Hepatotoxicity**: Cholestatic jaundice (erythromycin estolate)
4. **Drug interactions**: CYP3A4 inhibitor (increases levels of warfarin, statins, cyclosporine)

**NEET PG**: Erythromycin is a **motilin agonist** (used as prokinetic)

---

## TETRACYCLINES

**Tetracycline, Doxycycline, Minocycline, Tigecycline**

**Mechanism**: Inhibit **30S ribosomal subunit** (block aminoacyl-tRNA binding)
**Effect**: **Bacteriostatic**

**Spectrum**: Broad (Gram+, Gram-, atypicals, Rickettsiae, spirochetes)

**Uses**:
- **Doxycycline**:
  - Atypical pneumonia (Mycoplasma, Chlamydia)
  - **Rickettsial infections** (DOC)
  - Lyme disease
  - Malaria prophylaxis
  - Acne
  - Brucellosis
- **Tigecycline**: MRSA, VRE, multidrug-resistant Gram-negatives

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- **DOC for Rickettsiae** (scrub typhus, RMSF)
- **DOC for Chlamydia trachomatis** (genital infections)

**Adverse Effects**:
1. **GI upset**
2. **Tooth discoloration & bone growth inhibition**: **Contraindicated in children <8 years & pregnancy**
3. **Photosensitivity**: Severe sunburn
4. **Hepatotoxicity**: Pregnant women
5. **Fanconi syndrome**: Expired tetracycline
6. **Chelation**: Binds Ca2+, Mg2+, Fe2+ (avoid dairy, antacids)

**NEET PG**:
- **Contraindicated**: Children <8 years, pregnancy
- Avoid with milk/antacids (chelation)

---

## FLUOROQUINOLONES

**Ciprofloxacin, Levofloxacin, Moxifloxacin, Norfloxacin**

**Mechanism**: Inhibit **DNA gyrase** (topoisomerase II) & **topoisomerase IV**
**Effect**: **Bactericidal** (concentration-dependent)

**Spectrum**:

| Drug | Gram-negative | Gram-positive | Atypicals | Anaerobes |
|------|---------------|---------------|-----------|-----------|
| Ciprofloxacin | ++++ (Pseudomonas) | + | ++ | - |
| Levofloxacin | +++ | +++ | +++ | + |
| Moxifloxacin | ++ | +++ | +++ | +++ |

**Uses**:
- **Ciprofloxacin**: UTI, GI infections (Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter), anthrax, Pseudomonas
- **Levofloxacin**: CAP, TB (second-line)
- **Moxifloxacin**: CAP, TB (second-line), better anaerobic coverage
- **Norfloxacin**: UTI only (does not achieve systemic levels)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- **Best bioavailability** among antibiotics (oral ≈ IV)
- **Ciprofloxacin**: Best for Pseudomonas
- **Contraindicated**: Children, pregnancy (cartilage damage)

**Adverse Effects**:
1. **Tendinitis & tendon rupture**: Achilles tendon (elderly, steroids)
2. **Cartilage damage**: Contraindicated in children & pregnancy
3. **QT prolongation**
4. **CNS**: Seizures, confusion (especially in elderly)
5. **Photosensitivity**
6. **Hepatotoxicity**
7. **Peripheral neuropathy**

**NEET PG**: Achilles tendon rupture = Fluoroquinolones

## ANTITUBERCULAR DRUGS

### First-Line Drugs (**RIPES**)

**R**ifampicin, **I**soniazid, **P**yrazinamide, **E**thambutol, **S**treptomycin

#### RIFAMPICIN
**Mechanism**: Inhibits **RNA polymerase**

**Uses**: TB, Leprosy, Meningococcal prophylaxis, Staphylococcal infections

**Adverse Effects**:
- **Orange/red discoloration** of body fluids (tears, urine, sweat)
- **Hepatotoxicity**
- **CYP450 inducer**: ↓ Effectiveness of OCP, warfarin, antiretrovirals

**NEET PG**: Most potent bactericidal anti-TB drug

#### ISONIAZID (INH)
**Mechanism**: Inhibits **mycolic acid synthesis** (via inhibition of enoyl-ACP reductase)

**Adverse Effects**:
- **Peripheral neuropathy**: Give **pyridoxine (Vit B6)** prophylaxis
- **Hepatotoxicity**: Most common cause of anti-TB drug-induced hepatitis
- **Drug-induced lupus**

**NEET PG**: Give pyridoxine with INH to prevent neuropathy

#### PYRAZINAMIDE (PZA)
**Mechanism**: Unknown (requires acidic pH)

**Adverse Effects**:
- **Hepatotoxicity**
- **Hyperuricemia** (inhibits uric acid excretion) → Gout

**NEET PG**: Causes hyperuricemia & gout

#### ETHAMBUTOL
**Mechanism**: Inhibits **arabinosyl transferase** (cell wall synthesis)

**Adverse Effects**:
- **Optic neuritis**: Decreased visual acuity, red-green color blindness (dose-related, reversible)

**NEET PG**: Monitor visual acuity & color vision

#### STREPTOMYCIN
**Mechanism**: Aminoglycoside (30S inhibition)

**Adverse Effects**: Ototoxicity, nephrotoxicity

**NEET PG**: Now second-line (replaced by ethambutol in first-line regimen)

---

### TB Treatment Regimens

**Category 1** (New smear-positive PTB):
- **Intensive phase** (2 months): HRZE (daily)
- **Continuation phase** (4 months): HR (daily)
- Total: **6 months**

**Category 2** (Relapse, failure, TAD after default):
- **Intensive phase** (3 months): HRZES (2 months) + HRZE (1 month)
- **Continuation phase** (5 months): HRE
- Total: **8 months**

**NEET PG**: Standard ATT = 6 months (2HRZE + 4HR)

## ANTIFUNGAL DRUGS

### POLYENES
**Amphotericin B, Nystatin**

**Mechanism**: Binds **ergosterol** in fungal cell membrane → Pore formation → Leakage

**Uses**:
- **Amphotericin B**: Systemic mycoses (DOC for serious fungal infections), cryptococcal meningitis, mucormycosis
- **Nystatin**: Topical use (oral thrush, vaginal candidiasis)

**Adverse Effects** (Amphotericin B):
- **Nephrotoxicity** ("Ampho-terrible")
- **Infusion-related**: Fever, chills, rigors ("shake and bake")
- **Hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia**
- **Anemia**

**NEET PG**:
- **Liposomal formulation**: ↓ Nephrotoxicity
- **DOC for mucormycosis**

---

### AZOLES

**Fluconazole, Itraconazole, Voriconazole, Posaconazole**

**Mechanism**: Inhibit **14α-demethylase** (fungal CYP450) → ↓ Ergosterol synthesis

**Uses**:
- **Fluconazole**: Candida (except C. krusei), cryptococcal meningitis (AIDS), prophylaxis
- **Itraconazole**: Aspergillus, Blastomyces, Histoplasma, Sporothrix, onychomycosis
- **Voriconazole**: **DOC for Aspergillus**, Candida (including fluconazole-resistant)

**Adverse Effects**:
- **Hepatotoxicity**
- **Drug interactions**: CYP450 inhibitors
- **Voriconazole**: Visual disturbances, photosensitivity

**NEET PG**:
- **Voriconazole**: DOC for Aspergillus
- **Fluconazole**: Ineffective against Aspergillus, C. krusei

## Practice MCQs

1. **DOC for MRSA?**
   Answer: Vancomycin

2. **Only cephalosporin covering MRSA?**
   Answer: Ceftaroline (5th generation)

3. **DOC for Listeria?**
   Answer: Ampicillin

4. **Antibiotic causing red man syndrome?**
   Answer: Vancomycin

5. **DOC for Rickettsiae?**
   Answer: Doxycycline

6. **Antibiotic safe in penicillin allergy?**
   Answer: Aztreonam

7. **Most common adverse effect of aminoglycosides?**
   Answer: Nephrotoxicity & ototoxicity

8. **Anti-TB drug causing optic neuritis?**
   Answer: Ethambutol
