# NEONATAL JAUNDICE - NEET PG Study Material

## Definition
Yellow discoloration of skin and sclera due to ↑ serum bilirubin (>5 mg/dL)

**Visible jaundice**: When bilirubin >5-7 mg/dL

**Clinical examination**: Progresses **cephalocaudal** (head → toe)

**NEET PG**: Jaundice visible when bilirubin >5 mg/dL

---

## Bilirubin Metabolism in Newborn

```
RBC breakdown → Heme
      ↓
Unconjugated bilirubin (indirect, lipid-soluble)
      ↓
Transported bound to albumin
      ↓
Liver uptake + Conjugation (via UDP-glucuronyl transferase)
      ↓
Conjugated bilirubin (direct, water-soluble)
      ↓
Excreted in bile → Intestine
      ↓
Bacterial flora → Urobilinogen → Excreted
```

**Why newborns prone to jaundice?**
1. ↑ RBC breakdown (shorter RBC lifespan: 70-90 days vs 120 days)
2. ↑ RBC mass (higher hematocrit)
3. Immature liver enzyme (UDP-glucuronyl transferase)
4. ↑ Enterohepatic circulation (β-glucuronidase in gut)
5. Delayed gut colonization (sterile gut → no bacterial conversion)

---

## Classification

### By Onset

| Type | Onset | Causes |
|------|-------|--------|
| **Early** | <24 hours | **ALWAYS PATHOLOGICAL** (hemolysis, sepsis) |
| **Physiological** | 2-3 days | Normal adaptation |
| **Late** | >2 weeks | Breast milk jaundice, hypothyroidism, cholestasis |

**NEET PG High-Yield**: Jaundice in **first 24 hours** is ALWAYS pathological (requires investigation)

### By Bilirubin Type

**1. Unconjugated Hyperbilirubinemia** (Indirect > Direct):
- Increased production or decreased conjugation
- **Most common** in neonates

**2. Conjugated Hyperbilirubinemia** (Direct >2 mg/dL or >20% of total):
- Cholestasis, biliary obstruction
- **ALWAYS PATHOLOGICAL**

---

## UNCONJUGATED HYPERBILIRUBINEMIA

### 1. PHYSIOLOGICAL JAUNDICE

**Definition**: Benign, self-limiting jaundice due to normal neonatal adaptation

**Characteristics**:
- **Onset**: 2-3 days (NOT <24 hours, NOT >2 weeks)
- **Peak**: Day 3-5
- **Duration**: 1 week (term), 2 weeks (preterm)
- **Bilirubin**: <12 mg/dL (term), <15 mg/dL (preterm)
- **Type**: Unconjugated
- **Baby**: Well, feeding normally

**Pathophysiology**:
- ↑ RBC breakdown
- Immature liver conjugation
- ↑ Enterohepatic circulation

**Management**:
- **No treatment** needed
- Continue breastfeeding
- Monitor

**NEET PG**: Physiological jaundice appears after 24 hours, peaks on day 3-5, resolves by 1 week

---

### 2. BREASTFEEDING JAUNDICE (Early Onset)

**Definition**: Jaundice in **first week** due to inadequate breastfeeding

**Onset**: Days 2-5

**Cause**:
- **Insufficient milk intake** → Dehydration
- ↓ Caloric intake → ↓ Hepatic uptake
- ↑ Enterohepatic circulation

**Diagnosis**:
- Poor weight gain
- Fewer wet diapers (<6/day)
- Concentrated urine

**Management**:
- **Increase frequency** of breastfeeding (8-12 times/day)
- **Do NOT stop breastfeeding**
- Lactation support
- Supplementation if needed

**NEET PG**: Breastfeeding jaundice = Inadequate intake, improve feeding (do NOT stop breastfeeding)

---

### 3. BREAST MILK JAUNDICE (Late Onset)

**Definition**: Prolonged jaundice (>2 weeks) in **well-fed, thriving** breastfed baby

**Onset**: After 1 week (peaks at 2-3 weeks)

**Duration**: Can persist 3-12 weeks

**Cause**:
- **β-glucuronidase** in breast milk → ↑ Deconjugation → ↑ Enterohepatic circulation
- **Pregnanediol** in breast milk (inhibits conjugation)

**Diagnosis**:
- Thriving baby, good weight gain
- Unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia
- Bilirubin falls if breastfeeding stopped (diagnostic test)

**Management**:
- **Continue breastfeeding** (safe, no treatment needed)
- Reassure parents
- If bilirubin very high: Temporarily stop breastfeeding × 24-48 hours (bilirubin drops) → Resume

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- **Breast milk jaundice**: Prolonged (>2 weeks), thriving baby, continue breastfeeding
- **Breastfeeding jaundice**: Early (<1 week), poor feeding, increase feeding frequency

---

### 4. HEMOLYTIC JAUNDICE

#### A. ABO Incompatibility (Most Common)

**Setup**:
- **Mother**: Blood group O
- **Baby**: Blood group A or B

**Pathophysiology**:
- Maternal anti-A or anti-B IgG antibodies cross placenta
- Bind to fetal RBCs → Hemolysis

**Features**:
- **Onset**: First 24 hours (can be delayed to 2-3 days)
- **Severity**: Mild to moderate (less severe than Rh)
- **Subsequent pregnancies**: Can occur in **first pregnancy**
- **Anemia**: Mild
- **Direct Coombs test (DAT)**: Weakly positive or negative

**NEET PG**:
- **ABO incompatibility**: Most common hemolytic disease
- Can occur in **first pregnancy** (unlike Rh)
- Mother O, Baby A/B

---

#### B. Rh Incompatibility

**Setup**:
- **Mother**: Rh-negative
- **Baby**: Rh-positive

**Pathophysiology**:
- Prior sensitization (previous pregnancy, transfusion, abortion)
- Maternal anti-D IgG crosses placenta → Hemolysis

**Features**:
- **First pregnancy**: Usually NOT affected (unless prior sensitization)
- **Subsequent pregnancies**: Worsening severity
- **Onset**: First 24 hours
- **Severity**: **Severe** (can cause hydrops fetalis)
- **Anemia**: Severe
- **Direct Coombs test**: **Strongly positive**

**Complications**:
- Severe anemia
- Hydrops fetalis (in utero)
- Kernicterus (if untreated)

**Prevention**:
- **Anti-D immunoglobulin** (RhoGAM) to Rh-negative mother:
  - At **28 weeks** gestation
  - Within **72 hours** of delivery (if baby Rh+)
  - After any sensitizing event (abortion, amniocentesis, APH)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- **Rh disease**: Severe, first baby usually spared, subsequent pregnancies worsen
- **Anti-D given at 28 weeks + within 72 hours of delivery**
- **Direct Coombs test strongly positive**

---

#### C. G6PD Deficiency

**Inheritance**: X-linked recessive (males affected)

**Trigger**: Oxidative stress (infection, drugs, fava beans)

**Drugs to avoid**:
- Antimalarials (primaquine, chloroquine)
- Sulfonamides
- Nitrofurantoin
- Aspirin
- Vitamin K (large doses)

**Features**:
- Acute hemolysis
- Heinz bodies, bite cells on blood smear

**Diagnosis**: G6PD enzyme assay (after acute episode resolves)

**NEET PG**: G6PD deficiency = X-linked, avoid oxidant drugs

---

#### D. Hereditary Spherocytosis

**Inheritance**: Autosomal dominant

**Features**:
- Spherical RBCs (lack central pallor)
- ↑ Osmotic fragility
- Splenomegaly (later)

**Diagnosis**: Osmotic fragility test

---

### 5. CRIGLER-NAJJAR SYNDROME

**Type I** (Severe):
- **Complete absence** of UDP-glucuronyl transferase
- **Bilirubin**: Very high (>20 mg/dL)
- **Onset**: First days of life
- **Treatment**: Phototherapy (lifelong), liver transplant
- **Prognosis**: High risk of kernicterus

**Type II** (Mild):
- **Partial deficiency** of enzyme
- **Bilirubin**: 6-20 mg/dL
- **Treatment**: **Phenobarbital** (induces enzyme)
- **Prognosis**: Better

**NEET PG**: Crigler-Najjar Type I = Lifelong phototherapy, Type II = Phenobarbital responsive

---

### 6. GILBERT SYNDROME

**Inheritance**: Autosomal recessive

**Pathophysiology**: Mild ↓ UDP-glucuronyl transferase

**Features**:
- Mild unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia (<3 mg/dL)
- Triggered by stress, fasting, illness
- **Benign**, no treatment needed

**NEET PG**: Gilbert syndrome = Benign, no treatment needed

---

### 7. PREMATURITY

Premature infants more prone to jaundice:
- Immature liver enzymes
- ↑ RBC breakdown
- Delayed gut colonization

**Bilirubin levels**: Higher and prolonged compared to term infants

---

### 8. POLYCYTHEMIA

**Causes**: Twin-twin transfusion, maternal diabetes, delayed cord clamping

**Mechanism**: ↑ RBC mass → ↑ Breakdown → ↑ Bilirubin

**NEET PG**: Maternal diabetes → Polycythemia → Jaundice

---

### 9. CEPHALOHEMATOMA/BRUISING

Large extravascular blood collection → Breakdown → ↑ Bilirubin

**NEET PG**: Birth trauma → Cephalohematoma → Jaundice

---

### 10. SEPSIS

Infection → Hemolysis + impaired hepatic function → Jaundice

**Features**: Sick baby, fever/hypothermia, poor feeding, lethargy

---

## CONJUGATED HYPERBILIRUBINEMIA (Cholestatic Jaundice)

**Definition**: Direct bilirubin >2 mg/dL OR >20% of total bilirubin

**Significance**: **ALWAYS PATHOLOGICAL** (requires urgent investigation)

### Causes

#### Biliary Obstruction

**1. Biliary Atresia** (Most Common):
- **Onset**: After 2 weeks
- **Features**: Dark urine, pale/acholic stools, hepatomegaly
- **Investigation**: USG (absent/small gallbladder), HIDA scan (no excretion), liver biopsy
- **Treatment**: **Kasai procedure** (hepatoportoenterostomy) - best if <60 days
- **Prognosis**: Poor if delayed (liver transplant needed)

**NEET PG**: Biliary atresia = Acholic stools, do Kasai before 60 days

**2. Choledochal Cyst**:
- Congenital cystic dilatation of bile duct
- **Triad**: Jaundice + Abdominal mass + Pain
- **Diagnosis**: USG
- **Treatment**: Surgical excision

**3. Inspissated Bile Syndrome**:
- Thick bile plugs obstruct ducts
- Associated with hemolysis, TPN

---

#### Hepatocellular

**1. Neonatal Hepatitis**:
- **Idiopathic** (most common)
- Viral: CMV, HSV, Rubella, Hepatitis B
- **Features**: Conjugated jaundice, hepatomegaly
- **Diagnosis**: Viral serology, liver biopsy
- **Treatment**: Supportive

**2. Metabolic Disorders**:
- **Galactosemia**: Reducing substances in urine, hepatomegaly, cataracts
- **Tyrosinemia**
- **α1-antitrypsin deficiency**

**3. TORCH Infections**:
- Toxoplasma, Rubella, CMV, HSV
- **Features**: IUGR, hepatosplenomegaly, thrombocytopenia, microcephaly

**NEET PG**: Conjugated jaundice + acholic stools = Biliary atresia (do Kasai <60 days)

---

## Complications of Hyperbilirubinemia

### KERNICTERUS (Bilirubin Encephalopathy)

**Definition**: **Permanent neurological damage** due to bilirubin deposition in basal ganglia

**Risk Factors**:
- Very high unconjugated bilirubin (>25-30 mg/dL)
- Prematurity
- Acidosis, hypoxia, sepsis
- Hypoalbuminemia
- Hemolysis

**Clinical Phases**:

**Phase 1** (Early - first days):
- Poor feeding, lethargy
- Hypotonia
- High-pitched cry

**Phase 2** (Mid - 1 week):
- Hypertonia, opisthotonos
- Fever
- Shrill cry

**Phase 3** (Advanced - after 1 week):
- **Irreversible damage**

**Chronic Sequelae** (Tetrad):
1. **Choreoathetoid cerebral palsy** (movement disorder)
2. **Sensorineural hearing loss** (high-frequency)
3. **Upward gaze palsy**
4. **Dental enamel hypoplasia**

**Prevention**:
- Aggressive phototherapy
- Exchange transfusion if indicated

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- **Kernicterus**: Unconjugated bilirubin >25-30 mg/dL
- **Tetrad**: Choreoathetosis + Hearing loss + Gaze palsy + Dental dysplasia

---

## Investigations

### All Cases
1. **Serum bilirubin** (Total, Direct, Indirect)
2. **Blood group** (Mother & Baby) + Rh type
3. **Direct Coombs test (DAT)**: Hemolysis
4. **CBC**: Anemia, polycythemia, sepsis
5. **Peripheral smear**: Spherocytes, fragmented RBCs
6. **Reticulocyte count**: ↑ in hemolysis

### If Indicated
- **G6PD assay**: If hemolysis suspected
- **Sepsis workup**: Blood culture, CRP
- **Thyroid function**: If prolonged jaundice
- **Urine for reducing substances**: Galactosemia
- **Liver function tests**: If conjugated jaundice
- **USG abdomen**: Biliary atresia, choledochal cyst

**NEET PG**: Direct Coombs test (DAT) positive = Immune hemolysis

---

## Management

### General Measures
- **Adequate hydration** & nutrition
- **Increase feeding frequency** (↓ enterohepatic circulation)
- **Avoid drugs** that displace bilirubin from albumin (sulfonamides, aspirin)

---

### Phototherapy

**Mechanism**: Converts unconjugated bilirubin → Water-soluble isomers (excreted without conjugation)

**Light**: Blue-green light (wavelength 425-475 nm) most effective

**Indications** (depends on age, risk factors):

**Term healthy newborn**:

| Age | Phototherapy | Exchange Transfusion |
|-----|--------------|----------------------|
| <24 hours | ≥12 mg/dL | ≥20 mg/dL |
| 24-48 hours | ≥15 mg/dL | ≥25 mg/dL |
| 49-72 hours | ≥18 mg/dL | ≥25 mg/dL |
| >72 hours | ≥20 mg/dL | ≥25 mg/dL |

**Preterm/sick**: Lower thresholds

**Technique**:
- Baby undressed (only diaper)
- **Eye protection** (prevent retinal damage)
- Monitor temperature (risk of hypothermia/hyperthermia)
- Ensure adequate hydration

**Side Effects**:
- **Bronze baby syndrome**: Grayish-brown discoloration (if conjugated hyperbilirubinemia)
- Loose stools
- Skin rash
- Dehydration

**NEET PG**: Phototherapy contraindicated in conjugated hyperbilirubinemia (bronze baby syndrome)

---

### Exchange Transfusion

**Indication**:
- Bilirubin **>25 mg/dL** (term)
- **Failure of phototherapy**
- **Signs of kernicterus**
- **Severe hemolysis**

**Procedure**:
- Remove baby's blood, replace with donor blood (double volume exchange)
- Via umbilical vein

**Complications**:
- Infection, thrombosis
- Electrolyte imbalance (hypocalcemia, hyperkalemia)
- Necrotizing enterocolitis
- Thrombocytopenia

**NEET PG**: Exchange transfusion if bilirubin >25 mg/dL or signs of kernicterus

---

### IV Immunoglobulin (IVIG)

**Indication**: **Isoimmune hemolysis** (Rh, ABO incompatibility) with rising bilirubin despite phototherapy

**Dose**: 0.5-1 g/kg IV

**Mechanism**: Blocks Fc receptors → ↓ Hemolysis

**NEET PG**: IVIG used in isoimmune hemolysis (reduces need for exchange transfusion)

---

## Approach to Neonatal Jaundice

```
Jaundice detected
      ↓
Check onset, bilirubin level, baby's condition
      ↓
├─ <24 hours → PATHOLOGICAL
│    ↓
│  Investigate (Blood group, Coombs, CBC, smear)
│    ↓
│  Hemolysis likely → Phototherapy/Exchange
│
├─ 2-14 days, well baby, unconjugated
│    ↓
│  Likely physiological/breast milk jaundice
│    ↓
│  Monitor, continue breastfeeding
│
└─ >2 weeks OR conjugated
     ↓
   Prolonged/cholestatic jaundice
     ↓
   Investigate (LFT, USG, TORCH, metabolic screen)
     ↓
   Treat underlying cause
```

## Memory Aids

### Causes of Jaundice <24 hours
"**HIS**"
- **H**emolysis (ABO, Rh, G6PD)
- **I**nfection (sepsis, TORCH)
- **S**ubgaleal hemorrhage/Cephalohematoma

### Kernicterus Tetrad
"**CHUD**"
- **C**horeoathetoid CP
- **H**earing loss
- **U**pward gaze palsy
- **D**ental dysplasia

### Breastfeeding vs Breast Milk Jaundice
"**Early Eat, Late Leak**"
- **Breastfeeding jaundice**: **Early** (<1 week), inadequate **eating**
- **Breast milk jaundice**: **Late** (>1 week), substance in breast milk **leaking**
