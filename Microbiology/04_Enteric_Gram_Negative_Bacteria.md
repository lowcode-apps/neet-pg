# ENTERIC GRAM-NEGATIVE BACTERIA - NEET PG Study Material

## ENTEROBACTERIACEAE - Family Overview

### General Characteristics

**Morphology**:
- **Gram-negative bacilli** (rods)
- **Facultative anaerobes**
- **Non-spore forming**
- Most are **motile** (peritrichous flagella) - except Klebsiella, Shigella

**Growth**:
- **MacConkey agar**: Lactose fermentation differentiates
  - **Lactose fermenters**: Pink colonies (E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterobacter)
  - **Non-lactose fermenters**: Colorless (Salmonella, Shigella, Proteus)

**Key tests**:
- **Catalase**: Positive
- **Oxidase**: **Negative** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (differentiates from Pseudomonas)

**Antigens**:
- **O antigen** (somatic) - LPS, heat-stable
- **H antigen** (flagellar) - protein, heat-labile
- **K antigen** (capsular) - polysaccharide

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Enterobacteriaceae = Oxidase negative, facultative anaerobes
- MacConkey agar differentiates lactose fermenters (pink) from non-fermenters (colorless)

---

## ESCHERICHIA COLI

### General Characteristics

**Normal flora**:
- **Most common aerobic** commensal in colon
- Vitamin K synthesis
- **Pathogenic strains** have virulence factors

**Identification**:
- **Gram-negative bacilli**
- **Lactose fermenter** (pink on MacConkey)
- **Indole positive**
- **Motile**

**NEET PG**: E. coli = Most common aerobic gut commensal, lactose fermenter

---

### Pathogenic E. coli (Diarrheagenic)

**Classification by pathotype** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:

#### 1. ETEC (Enterotoxigenic E. coli)

**Mechanism**:
- **Heat-labile toxin (LT)**: Similar to cholera toxin
  - ↑ cAMP → ↑ Cl⁻/H₂O secretion
- **Heat-stable toxin (ST)**: ↑ cGMP

**Clinical**:
- **Traveler's diarrhea** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (most common cause)
- **Watery diarrhea** (secretory)
- **Children in developing countries**
- Self-limited (3-5 days)

**Treatment**: Rehydration (usually self-limited)

**NEET PG**: ETEC = Traveler's diarrhea, watery diarrhea, heat-labile & heat-stable toxins

---

#### 2. EPEC (Enteropathogenic E. coli)

**Mechanism**:
- **Attaching & effacing** (destroys microvilli)
- **Bundle-forming pili** (attachment)

**Clinical**:
- **Infantile diarrhea** (developing countries)
- Watery diarrhea
- **No toxin production**

**NEET PG**: EPEC = Infantile diarrhea, attaching-effacing

---

#### 3. EIEC (Enteroinvasive E. coli)

**Mechanism**:
- **Invades colonic epithelium** (like Shigella)
- **Inflammatory diarrhea**

**Clinical**:
- **Dysentery** (bloody diarrhea, fever, cramps)
- Similar to Shigellosis
- Rare

**NEET PG**: EIEC = Dysentery (like Shigella), invasive

---

#### 4. EHEC (Enterohemorrhagic E. coli) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Serotype**: **E. coli O157:H7** (most common)

**Mechanism**:
- **Shiga-like toxin (Verotoxin)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - Inhibits protein synthesis (60S ribosome)
  - Damages vascular endothelium

**Clinical**:
- **Hemorrhagic colitis** (bloody diarrhea, no fever initially)
- **Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (HUS)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
  - **Triad**: Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia + Thrombocytopenia + Acute renal failure
  - **Most common cause** of acute renal failure in children
  - **5-10%** of EHEC infections
  - **Mechanism**: Toxin damages glomerular endothelium

**Source**:
- **Undercooked ground beef** (hamburgers)
- Unpasteurized milk, contaminated water

**Diagnosis**:
- **Sorbitol-MacConkey agar**: O157:H7 does NOT ferment sorbitol (colorless)
- Shiga toxin detection

**Treatment**:
- **Supportive** (hydration)
- **Avoid antibiotics** (may ↑ toxin release → ↑ HUS risk)
- **Avoid antidiarrheals** (prolong pathogen shedding)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- EHEC O157:H7 = Shiga-like toxin, bloody diarrhea, HUS
- HUS = Triad (hemolytic anemia + thrombocytopenia + renal failure)
- Source: Undercooked beef
- Treatment: Supportive, NO antibiotics (↑ HUS risk)

---

#### 5. EAEC (Enteroaggregative E. coli)

**Mechanism**:
- **"Stacked brick" appearance** (aggregation)
- Biofilm formation

**Clinical**:
- **Persistent diarrhea** (>14 days)
- Watery/mucoid
- Children, travelers, HIV+

---

### Extra-intestinal E. coli Infections

**1. Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Most common cause** of UTI (80-90%)
- **Virulence factors**:
  - **P pili** (bind to uroepithelium)
  - K antigen (capsule)
- **Ascending infection** (urethra → bladder → kidney)
- **Cystitis, pyelonephritis**

**2. Neonatal Meningitis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **K1 capsular antigen** (antiphagocytic)
- **#2 cause** (after Group B Strep)
- High mortality/morbidity

**3. Sepsis/Bacteremia**:
- Especially nosocomial, immunocompromised

**4. Intra-abdominal infections**:
- Peritonitis, cholecystitis (mixed with anaerobes)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- E. coli = #1 cause of UTI, #2 cause of neonatal meningitis
- P pili for UTI, K1 capsule for meningitis

---

### Treatment

**Uncomplicated UTI**:
- **Nitrofurantoin, TMP-SMX, Fluoroquinolones**

**Pyelonephritis, severe infections**:
- **Fluoroquinolones** (ciprofloxacin)
- **3rd gen cephalosporins** (ceftriaxone)
- **Carbapenems** (if ESBL - extended-spectrum β-lactamase)

**Diarrhea**:
- **Rehydration** (most important)
- Antibiotics (fluoroquinolones, azithromycin) - if severe, not EHEC

**NEET PG**: UTI = Nitrofurantoin, TMP-SMX; Severe = Fluoroquinolones, ceftriaxone

## SALMONELLA

### General Characteristics

**Classification**:
- **S. enterica** (>2500 serotypes):
  - **S. typhi** (typhoid fever) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **S. paratyphi A, B, C** (paratyphoid)
  - **Non-typhoidal Salmonella** (NTS) - S. enteritidis, S. typhimurium (gastroenteritis)

**Identification**:
- **Gram-negative bacilli**
- **Non-lactose fermenter** (colorless on MacConkey)
- **Motile**
- **H₂S production** (TSI agar - black)

**Antigens**:
- **O antigen** (somatic)
- **H antigen** (flagellar)
- **Vi antigen** (virulence - capsule) - S. typhi only

**NEET PG**: Salmonella = Non-lactose fermenter, H₂S positive, motile

---

### TYPHOID FEVER (Enteric Fever) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Organism**: **Salmonella typhi**

**Transmission**:
- **Fecal-oral** route
- **Human reservoir** only (no animal reservoir)
- Contaminated food/water
- **Chronic carriers** (gallbladder - 2-5%) - "Typhoid Mary"

**Pathogenesis**:
1. **Ingestion** → Small intestine
2. **M cells of Peyer's patches** (invasion)
3. **Mesenteric lymph nodes** (multiplication)
4. **Bacteremia** (dissemination)
5. **Reticuloendothelial system** (liver, spleen, bone marrow)
6. **Re-infection of intestine** (via bile)

**NEET PG**: Typhoid = Invades via M cells (Peyer's patches), disseminates to RES

---

### Clinical Features (Week by Week)

**Week 1**:
- **Fever** (stepladder pattern - gradually rising)
- Headache, malaise
- **Relative bradycardia** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (pulse slower than expected for fever)
- Constipation (adults) > Diarrhea (children)

**Week 2**:
- **High fever** (sustained)
- **Rose spots** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (salmon-colored maculopapular rash on trunk - 30%)
- **Hepatosplenomegaly**
- **Abdominal pain, tenderness** (right lower quadrant)

**Week 3** (complications):
- **Intestinal perforation** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (ileum - Peyer's patches, 1-3%)
  - **Most serious complication**
  - **Peritonitis, shock**
- **Intestinal hemorrhage** (5-10%)
- Cholecystitis
- Encephalopathy ("typhoid state")

**Week 4**: Recovery (if no complications)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Typhoid = Stepladder fever, relative bradycardia, rose spots, hepatosplenomegaly
- Most serious complication = Intestinal perforation (Week 3)

---

### Diagnosis

**Blood culture** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **1st week**: **Most sensitive** (90%)
- Sensitivity decreases after antibiotics

**Stool culture**:
- **3rd-4th week** (best yield)
- Chronic carriers (>1 year)

**Bone marrow culture**:
- **Most sensitive** (even after antibiotics)
- Not routinely done

**Serology - Widal test** ⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- Detects **antibodies** to O & H antigens
- **O antibody** (IgM) - acute infection
- **H antibody** (IgG) - past infection/vaccination
- **≥4-fold rise** in titer (acute vs convalescent) - diagnostic
- **Limitations**:
  - **Low sensitivity/specificity**
  - False positives (endemic areas, vaccination, other infections)
  - **Not useful in endemic areas**
  - Single high titer (≥1:160) suggestive but not diagnostic

**Typhidot**:
- **IgM & IgG** against 50 kDa OMP (outer membrane protein)
- Rapid

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Typhoid diagnosis: Blood culture (Week 1 - best), Stool culture (Week 3-4)
- Widal test = Low sensitivity, ≥4-fold rise diagnostic
- Bone marrow culture = Most sensitive

---

### Treatment

**Antibiotics** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:

**1st line**:
- **Fluoroquinolones** (ciprofloxacin, ofloxacin) - **DOC** in adults
- **Ceftriaxone, Cefotaxime** (3rd gen cephalosporins) - children, pregnancy
- **Azithromycin** - increasing use (fluoroquinolone resistance)

**Older drugs** (now resistance):
- **Chloramphenicol** (historical drug of choice)
- **Ampicillin, TMP-SMX**

**Duration**: **10-14 days**

**MDR typhoid** (multidrug-resistant):
- Resistant to chloramphenicol, ampicillin, TMP-SMX
- Use fluoroquinolones or ceftriaxone

**XDR typhoid** (extensively drug-resistant):
- Resistant to fluoroquinolones also
- Use **azithromycin or carbapenems**

**Chronic carriers**:
- **Ciprofloxacin** × 4-6 weeks
- **Cholecystectomy** (if gallstones)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Typhoid treatment = Fluoroquinolones (adults), Ceftriaxone (children/pregnancy), Azithromycin
- Duration 10-14 days

---

### Prevention

**Sanitation**:
- **Most important** (fecal-oral transmission)
- Safe water, food hygiene

**Vaccines** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:

**1. Ty21a (Oral live attenuated)**:
- **3-4 doses** (every other day)
- Booster every **5 years**
- **Contraindicated**: Immunocompromised, <6 years

**2. Vi polysaccharide (Injectable)**:
- **Single dose** (IM)
- Booster every **2-3 years**
- **>2 years** of age

**3. Typhoid conjugate vaccine (TCV)**:
- Newer, better immune response
- **Single dose**
- Can be given as early as **6 months**

**Efficacy**: 50-80% (NOT 100% - still need food/water precautions)

**NEET PG**: Typhoid vaccines = Ty21a (oral, live), Vi (injectable, polysaccharide)

---

### NON-TYPHOIDAL SALMONELLA (NTS)

**Organisms**: S. enteritidis, S. typhimurium

**Source**:
- **Poultry, eggs** (most common)
- **Animal reservoir** (unlike S. typhi)

**Clinical**:
- **Gastroenteritis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
  - **Watery/bloody diarrhea**
  - **Fever, abdominal cramps**
  - **Self-limited** (3-7 days)
- **Bacteremia** (immunocompromised, sickle cell)
- **Osteomyelitis** (sickle cell disease) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Treatment**:
- **Supportive** (rehydration) - **NO antibiotics** (prolongs carrier state)
- Antibiotics **only if**:
  - Severe disease, immunocompromised, <3 months, >50 years, bacteremia

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- NTS = Gastroenteritis (poultry, eggs), self-limited
- Osteomyelitis in sickle cell disease
- Treatment: Supportive, NO antibiotics (unless severe)

## SHIGELLA

### General Characteristics

**Species**:
- **S. dysenteriae** (Group A) - most severe
- **S. flexneri** (Group B)
- **S. boydii** (Group C)
- **S. sonnei** (Group D) - most common in developed countries

**Identification**:
- **Gram-negative bacilli**
- **Non-lactose fermenter** (colorless on MacConkey)
- **Non-motile** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (vs other Enterobacteriaceae)
- **No H₂S** production

**Transmission**:
- **Fecal-oral** (most contagious enteric pathogen)
- **Very low infectious dose** (10-100 organisms)
- **Human reservoir** only
- **4 Fs**: Fingers, Flies, Food, Feces

**NEET PG**: Shigella = Non-motile, very low infectious dose (10-100)

---

### Pathogenesis

**Mechanism**:
1. **Invades colonic epithelium** (M cells)
2. **Intracellular multiplication**
3. **Cell-to-cell spread** (actin polymerization)
4. **Mucosal ulceration, inflammation**

**Shiga toxin** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (S. dysenteriae type 1):
- Inhibits protein synthesis (60S ribosome)
- **Cytotoxic, enterotoxic, neurotoxic**
- Can cause **HUS** (like EHEC)

**NEET PG**: Shigella invades colonic mucosa, Shiga toxin (S. dysenteriae) causes HUS

---

### Clinical Features

**Dysentery (Bacillary dysentery)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Bloody, mucoid diarrhea** (pus, blood, mucus - "currant jelly")
- **Tenesmus** (painful straining)
- **Fever, abdominal cramps**
- **Lower abdominal pain** (left lower quadrant - sigmoid colon)

**Complications**:
- **Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (HUS)** (S. dysenteriae)
- **Toxic megacolon**
- **Seizures** (children - Shiga toxin)
- **Reactive arthritis** (Reiter's syndrome - HLA-B27)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Shigellosis = Bloody diarrhea, tenesmus, fever
- S. dysenteriae can cause HUS (Shiga toxin)

---

### Diagnosis

**Stool culture**:
- **MacConkey, XLD agar**
- Non-lactose fermenter

**Stool microscopy**:
- **Fecal leukocytes** (PMNs) - abundant
- RBCs

**NEET PG**: Shigellosis = Abundant fecal leukocytes

---

### Treatment

**Antibiotics** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Fluoroquinolones** (ciprofloxacin) - DOC
- **Azithromycin** (children, pregnancy, resistance)
- **Ceftriaxone** (if resistant)

**Duration**: **3-5 days**

**Rehydration**: Important

**AVOID antidiarrheals** (prolong illness)

**NEET PG**: Shigellosis = Fluoroquinolones or azithromycin, 3-5 days

---

## VIBRIO CHOLERAE

### General Characteristics

**Morphology**:
- **Gram-negative, curved rod** (comma-shaped) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Motile** (single polar flagellum - "darting motility")

**Serogroups**:
- **O1** (classical, El Tor biotypes) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - epidemic cholera
- **O139** (Bengal strain) - epidemic
- **Non-O1/non-O139** - mild diarrhea

**Growth**:
- **Alkaline pH** (8.5-9.5)
- **TCBS agar** (thiosulfate-citrate-bile salts-sucrose) - **yellow colonies**
- **Alkaline peptone water** (enrichment)

**Transmission**:
- **Fecal-oral** (contaminated water)
- **Seafood** (shellfish)

**NEET PG**: V. cholerae = Comma-shaped, motile, alkaline pH, TCBS agar

---

### Pathogenesis

**Cholera toxin** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **A-B toxin** (classic example)
- **B subunit**: Binds to **GM1 ganglioside** (intestinal epithelium)
- **A subunit**: **ADP-ribosylates Gs protein**
  - **Permanent activation** of adenylate cyclase
  - **↑↑ cAMP**
  - **↑ Cl⁻ secretion** + **↓ Na⁺ absorption**
  - **Massive water secretion** ("rice water stools")

**No mucosal invasion** (non-inflammatory diarrhea)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Cholera toxin = A-B toxin, ↑ cAMP → massive water secretion
- Binds GM1 ganglioside
- Non-inflammatory (no fecal leukocytes)

---

### Clinical Features

**Cholera** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Sudden onset**
- **Profuse watery diarrhea** ("rice water stools" - flecks of mucus)
  - **1 liter/hour** (can lose 10-20 L/day)
- **Vomiting** (effortless, watery)
- **Severe dehydration**:
  - **Hypotension, tachycardia**
  - **Sunken eyes, dry mucous membranes**
  - **Poor skin turgor**
  - **Muscle cramps** (electrolyte loss)
- **No fever** (non-inflammatory)
- **Metabolic acidosis** (bicarbonate loss)

**Death**: Hypovolemic shock (if untreated)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Cholera = Rice water stools, severe dehydration, NO fever
- Can lose >10 L/day, hypovolemic shock

---

### Diagnosis

**Clinical** (in endemic areas):
- Severe watery diarrhea + dehydration

**Stool culture**:
- **TCBS agar** (yellow colonies)
- **Hanging drop**: Darting motility (stopped by anti-O1 serum)

**Rapid tests**:
- Dipstick (detects LPS)

**Stool microscopy**:
- **NO fecal leukocytes** (non-inflammatory)

**NEET PG**: Cholera = NO fecal leukocytes (vs Shigella - abundant)

---

### Treatment

**Rehydration** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (MOST IMPORTANT):
- **Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS)**:
  - **Mild-moderate**: Oral
  - Contains glucose (↑ Na⁺ absorption via co-transport)
- **IV fluids** (Ringer's lactate):
  - **Severe dehydration, shock**

**Antibiotics** (adjunct):
- **Doxycycline** (single dose or 3 days) - **DOC**
- **Azithromycin** (single dose) - children, pregnancy, resistance
- **Ciprofloxacin**
- **Reduce duration, volume of diarrhea**
- **Reduce shedding**

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Cholera treatment = ORS (most important), antibiotics (adjunct - doxycycline)
- Severe: IV fluids (Ringer's lactate)

---

### Prevention

**Sanitation**:
- **Safe water, hygiene** (most important)

**Vaccines**:
- **Dukoral** (oral, killed whole-cell + B subunit)
- **Shanchol, Euvichol** (oral, killed whole-cell)
- **2 doses**, 50-60% efficacy, short duration (2 years)

**NEET PG**: Cholera vaccine = Oral, killed, moderate efficacy

## KLEBSIELLA

### K. pneumoniae

**Identification**:
- **Gram-negative bacilli**
- **Lactose fermenter** (pink on MacConkey)
- **Non-motile** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Large mucoid capsule** (antiphagocytic)

**Clinical**:

**1. Pneumonia** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Alcoholics, diabetics, COPD** (aspiration)
- **"Currant jelly" sputum** (thick, bloody, mucoid)
- **Upper lobes** (right > left)
- **Necrotizing** (cavitation, abscess formation)
- **Poor prognosis**

**2. UTI**:
- **Nosocomial** (catheter-related)
- **#2-3 cause** (after E. coli)

**3. Sepsis, liver abscess** (diabetes, East Asia)

**Resistance**:
- **ESBL** (Extended-Spectrum β-Lactamase) - common
- **Carbapenemase** (KPC - Klebsiella pneumoniae carbapenemase)

**Treatment**:
- **Ceftriaxone** (if susceptible)
- **Carbapenems** (if ESBL)
- **Colistin, Tigecycline** (if carbapenem-resistant)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Klebsiella = Currant jelly sputum, alcoholics, ESBL-producing
- Nosocomial UTI

## PROTEUS

### P. mirabilis, P. vulgaris

**Identification**:
- **Gram-negative bacilli**
- **Non-lactose fermenter**
- **Highly motile** - **"Swarming"** on agar ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Urease positive** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (strong - alkaline urine)
- **H₂S positive**

**Clinical**:

**1. UTI** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Complicated UTI** (catheters, obstruction)
- **Urease production**:
  - Urea → Ammonia → **Alkaline urine** (pH >8)
  - **Struvite stones** (magnesium ammonium phosphate) - **"staghorn calculi"**
- **"Fishy" odor** of urine

**2. Wound infections, sepsis** (nosocomial)

**Treatment**:
- **Fluoroquinolones, Ceftriaxone**

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Proteus = Urease positive, swarming motility
- UTI with struvite stones (staghorn calculi), alkaline urine

---

## YERSINIA

### Y. enterocolitica

**Transmission**: Contaminated food (pork), water

**Clinical**:
- **Enterocolitis** (diarrhea, fever, abdominal pain)
- **Mesenteric adenitis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐:
  - **Mimics appendicitis** (right lower quadrant pain)
  - **"Pseudoappendicitis"**
- **Reactive arthritis**

**Treatment**: Usually self-limited; antibiotics if severe

**NEET PG**: Y. enterocolitica = Pseudoappendicitis (mesenteric adenitis)

---

### Y. pestis (Plague)

**Transmission**:
- **Flea bite** (from infected rodents - rats)
- Respiratory droplets (pneumonic plague)

**Clinical**:

**1. Bubonic plague** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Painful swollen lymph nodes** ("buboes") - inguinal, axillary
- Fever, chills, headache
- **"Black Death"** (historical pandemics)

**2. Septicemic plague**:
- Bacteremia, DIC
- **Purpuric lesions** (necrotic - "Black Death")

**3. Pneumonic plague** ⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Most deadly**
- **Person-to-person** (respiratory droplets)
- Severe pneumonia, hemoptysis
- High mortality

**Diagnosis**:
- Blood/lymph node culture
- **Bipolar staining** ("safety pin" appearance) - Wayson stain

**Treatment**:
- **Streptomycin or Gentamicin**
- **Doxycycline, Fluoroquinolones**
- **Isolation** (pneumonic plague)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Plague = Bubonic (buboes, flea bite), Pneumonic (person-to-person, most deadly)
- Bipolar staining (safety pin)

   Answer: E. coli

   Answer: ETEC (heat-labile & heat-stable toxins)

   Answer: EHEC O157:H7 (Shiga-like toxin)

4. **Treatment for EHEC?**
   Answer: Supportive, NO antibiotics (↑ HUS risk)

5. **Most serious complication of typhoid?**
   Answer: Intestinal perforation (Week 3)

   Answer: Blood culture

7. **Treatment for typhoid?**
   Answer: Fluoroquinolones, Ceftriaxone, Azithromycin

8. **Most contagious enteric pathogen?**
   Answer: Shigella (infectious dose 10-100)

9. **Treatment for shigellosis?**
   Answer: Fluoroquinolones or Azithromycin
