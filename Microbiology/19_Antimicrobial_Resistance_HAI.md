# Antimicrobial Resistance & Hospital-Acquired Infections

## ANTIMICROBIAL RESISTANCE (AMR) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Definition**: Ability of microorganisms to resist antimicrobial agents that were previously effective ⭐

**Global Threat**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Leading cause of death worldwide (>1 million deaths/year)
- Threatens modern medicine (surgery, chemotherapy, transplants)
- Economic burden (prolonged hospitalization, expensive drugs)

---

## MECHANISMS OF RESISTANCE ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

### 1. ENZYMATIC INACTIVATION ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**β-Lactamases**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Hydrolyze β-lactam ring** → inactivate penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems
- **Most common resistance mechanism** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Types**:

**a) Penicillinases**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Staph aureus** (plasmid-mediated)
- Inactivate penicillin G, ampicillin
- **Overcome with**: β-lactamase inhibitors (clavulanate, sulbactam, tazobactam) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Example: **Amoxicillin-clavulanate** (Augmentin)

**b) Extended-Spectrum β-Lactamases (ESBLs)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Gram-negative** (E. coli, Klebsiella, Proteus) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Hydrolyze**: Penicillins + **cephalosporins (including 3rd gen)** + aztreonam ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **NOT carbapenems** (remain active)
- Plasmid-mediated (CTX-M most common) ⭐
- **Treatment of choice**: **Carbapenems** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (meropenem, imipenem, ertapenem)
- **NOT 3rd gen cephalosporins** (even if sensitive in vitro - clinical failures)

**c) AmpC β-Lactamases**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Chromosomal** (Enterobacter, Citrobacter, Serratia, Pseudomonas - "**CESPS**") ⭐⭐⭐
- Inducible (expression increases with exposure to β-lactams)
- **Hydrolyze**: Penicillins + cephalosporins (including 3rd gen) + β-lactamase inhibitors
- **NOT**: 4th gen cephalosporins (cefepime), carbapenems
- Treatment: **Carbapenems or cefepime** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**d) Carbapenemases**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Hydrolyze ALL β-lactams** including carbapenems ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Most concerning** (limited treatment options)

**Types**:
- **KPC** (Klebsiella pneumoniae carbapenemase): K. pneumoniae, E. coli ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **NDM** (New Delhi metallo-β-lactamase): Gram-negatives ⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Metallo-β-lactamase** (requires zinc)
  - **NOT inhibited by clavulanate**
- **OXA** (Oxacillinase): Acinetobacter
- **VIM, IMP**: Pseudomonas

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Polymyxins** (colistin, polymyxin B) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Tigecycline** ⭐⭐⭐
- **Ceftazidime-avibactam** ⭐⭐⭐ (for KPC)
- **Combination therapy** (e.g., meropenem + colistin)
- **Last resort drugs** (high toxicity)

**Other Enzymes**:
- **Aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes**: Acetylation, phosphorylation, adenylation
- **Chloramphenicol acetyltransferase**: Inactivates chloramphenicol

---

### 2. ALTERED TARGET SITE ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**MRSA (Methicillin-Resistant S. aureus)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **mecA gene** → **PBP2a** (altered penicillin-binding protein) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- PBP2a has low affinity for β-lactams
- **Resistant to ALL β-lactams** (penicillins, cephalosporins, carbapenems) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Vancomycin** (first-line IV) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Linezolid** (alternative, oral available)
  - **Daptomycin** (bacteremia, endocarditis)
  - **Ceftaroline** (5th gen cephalosporin - only β-lactam active against MRSA)
  - **Teicoplanin, tigecycline, dalbavancin**

**VRE (Vancomycin-Resistant Enterococcus)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **vanA gene** → altered D-Ala-D-Lac (vs normal D-Ala-D-Ala) ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Vancomycin cannot bind
- E. faecium > E. faecalis
- **Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Linezolid** (first-line) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Daptomycin** (bacteremia)
  - **Tigecycline**
  - **Quinupristin-dalfopristin** (E. faecium only)

**Penicillin-Resistant S. pneumoniae (PRSP)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Altered PBPs
- Treatment: High-dose penicillin (meningitis), ceftriaxone, vancomycin, fluoroquinolones

**Rifampin Resistance** (TB, S. aureus): ⭐⭐⭐
- Mutation in **rpoB gene** (RNA polymerase)
- Marker of **MDR-TB** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Fluoroquinolone Resistance**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Mutations in **DNA gyrase (gyrA)** and **topoisomerase IV (parC)**
- Common in Gram-negatives (E. coli, Pseudomonas)

**Macrolide Resistance** (Strep, Staph): ⭐⭐⭐
- **erm gene** → methylation of **23S rRNA** (ribosomal target)

---

### 3. DECREASED PERMEABILITY ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Gram-Negative Bacteria**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Loss of porins** (outer membrane channels) → decreased drug entry
- **Pseudomonas**: Loss of OprD porin → carbapenem resistance ⭐⭐⭐
- **E. coli, Klebsiella**: Reduced OmpC/OmpF → β-lactam resistance

**Mycobacteria**: ⭐⭐⭐
- **Thick, waxy cell wall** → intrinsic resistance to many antibiotics
- Requires prolonged therapy with specialized drugs

---

### 4. EFFLUX PUMPS ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Mechanism**: Active pumping of drugs OUT of bacterial cell ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Reduces intracellular drug concentration

**Examples**:
- **Tetracycline resistance** (Gram-negatives) ⭐⭐⭐
- **Fluoroquinolone resistance** (Pseudomonas, Staph)
- **Macrolide resistance** (mef gene)

**Multi-drug efflux pumps**: ⭐⭐⭐
- **Pseudomonas MexAB-OprM** → resistance to multiple drug classes

---

### 5. BYPASS PATHWAY ⭐⭐⭐

**Sulfonamide/Trimethoprim Resistance**: ⭐⭐⭐
- Altered **dihydropteroate synthase (DHPS)** or **dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR)**
- Bacteria use alternative pathways for folate synthesis

**Vancomycin-Intermediate S. aureus (VISA)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Thickened cell wall** → vancomycin "trapped" in outer layers, cannot reach target
- MIC 4-8 μg/mL (intermediate)

---

## GENETIC MECHANISMS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

### VERTICAL TRANSMISSION (Mutation): ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Spontaneous mutations** in bacterial DNA
- **Selection pressure** from antibiotic use
- Examples: Rifampin resistance (TB), fluoroquinolone resistance

### HORIZONTAL GENE TRANSFER: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**1. Transformation**: ⭐⭐⭐
- Uptake of naked DNA from environment
- Example: **S. pneumoniae** (naturally competent)

**2. Transduction**: ⭐⭐⭐
- Transfer via **bacteriophage** (virus)
- Example: **Toxin genes** (diphtheria, cholera)

**3. Conjugation**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Transfer of **plasmids** via **pilus** (cell-to-cell contact) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Most common mechanism for resistance spread** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Examples: **ESBL, carbapenemases, MRSA (SCCmec)**

**4. Transposons**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- "Jumping genes" - move between plasmids and chromosomes
- Spread resistance genes efficiently

## MULTIDRUG-RESISTANT ORGANISMS (MDROs) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

### MRSA (Methicillin-Resistant S. aureus) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Types**:
- **HA-MRSA** (Hospital-Acquired): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - Nosocomial infections (pneumonia, bacteremia, SSI)
  - Multi-drug resistant
  - Sicker patients (ICU, post-op, devices)
- **CA-MRSA** (Community-Acquired): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Skin/soft tissue infections** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (abscesses, furuncles, cellulitis)
  - **PVL toxin** ⭐⭐⭐ (Panton-Valentine leukocidin) → necrotizing pneumonia
  - Younger, healthier patients
  - **More susceptible to non-β-lactams** (clindamycin, TMP-SMX)

**Screening**: Nasal swab (colonization) ⭐

**Decolonization**: ⭐⭐⭐
- **Mupirocin nasal ointment** + chlorhexidine baths
- Pre-operative (cardiac, orthopedic surgery)

---

### VRE (Vancomycin-Resistant Enterococcus) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Species**: E. faecium (more resistant) > E. faecalis ⭐

**Risk Factors**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Prolonged hospitalization, ICU
- **Prior vancomycin use** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Immunosuppression, malignancy
- Renal failure, hemodialysis

**Infections**: UTI, bacteremia, endocarditis, intra-abdominal

**Intrinsic Resistance**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Cephalosporins** (all) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Aminoglycosides** (low-level)
- **TMP-SMX**

---

### ESBL-Producing Enterobacteriaceae ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Organisms**: E. coli, Klebsiella pneumoniae, Proteus mirabilis ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Infections**: UTI, pneumonia, bacteremia, intra-abdominal ⭐

**Risk Factors**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Prior antibiotic use** (esp. 3rd gen cephalosporins, fluoroquinolones) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Healthcare exposure (hospitalization, nursing home)
- Urinary catheter, ICU
- International travel (India, Middle East)

**Detection**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Double-disk diffusion test** ⭐⭐⭐ (ceftazidime/cefotaxime ± clavulanate)
- **Synergy** between 3rd gen cephalosporin and clavulanate = ESBL

---

### CRE (Carbapenem-Resistant Enterobacteriaceae) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**"Nightmare bacteria"** - CDC ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Organisms**: Klebsiella pneumoniae, E. coli, Enterobacter ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Mechanisms**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Carbapenemases** (KPC, NDM) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- ESBL + porin loss
- AmpC + porin loss

**High Mortality**: 40-50% (bloodstream infections) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Treatment**: Limited options (colistin, tigecycline, ceftazidime-avibactam) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

### MDR Pseudomonas aeruginosa ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Intrinsically Resistant to**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Most β-lactams (except antipseudomonals)
- Aminoglycosides (efflux pumps)
- Fluoroquinolones (efflux pumps)

**Antipseudomonal Agents** ("4 P's"): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
1. **P**enicillins: Piperacillin-tazobactam ⭐
2. **P**olyenes: Polymyxins (colistin) ⭐
3. **P**hosphorylation inhibitors: Carbapenems (meropenem, imipenem) ⭐
4. **P**rotein synthesis inhibitors: Aminoglycosides (tobramycin, amikacin) ⭐

**Plus**: Ceftazidime, cefepime, aztreonam, ciprofloxacin

**MDR**: Resistant to ≥3 drug classes ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**XDR**: Susceptible to ≤2 drug classes ⭐⭐⭐

---

### MDR Acinetobacter baumannii ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**"Iraqibacter"** - outbreaks in military hospitals ⭐

**Characteristics**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Survives on dry surfaces** (weeks-months) ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Biofilm formation
- Intrinsic resistance to many antibiotics

**Infections**: VAP, bacteremia, wound infections (ICU) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Carbapenems** (if susceptible)
- **Colistin** (often only option) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Tigecycline**, **sulbactam**
- Combination therapy preferred

---

## MULTIDRUG-RESISTANT TUBERCULOSIS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

### MDR-TB ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Definition**: Resistance to **isoniazid (INH) + rifampin (RIF)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Epidemiology**: ⭐⭐⭐
- 3-5% of new TB cases
- Higher in previously treated, HIV+, high-burden countries (India, China, Russia)

**Diagnosis**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **GeneXpert MTB/RIF** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (detects rifampin resistance as marker of MDR)
- **Culture + DST** (drug susceptibility testing) - gold standard

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Duration**: ≥18-20 months ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Intensive phase** (6-8 months): ≥4 effective drugs
  - **Fluoroquinolone** (levofloxacin, moxifloxacin) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Injectable** (amikacin, kanamycin, capreomycin) ⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Pyrazinamide** (if susceptible)
  - **Ethambutol**
  - **Newer drugs**: Bedaquiline, linezolid, delamanid ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Continuation phase** (12+ months): 3-4 drugs

**Success Rate**: 50-70% (vs >95% for drug-susceptible TB) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

### XDR-TB ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Definition**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- MDR-TB **PLUS** resistance to:
  - **Any fluoroquinolone** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **≥1 injectable** (amikacin, kanamycin, capreomycin) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Mortality**: Very high (50-80%) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Newer agents**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Bedaquiline** (ATP synthase inhibitor) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Delamanid** (mycolic acid synthesis inhibitor)
  - **Linezolid** (protein synthesis inhibitor)
  - **Pretomanid**
- Longer duration (≥24 months)
- Lower cure rates

---

### TDR-TB (Totally Drug-Resistant) ⭐⭐⭐

- Resistance to **ALL first-line + second-line drugs**
- Rare but reported (India, Iran, Italy)
- **Virtually untreatable** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Palliative care

---

## HOSPITAL-ACQUIRED INFECTIONS (HAIs) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Definition**: Infections acquired ≥**48 hours after hospital admission** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- NOT present or incubating at admission

**Also called**: Nosocomial infections ⭐

**Epidemiology**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Affect **5-10%** of hospitalized patients
- **4th leading cause of death** in hospitals
- Increased morbidity, mortality, costs, length of stay

---

## COMMON HAIs ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

### 1. CATHETER-ASSOCIATED UTI (CAUTI) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Most Common HAI** (30-40% of all HAIs) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Risk Factor**: **Urinary catheter** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Risk increases **5-10% per day** of catheterization

**Organisms**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **E. coli** (most common) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Klebsiella, Proteus, Pseudomonas**
- **Enterococcus** (esp. with catheters)
- **Candida** (prolonged catheter, antibiotics)

**Pathogenesis**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Biofilm formation** on catheter ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Bacteria ascend from perineum along catheter

**Diagnosis**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **≥10³ CFU/mL** (catheterized patient) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - vs ≥10⁵ CFU/mL (non-catheterized)
- **Pyuria** (WBCs in urine)

**Prevention**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Avoid unnecessary catheterization** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (most important!)
- **Remove catheter ASAP** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Aseptic insertion technique
- **Closed drainage system** (don't break seal)
- Catheter care (perineal hygiene)

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐
- **Remove/replace catheter** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Antibiotics based on culture/susceptibility
- **Do NOT treat asymptomatic bacteriuria** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (unless pregnant, pre-urologic procedure)

---

### 2. CENTRAL LINE-ASSOCIATED BLOODSTREAM INFECTION (CLABSI) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**2nd Most Common HAI** (14% of HAIs) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Risk Factor**: **Central venous catheter** (CVC) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Subclavian, internal jugular, femoral lines
- PICC lines

**Organisms**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Coagulase-negative Staphylococci** (S. epidermidis) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (most common)
- **S. aureus** (including MRSA) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Enterococcus** (including VRE)
- **Gram-negatives** (E. coli, Klebsiella, Pseudomonas)
- **Candida** (immunocompromised, TPN, broad-spectrum antibiotics)

**Pathogenesis**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Skin flora** at insertion site → catheter colonization → biofilm → bloodstream
- Contaminated catheter hub/infusate (rare)

**Diagnosis**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Blood cultures** (peripheral + catheter) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Differential time to positivity** ⭐⭐⭐:
  - Catheter blood culture positive ≥**2 hours earlier** than peripheral = CLABSI
- **Same organism** from blood + catheter tip culture (semi-quantitative, ≥15 CFU)

**Prevention** (Central Line Bundle): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
1. **Hand hygiene** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
2. **Maximal barrier precautions** (sterile gown, gloves, full drape) ⭐⭐⭐⭐
3. **Chlorhexidine skin antisepsis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
4. **Optimal catheter site** (subclavian > internal jugular > femoral) ⭐⭐⭐⭐
5. **Daily review of line necessity** → remove ASAP ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Dramatically reduces CLABSI rates** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Remove catheter** (if possible) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Empiric antibiotics**: Vancomycin ± Gram-negative coverage (piperacillin-tazobactam, cefepime)
- **De-escalate** based on culture/susceptibility
- **Duration**:
  - **Uncomplicated** (CoNS): 5-7 days (can treat through catheter if retained)
  - **S. aureus**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
    - **Remove catheter** (mandatory) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
    - **2 weeks** (uncomplicated bacteremia)
    - **4-6 weeks** (complicated - endocarditis, osteomyelitis, persistent bacteremia)
    - **TEE** (transesophageal echo) to rule out endocarditis ⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Candida**: Remove catheter + **fluconazole/echinocandin × 2 weeks** after clearance ⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

### 3. VENTILATOR-ASSOCIATED PNEUMONIA (VAP) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Definition**: Pneumonia developing ≥**48 hours after endotracheal intubation** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**3rd Most Common HAI** (22% of ICU infections) ⭐⭐⭐

**Risk Factors**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Mechanical ventilation** (duration) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Supine position
- Aspiration of gastric/oropharyngeal secretions
- Sedation, paralysis
- Re-intubation, prolonged ICU stay

**Organisms**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Early-onset VAP** (<5 days): ⭐⭐⭐
  - S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, MSSA
  - Community-acquired organisms
- **Late-onset VAP** (≥5 days): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Pseudomonas aeruginosa** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (most common)
  - **MRSA** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Acinetobacter baumannii** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Klebsiella, Enterobacter** (ESBL, CRE)
  - Often MDR

**Diagnosis**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Clinical**: New infiltrate on CXR + ≥2 of:
  - Fever >38°C
  - Leukocytosis/leukopenia
  - **Purulent tracheal secretions** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Microbiologic**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Quantitative cultures** (preferred): ⭐⭐⭐⭐
    - **BAL** (bronchoalveolar lavage): ≥10⁴ CFU/mL ⭐⭐⭐
    - **Protected specimen brush**: ≥10³ CFU/mL
  - **Endotracheal aspirate** (non-quantitative): Less specific

**Prevention** (VAP Bundle): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
1. **Elevate head of bed 30-45°** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (prevents aspiration)
2. **Daily sedation vacation** + **spontaneous breathing trial** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
3. **Oral care with chlorhexidine** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
4. **Stress ulcer prophylaxis** (if indicated)
5. **DVT prophylaxis**
6. **Avoid unnecessary re-intubation**

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Empiric** (late-onset/risk factors for MDR): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Antipseudomonal β-lactam**: Piperacillin-tazobactam, cefepime, meropenem ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **PLUS Vancomycin or linezolid** (MRSA coverage) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **PLUS Aminoglycoside or fluoroquinolone** (double Pseudomonas coverage if severe)
- **De-escalate** to narrow-spectrum based on cultures
- **Duration**: 7-8 days (non-Pseudomonas), 14 days (Pseudomonas, Acinetobacter)

---

### 4. SURGICAL SITE INFECTION (SSI) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Definition**: Infection at surgical incision site within **30 days** (or 90 days if implant) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Classification**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
1. **Superficial incisional**: Skin/subcutaneous tissue ⭐
2. **Deep incisional**: Fascia/muscle layers ⭐⭐
3. **Organ/space**: Intra-abdominal, mediastinitis, etc. ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Risk Factors**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Patient**: Diabetes, obesity, smoking, malnutrition, immunosuppression
- **Surgical**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Wound class**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
    - **Clean**: 1-2% SSI rate (elective, no GI/GU, primary closure)
    - **Clean-contaminated**: 3-11% (GI/GU/respiratory entered, no spillage)
    - **Contaminated**: 10-17% (GI spillage, traumatic wound <4h)
    - **Dirty**: >27% (pus, perforation, traumatic wound >4h)
  - Prolonged duration, emergency, poor technique
  - **Hypothermia, hyperglycemia** (intra-op)

**Organisms**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **S. aureus** (most common overall, esp. clean wounds) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **CoNS** (implants - S. epidermidis)
- **Enterococcus**
- **Gram-negatives** (E. coli, Klebsiella) - abdominal surgery
- **Anaerobes** (Bacteroides) - colorectal surgery
- **Polymicrobial** (contaminated/dirty wounds)

**Prevention**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Pre-operative**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Chlorhexidine shower** night before ⭐⭐⭐
  - **MRSA decolonization** (if colonized - cardiac/orthopedic) ⭐⭐⭐
  - **Avoid shaving** (clipping if necessary) ⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Glucose control** (<200 mg/dL) ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Intra-operative**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Surgical antibiotic prophylaxis** (within 60 min of incision) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
    - **Cefazolin** (most common - clean/clean-contaminated) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
    - **Vancomycin** (if MRSA colonized, β-lactam allergy)
    - **Single dose** (re-dose if surgery >4h or blood loss >1.5L)
    - **Discontinue within 24h** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (prolonged = no benefit, ↑ resistance)
  - **Chlorhexidine-alcohol skin prep** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Normothermia** (maintain body temperature)
  - **Oxygenation** (FiO₂ 80% if indicated)
- **Post-operative**:
  - **Sterile dressing** × 24-48h
  - Glucose control

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Superficial**: Open incision, drainage, oral antibiotics (cephalexin, TMP-SMX)
- **Deep/organ-space**: **Surgical debridement** + IV antibiotics ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

### 5. CLOSTRIDIOIDES DIFFICILE INFECTION (CDI) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Most Common Cause of Healthcare-Associated Diarrhea** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Risk Factors**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Antibiotic use** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (esp. clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, cephalosporins, amoxicillin-clavulanate)
- Hospitalization, long-term care facilities
- PPI use, age >65, immunosuppression

**See Topic 09 for detailed coverage** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Prevention**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Antibiotic stewardship** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (avoid unnecessary antibiotics)
- **Hand hygiene with soap and water** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (alcohol doesn't kill spores)
- **Contact precautions**, private room
- **Bleach disinfection** (sporicidal)

## INFECTION CONTROL MEASURES ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

### HAND HYGIENE ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**MOST IMPORTANT INFECTION CONTROL MEASURE** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Methods**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
1. **Alcohol-based hand rub** (preferred for most situations) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
   - **60-95% alcohol** (ethanol, isopropanol)
   - Rapid action, broad-spectrum
   - **Does NOT kill C. diff spores** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
2. **Soap and water** (15-30 seconds): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
   - **Required for**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
     - **C. difficile** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
     - Norovirus, Cryptosporidium (non-enveloped)
     - Visibly soiled hands

**5 Moments of Hand Hygiene** (WHO): ⭐⭐⭐⭐
1. Before patient contact
2. Before aseptic procedure
3. After body fluid exposure risk
4. After patient contact
5. After touching patient surroundings

### STANDARD PRECAUTIONS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Applied to ALL Patients** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Components**:
- **Hand hygiene** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - Gloves (if contact with blood/body fluids)
  - Gown (if splash/spray likely)
  - Mask/face shield (if splash/spray to face)
- **Safe injection practices** (one needle/syringe per patient)
- **Respiratory hygiene/cough etiquette**
- **Safe handling of sharps**

### TRANSMISSION-BASED PRECAUTIONS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**In ADDITION to Standard Precautions** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**1. Contact Precautions**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Indications**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **MRSA, VRE, CRE, MDR Acinetobacter** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **C. difficile** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - Norovirus, RSV (in infants)
  - Scabies, lice
- **Measures**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Private room** (or cohorting) ⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Gown + gloves** for all contact ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Dedicated equipment** (stethoscope, BP cuff)

**2. Droplet Precautions**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Indications**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Influenza, adenovirus, rhinovirus** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Pertussis** (until 5 days of macrolides)
  - **Meningococcal meningitis** (until 24h of antibiotics) ⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Mumps, rubella** (if susceptible HCW)
  - **Pneumonic plague**
- **Transmission**: Large droplets (>5 μm) travel <6 feet ⭐⭐⭐
- **Measures**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Private room** (or cohorting, >3 feet apart)
  - **Surgical mask** for those within 6 feet ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**3. Airborne Precautions**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Indications**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Tuberculosis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Measles, varicella (chickenpox), disseminated zoster** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **COVID-19** (during aerosol-generating procedures) ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Transmission**: Small droplet nuclei (<5 μm) remain airborne, travel long distances ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Measures**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Airborne infection isolation room (AIIR)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
    - **Negative pressure** (6-12 air changes/hour) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
    - Door closed
  - **N95 respirator** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (fit-tested, forms seal)

## ANTIBIOTIC STEWARDSHIP ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Definition**: Coordinated interventions to improve antibiotic use ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Goals**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Optimize clinical outcomes** (cure infection, prevent complications)
- **Minimize toxicity** (adverse effects, C. difficile)
- **Reduce resistance** (preserve future efficacy)
- **Reduce costs**

**Core Elements** (CDC): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
1. **Leadership commitment** (hospital administration)
2. **Accountability** (physician leader responsible)
3. **Expertise** (infectious disease pharmacist, ID physician)
4. **Action** (interventions):
   - **Prospective audit and feedback** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (review prescriptions, provide recommendations)
   - **Formulary restriction + pre-authorization** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
   - **Clinical pathways/guidelines**
   - **De-escalation** (narrow spectrum after cultures) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
   - **Dose optimization** (PK/PD, renal dosing)
   - **IV-to-oral switch** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
   - **Shorter durations** (5-7 days for most infections) ⭐⭐⭐⭐
5. **Tracking** (monitor antibiotic use, resistance patterns)
6. **Reporting** (feedback to prescribers, leadership)
7. **Education** (training programs)

**Benefits**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **↓ Antibiotic resistance** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **↓ C. difficile infections** (30-50% reduction) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **↓ Adverse drug events**
- **↓ Healthcare costs**
- **Improved patient outcomes**

## NEET PG MCQS

**Q1.** ESBL-producing E. coli UTI. Treatment of choice?
A) Ceftriaxone B) Ciprofloxacin C) Meropenem D) TMP-SMX

**Answer: C** - Carbapenems are first-line for ESBL infections (NOT 3rd gen cephalosporins)

**Q2.** Patient with VAP from Pseudomonas. Empiric coverage should include?
A) Vancomycin alone B) Piperacillin-tazobactam + vancomycin C) Ceftriaxone D) Azithromycin

**Answer: B** - VAP needs antipseudomonal β-lactam + MRSA coverage (vancomycin/linezolid)

**Q3.** Most important measure to prevent CLABSI?
A) Prophylactic antibiotics B) Chlorhexidine skin prep C) Daily line changes D) Femoral site

**Answer: B** - Chlorhexidine skin antisepsis (part of central line bundle). Avoid femoral site!

**Q4.** Hand hygiene method required for C. difficile?
A) Alcohol rub B) Soap and water C) Either is fine D) No hand hygiene needed

**Answer: B** - Soap and water required (alcohol doesn't kill spores)

**Q5.** Patient with MRSA colonization undergoing cardiac surgery. Pre-op prophylaxis?
A) Cefazolin B) Vancomycin C) Mupirocin nasal ointment D) No prophylaxis

**Answer: C** - MRSA decolonization with mupirocin + chlorhexidine baths before surgery. Surgical prophylaxis would be vancomycin (not cefazolin).
