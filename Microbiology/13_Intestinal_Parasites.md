# Intestinal Parasites

## Overview
Intestinal parasites include protozoa and helminths that infect the gastrointestinal tract, causing diarrhea, malabsorption, and other GI symptoms.

---

## INTESTINAL PROTOZOA

### 1. ENTAMOEBA HISTOLYTICA ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Classification**: Protozoan (am

oe ba)

**Transmission**: Fecal-oral (cysts in contaminated water/food)

**Life Cycle**:
- **Cyst** (infective stage, 4 nuclei) → Ingested → Excystation in small intestine
- **Trophozoite** (motile, invasive) → Colonizes colon → Encystment
- Only cysts survive outside host

**Clinical Features**:
```
INTESTINAL AMEBIASIS:
• Asymptomatic carrier (90%)
• Amoebic colitis/dysentery:
  - Gradual onset (weeks)
  - Bloody diarrhea with mucus ⭐
  - "Anchovy paste" or "raspberry jelly" stools
  - Abdominal pain, tenesmus
  - Low-grade fever (vs bacillary dysentery: high fever)
• Flask-shaped ulcers in colon ⭐

EXTRAINTESTINAL:
• Amoebic liver abscess ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - Right lobe most common
  - "Anchovy paste" or "chocolate sauce" pus
  - RUQ pain, fever
  - May rupture → pleura, pericardium, peritoneum
```

**Diagnosis**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Stool microscopy**: Trophozoites with ingested RBCs ⭐ (pathognomonic)
- Cysts: 1-4 nuclei with central karyosome
- **Stool antigen**: ELISA (detects E. histolytica specifically, not E. dispar)
- **Serology**: IgG antibodies (liver abscess - 95% positive)
- Liver abscess: Ultrasound/CT, aspiration if needed

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Invasive disease**: **Metronidazole 750 mg TID × 7-10 days** → **Paromomycin** (intraluminal agent) to eliminate cysts
- **Asymptomatic cyst passers**: Paromomycin or iodoquinol alone
- Liver abscess: Metronidazole ± aspiration if large/risk of rupture

---

### 2. GIARDIA LAMBLIA (G. intestinalis) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Classification**: Flagellated protozoan

**Transmission**: Fecal-oral, waterborne (most common cause of waterborne diarrhea in US)

**Morphology**:
- **Trophozoite**: Pear-shaped, 2 nuclei ("owl's eye"), 4 pairs of flagella, ventral sucking disk
- **Cyst**: Oval, 4 nuclei (mature)

**Clinical Features**:
- **Chronic diarrhea** (>2 weeks) ⭐
- Greasy, foul-smelling stools (steatorrhea) ⭐
- Malabsorption, weight loss
- Bloating, flatulence, cramping
- NO blood/mucus in stool ⭐
- Common in: Children, travelers, immunocompromised (especially IgA deficiency)

**Diagnosis**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Stool ova & parasites** (×3): Cysts or trophozoites
- **Stool antigen** (ELISA/immunofluorescence): More sensitive
- **String test** (Entero-Test): Duodenal sampling
- Duodenal biopsy (rarely needed): Trophozoites on mucosa

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Metronidazole 250 mg TID × 5-7 days** OR
- **Tinidazole 2 g single dose** (higher cure rate) OR
- **Nitazoxanide 500 mg BID × 3 days**

**Prevention**: Water purification, hand hygiene (cysts resistant to chlorination)

---

### 3. CRYPTOSPORIDIUM ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Classification**: Coccidian protozoan (Apicomplexa)

**Transmission**: Fecal-oral, waterborne (resistant to chlorine)

**Clinical Features**:
- **Immunocompetent**: Self-limited watery diarrhea (1-2 weeks)
- **Immunocompromised (AIDS, CD4 <100)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - Severe, chronic, profuse watery diarrhea (cholera-like)
  - Dehydration, wasting
  - Biliary tract involvement (cholecystitis, sclerosing cholangitis)

**Diagnosis**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Modified acid-fast stain** ⭐ of stool (oocysts are acid-fast)
- **Stool antigen** (DFA, ELISA)
- PCR

**Treatment**:
- **Immunocompetent**: Supportive (self-limited)
- **Immunocompromised**: **Nitazoxanide** + **ART** (restore immunity most important)

---

### 4. CYCLOSPORA CAYETANENSIS ⭐⭐⭐

**Transmission**: Contaminated fresh produce (berries, lettuce)

**Clinical**: Watery diarrhea, fatigue, weight loss (travelers, foodborne outbreaks)

**Diagnosis**: Modified acid-fast stain (oocysts), autofluorescence under UV

**Treatment**: **TMP-SMX DS BID × 7-10 days** ⭐

## INTESTINAL HELMINTHS

### NEMATODES (Roundworms)

#### 1. ASCARIS LUMBRICOIDES (Giant Roundworm) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Epidemiology**: Most common helminth worldwide (1 billion infected)

**Transmission**: Fecal-oral (eggs in soil)

**Life Cycle**:
- Eggs ingested → Larvae hatch in intestine → Penetrate intestinal wall → Bloodstream → **Lungs** → Coughed up, swallowed → Mature in small intestine (6-8 weeks)
- Adults (15-35 cm long) live in small intestine (1-2 years)

**Clinical**:
- **Asymptomatic** (most common)
- **Pulmonary** (larval migration): Löffler syndrome ⭐
  - Cough, wheezing, eosinophilia
  - Transient pulmonary infiltrates
- **Intestinal** (heavy infection):
  - Malnutrition, growth retardation (children)
  - **Intestinal obstruction** ⭐ (ball of worms)
  - Biliary/pancreatic obstruction (worms migrate)
  - Appendicitis

**Diagnosis**: ⭐
- **Stool O&P**: Eggs (oval, thick shell, mammillated coating)
- Adult worms in stool/vomitus

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Albendazole 400 mg single dose** OR
- **Mebendazole 100 mg BID × 3 days** OR
- **Pyrantel pamoate** (alternative)
- Surgical intervention if obstruction

---

#### 2. ENTEROBIUS VERMICULARIS (Pinworm) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Epidemiology**: Most common helminth in US/developed countries, children

**Transmission**: Fecal-oral, auto-infection (eggs on fingers)

**Life Cycle**:
- Eggs ingested → Hatch in small intestine → Mature in colon
- **Female migrates to perianal area at NIGHT** ⭐ to lay eggs → Causes itching

**Clinical**:
- **Perianal itching** (especially at night) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Disturbed sleep, irritability
- Vulvovaginitis (girls)

**Diagnosis**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Scotch tape test** (cellophane tape paddle) ⭐
  - Apply to perianal area in MORNING before bathing
  - Examine under microscope for eggs
  - Eggs: Oval, flattened on one side
- Stool O&P usually negative (eggs not in stool)

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Albendazole 400 mg × 1 dose, repeat in 2 weeks** OR
- **Mebendazole 100 mg × 1 dose, repeat in 2 weeks** OR
- **Pyrantel pamoate** × 1 dose, repeat in 2 weeks
- **Treat entire household** ⭐ (high transmission)
- Hygiene: Wash bedding/clothing, trim nails, wash hands

---

#### 3. TRICHURIS TRICHIURA (Whipworm) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Transmission**: Fecal-oral (eggs in soil)

**Morphology**: Whip-shaped worm (thin anterior, thick posterior)

**Life Cycle**: Eggs → Larvae in small intestine → Adults in cecum/colon (no migration)

**Clinical**:
- Light infection: Asymptomatic
- Heavy infection:
  - Bloody diarrhea, abdominal pain
  - **Rectal prolapse** ⭐ (children, severe cases)
  - Anemia, growth retardation

**Diagnosis**: Stool O&P - eggs (barrel-shaped with bipolar plugs) ⭐

**Treatment**: **Albendazole or Mebendazole × 3 days**

---

#### 4. HOOKWORMS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Species**:
- **Necator americanus** (New World)
- **Ancylostoma duodenale** (Old World)

**Transmission**: **Skin penetration** ⭐ (larvae in soil, walking barefoot)

**Life Cycle**:
- Larvae penetrate skin → Bloodstream → Lungs → Coughed up, swallowed → Mature in small intestine
- Adults attach to intestinal mucosa, feed on blood

**Clinical**:
- **Cutaneous** (larval penetration): "Ground itch" ⭐
- **Pulmonary** (migration): Löffler syndrome
- **Intestinal** (adult worms):
  - **Iron deficiency anemia** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (chronic blood loss)
  - Hypoproteinemia
  - Abdominal pain, diarrhea
  - Growth retardation, cognitive impairment (children)

**Diagnosis**: Stool O&P - eggs (oval, thin-shelled, cannot differentiate species)

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Albendazole 400 mg × 3 days** OR
- **Mebendazole 100 mg BID × 3 days**
- **Iron supplementation** for anemia

**Prevention**: Shoes, sanitation

---

#### 5. STRONGYLOIDES STERCORALIS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Unique Features**:
- **Auto-infection** ⭐ (can complete life cycle in human host)
- **Hyperinfection syndrome** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (immunocompromised)
- Can persist for decades

**Transmission**: Skin penetration (filariform larvae in soil)

**Life Cycle**:
- Larvae penetrate skin → Lungs → Small intestine
- Adults in duodenal mucosa → Eggs hatch → **Rhabditiform larvae** in stool
- Some larvae → Filariform in colon → **Auto-infect** (penetrate colonic mucosa or perianal skin)

**Clinical**:
- **Asymptomatic** (common)
- **Cutaneous**: "Larva currens" ⭐ (rapidly moving urticarial track, pathognomonic)
- **GI**: Diarrhea, abdominal pain, nausea
- **Pulmonary**: Cough, wheezing (larval migration)
- **Hyperinfection syndrome**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - Immunosuppressed (steroids, HIV, HTLV-1)
  - Massive larval dissemination
  - Severe GI symptoms, malabsorption
  - Pneumonia, sepsis (Gram-negative bacteremia from larval translocation)
  - High mortality

**Diagnosis**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Stool O&P**: Rhabditiform larvae (NOT eggs) ⭐
  - Multiple samples (low yield, intermittent shedding)
- **Serology** (ELISA): IgG antibodies - useful for screening
- Duodenal aspirate/biopsy (string test)

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Ivermectin 200 μg/kg daily × 2 days** ⭐ (preferred)
  - Hyperinfection: Daily × 7-14 days (until larvae clearance)
- Alternative: Albendazole (less effective)
- **Screen before immunosuppression** (transplant, chemotherapy) in endemic areas

### CESTODES (Tapeworms)

#### 1. TAENIA SPECIES ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Species**:
- **Taenia solium** (pork tapeworm) - Causes cysticercosis ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Taenia saginata** (beef tapeworm)

**Transmission**:
- **Intestinal infection**: Undercooked meat with cysticerci (larval stage)
- **Cysticercosis** (T. solium only): Ingestion of eggs (fecal-oral, auto-infection)

**Clinical**:
- **Intestinal taeniasis**: Usually asymptomatic, segments in stool, mild GI symptoms
- **Cysticercosis** (T. solium): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Neurocysticercosis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (most serious)
    - **Seizures** (most common presentation) ⭐
    - Headache, hydrocephalus, meningitis
    - Imaging: Cysts in brain (ring-enhancing lesions), calcifications
    - Single or multiple lesions
    - Complications: Intraventricular cysts, racemose cysts
  - Subcutaneous: Palpable nodules
  - Ocular: Vision loss

**Diagnosis**:
- Intestinal: Stool O&P - eggs (indistinguishable between species), proglottids (segments - T. saginata >15 uterine branches, T. solium <13)
- Cysticercosis: CT/MRI (cysts, calcifications), serology (ELISA for antibodies/antigens)

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Intestinal taeniasis**: **Praziquantel 5-10 mg/kg single dose** ⭐
- **Neurocysticercosis**:
  - **Albendazole 15 mg/kg/day × 8-30 days** ⭐
  - **+ Corticosteroids** (dexamethasone) to reduce inflammation
  - + Antiepileptics for seizures
  - May need surgery (hydrocephalus, large cysts)
  - Some cysts: Observation only (calcified, inactive)

---

#### 2. DIPHYLLOBOTHRIUM LATUM (Fish Tapeworm) ⭐⭐⭐

**Transmission**: Raw/undercooked freshwater fish (sushi, ceviche)

**Clinical**:
- Usually asymptomatic
- **Vitamin B12 deficiency** ⭐ → Megaloblastic anemia (worm competes for B12)
- Segments in stool

**Diagnosis**: Stool O&P - eggs (operculated, oval)

**Treatment**: **Praziquantel**

## HIGH-YIELD MNEMONICS

**Acid-Fast Oocysts**: "**C**rypto **C**yclospora are **C**ocky" (acid-fast)

**Scotch Tape Test**: **P**inworm → **P**erianal → **P**addle (tape)

**Hookworm = Blood Loss**: Walk barefoot → Ground itch → Anemia

**Strongyloides Screen**: Before **S**teroids, check **S**trongyloides (hyperinfection risk)

## NEET PG MCQS

**Q1.** Trophozoites with ingested RBCs are pathognomonic for:
A) Giardia B) Entamoeba histolytica C) Cryptosporidium D) Balantidium

**Answer: B** - E. histolytica trophozoites ingest RBCs (pathognomonic)

**Q2.** Most common helminth in US/developed countries:
A) Ascaris B) Hookworm C) Pinworm D) Trichuris

**Answer: C** - Enterobius (pinworm) most common in developed countries

**Q3.** Treatment for neurocysticercosis:
A) Praziquantel alone B) Albendazole + corticosteroids C) Metronidazole D) Ivermectin

**Answer: B** - Neurocysticercosis: Albendazole + corticosteroids (reduce inflammation) + antiepileptics
