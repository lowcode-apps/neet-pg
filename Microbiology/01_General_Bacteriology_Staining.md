# GENERAL BACTERIOLOGY & STAINING - NEET PG Study Material

## BACTERIAL STRUCTURE

### Basic Components

**1. Essential structures**:
- **Cell membrane** (cytoplasmic membrane)
- **Cytoplasm** (ribosomes, inclusion bodies)
- **Nucleoid** (circular DNA - chromosome)
- **Cell wall** (peptidoglycan)

**2. Non-essential structures**:
- **Capsule** (polysaccharide)
- **Flagella** (motility)
- **Pili/Fimbriae** (adhesion, conjugation)
- **Spores** (dormant form)
- **Plasmids** (extrachromosomal DNA)

**NEET PG**: All bacteria have cell membrane, cytoplasm, nucleoid, cell wall (except Mycoplasma)

---

## BACTERIAL CELL WALL

### Gram-Positive vs Gram-Negative

**Gram-Positive**:
- **Thick peptidoglycan layer** (20-80 nm, 40-90%)
- **Teichoic acid** (in peptidoglycan)
- **Lipoteichoic acid** (LTA - extends to membrane)
- **NO outer membrane**
- **NO lipopolysaccharide (LPS)**

**Gram-Negative**:
- **Thin peptidoglycan layer** (2-3 nm, 10%)
- **Outer membrane** (lipid bilayer)
- **Lipopolysaccharide (LPS)** - endotoxin
  - Lipid A (toxic component)
  - Core polysaccharide
  - O antigen (somatic - variable)
- **Periplasmic space** (between membranes)
- **Porins** (outer membrane proteins)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Gram-positive = Thick peptidoglycan + teichoic acid
- Gram-negative = Thin peptidoglycan + outer membrane + LPS (endotoxin)

---

### Peptidoglycan (Murein)

**Structure**:
- **N-acetylglucosamine (NAG)**
- **N-acetylmuramic acid (NAM)**
- **Tetrapeptide side chains** (L-Ala, D-Glu, L-Lys/DAP, D-Ala)
- **Cross-links** (pentaglycine bridge in Gram-positive)

**Function**:
- **Rigidity, shape**
- **Osmotic protection**

**Target of antibiotics**:
- **β-lactams** (penicillins, cephalosporins) - inhibit transpeptidase (PBPs)
- **Glycopeptides** (vancomycin) - bind D-Ala-D-Ala

**NEET PG**: Peptidoglycan = NAG + NAM, target of β-lactams & vancomycin

---

## BACTERIAL SHAPES & ARRANGEMENTS

### Shapes (Morphology)

**1. Cocci** (spherical):
- Staphylococcus (clusters - "bunch of grapes")
- Streptococcus (chains)
- Diplococci (pairs) - Pneumococcus, Neisseria
- Tetrads (groups of 4) - Micrococcus
- Sarcina (cubes of 8)

**2. Bacilli** (rod-shaped):
- Single, chains, palisades (Chinese letters - Corynebacterium)

**3. Spiral**:
- **Vibrio** (comma-shaped)
- **Spirillum** (rigid spiral)
- **Spirochete** (flexible spiral) - Treponema, Leptospira, Borrelia

**4. Other**:
- **Coccobacilli** - Haemophilus, Brucella
- **Filamentous** - Actinomyces, Nocardia
- **Pleomorphic** - No fixed shape (Mycoplasma)

**NEET PG**: Know characteristic arrangements (staph = clusters, strep = chains)

## GRAM STAINING ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

### Procedure

**1. Primary stain**: **Crystal violet** (1 minute)
   - All bacteria purple

**2. Mordant**: **Iodine** (1 minute)
   - Forms crystal violet-iodine complex

**3. Decolorization**: **Alcohol or acetone** (few seconds)
   - **Gram-positive**: Retain purple (thick peptidoglycan)
   - **Gram-negative**: Lose purple (thin peptidoglycan, alcohol dissolves lipids)

**4. Counterstain**: **Safranin** (red/pink) (1 minute)
   - **Gram-negative**: Pink/red
   - **Gram-positive**: Remain purple

**Result**:
- **Gram-positive**: **Purple/blue**
- **Gram-negative**: **Pink/red**

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Gram stain steps: Crystal violet → Iodine → Alcohol → Safranin
- Gram-positive = Purple, Gram-negative = Pink

---

### Mechanism

**Gram-positive**:
- **Thick peptidoglycan** traps crystal violet-iodine complex
- Alcohol **dehydrates** peptidoglycan → tightens mesh → **retains dye**

**Gram-negative**:
- **Thin peptidoglycan**, **outer membrane**
- Alcohol **dissolves lipids** of outer membrane
- Crystal violet-iodine complex **washes out**
- **Takes up safranin** (pink)

---

### Gram-Variable Bacteria

**Bacteria that don't stain predictably**:
- **Old cultures** (peptidoglycan degraded)
- **Mycobacteria** (waxy cell wall - need acid-fast stain)
- **Mycoplasma** (no cell wall)

---

## ACID-FAST STAINING ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**For**: **Mycobacteria** (M. tuberculosis, M. leprae), Nocardia

### Ziehl-Neelsen (ZN) Stain - Hot Method

**Procedure**:

**1. Primary stain**: **Carbol fuchsin** (red)
   - **Heat** applied (steam)
   - Penetrates waxy cell wall

**2. Decolorization**: **Acid-alcohol** (3% HCl + 95% alcohol)
   - **Acid-fast**: Retain red (resist decolorization)
   - **Non-acid-fast**: Lose red

**3. Counterstain**: **Methylene blue** (blue)
   - Non-acid-fast bacteria blue

**Result**:
- **Acid-fast bacilli (AFB)**: **Red** (pink rods)
- **Non-acid-fast**: **Blue**

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Acid-fast stain for Mycobacterium tuberculosis
- AFB = Red rods on blue background
- Principle: Mycolic acid in cell wall resists acid-alcohol decolorization

---

### Kinyoun Stain - Cold Method

**Similar to ZN** but:
- **No heating** required (cold method)
- Higher concentration of carbol fuchsin

---

### Auramine-Rhodamine Stain

**Fluorescent stain**:
- **Auramine O** + **Rhodamine** (fluorescent dyes)
- **Viewed under fluorescence microscope**
- **More sensitive** than ZN (easier to detect low numbers of AFB)
- **Yellow-green fluorescence**

**NEET PG**: Auramine-rhodamine = Fluorescent, more sensitive than ZN

---

### Modified Acid-Fast Stain

**For**: **Nocardia**, **Cryptosporidium**, **Isospora**, **Cyclospora**

**Uses**: **Weaker acid** (1% H₂SO₄ or 0.5-1% HCl)
- These organisms partially acid-fast

**NEET PG**: Nocardia = Modified acid-fast positive

---

## OTHER STAINING METHODS

### Albert Stain

**For**: **Corynebacterium diphtheriae**

**Detects**: **Metachromatic granules** (volutin granules)
- Polyphosphate storage granules
- Appear as **bluish-black granules** at poles
- **"Beaded" appearance**

**NEET PG**: Albert stain for C. diphtheriae metachromatic granules

---

### Capsule Stain (Negative Staining)

**For**: **Capsulated bacteria** (Streptococcus pneumoniae, Klebsiella, Cryptococcus)

**Method**: **India ink** or **Nigrosin**

**Principle**:
- Capsule **doesn't take up stain** (acidic)
- Background dark, bacteria stained, **capsule clear halo**

**NEET PG**: Capsule stain = Negative staining, clear halo around bacteria

---

### Spore Stain

**For**: **Spore-forming bacteria** (Bacillus, Clostridium)

**Method**: **Malachite green** (with heat) + **Safranin**

**Result**:
- **Spores**: **Green**
- **Vegetative cells**: **Pink/red**

**NEET PG**: Spore stain - spores green, vegetative cells red

---

### Giemsa Stain

**For**:
- **Blood parasites** (Plasmodium, Leishmania, Trypanosoma)
- **Chlamydia** (intracellular inclusions)
- **Rickettsia**
- **Borrelia**

**Result**: **Purple-blue** organisms

**NEET PG**: Giemsa for blood parasites, Chlamydia, Rickettsia

---

### Silver Stains

**Gomori Methenamine Silver (GMS)**:
- **Fungi** (Pneumocystis, Aspergillus, Candida)
- **Black** organisms on green/yellow background

**Warthin-Starry**:
- **Spirochetes** (Treponema, Leptospira)
- **Bartonella, Helicobacter**

**NEET PG**: GMS for fungi (black), Warthin-Starry for spirochetes

---

### Flagellar Stain

**For**: Demonstrating **flagella**

**Method**: **Leifson stain**, **Gray's method**

**Principle**: Mordants thicken flagella (otherwise too thin to see)

## BACTERIAL GROWTH REQUIREMENTS

### Oxygen Requirements

**1. Obligate aerobes**:
- **Require O₂**
- Examples: Mycobacterium tuberculosis, Pseudomonas, Nocardia, Bacillus

**2. Obligate anaerobes**:
- **Killed by O₂** (lack catalase, superoxide dismutase)
- Examples: **Clostridium**, Bacteroides, Actinomyces, Peptostreptococcus

**3. Facultative anaerobes**:
- **Prefer O₂** but can grow without
- **Most pathogenic bacteria**
- Examples: **Staphylococcus**, **Streptococcus**, **E. coli**, Salmonella

**4. Microaerophiles**:
- Require **low O₂** (5-10%)
- Examples: **Campylobacter**, **Helicobacter**

**5. Aerotolerant anaerobes**:
- Don't use O₂ but tolerate it
- Example: **Enterococcus**

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Clostridium = Obligate anaerobe
- Campylobacter, Helicobacter = Microaerophiles
- Most pathogens = Facultative anaerobes

---

### Temperature

**1. Psychrophiles**: 0-20°C (cold-loving)

**2. Mesophiles**: **20-40°C** (most human pathogens - **37°C**)

**3. Thermophiles**: 50-80°C (heat-loving)

---

### pH

Most bacteria prefer **neutral pH (7.0-7.5)**

Exceptions:
- **Vibrio cholerae**: Alkaline (pH 8.5-9.5)
- **Lactobacillus**: Acidic (pH 4-5)

---

## BACTERIAL GROWTH CURVE

### Phases

**1. Lag phase**:
- Adaptation, enzyme synthesis
- **No division**

**2. Log (Exponential) phase**:
- **Rapid exponential growth**
- **Most susceptible to antibiotics** (cell wall synthesis active)
- **Metabolically active**

**3. Stationary phase**:
- Growth rate = Death rate
- Nutrient depletion, waste accumulation
- **Spore formation** (if capable)

**4. Death (Decline) phase**:
- Death rate > Growth rate
- Exponential death

**NEET PG**: Log phase = Most susceptible to antibiotics

---

## BACTERIAL GENETICS

### Genetic Material

**Chromosome**:
- **Single circular dsDNA**
- Nucleoid region (no nucleus)
- **Haploid**

**Plasmids**:
- **Extrachromosomal circular DNA**
- **Self-replicating**
- Carry **resistance genes** (R plasmids), virulence factors
- **Can be transferred** (conjugation)

---

### Gene Transfer (Horizontal)

**1. Transformation**:
- Uptake of **naked DNA** from environment
- **Competent bacteria** (Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus, Neisseria)
- **Griffith's experiment** (S vs R strains of S. pneumoniae)

**2. Transduction**:
- Transfer via **bacteriophage** (virus)
- **Generalized**: Random bacterial DNA packaged
- **Specialized**: Specific genes near prophage
- Example: **Toxin genes** in C. diphtheriae, Strep pyogenes (erythrogenic toxin)

**3. Conjugation**:
- Transfer via **sex pili** (F pilus)
- **Direct cell-to-cell contact**
- **Plasmid** (commonly R plasmids - antibiotic resistance) or chromosomal DNA
- **F+ (donor)** → **F- (recipient)**

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Transformation = Naked DNA uptake (Griffith's experiment)
- Transduction = Phage-mediated (toxin genes)
- Conjugation = Sex pili, plasmid transfer (antibiotic resistance)

---

## BACTERIAL SPORES ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Spore-forming bacteria**:
- **Bacillus** (aerobic)
- **Clostridium** (anaerobic)

### Characteristics

**1. Dormant form**:
- **Resistant** to heat, desiccation, chemicals, radiation
- **Can survive for years**

**2. Structure**:
- **Core** (DNA, ribosomes, minimal water)
- **Cortex** (peptidoglycan)
- **Coat** (keratin-like proteins)
- **Exosporium** (outermost layer)
- **Dipicolinic acid + Ca²⁺** (heat resistance)

**3. Position**:
- **Central**: Bacillus anthracis
- **Subterminal**: Clostridium tetani (drumstick appearance)
- **Terminal**: Clostridium tetani

**4. Germination**:
- Favorable conditions → vegetative cell

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Only Bacillus & Clostridium form spores
- Spores most resistant form (heat, chemicals)
- C. tetani = Drumstick (terminal spore)

---

### Sterilization

**Spore resistance**:
- **Heat** (dry): 160-170°C for 1-2 hours
- **Heat** (moist/steam - **Autoclave**): **121°C, 15 psi, 15 minutes** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Most effective sterilization**
- **Chemicals**: Ethylene oxide, formaldehyde

**NEET PG**: Autoclave (121°C, 15 psi, 15 min) = Gold standard for sterilization

---

## BACTERIAL TOXINS

### Exotoxins

**Secreted proteins**:
- **Gram-positive & Gram-negative**
- **Highly toxic** (small amounts)
- **Heat-labile** (destroyed by heat)
- **Immunogenic** (antibodies formed, **toxoids** can be made)
- **Specific action**

**Types**:
1. **Neurotoxins**: Tetanus, Botulinum
2. **Enterotoxins**: Cholera, E. coli (ETEC)
3. **Cytotoxins**: Diphtheria toxin, Shiga toxin

**NEET PG**: Exotoxins = Secreted proteins, heat-labile, toxoids possible

---

### Endotoxins

**LPS** (lipopolysaccharide) of **Gram-negative bacteria**:
- **Part of cell wall** (released on bacterial death/lysis)
- **Lipid A** = Toxic component
- **Heat-stable**
- **Weakly immunogenic** (no toxoid)
- **Non-specific** (fever, shock)

**Effects**:
- **Fever** (IL-1, TNF-α via macrophages)
- **Hypotension, shock** (septic shock)
- **DIC** (disseminated intravascular coagulation)
- **Acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS)**

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Endotoxin = LPS (Gram-negative), heat-stable
- Causes fever, septic shock, DIC

---

### Exotoxin vs Endotoxin

| Feature | Exotoxin | Endotoxin |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| **Source** | Secreted protein | LPS (cell wall) |
| **Bacteria** | Gram-positive & Gram-negative | **Gram-negative only** |
| **Toxicity** | **High** | Low-moderate |
| **Heat** | **Labile** (destroyed) | **Stable** |
| **Immunogenicity** | **High** (toxoids made) | **Low** (no toxoid) |
| **Specificity** | Specific actions | Non-specific (fever, shock) |
| **Examples** | Tetanus, Diphtheria, Cholera | LPS of E. coli, Salmonella |

**NEET PG**: Know exotoxin vs endotoxin differences

## Practice MCQs

   Answer: Crystal violet → Iodine → Alcohol → Safranin

   Answer: Acid-fast stain (Ziehl-Neelsen)

   Answer: Mycolic acid in cell wall

   Answer: Mycobacteria, Mycoplasma, Spirochetes

   Answer: 121°C, 15 psi, 15 minutes

   Answer: Bacillus, Clostridium

   Answer: Exotoxin = secreted protein (heat-labile), Endotoxin = LPS (heat-stable)

   Answer: Transduction

9. **Most bacteria susceptible to antibiotics in which phase?**
   Answer: Log (exponential) phase

10. **Negative staining for?**
    Answer: Capsule demonstration
