# STAPHYLOCOCCUS & STREPTOCOCCUS - NEET PG Study Material

## STAPHYLOCOCCUS

### General Characteristics

**Morphology**:
- **Gram-positive cocci**
- **Clusters** ("bunch of grapes")
- Non-motile, non-spore forming

**Growth**:
- **Facultative anaerobes**
- **Catalase positive** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (differentiates from Streptococcus)
- Salt-tolerant (7.5% NaCl)
- **Blood agar**: β-hemolysis (S. aureus)
- **Mannitol salt agar**: S. aureus ferments mannitol (yellow)

**Species**:
1. **Staphylococcus aureus** (coagulase-positive)
2. **Coagulase-negative staph** (CONS): S. epidermidis, S. saprophyticus

**NEET PG High-Yield**: Staphylococcus = Catalase positive, clusters

---

## STAPHYLOCOCCUS AUREUS

### Identification

**Key tests**:
- **Catalase**: **Positive** (vs Streptococcus)
- **Coagulase**: **Positive** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (differentiates S. aureus from CONS)
  - Tube coagulase (gold standard)
  - Slide coagulase (clumping factor)
- **Mannitol fermentation**: Positive (yellow on MSA)
- **Protein A**: Binds IgG Fc region (antiphagocytic)
- **Golden pigment** on agar

**NEET PG**: S. aureus = Coagulase positive, mannitol fermenter

---

### Virulence Factors

**1. Surface proteins**:
- **Protein A**: Binds IgG Fc → Antiphagocytic
- **Clumping factor** (bound coagulase)
- **Adhesins**: Fibronectin-binding protein, collagen-binding protein

**2. Enzymes**:
- **Coagulase**: Clots plasma → Fibrin barrier (protects from phagocytes)
- **Catalase**: Breaks down H₂O₂ (resist oxidative killing)
- **Hyaluronidase**: Spreads infection
- **Lipases, Nucleases, Proteases**
- **β-lactamase** (penicillinase): Resistance to penicillin

**3. Toxins** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:

**a) Hemolysins**:
- **α-toxin**: Pore-forming, dermonecrotic, hemolytic
- β, γ, δ toxins

**b) Exfoliative toxins (ET-A, ET-B)**:
- **Serine protease** (epidermolytic)
- Cleaves desmoglein-1 (epidermal adhesion)
- **Staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome (SSSS)**

**c) Enterotoxins (A-E)**:
- **Heat-stable superantigens**
- **Food poisoning** (preformed toxin)
- Rapid onset (1-6 hours)
- Vomiting, diarrhea (self-limited)

**d) Toxic shock syndrome toxin-1 (TSST-1)**:
- **Superantigen** (polyclonal T-cell activation)
- **Toxic shock syndrome (TSS)**

**e) Panton-Valentine Leukocidin (PVL)**:
- Necrotizing pneumonia, skin abscesses
- CA-MRSA (community-acquired MRSA)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Exfoliative toxin → SSSS (scalded skin)
- Enterotoxin → Food poisoning (heat-stable, rapid onset)
- TSST-1 → Toxic shock syndrome

---

### Clinical Manifestations

**1. Skin & Soft Tissue Infections** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Furuncle** (boil) - hair follicle abscess
- **Carbuncle** - multiple furuncles, deeper
- **Impetigo** - crusted, "honey-colored" lesions
- **Cellulitis**
- **Wound infections**
- **Abscesses** (pus formation - hallmark)

**2. Scalded Skin Syndrome (SSSS)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Exfoliative toxin** (ET-A, ET-B)
- **Children <5 years** (adults have neutralizing antibodies)
- **Nikolsky sign positive** (skin sloughs off with pressure)
- **Desquamation** (peeling), blisters
- **Superficial** (vs TEN - full thickness)
- Treatment: **Antistaphylococcal antibiotics** (NOT steroids)

**3. Food Poisoning** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Preformed enterotoxin** (heat-stable)
- **Rapid onset** (1-6 hours)
- **Profuse vomiting** > diarrhea
- **Self-limited** (24 hours)
- Source: Contaminated food (dairy, meat) left at room temperature

**4. Toxic Shock Syndrome (TSS)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **TSST-1** (superantigen)
- **Criteria**:
  - **Fever** >38.9°C
  - **Hypotension** (shock)
  - **Diffuse macular rash** ("sunburn-like")
  - **Desquamation** (palms, soles - 1-2 weeks later)
  - **Multiorgan involvement** (≥3 systems)
- **Associated with**:
  - **Tampon use** (menstrual TSS)
  - Nasal packing, surgical wounds
- Treatment: Remove source, fluids, antistaphylococcal antibiotics

**5. Pneumonia** ⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Post-influenza** (secondary bacterial pneumonia)
- **Hospital-acquired** (ventilator-associated)
- **CA-MRSA**: Necrotizing (PVL toxin), cavitation
- **Severe**, high mortality

**6. Osteomyelitis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Most common cause** (especially hematogenous in children)
- **Long bones** (metaphysis - rich blood supply)
- **Vertebral** (adults, IVDU)

**7. Septic Arthritis**:
- **Most common cause** (monoarticular)

**8. Endocarditis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **IVDU** (right-sided - tricuspid valve)
- **Prosthetic valves**
- **Acute, destructive**

**9. Bacteremia/Sepsis**:
- **IV catheter-related**
- Can seed any organ

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- S. aureus = Most common cause of osteomyelitis, septic arthritis
- Post-influenza pneumonia
- SSSS (children, Nikolsky+)
- TSS (tampon, TSST-1, desquamation of palms/soles)

---

### Treatment

**Methicillin-sensitive S. aureus (MSSA)**:
- **Nafcillin, Oxacillin** (antistaphylococcal penicillins)
- **Cefazolin** (1st gen cephalosporin)
- **Cloxacillin, Dicloxacillin** (oral)

**Methicillin-resistant S. aureus (MRSA)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Vancomycin** (IV) - first-line
- **Linezolid** (oral/IV) - good for pneumonia, CNS
- **Daptomycin** (IV) - NOT for pneumonia (inactivated by surfactant)
- **Tigecycline**
- **Ceftaroline** (5th gen cephalosporin)

**CA-MRSA** (community-acquired):
- **TMP-SMX** (Bactrim)
- **Clindamycin**
- **Doxycycline**

**NEET PG**: MRSA = Vancomycin (IV), Linezolid (oral/IV)

---

### MRSA Mechanism

**mecA gene** (on SCCmec cassette):
- Encodes **altered PBP2a** (penicillin-binding protein)
- **Low affinity** for β-lactams
- **Resistance** to all β-lactams (except ceftaroline)

**Types**:
- **HA-MRSA** (hospital-acquired): Multi-drug resistant
- **CA-MRSA** (community-acquired): Often PVL+, fewer resistances

**NEET PG**: MRSA resistance via mecA gene (altered PBP2a)

---

## COAGULASE-NEGATIVE STAPHYLOCOCCI (CONS)

### S. epidermidis

**Characteristics**:
- **Coagulase negative**
- **Normal skin flora**
- **Biofilm formation** (slime layer - polysaccharide)

**Clinical significance**:
- **Prosthetic device infections** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - IV catheters, prosthetic valves, joints, shunts
- **Nosocomial bacteremia**
- **Prosthetic valve endocarditis** (subacute)

**Treatment**:
- **Vancomycin** (often MRSE - resistant)
- **Remove device**

**NEET PG**: S. epidermidis = Prosthetic device infections, biofilm

---

### S. saprophyticus

**Characteristics**:
- Coagulase negative
- **Novobiocin resistant** (vs S. epidermidis - sensitive)

**Clinical significance**:
- **UTI in young sexually active women** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Second most common** cause (after E. coli)

**NEET PG**: S. saprophyticus = UTI in young women, novobiocin resistant

---

## STREPTOCOCCUS

### General Characteristics

**Morphology**:
- **Gram-positive cocci**
- **Chains** or **pairs**
- Non-motile, non-spore forming

**Growth**:
- **Facultative anaerobes**
- **Catalase negative** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (vs Staphylococcus)
- **Blood agar**: Hemolysis patterns

**NEET PG**: Streptococcus = Catalase negative, chains

---

### Classification

**A. By Hemolysis on Blood Agar** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:

**1. β-hemolysis** (complete hemolysis - clear zone):
- **Group A Strep** (S. pyogenes) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Group B Strep** (S. agalactiae) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**2. α-hemolysis** (partial hemolysis - green zone):
- **S. pneumoniae** (pneumococcus) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Viridans streptococci** (S. mutans, S. sanguinis, etc.)

**3. γ-hemolysis** (no hemolysis):
- **Enterococcus** (E. faecalis, E. faecium)
- Group D strep (non-enterococcus)

**B. By Lancefield Grouping** (cell wall carbohydrate):
- Groups A, B, C, D, F, G (based on C carbohydrate)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- β-hemolysis = Complete (S. pyogenes, S. agalactiae)
- α-hemolysis = Partial/green (S. pneumoniae, viridans)
- γ-hemolysis = No hemolysis (Enterococcus)

---

## GROUP A STREPTOCOCCUS (S. pyogenes)

### Identification

- **Gram-positive cocci in chains**
- **β-hemolytic**
- **Lancefield Group A**
- **Bacitracin sensitive** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (vs Group B - resistant)
- **PYR test positive**

**NEET PG**: Group A Strep = β-hemolytic, bacitracin sensitive

---

### Virulence Factors

**1. Surface antigens**:
- **M protein** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
  - **Major virulence factor**
  - **Antiphagocytic** (inhibits complement)
  - **>80 serotypes** (type-specific immunity)
  - **Molecular mimicry** (cross-reacts with cardiac myosin, kidney → rheumatic fever, glomerulonephritis)
- **Hyaluronic acid capsule**: Antiphagocytic (mimics human tissue)

**2. Enzymes**:
- **Streptokinase** (fibrinolysin): Dissolves clots, spreads infection
- **Streptodornase** (DNase): Liquefies pus
- **Hyaluronidase**: Spreads through tissues
- **Streptolysin O** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
  - Oxygen-labile hemolysin
  - **ASO antibodies** (anti-streptolysin O) - marker of recent infection
- **Streptolysin S**: Oxygen-stable, β-hemolysis on agar

**3. Toxins**:
- **Erythrogenic toxin** (pyrogenic exotoxin):
  - **Superantigen**
  - **Scarlet fever rash** (sandpaper-like, strawberry tongue)
  - **Streptococcal toxic shock syndrome**

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- M protein = Major virulence (antiphagocytic, molecular mimicry)
- ASO (anti-streptolysin O) = Marker of GAS infection

---

### Clinical Manifestations

**1. Suppurative (Pus-forming) Infections**:

**a) Pharyngitis (Strep throat)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Most common bacterial pharyngitis**
- **Children 5-15 years**
- Sudden onset, fever, sore throat
- **Tonsillar exudate**, cervical lymphadenopathy
- **NO cough** (vs viral)
- **Centor criteria** (if 3-4 → treat empirically)
- Diagnosis: **Rapid antigen test**, throat culture
- Complications: **Rheumatic fever, glomerulonephritis**

**b) Scarlet Fever** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Pharyngitis + erythrogenic toxin-mediated rash**
- **"Sandpaper" rash** (erythematous, blanching)
- **Strawberry tongue** (white → red)
- **Pastia's lines** (rash in skin folds)
- **Circumoral pallor**
- **Desquamation** (1-2 weeks later)

**c) Pyoderma (Impetigo)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Honey-crusted lesions**
- Face, extremities
- Can lead to **post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis** (NOT rheumatic fever)

**d) Erysipelas** ⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Superficial skin infection**
- **Sharply demarcated**, raised, erythematous
- **"Orange peel" appearance**
- Face (butterfly pattern), legs

**e) Cellulitis**:
- Deeper than erysipelas
- Less well-demarcated

**f) Necrotizing Fasciitis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **"Flesh-eating" bacteria**
- **Rapidly spreading**, deep tissue
- **Pain out of proportion** to appearance
- **Crepitus** (gas), skin necrosis
- **Surgical emergency** (debridement)
- High mortality

**g) Puerperal Sepsis**:
- Postpartum endometritis, bacteremia

**2. Non-suppurative (Immunological) Complications** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:

**a) Acute Rheumatic Fever (ARF)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **2-3 weeks** after pharyngitis (NOT skin infection)
- **Molecular mimicry** (M protein cross-reacts with cardiac tissue)
- **Jones Criteria** (2 major OR 1 major + 2 minor + evidence of GAS):

**Major criteria (CASES)**:
- **C**arditis (pancarditis - most serious)
  - Mitral regurgitation (most common)
  - Aschoff bodies (granulomas)
- **A**rthritis (migratory polyarthritis - large joints)
- **S**ubcutaneous nodules (painless)
- **E**rythema marginatum (annular, serpiginous rash)
- **S**ydenham's chorea (involuntary movements - "St. Vitus dance")

**Minor criteria**:
- Fever, arthralgia, ↑ ESR/CRP, ↑ PR interval

**Evidence of GAS**: ↑ ASO titer, positive throat culture

**Treatment**:
- **Penicillin** (eradicate GAS)
- **Aspirin/NSAIDs** (arthritis, carditis)
- **Corticosteroids** (severe carditis)
- **Secondary prophylaxis**: Monthly benzathine penicillin G (prevent recurrence)

**b) Post-Streptococcal Glomerulonephritis (PSGN)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **1-3 weeks** after pharyngitis OR **3-6 weeks** after skin infection
- **Type III hypersensitivity** (immune complex deposition)
- **Nephritic syndrome**:
  - Hematuria (tea/cola-colored urine), RBC casts
  - Proteinuria (mild), oliguria
  - Hypertension, edema
  - ↑ BUN, ↑ Creatinine
- **↓ C3 complement** (returns to normal in 6-8 weeks)
- **↑ ASO** (if pharyngitis)
- **Self-limited** (good prognosis in children)
- No specific treatment (supportive)
- **NO secondary prophylaxis**

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- ARF: 2-3 weeks post-pharyngitis, Jones criteria, monthly penicillin prophylaxis
- PSGN: 1-3 weeks post-pharyngitis, nephritic syndrome, ↓ C3, self-limited

---

### Treatment

**Pharyngitis**:
- **Penicillin V** (oral) - 10 days
- **Benzathine penicillin G** (IM) - single dose
- **Amoxicillin** (better taste for children)
- If allergic: **Azithromycin, Cephalexin**

**Severe infections** (necrotizing fasciitis):
- **Penicillin G + Clindamycin** (IV)
  - Clindamycin: ↓ Toxin production, better in stationary phase
- **Surgical debridement**

**NEET PG**: GAS pharyngitis = Penicillin (10 days)

---

## GROUP B STREPTOCOCCUS (S. agalactiae)

### Identification

- **Gram-positive cocci in chains**
- **β-hemolytic**
- **Lancefield Group B**
- **Bacitracin resistant** (vs Group A - sensitive)
- **CAMP test positive** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (arrow-head hemolysis with S. aureus)
- **Hippurate hydrolysis positive**

**NEET PG**: Group B Strep = β-hemolytic, bacitracin resistant, CAMP+

---

### Clinical Significance

**Colonization**:
- **Normal vaginal/GI flora** (20-30% women)

**Neonatal infections** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:

**1. Early-onset** (<7 days, usually <24 hours):
- **Vertical transmission** (during delivery)
- **Sepsis, pneumonia, meningitis**
- High mortality

**2. Late-onset** (7 days - 3 months):
- **Meningitis** (most common)
- Horizontal or vertical transmission

**Risk factors**:
- Preterm delivery, prolonged rupture of membranes (>18 hours)
- Maternal fever, previous GBS infant

**Adults**:
- **Postpartum endometritis**
- UTI, bacteremia (elderly, diabetics)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- GBS = Most common cause of neonatal meningitis & sepsis
- Early-onset (<7 days), late-onset (7 days-3 months)

---

### Prevention & Treatment

**Intrapartum Antibiotic Prophylaxis (IAP)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Screen all pregnant women** at 35-37 weeks (vaginal/rectal culture)
- **Give if**:
  - GBS positive
  - Previous GBS infant
  - GBS bacteriuria during pregnancy
  - Unknown status + risk factors (preterm labor, ROM >18 hours, fever)
- **Penicillin G (IV)** - during labor (≥4 hours before delivery)
- Alternative: **Ampicillin, Cefazolin**
- If penicillin allergy: **Clindamycin or Vancomycin**

**Treatment of infection**:
- **Ampicillin + Gentamicin** (neonatal sepsis/meningitis)
- **Penicillin G** (adults)

**NEET PG**: GBS prophylaxis = Screen at 35-37 weeks, Penicillin G during labor

---

## STREPTOCOCCUS PNEUMONIAE (Pneumococcus)

### Identification

- **Gram-positive diplococci** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (lancet-shaped, pairs)
- **α-hemolytic** (green zone)
- **Optochin sensitive** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (vs viridans - resistant)
- **Bile soluble** (lysed by bile salts)
- **Quellung reaction**: Capsular swelling with antisera

**NEET PG**: S. pneumoniae = α-hemolytic, optochin sensitive, diplococci

---

### Virulence Factors

**Polysaccharide capsule** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Most important virulence factor**
- **>90 serotypes**
- **Antiphagocytic**
- **Vaccine target**

**IgA protease**: Cleaves IgA (mucosal immunity)

**Pneumolysin**: Pore-forming toxin

**NEET PG**: Pneumococcal capsule = Most important virulence factor

---

### Clinical Manifestations

**1. Pneumonia** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Most common cause** of community-acquired pneumonia (CAP)
- **Lobar pneumonia** (classic):
  - Abrupt onset, high fever, productive cough ("rusty" sputum)
  - Pleuritic chest pain
  - **Consolidation** on CXR
- **Complications**: Empyema, bacteremia

**2. Meningitis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Most common cause** in adults
- High mortality
- **Complications**: Hearing loss, neurological sequelae

**3. Otitis Media** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Most common cause** in children

**4. Sinusitis**:
- Common cause

**5. Bacteremia/Sepsis**:
- Especially in **asplenic patients** (overwhelming post-splenectomy infection - OPSI)
- **Sickle cell disease** (functional asplenia)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- S. pneumoniae = Most common CAP, meningitis (adults), otitis media (children)
- Asplenic patients at high risk (give vaccine)

---

### Treatment

**Penicillin-sensitive**:
- **Penicillin G or Amoxicillin**

**Penicillin-resistant**:
- **Ceftriaxone, Cefotaxime** (3rd gen cephalosporin)
- **Vancomycin** (if highly resistant)
- **Fluoroquinolones** (levofloxacin, moxifloxacin)

**Meningitis**: **Vancomycin + Ceftriaxone** (empiric)

**NEET PG**: Pneumococcal meningitis = Vancomycin + Ceftriaxone

---

### Vaccines ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**1. Pneumococcal Conjugate Vaccine (PCV13)**:
- **13 serotypes**
- **T-cell dependent** (conjugated to protein)
- **Children <2 years** (2, 4, 6, 12-15 months)
- **Better immune response** in young children

**2. Pneumococcal Polysaccharide Vaccine (PPSV23)**:
- **23 serotypes** (covers 85-90% of disease-causing strains)
- **T-cell independent** (polysaccharide only)
- **Adults ≥65 years**
- **High-risk**: Asplenia, sickle cell, immunocompromised, chronic diseases

**Recommendations**:
- **All children**: PCV13 series
- **Adults ≥65**: PCV13 → PPSV23 (≥1 year apart)
- **Asplenic patients**: PCV13 + PPSV23 (before splenectomy if elective)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- PCV13 = Children, conjugate (T-dependent)
- PPSV23 = Adults ≥65, asplenic, polysaccharide (T-independent)

---

## VIRIDANS STREPTOCOCCI

### Characteristics

- **α-hemolytic**
- **Optochin resistant** (vs S. pneumoniae)
- **Bile insoluble**
- **Normal oral flora**

**Species**: S. mutans, S. sanguinis, S. salivarius, S. mitis

**NEET PG**: Viridans = α-hemolytic, optochin resistant

---

### Clinical Significance

**1. Dental caries** ⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **S. mutans** (most cariogenic)
- Produces dextran (biofilm on teeth)

**2. Subacute bacterial endocarditis (SBE)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Most common cause**
- **Damaged/abnormal valves** (rheumatic heart disease, congenital)
- **Following dental procedures**
- **Indolent course** (vs S. aureus - acute)

**Prophylaxis**:
- **Amoxicillin** before dental procedures (if high-risk cardiac conditions)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Viridans = Subacute endocarditis (damaged valves), dental caries
- Prophylaxis before dental procedures

---

## ENTEROCOCCUS

### Characteristics

- **Gram-positive cocci in chains or pairs**
- **γ-hemolysis** (or α-hemolysis)
- **Group D antigen** (Lancefield)
- **Catalase negative**
- Grow in **6.5% NaCl**, **bile**, **40°C**
- **PYR positive**, **Bile esculin positive**

**Species**: **E. faecalis** (90%), **E. faecium** (10%)

**NEET PG**: Enterococcus = Group D, grow in 6.5% NaCl, bile esculin+

---

### Clinical Significance

**Normal gut flora**

**Opportunistic infections**:
- **UTI** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (especially nosocomial, catheter-related)
- **Intra-abdominal infections** (polymicrobial)
- **Endocarditis** (subacute)
- **Bacteremia** (nosocomial)

**NEET PG**: Enterococcus = UTI (nosocomial), biliary tract infections

---

### Resistance

**Intrinsic resistance**:
- **Cephalosporins** (all)
- **Aminoglycosides** (low-level - monotherapy ineffective)

**Acquired resistance**:
- **Vancomycin-resistant Enterococcus (VRE)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **vanA, vanB genes** (altered D-Ala-D-Ala target)
  - **Major nosocomial problem**

**NEET PG**: Enterococcus intrinsically resistant to cephalosporins

---

### Treatment

**Uncomplicated UTI**:
- **Ampicillin or Nitrofurantoin**

**Serious infections** (endocarditis, bacteremia):
- **Ampicillin + Gentamicin** (synergy)
- **Vancomycin + Gentamicin** (if β-lactam resistant)

**VRE**:
- **Linezolid**
- **Daptomycin**
- **Tigecycline**

**NEET PG**: VRE = Linezolid, daptomycin

## Practice MCQs

1. **Test to differentiate Staphylococcus from Streptococcus?**
   Answer: Catalase (Staph +, Strep -)

2. **Test to differentiate S. aureus from CONS?**
   Answer: Coagulase (S. aureus +, CONS -)

3. **Toxin causing scalded skin syndrome?**
   Answer: Exfoliative toxin (S. aureus)

4. **Treatment for MRSA?**
   Answer: Vancomycin (IV), Linezolid (oral/IV)

5. **Test to differentiate Group A from Group B Strep?**
   Answer: Bacitracin (A sensitive, B resistant)

6. **Complications of Group A Strep pharyngitis?**
   Answer: Rheumatic fever, post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis

7. **When to screen for GBS in pregnancy?**
   Answer: 35-37 weeks

8. **Test to differentiate S. pneumoniae from viridans?**
   Answer: Optochin (Pneumococcus sensitive, viridans resistant)

9. **Most common cause of CAP?**
   Answer: Streptococcus pneumoniae

10. **Most common cause of subacute endocarditis?**
    Answer: Viridans streptococci
