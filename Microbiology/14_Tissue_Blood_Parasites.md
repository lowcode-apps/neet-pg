# Tissue & Blood Parasites

## BLOOD & TISSUE PROTOZOA

### 1. TOXOPLASMA GONDII ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Definitive Host**: Cats (sexual reproduction) ⭐
**Intermediate Hosts**: Humans, birds, mammals

**Transmission**:
- **Oocysts** in cat feces (litter boxes, soil)
- **Tissue cysts** in undercooked meat (lamb, pork, venison)
- **Transplacental** (congenital)
- Transplantation, transfusion (rare)

**Clinical**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
```
IMMUNOCOMPETENT: Asymptomatic (90%) or mild lymphadenopathy

CONGENITAL TOXOPLASMOSIS: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
• Primary maternal infection during pregnancy (especially 1st/early 2nd trimester)
• Classic Triad:
  1. Hydrocephalus
  2. Chorioretinitis ⭐ (bilateral, macular scarring)
  3. Intracranial calcifications ⭐ (diffuse, scattered)
• Seizures, developmental delay, hearing loss
• Prevention: Pregnant women avoid cat litter, undercooked meat

IMMUNOCOMPROMISED (AIDS, CD4 <100): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
• CNS Toxoplasmosis (most common):
  - **Multiple ring-enhancing lesions** on MRI/CT ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - Basal ganglia, corticomedullary junction
  - Headache, confusion, seizures, focal deficits
• Chorioretinitis
• Pneumonitis, myocarditis (rare)
```

**Diagnosis**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Serology**: IgM (acute), IgG (past/chronic)
  - Acute infection: IgM+, rising IgG
  - IgG avidity: Low = recent, High = remote
- **CNS toxoplasmosis**: Presumptive diagnosis (serology + imaging) → Empiric treatment → Response confirms
- **PCR**: Amniotic fluid (congenital), CSF, blood
- Brain biopsy (if no response to treatment)

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Acute infection** (pregnant, immunocompromised):
  - **Pyrimethamine** + **Sulfadiazine** + **Leucovorin** (folinic acid) ⭐
  - Pregnancy: Spiramycin (1st trimester), pyrimethamine-sulfadiazine (2nd/3rd)
- **Prophylaxis** (AIDS, CD4 <100):
  - **TMP-SMX DS daily** (also covers PCP, same regimen)
  - Stop when CD4 >200 × 3 months on ART

### 2. TRYPANOSOMA Species ⭐⭐⭐⭐

#### **African Trypanosomiasis (Sleeping Sickness)**:
- **T. brucei gambiense** (West/Central Africa) - Chronic (months-years)
- **T. brucei rhodesiense** (East Africa) - Acute (weeks-months)
- **Vector**: Tsetse fly ⭐

**Clinical**:
1. **Hemolymphatic stage**: Fever, lymphadenopathy (Winterbottom's sign - posterior cervical), hepatosplenomegaly
2. **CNS stage** (meningoencephalitis): ⭐
   - Altered sleep-wake cycle ⭐ ("sleeping sickness")
   - Behavioral changes, confusion, seizures
   - Coma, death if untreated

**Diagnosis**: Blood smear (trypomastigotes), CSF examination

**Treatment**: Stage-dependent
- Early (no CNS): Pentamidine (T.b. gambiense) or Suramin (T.b. rhodesiense)
- Late (CNS): Eflornithine + nifurtimox

#### **American Trypanosomiasis (Chagas Disease)**:
- **T. cruzi**
- **Vector**: Reduviid bug (kissing bug, triatomine) ⭐
- Transmission: Bug feces in bite wound (also transfusion, transplant, congenital)

**Clinical**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
1. **Acute** (often asymptomatic):
   - **Romaña's sign** ⭐ (unilateral palpebral edema if bite near eye)
   - Chagoma (nodule at bite site)
   - Fever, lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly
2. **Chronic** (10-30 years later):
   - **Cardiomyopathy** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (dilated, arrhythmias, heart failure, sudden death)
   - **Megaesophagus, megacolon** ⭐⭐⭐ (achalasia-like, constipation)

**Diagnosis**: Blood smear (acute - trypomastigotes), serology (chronic), PCR

**Treatment**:
- Acute: **Benznidazole** or **Nifurtimox** ⭐
- Chronic: Limited efficacy (treat early chronic, controversial in late)

### 3. LEISHMANIA Species ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Vector**: Sandfly ⭐
**Forms**: Amastigotes (in macrophages), Promastigotes (in sandfly)

**Clinical Forms**:
```
CUTANEOUS LEISHMANIASIS (CL): ⭐⭐⭐⭐
• Most common form
• Papule → ulcer with raised border ("volcano crater")
• Face, extremities
• Self-healing (months) but scars
• Species: L. major, L. tropica, L. mexicana
• Treatment: Local (small) or systemic

MUCOCUTANEOUS LEISHMANIASIS (MCL): ⭐⭐⭐
• L. braziliensis
• South America (Amazon)
• Destruction of nasal/oral mucosa
• "Espundia" - disfiguring
• Treatment required

VISCERAL LEISHMANIASIS (Kala-azar): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
• L. donovani, L. infantum
• Disseminates to liver, spleen, bone marrow
• Triad:
  1. Massive splenomegaly ⭐
  2. Fever (undulant, "dum-dum fever")
  3. Pancytopenia
• Hyperpigmentation ("kala-azar" = black fever)
• Fatal if untreated
• Post-kala-azar dermal leishmaniasis (PKDL)
```

**Diagnosis**: ⭐
- Tissue aspirate/biopsy: Amastigotes in macrophages (Giemsa stain)
- Splenic aspirate (gold standard for VL, highest yield but risky)
- Bone marrow aspirate
- PCR, serology (rK39 antigen - VL)

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Visceral**: **Liposomal amphotericin B** (preferred) ⭐ OR Miltefosine OR Paromomycin OR Sodium stibogluconate (antimonials)
- **Cutaneous**: Local therapy (small) or systemic (multiple/large lesions)
- **Mucocutaneous**: Liposomal amphotericin B or antimonials

## BLOOD & TISSUE HELMINTHS

### FILARIAL NEMATODES ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

#### **1. WUCHERERIA BANCROFTI / BRUGIA MALAYI** (Lymphatic Filariasis)

**Vector**: Mosquito ⭐
**Endemic**: Tropical/subtropical

**Clinical**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Acute**: Lymphangitis, lymphadenitis, fever
- **Chronic**:
  - **Lymphedema** → **Elephantiasis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (massive swelling of legs, scrotum, breasts)
  - **Hydrocele** (scrotal swelling)
  - **Chyluria** (lymphatic rupture into urinary tract)
- **Tropical pulmonary eosinophilia**: Cough, wheezing, eosinophilia (hypersensitivity to microfilariae)

**Diagnosis**: ⭐
- **Blood smear** (thick drop, Giemsa): Microfilariae ⭐
  - **Nocturnal periodicity** ⭐ (sample at NIGHT, 10 PM-2 AM)
  - Sheathed microfilariae
- Antigen tests (W. bancrofti), ultrasound ("filarial dance sign" - adult worms in scrotum)

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **DEC (Diethylcarbamazine)** ⭐ + Albendazole
- Alternative: Ivermectin + Albendazole
- **Mass drug administration** (MDA) for elimination
- Manage lymphedema: Hygiene, elevation, compression

---

#### **2. ONCHOCERCA VOLVULUS** (River Blindness)

**Vector**: Blackfly (Simulium) ⭐ (near rivers)
**Endemic**: Sub-Saharan Africa, Yemen, Central/South America

**Clinical**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Cutaneous**: Pruritus, dermatitis, subcutaneous nodules (onchocercomas - contain adult worms)
- **Ocular**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - Microfilariae in anterior chamber, cornea
  - **Keratitis → Blindness** ⭐ ("river blindness")
  - Leading infectious cause of blindness worldwide

**Diagnosis**:
- **Skin snip** ⭐: Microfilariae (bloodless skin biopsy)
- Slit-lamp exam (eyes): Microfilariae
- PCR

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Ivermectin 150 μg/kg every 6-12 months** ⭐ (kills microfilariae, NOT adults)
- Repeat dosing required (adults live 10-15 years)
- Surgical removal of nodules (cosmetic, near eyes)
- **Mass drug administration** (MDA) programs

---

#### **3. LOA LOA** (African Eye Worm)

**Vector**: Mango/deer fly (Chrysops) ⭐
**Endemic**: Central/West Africa

**Clinical**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Calabar swellings** ⭐ (transient subcutaneous edema, hands/wrists)
- **Subconjunctival migration** ⭐ (worm crosses eye - visible, patient feels it!)
- Pruritus, urticaria

**Diagnosis**:
- **Blood smear**: Microfilariae (diurnal periodicity - sample during DAY) ⭐
- Visualize adult worm in eye

**Treatment**:
- **DEC** (caution - can cause encephalopathy if heavy microfilaremia)
- Surgical removal of worm from eye

### SCHISTOSOMA (Blood Flukes) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Species**:
- **S. mansoni** (Africa, South America) - Intestinal
- **S. japonicum** (Asia) - Intestinal
- **S. haematobium** (Africa, Middle East) - Urogenital ⭐

**Transmission**: **Freshwater exposure** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Cercariae (larval form) released from snails → Penetrate human skin

**Life Cycle**:
- Cercariae penetrate skin → Bloodstream → Lungs → Liver → Mature
- Adults live in mesenteric veins (S. mansoni/japonicum) or vesical plexus (S. haematobium)
- Eggs deposited in vessels → **Trapped in tissues** ⭐ (not excreted) → Granulomas → Fibrosis

**Clinical**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
```
ACUTE (Katayama Fever): ⭐
• 4-8 weeks after exposure
• Fever, urticaria, cough, hepatosplenomegaly
• Eosinophilia
• Hypersensitivity to eggs

CHRONIC (months-years):
S. MANSONI / S. JAPONICUM: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
• Intestinal: Bloody diarrhea, abdominal pain
• Hepatic:
  - **Periportal fibrosis** ⭐ ("Symmers' pipestem fibrosis")
  - Portal hypertension → varices, splenomegaly, ascites
  - Liver function preserved (presinusoidal obstruction)
• Complications: GI bleeding, hepatosplenomegaly

S. HAEMATOBIUM: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
• **Urogenital**:
  - **Terminal hematuria** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (painless, end of urination)
  - Dysuria, frequency
  - **Bladder wall calcification** (X-ray) ⭐
  - Hydronephrosis, chronic kidney disease
  - **Squamous cell carcinoma of bladder** ⭐⭐⭐ (chronic complication)
• Genital: Lesions on cervix, vagina; infertility
```

**Diagnosis**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Stool (S. mansoni/japonicum)** or **Urine (S. haematobium)**: ⭐
  - **Eggs** with **lateral spine** (S. mansoni), terminal spine (S. haematobium), rudimentary spine (S. japonicum)
  - Sample midday urine (S. haematobium - highest egg count)
  - Concentration techniques
- **Serology**: Antibodies (exposure, not active infection)
- **PCR**, rectal/bladder biopsy

**Treatment**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Praziquantel 40 mg/kg (single dose or divided)** ⭐
  - S. haematobium/mansoni: 40 mg/kg
  - S. japonicum: 60 mg/kg (3 doses)
- **Mass drug administration** in endemic areas
- Prevention: Avoid freshwater exposure, snail control

## LARVA MIGRANS SYNDROMES

### **Cutaneous Larva Migrans (CLM)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Ancylostoma braziliense** (dog/cat hookworm)
- Transmission: Walking barefoot on contaminated sand/soil (beaches)
- Clinical: **Serpiginous, pruritic, erythematous track** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (larvae migrate in epidermis)
  - "Creeping eruption"
  - Feet, buttocks
  - Self-limited (weeks-months)
- Treatment: **Ivermectin** or **Albendazole**

### **Visceral Larva Migrans (VLM)** ⭐⭐⭐
- **Toxocara canis/cati** (dog/cat roundworm)
- Transmission: Ingestion of eggs (soil, feces) - children (pica, geophagia)
- Clinical:
  - **Fever, hepatomegaly, eosinophilia** ⭐
  - Pneumonitis, cough, wheezing
  - **Ocular larva migrans** (OLM): Granuloma in retina → vision loss
- Diagnosis: Serology (ELISA), eosinophilia, imaging
- Treatment: Albendazole (VLM), observation or steroids (OLM)

## NEET PG MCQS

**Q1.** Multiple ring-enhancing brain lesions in AIDS patient (CD4 <100). Most likely diagnosis?
A) Tuberculoma B) Toxoplasmosis C) Neurocysticercosis D) Brain abscess

**Answer: B** - Toxoplasma encephalitis (most common cause of focal brain lesions in AIDS)

**Q2.** Painless terminal hematuria in patient from Africa. Causative organism?
A) S. mansoni B) S. japonicum C) S. haematobium D) S. intercalatum

**Answer: C** - S. haematobium causes urogenital schistosomiasis (terminal hematuria, bladder calcification)

**Q3.** Blood sample for W. bancrofti microfilariae should be collected:
A) Morning B) Afternoon C) Night (10 PM-2 AM) D) Anytime

**Answer: C** - W. bancrofti has nocturnal periodicity (sample at night)
