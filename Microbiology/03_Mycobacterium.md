# MYCOBACTERIUM - NEET PG Study Material

## GENERAL CHARACTERISTICS

### Unique Features

**Cell wall**:
- **High lipid content** (60%) - **Mycolic acid**
- **Waxy, hydrophobic**
- **Acid-fast** (resists decolorization by acid-alcohol)
- Slow growth (long generation time - 12-24 hours)

**Staining**:
- **Acid-fast stain** (Ziehl-Neelsen, Kinyoun) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Does NOT stain with Gram stain** (waxy cell wall)
- **Auramine-rhodamine** (fluorescent - more sensitive)

**Growth**:
- **Obligate aerobes**
- **Slow-growing** (weeks to months)
- **Special media**: Löwenstein-Jensen (LJ), Middlebrook 7H10/7H11

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Mycobacterium = Acid-fast (mycolic acid), slow-growing, obligate aerobe

---

## CLASSIFICATION

### By Growth Rate

**Slow-growers** (>7 days):
- **M. tuberculosis complex** (M. tuberculosis, M. bovis, M. africanum)
- **M. leprae**
- **M. avium complex (MAC)**

**Rapid-growers** (<7 days):
- **M. fortuitum, M. chelonae, M. abscessus**

---

### By Pigment Production (Runyon Classification)

**Photochromogens** (Group I):
- **Pigment in light** only
- M. kansasii, M. marinum

**Scotochromogens** (Group II):
- **Pigment in dark**
- M. scrofulaceum, M. gordonae

**Nonchromogens** (Group III):
- **No pigment**
- M. avium complex, M. intracellulare

**Rapid growers** (Group IV):
- M. fortuitum, M. chelonae, M. abscessus

**NEET PG**: Know Runyon classification (not very high-yield)

---

## MYCOBACTERIUM TUBERCULOSIS

### Epidemiology

**Global burden**:
- **1/4 of world population** infected (latent TB)
- **#1 infectious disease killer** (until COVID-19)
- **India**: Highest TB burden country

**Transmission**:
- **Airborne droplets** (cough, sneeze, talk)
- **Infectious dose**: 1-10 bacilli
- **Prolonged close contact** required

**Risk factors**:
- **HIV/AIDS** (most important)
- Diabetes, malnutrition, alcoholism
- Immunosuppression (steroids, TNF-α inhibitors)
- Silicosis
- Crowding, poverty

**NEET PG**: TB transmission = Airborne droplets, HIV biggest risk factor

---

### Pathogenesis

**Primary TB** (initial infection):

1. **Inhalation** of M. tb → Alveolar macrophages

2. **Macrophage phagocytosis**:
   - Bacteria survive intracellularly (inhibit phagolysosome fusion)
   - Multiply within macrophages

3. **Cell-mediated immunity** (2-8 weeks):
   - **T helper cells (Th1)** activate macrophages
   - **Granuloma formation** (containment)

4. **Ghon complex** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
   - **Ghon focus**: Lung parenchyma (usually lower lobe)
   - **+ Hilar lymph node** involvement
   - **Calcified** (visible on CXR)

5. **Outcomes**:
   - **90%**: **Latent TB** (contained, no symptoms, non-infectious)
   - **10%**: **Primary progressive TB** (especially children, immunocompromised)

**Secondary (Reactivation) TB**:
- **Reactivation** of latent infection (years later)
- **Apical segments** of lungs ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (high O₂)
- **Cavity formation** (necrosis, caseation)
- **Infectious** (open cavities)

**Miliary TB** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Hematogenous dissemination**
- **"Millet seed"** appearance on CXR (diffuse nodules)
- **Severe, life-threatening**
- All organs involved (lungs, liver, spleen, bone marrow, meninges)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Primary TB = Ghon complex (lung + hilar LN), usually latent
- Secondary TB = Apical cavities, reactivation
- Miliary TB = Hematogenous spread, millet seed pattern

---

### Clinical Manifestations

**Pulmonary TB** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (most common):

**Symptoms**:
- **Chronic cough** (>2-3 weeks)
- **Hemoptysis** (blood in sputum)
- **Fever** (low-grade, evening rise)
- **Night sweats**
- **Weight loss** ("consumption")
- **Fatigue, anorexia**

**Signs**:
- **Apical findings**: Dullness, crackles, bronchial breathing
- Cachexia

**CXR findings**:
- **Apical cavities** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (reactivation TB)
- **Fibronodular infiltrates**
- **Pleural effusion**
- **Miliary pattern** (disseminated)
- **Ghon complex** (healed primary TB)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Pulmonary TB = Chronic cough, hemoptysis, fever, night sweats, weight loss
- CXR = Apical cavities (reactivation)

---

**Extrapulmonary TB** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (30-40% in HIV+):

**1. Tuberculous Lymphadenitis** (scrofula):
- **Most common** extrapulmonary site
- **Cervical lymph nodes** (matted, non-tender)
- **Cold abscess** (no warmth, fluctuant)

**2. Tuberculous Meningitis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Most serious** (high mortality/morbidity)
- **Subacute** onset (weeks)
- **Basal meningitis** (cranial nerve palsies - II, III, VI)
- **CSF**:
  - ↑ Protein, ↓ Glucose, lymphocytic pleocytosis
  - **AFB smear** (low sensitivity <50%)
  - **GeneXpert** (better sensitivity)
- **Complications**: Hydrocephalus, infarcts, tuberculomas
- **Treatment**: 4 drugs + **steroids** (dexamethasone)

**3. Skeletal TB** (Pott's disease):
- **Vertebral TB** (thoracic > lumbar)
- **Gibbus deformity** (kyphosis)
- **Paravertebral abscess** (cold abscess)
- **Psoas abscess**
- Can cause **spinal cord compression**

**4. Genitourinary TB**:
- **Sterile pyuria** (WBCs but negative cultures)
- Dysuria, hematuria, frequency
- **Renal TB**: Scarring, calcification

**5. Gastrointestinal TB**:
- **Ileocecal region** (most common)
- Abdominal pain, obstruction, fistulas
- **Peritoneal TB**: Ascites (exudative, high protein, lymphocytic)

**6. Pericardial TB**:
- **Constrictive pericarditis**
- Pericardial effusion

**7. Adrenal TB**:
- **Addison's disease** (adrenal insufficiency)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Most common extrapulmonary = Lymphadenitis (scrofula)
- TB meningitis = Basal, cranial nerve palsies, give steroids
- Pott's disease = Vertebral TB, gibbus deformity

---

### Diagnosis

**1. Sputum Smear Microscopy** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Ziehl-Neelsen (ZN) stain** or **Auramine-rhodamine** (fluorescent)
- **3 sputum samples** (early morning)
- **AFB (Acid-Fast Bacilli)** = Red rods on blue background
- **Sensitivity**: 50-70% (needs 5,000-10,000 bacilli/ml)
- **Cannot differentiate** M. tb from NTM (non-tuberculous mycobacteria)
- **Fast, cheap**

**2. Sputum Culture** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Gold standard** (sensitivity & specificity)
- **Löwenstein-Jensen (LJ) medium** or **Middlebrook**
- **Slow** (4-8 weeks for growth)
- **Drug susceptibility testing (DST)** possible
- **MGIT** (Mycobacteria Growth Indicator Tube) - faster (2 weeks), automated

**3. GeneXpert MTB/RIF** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Nucleic acid amplification test (NAAT)**
- Detects **M. tb DNA** + **Rifampicin resistance** (rpoB gene mutations)
- **Rapid** (2 hours)
- **High sensitivity** (90%) & specificity (98%)
- **WHO-recommended** initial test (replacing smear)
- **Preferred** in:
  - HIV+, presumed drug-resistant TB
  - Extrapulmonary TB (CSF, pleural fluid)
  - Children

**4. Tuberculin Skin Test (TST, Mantoux)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Intradermal injection** of **purified protein derivative (PPD)**
- Read at **48-72 hours** (measure **induration**, not erythema)

**Interpretation** (induration diameter):
- **≥5 mm**: Positive if:
  - HIV+, recent contact, CXR consistent with TB, immunosuppressed
- **≥10 mm**: Positive if:
  - Recent immigrants, IVDU, healthcare workers, chronic diseases, children <4 years
- **≥15 mm**: Positive in low-risk individuals

**Limitations**:
- **Does NOT differentiate active from latent TB**
- **False positive**: BCG vaccination (usually <10 mm), NTM
- **False negative**: Anergy (HIV, overwhelming TB, malnutrition)

**5. Interferon-Gamma Release Assays (IGRAs)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Blood test** (QuantiFERON-TB Gold, T-SPOT.TB)
- Measures **IFN-γ** release by T-cells in response to TB antigens
- **More specific** than TST (NOT affected by BCG)
- **Does NOT differentiate active from latent TB**
- Preferred in **BCG-vaccinated** populations

**6. Chest X-ray**:
- See findings above

**7. Histopathology**:
- **Caseating granulomas** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Caseous necrosis** (cheese-like)
  - **Epithelioid cells, Langhans giant cells** (horseshoe nucleus)
  - **Lymphocytes** surrounding
- **Ziehl-Neelsen stain** on tissue

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Sputum culture = Gold standard (slow, 4-8 weeks)
- GeneXpert = Rapid (2 hours), detects rifampicin resistance
- TST = Latent TB screening, read at 48-72 hours
- IGRA = More specific than TST (BCG doesn't affect)
- Histology = Caseating granulomas (Langhans giant cells)

---

### Treatment

**Principles**:
- **Multiple drugs** (prevent resistance)
- **Prolonged therapy** (slow-growing bacteria, intracellular)
- **Directly Observed Therapy (DOT)** (ensure compliance)

---

#### Drug-Sensitive TB (First-Line Drugs) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Mnemonic: RIPE**

**Intensive Phase** (2 months):
- **R**ifampicin
- **I**soniazid (INH)
- **P**yrazinamide (PZA)
- **E**thambutol

**Continuation Phase** (4 months):
- **R**ifampicin
- **I**soniazid (INH)

**Total duration**: **6 months** (2HRZE + 4HR)

**Extrapulmonary TB**:
- **TB meningitis, bone/joint TB**: **12 months** (2HRZE + 10HR)
- **Add steroids** for TB meningitis, pericarditis

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Drug-sensitive TB = RIPE (2 months) then RH (4 months) = 6 months total
- TB meningitis = 12 months + steroids

---

#### First-Line Anti-TB Drugs

**1. RIFAMPICIN** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:

**Mechanism**:
- Inhibits **DNA-dependent RNA polymerase** (bacterial transcription)

**Spectrum**: Broad (also used for leprosy, MAC)

**Adverse effects**:
- **Orange discoloration** of body fluids (tears, urine, sweat)
- **Hepatotoxicity** (monitor LFTs)
- **GI upset**
- **Flu-like syndrome** (intermittent dosing)
- **Thrombocytopenia**
- **Drug interactions** (potent **CYP450 inducer**):
  - ↓ Efficacy of: OCP, warfarin, antiretrovirals, anticonvulsants

**NEET PG**: Rifampicin = Orange body fluids, hepatotoxic, CYP inducer

---

**2. ISONIAZID (INH)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:

**Mechanism**:
- **Prodrug** (activated by bacterial catalase-peroxidase)
- Inhibits **mycolic acid synthesis** (cell wall)

**Adverse effects**:
- **Peripheral neuropathy** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
  - ↓ Vitamin B6 (pyridoxine)
  - **Prevention**: **Pyridoxine supplementation** (vitamin B6)
  - **Especially**: Diabetes, HIV, alcoholics, malnutrition, pregnancy
- **Hepatotoxicity** (most common serious effect)
  - Monitor LFTs
- **Drug-induced lupus**
- **Seizures** (overdose)
- **Optic neuritis** (rare)

**Drug interactions**:
- **CYP450 inhibitor**: ↑ Phenytoin, warfarin levels

**Prophylaxis** (Latent TB):
- **Isoniazid alone** × 6-9 months
- For: TST/IGRA positive, recent contact, HIV+

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- INH = Peripheral neuropathy (give pyridoxine), hepatotoxic
- INH alone for latent TB prophylaxis (6-9 months)

---

**3. PYRAZINAMIDE (PZA)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐:

**Mechanism**:
- **Prodrug** (converted by bacterial pyrazinamidase)
- Acidic pH → Disrupts membrane (effective against intracellular, dormant bacilli)

**Adverse effects**:
- **Hyperuricemia** (gout) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
  - Inhibits renal uric acid excretion
  - **Most common**
- **Hepatotoxicity**
- **Arthralgias**

**NEET PG**: PZA = Hyperuricemia (gout), hepatotoxic

---

**4. ETHAMBUTOL** ⭐⭐⭐⭐:

**Mechanism**:
- Inhibits **arabinosyl transferase** (cell wall synthesis)

**Adverse effects**:
- **Optic neuritis** (retrobulbar neuritis) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
  - **Red-green color blindness** (early sign)
  - **Decreased visual acuity**
  - **Dose-dependent, reversible** (if caught early)
  - **Contraindicated** in children <5 years (cannot report visual changes)
  - **Monitor**: Visual acuity, color vision (baseline & monthly)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Ethambutol = Optic neuritis (red-green color blindness)
- Contraindicated <5 years (cannot report symptoms)

---

#### Second-Line Drugs (Drug-Resistant TB)

**Fluoroquinolones**:
- **Levofloxacin, Moxifloxacin, Gatifloxacin**
- **Most effective** second-line drugs

**Injectable agents**:
- **Amikacin, Kanamycin** (aminoglycosides - ototoxic, nephrotoxic)
- **Capreomycin** (similar to aminoglycosides)

**Others**:
- **Ethionamide** (hepatotoxic, GI)
- **Cycloserine** (psychiatric, seizures)
- **PAS** (para-aminosalicylic acid) - GI
- **Linezolid** (myelosuppression, neuropathy)
- **Bedaquiline** ⭐⭐⭐ (new, for MDR-TB, QT prolongation)
- **Delamanid** (new, for MDR-TB)

**NEET PG**: Second-line for MDR-TB = Fluoroquinolones (best), aminoglycosides, bedaquiline

---

#### Drug-Resistant TB

**Multidrug-Resistant TB (MDR-TB)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- Resistant to **Rifampicin + Isoniazid** (at minimum)
- **Treatment**: 18-24 months (fluoroquinolone + injectable + others)
- **GeneXpert** detects rifampicin resistance (surrogate for MDR)

**Extensively Drug-Resistant TB (XDR-TB)**:
- MDR-TB + resistance to fluoroquinolone + injectable (amikacin/kanamycin/capreomycin)
- Very difficult to treat, high mortality

**Totally Drug-Resistant TB (TDR-TB)**:
- Resistant to all first & second-line drugs
- Rare

**NEET PG**: MDR-TB = Resistant to rifampicin + INH, treat 18-24 months

---

### BCG Vaccine ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Bacille Calmette-Guérin**:
- **Live attenuated** M. bovis
- **Intradermal** (left deltoid)
- **At birth** or infancy

**Efficacy**:
- **Protects against severe childhood TB** (meningitis, miliary) - 70-80%
- **Poor protection** against pulmonary TB in adults (~50%)

**Scar**: Healing leaves **BCG scar** (vaccine "took")

**Side effects**:
- **BCG-itis** (local ulceration, lymphadenitis)
- **Disseminated BCG** (immunocompromised - contraindicated)

**Contraindications**:
- **HIV** (symptomatic), immunodeficiency
- Pregnancy

**Effect on TST**:
- Can cause **false-positive TST** (usually <10 mm, wanes over time)
- **IGRA NOT affected**

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- BCG = Live attenuated M. bovis, at birth
- Protects against severe childhood TB (meningitis, miliary)
- Causes false-positive TST (IGRA unaffected)

## MYCOBACTERIUM LEPRAE (Leprosy, Hansen's Disease)

### General Characteristics

**Unique features**:
- **Acid-fast** (modified - weakly positive)
- **Cannot be cultured** in vitro ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Grows in **armadillos, mouse footpads** (research)
- **Obligate intracellular**
- **Very slow** (doubling time 12-14 days)
- **Infects peripheral nerves & skin**

**Transmission**:
- **Respiratory droplets** (prolonged contact)
- Low infectivity

**Incubation**: **Long** (2-10 years)

**NEET PG**: M. leprae cannot be cultured in vitro, very slow-growing

---

### Pathogenesis & Spectrum

**Depends on cell-mediated immunity (CMI)**:

**Strong CMI** → **Tuberculoid leprosy** (TT, BT)
**Weak CMI** → **Lepromatous leprosy** (LL, BL)

**Ridley-Jopling Classification** (spectrum):
```
TT → BT → BB → BL → LL
(Tuberculoid) ← (Borderline) → (Lepromatous)
```

**NEET PG**: Leprosy spectrum depends on CMI (strong → tuberculoid, weak → lepromatous)

---

### Clinical Classification (WHO - Simplified)

**Paucibacillary (PB)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **1-5 skin lesions**
- **Skin smear negative** (AFB -)
- Includes: **TT, BT**
- **Better prognosis**

**Multibacillary (MB)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **>5 skin lesions**
- **Skin smear positive** (AFB +)
- Includes: **BB, BL, LL**
- **Worse prognosis**, more complications

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- PB = 1-5 lesions, smear negative
- MB = >5 lesions, smear positive

---

### Clinical Features

#### Tuberculoid Leprosy (TT) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Skin lesions**:
- **Few** (1-5), well-defined
- **Hypopigmented or erythematous**
- **Dry, scaly, anesthetic** (nerve involvement early)
- **Sharp borders**

**Nerve involvement**:
- **Early, severe**
- **Thick, palpable nerves** (greater auricular, ulnar, common peroneal)
- **Sensory loss** (touch, pain, temperature)
- **Motor loss** (claw hand, foot drop, wrist drop)
- **Nerve abscesses**

**Lepromin test**: **Positive** (good CMI)

**Bacilli**: **Few** (paucibacillary)

**Complications**:
- **Type 1 lepra reaction** (reversal reaction)
- Deformities (nerve damage)

**NEET PG**: Tuberculoid = Few lesions, anesthetic, thick nerves, lepromin+

---

#### Lepromatous Leprosy (LL) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Skin lesions**:
- **Multiple, poorly defined**
- **Nodules, plaques** (leonine facies - lion-like)
- **Symmetric distribution**
- **Late sensory loss** (bacilli, not nerve thickening)

**Nose**:
- **Nasal stuffiness, epistaxis**
- **Saddle nose** (septal perforation)

**Face**:
- **Loss of eyebrows** (madarosis - lateral 1/3 first)
- **Leonine facies** (thickened facial skin, nodules)

**Nerve involvement**:
- **Late, symmetric, gradual**
- **Glove-and-stocking** sensory loss

**Lepromin test**: **Negative** (poor CMI)

**Bacilli**: **Numerous** (multibacillary)
- **Globi** (clumps of bacilli in macrophages)

**Complications**:
- **Type 2 lepra reaction** (ENL - erythema nodosum leprosum)
- Glomerulonephritis, amyloidosis
- Testicular atrophy (sterility)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Lepromatous = Multiple lesions, leonine facies, madarosis, lepromin-
- Symmetric, late nerve involvement

---

### Lepra Reactions (Immunological Phenomena)

**Type 1 Reaction (Reversal Reaction)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Delayed hypersensitivity** (Type IV)
- **Upgrade in immunity** (towards tuberculoid)
- **Borderline patients** (BT, BB, BL)
- **During treatment** or post-treatment

**Features**:
- **Acute inflammation** of skin lesions (erythema, edema)
- **Acute neuritis** (pain, swelling, rapid loss of function)
- **Fever, constitutional symptoms**

**Treatment**:
- **Corticosteroids** (prednisolone)
- Continue anti-leprosy drugs

**Type 2 Reaction (ENL - Erythema Nodosum Leprosum)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Immune complex** (Type III) hypersensitivity
- **Lepromatous** or **Borderline lepromatous** patients
- **During or after treatment**

**Features**:
- **Painful erythematous nodules** (trunk, extremities)
- **Fever, arthritis, neuritis, iritis**
- **Orchitis** (testicular inflammation)

**Treatment**:
- **Thalidomide** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (drug of choice - **NOT in women of childbearing age**)
- **Corticosteroids**
- **Clofazimine** (anti-inflammatory)
- Continue anti-leprosy drugs

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Type 1 = Reversal, borderline, acute neuritis, treat with steroids
- Type 2 = ENL, lepromatous, erythematous nodules, treat with thalidomide

---

### Diagnosis

**1. Clinical** (most common):
- Skin lesions + sensory loss + nerve thickening

**2. Slit-skin smear** ⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Acid-fast bacilli** (AFB)
- **Bacteriological Index (BI)**: Bacterial load (0-6+ scale)
- **Morphological Index (MI)**: % viable (solid-staining) bacilli

**3. Skin biopsy**:
- **TT**: Non-caseating granulomas (epithelioid, Langhans giant cells), few bacilli
- **LL**: Foamy macrophages (Virchow cells, lepra cells), numerous bacilli (globi)

**4. Lepromin test (Mitsuda test)**:
- **NOT diagnostic** (tests CMI, not infection)
- Positive: TT (good CMI)
- Negative: LL (poor CMI)

**NEET PG**: Slit-skin smear for bacilli (BI, MI)

---

### Treatment (WHO-MDT Regimen) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Multidrug Therapy (MDT)** - prevents resistance

**Paucibacillary (PB)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Rifampicin** (600 mg monthly, supervised)
- **Dapsone** (100 mg daily, self-administered)
- **Duration: 6 months**

**Multibacillary (MB)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Rifampicin** (600 mg monthly, supervised)
- **Clofazimine** (300 mg monthly supervised + 50 mg daily self-administered)
- **Dapsone** (100 mg daily, self-administered)
- **Duration: 12 months**

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- PB leprosy = Rifampicin + Dapsone × 6 months
- MB leprosy = Rifampicin + Clofazimine + Dapsone × 12 months

---

### Anti-Leprosy Drugs

**DAPSONE**:
- **Sulfone** (similar to sulfonamides)
- Inhibits folate synthesis
- **Adverse effects**:
  - **Hemolysis** (especially G6PD deficiency)
  - **Methemoglobinemia**
  - Peripheral neuropathy
  - Dapsone syndrome (fever, rash, hepatitis)

**RIFAMPICIN**:
- Most potent (bactericidal)
- See above

**CLOFAZIMINE**:
- **Red-brown skin pigmentation** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (reversible after months-years)
- Anti-inflammatory (useful in ENL)
- GI upset

**NEET PG**: Dapsone = Hemolysis (G6PD), methemoglobinemia; Clofazimine = Red-brown pigmentation

---

### Deformities & Prevention

**Causes**:
- **Nerve damage** (motor, sensory, autonomic)
- **Secondary infections** (anesthetic skin)

**Common deformities**:
- **Claw hand** (ulnar/median nerve)
- **Wrist drop** (radial nerve)
- **Foot drop** (common peroneal nerve)
- **Facial palsy** (facial nerve)
- **Lagophthalmos** (inability to close eye - facial nerve)

**Prevention**:
- **Early detection & treatment**
- **Prevent neuritis** (treat reactions promptly)
- **Self-care** (inspect anesthetic areas, avoid injury)
- **Physiotherapy**

**NEET PG**: Leprosy deformities due to nerve damage (claw hand, foot drop)

---

## OTHER MYCOBACTERIA

### Mycobacterium Avium Complex (MAC)

**Clinical significance**:
- **Opportunistic** (HIV/AIDS with CD4 <50)
- **Disseminated disease** (fever, night sweats, weight loss, hepatosplenomegaly, pancytopenia)
- **Pulmonary infection** (elderly, pre-existing lung disease)

**Treatment**:
- **Clarithromycin or Azithromycin + Ethambutol + Rifabutin**

**Prophylaxis** (HIV, CD4 <50):
- **Azithromycin** (weekly) or **Clarithromycin** (daily)

**NEET PG**: MAC = HIV (CD4 <50), disseminated disease, prophylaxis with azithromycin

---

### M. marinum

- **Swimming pool granuloma**
- **Fish tank granuloma**
- Skin infection (nodules, ulcers on hands/arms)

---

### M. kansasii

- **Pulmonary infection** (similar to TB)
- Yellow colonies in light (photochromogen)

---

### Rapid growers (M. fortuitum, M. chelonae, M. abscessus)

- **Skin/soft tissue infections** (post-surgical, trauma)
- **Resistant** to standard TB drugs
- Treat with **macrolides, fluoroquinolones, amikacin**

## Practice MCQs

1. **Stain for Mycobacterium?**
   Answer: Acid-fast stain (Ziehl-Neelsen)

2. **Gold standard for TB diagnosis?**
   Answer: Sputum culture (Löwenstein-Jensen medium)

3. **Rapid test for TB detecting rifampicin resistance?**
   Answer: GeneXpert MTB/RIF

4. **When to read Mantoux test?**
   Answer: 48-72 hours (measure induration)

5. **First-line anti-TB drugs?**
   Answer: RIPE (Rifampicin, Isoniazid, Pyrazinamide, Ethambutol)

6. **INH side effect & prevention?**
   Answer: Peripheral neuropathy, prevent with pyridoxine (vitamin B6)

7. **Ethambutol toxicity?**
   Answer: Optic neuritis (red-green color blindness)

8. **Treatment duration for drug-sensitive pulmonary TB?**
   Answer: 6 months (2HRZE + 4HR)

9. **MDR-TB definition?**
   Answer: Resistant to rifampicin + isoniazid

10. **Leprosy classification based on lesions?**
    Answer: PB (1-5 lesions), MB (>5 lesions)

11. **Treatment for PB leprosy?**
    Answer: Rifampicin + Dapsone × 6 months

12. **Treatment for Type 2 lepra reaction (ENL)?**
    Answer: Thalidomide (or corticosteroids)

13. **MAC prophylaxis in HIV?**
    Answer: Azithromycin (CD4 <50)

## Memory Aids

### TB First-Line Drugs (RIPE)
- **R**ifampicin (orange body fluids, CYP inducer)
- **I**soniazid (peripheral neuropathy - give B6)
- **P**yrazinamide (hyperuricemia)
- **E**thambutol (optic neuritis - red-green)

### Leprosy Classification (PB vs MB)
**"PB = Poor Bacilli, MB = Many Bacilli"**
- PB = 1-5 lesions, smear negative
- MB = >5 lesions, smear positive

### Leprosy Treatment
**"PB = 2 drugs × 6, MB = 3 drugs × 12"**
- PB = RD × 6 months
- MB = RCD × 12 months

### Lepra Reactions
**"Type 1 Reverses (goes Towards tuberculoid), Type 2 ENLs (Erythema Nodosum Leprosum)"**
