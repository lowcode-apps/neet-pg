# Other Important Viruses

## RABIES VIRUS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Virology**:
- **Rhabdovirus** family (bullet-shaped) ⭐
- **Negative-sense ssRNA**
- **Neurotropic** virus
- **Zoonotic** (transmitted from animals)

**Transmission**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Animal bite** (dogs - 95% in India) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Bats, raccoons, skunks, foxes (worldwide)
- Scratches, licks on mucous membranes
- **NOT person-to-person** (except corneal transplant - rare)

---

### PATHOGENESIS ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Route**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
1. Virus enters at **bite site**
2. Replicates in muscle cells
3. Travels via **retrograde axonal transport** ⭐⭐⭐ (sensory/motor neurons)
4. Reaches **CNS** (spinal cord → brain)
5. **Centrifugal spread** to salivary glands, other organs
6. **Negri bodies** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ in hippocampus neurons (pathognomonic)

**Incubation**: 
- Average: **1-3 months** ⭐
- Range: 5 days - years (depends on bite location, viral load)
- Shorter if bite near head/face (closer to CNS)

---

### CLINICAL FEATURES ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Prodrome** (2-10 days):
- Fever, headache, malaise
- **Paresthesias at bite site** ⭐ (pathognomonic if present)
- Anxiety, agitation

**Acute Neurologic Phase**:

**1. Furious Rabies** (80%): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Hyperactivity, agitation**
- **Hydrophobia** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (fear of water - pharyngeal spasms when attempting to drink)
- **Aerophobia** ⭐⭐⭐ (spasms triggered by breeze/air)
- **Hypersalivation** ("foaming at mouth")
- Autonomic dysfunction (sweating, lacrimation)
- Fluctuating consciousness
- Death in 2-7 days

**2. Paralytic Rabies** (20%): ⭐⭐⭐
- **Ascending flaccid paralysis** (mimics Guillain-Barré)
- Less dramatic symptoms
- Longer survival (7-14 days)

**Coma → Death**: Respiratory failure, cardiac arrhythmias

**Survival**: Almost **100% fatal once symptoms develop** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

### DIAGNOSIS ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Antemortem** (difficult, low sensitivity):
- **RT-PCR**: Saliva, CSF (preferred)
- **Skin biopsy** (nuchal area): Direct fluorescent antibody (DFA) ⭐
- Serology: Serum/CSF antibodies (if not vaccinated)
- Corneal impression smear (rarely done)

**Postmortem** (gold standard):
- **Brain biopsy**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Negri bodies** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (eosinophilic cytoplasmic inclusions in hippocampus, Purkinje cells)
  - Direct fluorescent antibody (DFA) - more sensitive than Negri bodies
- Found in only 50-80% of cases (absence doesn't exclude rabies)

---

### POST-EXPOSURE PROPHYLAXIS (PEP) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Wound Management**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Immediate thorough washing** with soap/water × 15 min ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Single most important step** (reduces risk by 80%)
- Flush with virucidal agent (povidone-iodine, ethanol)
- **Do NOT suture** (except facial wounds if unavoidable)

**Risk Assessment**:
- **Category I** (Low risk): Touching animal, licks on intact skin → **No PEP needed**
- **Category II** (Medium risk): Minor scratches/abrasions, licks on broken skin → **PEP (vaccine only)**
- **Category III** (High risk): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Transdermal bites/scratches**
  - Licks on mucous membranes
  - Bat exposure (any contact)
  - **PEP = Vaccine + RIG** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**PEP Regimen**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**1. Rabies Immunoglobulin (RIG)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Human RIG (HRIG) 20 IU/kg** ⭐ OR
- **Equine RIG (ERIG) 40 IU/kg** ⭐
- **Infiltrate around wound** (as much as anatomically feasible) + remainder IM (distant site from vaccine)
- **Give once** (on day 0, same day as first vaccine dose)
- Provides **immediate passive immunity**
- **DO NOT give if >7 days since first vaccine** (interferes with antibody response)

**2. Rabies Vaccine**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Cell culture vaccine** (PCEC, HDCV, PVRV) ⭐
- **Intramuscular** (deltoid in adults, thigh in children) - NOT gluteal (lower antibody response)

**Updated WHO Regimen (2018)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **4-dose IM schedule**: Day **0, 3, 7, 14-28** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- (Old regimen was 5 doses: 0, 3, 7, 14, 28)

**Previously Vaccinated** (prior complete series):
- **2 doses only**: Day 0, 3 ⭐
- **NO RIG needed**

---

### PRE-EXPOSURE PROPHYLAXIS (PrEP) ⭐⭐⭐

**Indications**:
- High-risk occupations (veterinarians, animal handlers, lab workers)
- Travelers to endemic areas (long-term)
- Children in endemic areas

**Regimen**:
- **3 doses**: Day **0, 7, 21-28** ⭐
- IM or intradermal

**Booster**:
- Check antibody titers every 2 years (high-risk)
- Booster if titer <0.5 IU/mL

---

### TREATMENT ⭐⭐⭐

**No Proven Effective Treatment** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Almost universally fatal once symptoms develop**
- Supportive care (ICU)
- **Milwaukee Protocol** (induced coma + antivirals): Very low success rate, controversial
- **Prevention via PEP is the ONLY effective intervention** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

## ARBOVIRUSES ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Definition**: **AR**thropod-**BO**rne viruses ⭐
- Transmitted by mosquitoes, ticks, sandflies
- Replicate in arthropod vector

---

### DENGUE VIRUS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Virology**:
- **Flavivirus** family ⭐
- **4 serotypes** (DENV-1, 2, 3, 4)
- **ssRNA** virus

**Vector**: **Aedes aegypti** mosquito ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (day-biting, urban)

**Epidemiology**:
- Endemic in tropical/subtropical regions (Asia, Americas, Africa)
- **Most common arboviral disease worldwide** ⭐

---

#### CLINICAL FEATURES ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**1. Dengue Fever (DF)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Classic Triad**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **High fever** (sudden onset, 39-40°C, "saddleback" pattern)
- **Severe headache** (retro-orbital pain) ⭐⭐⭐
- **Rash** (maculopapular, "islands of white in sea of red") ⭐⭐⭐

**Other Features**:
- Myalgias, arthralgias ("**breakbone fever**") ⭐
- Nausea, vomiting
- Leukopenia, thrombocytopenia
- **Positive tourniquet test** ⭐⭐⭐ (petechiae after BP cuff inflation)
- Self-limited (5-7 days)

**2. Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever (DHF)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**WHO Criteria** (all 4 required):
1. **Fever** (2-7 days)
2. **Hemorrhagic manifestations**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
   - Positive tourniquet test
   - Petechiae, ecchymoses, purpura
   - Mucosal bleeding (epistaxis, gum bleeding, GI bleed)
3. **Thrombocytopenia** (<100,000/μL) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
4. **Plasma leakage**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
   - **Hemoconcentration** (Hct ↑ ≥20%) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
   - Pleural effusion, ascites
   - Hypoalbuminemia

**3. Dengue Shock Syndrome (DSS)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- DHF + **circulatory failure** (shock)
- **Narrow pulse pressure** (<20 mmHg) ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Hypotension, cold extremities, altered mental status
- **Critical period**: Day 3-7 of illness ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (defervescence phase)
- **Leading cause of death in children** in endemic areas

**Pathogenesis of DHF/DSS**:
- **Antibody-dependent enhancement (ADE)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - Secondary infection with different serotype
  - Pre-existing antibodies enhance viral entry → massive viremia
  - Cytokine storm → vascular permeability → plasma leakage

---

#### DIAGNOSIS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Early (<5 days)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **NS1 antigen** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (ELISA, rapid test)
  - Detectable day 1-9 of illness
  - High sensitivity/specificity
  - **Test of choice early in illness** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **RT-PCR** (viral RNA): Days 1-5

**Late (>5 days)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Serology** (IgM/IgG):
  - **IgM** ⭐: Detectable from day 5, peaks week 2, persists 2-3 months
  - **IgG**: Appears later, lifelong
  - **4-fold rise in IgG** (acute/convalescent) confirms recent infection

**Lab Findings**:
- **Thrombocytopenia** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (earliest sign, day 3-8)
- Leukopenia → leukocytosis (with atypical lymphocytes)
- **Hemoconcentration** (Hct ↑ ≥20%) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Elevated transaminases (AST > ALT)
- Prolonged PT/PTT, low fibrinogen (severe)

---

#### TREATMENT ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**No Specific Antiviral** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Dengue Fever** (uncomplicated):
- **Supportive care**:
  - **Oral/IV fluids** (maintain hydration) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Paracetamol** for fever ⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **AVOID aspirin/NSAIDs** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (bleeding risk)
- Monitor: CBC, Hct daily

**Dengue Hemorrhagic Fever**:
- **IV fluid resuscitation** (crystalloids) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Close monitoring** (vitals, Hct, platelets q6-12h)
- **Hct monitoring** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (rising Hct = plasma leakage)
- Platelet transfusion if <10,000 or active bleeding
- Fresh frozen plasma if coagulopathy

**Dengue Shock Syndrome**:
- **Aggressive IV fluids** (crystalloids, then colloids if needed) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- ICU care
- Blood products as needed
- Inotropes if refractory shock

**Prevention**:
- **Vaccine**: Dengvaxia (limited use - only if prior dengue infection) ⭐
- **Vector control**: Eliminate standing water, mosquito nets, repellents

---

### CHIKUNGUNYA ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Virology**: **Alphavirus** (Togaviridae) ⭐, ssRNA

**Vector**: **Aedes aegypti, Aedes albopictus** ⭐

**Clinical**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Acute**: Fever + **severe polyarthralgias** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (symmetric, small joints - hands, feet, ankles, wrists)
  - Rash (maculopapular, 2-5 days after fever)
  - Headache, myalgias, conjunctivitis
- **Chronic**: **Persistent arthralgias** (months-years) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - Key distinguishing feature from dengue
- **"Chikungunya" = "that which bends up"** ⭐ (stooped posture from joint pain)

**Diagnosis**:
- **RT-PCR** (early, <7 days)
- **Serology**: IgM (from day 5)

**Treatment**: Supportive (NSAIDs, rest) - **No specific antiviral**

---

### ZIKA VIRUS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Virology**: **Flavivirus** ⭐, ssRNA

**Transmission**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Aedes mosquitoes** (primary)
- **Sexual transmission** ⭐⭐⭐ (unique among arboviruses)
- **Vertical** (mother-to-fetus)
- Blood transfusion

**Clinical**:
- **80% asymptomatic** ⭐
- Mild: Fever, **maculopapular rash**, **conjunctivitis** ⭐⭐⭐, arthralgia
- **Guillain-Barré syndrome** ⭐⭐⭐ (neurologic complication)

**Congenital Zika Syndrome**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Microcephaly** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (severe brain abnormalities)
- Intracranial calcifications
- Eye abnormalities
- Arthrogryposis
- **Devastating in pregnancy** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Diagnosis**:
- RT-PCR (serum, urine - urine positive longer)
- Serology (IgM) - cross-reacts with dengue

**Treatment**: Supportive
- **Pregnant women**: AVOID travel to endemic areas ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

### YELLOW FEVER ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Virology**: **Flavivirus** ⭐, ssRNA

**Vector**: **Aedes aegypti** (urban), **Haemagogus** (jungle) ⭐

**Geography**: Sub-Saharan Africa, South America

**Clinical**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Biphasic illness**:
  1. **Acute phase**: Fever, headache, myalgias, nausea
  2. **Toxic phase** (15%, after brief remission): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
     - **Jaundice** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (hepatitis - eponym "yellow" fever)
     - **Hemorrhagic manifestations** (black vomit - "vomito negro") ⭐⭐⭐
     - **Renal failure**
     - **Faget sign** ⭐⭐⭐ (relative bradycardia despite high fever)
     - High mortality (20-50%)

**Diagnosis**:
- RT-PCR, serology (IgM)
- **Councilman bodies** ⭐⭐⭐ (apoptotic hepatocytes on liver biopsy)

**Treatment**: Supportive (no specific antiviral)

**Prevention**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Live attenuated vaccine (17D strain)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Single dose = lifelong protection** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Required for travel** to endemic areas
- Contraindications: Immunocompromised, infants <6 months, egg allergy

## POLIOVIRUS & ENTEROVIRUSES ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Virology**: **Picornavirus** family, **ssRNA** ⭐

### POLIOVIRUS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Serotypes**: 3 (types 1, 2, 3)
- **Type 1** most common cause of paralysis
- **Types 2 and 3** eradicated (last cases 1999 and 2012)

**Transmission**: **Fecal-oral** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (contaminated water, food)

**Pathogenesis**:
- Replicates in oropharynx, GI tract
- Viremia → invades CNS
- Destroys **anterior horn cells** (motor neurons) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Clinical**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **>95% asymptomatic** ⭐
- **Abortive poliomyelitis** (4-8%): Minor illness (fever, sore throat, GI symptoms)
- **Non-paralytic (aseptic meningitis)** (1-2%): Headache, neck stiffness
- **Paralytic poliomyelitis** (<1%): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Asymmetric flaccid paralysis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (legs > arms)
  - **NO sensory loss** ⭐⭐⭐ (pure motor)
  - Bulbar involvement: Respiratory failure, dysphagia
  - Permanence: Paralysis may be permanent

**Post-Polio Syndrome**: ⭐⭐⭐
- Occurs 15-40 years after acute infection
- Progressive muscle weakness, fatigue, pain

**Diagnosis**:
- **Viral culture** (stool, throat swab) - gold standard
- RT-PCR
- CSF: Lymphocytic pleocytosis, normal glucose, ↑ protein

**Treatment**: **Supportive only** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (no specific antiviral)
- Respiratory support if needed
- Physical therapy

**Prevention**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Vaccines**:
  1. **OPV (Oral Polio Vaccine, Sabin)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
     - **Live attenuated** (trivalent - all 3 types)
     - Provides **mucosal immunity** (IgA) ⭐⭐⭐
     - **Herd immunity** (excreted in stool, immunizes contacts)
     - **Risk**: Vaccine-associated paralytic polio (VAPP) - 1 in 2.4 million doses ⭐⭐⭐
     - **Used in eradication campaigns** (most of world)
  2. **IPV (Inactivated Polio Vaccine, Salk)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
     - **Killed virus** (IM injection)
     - **No VAPP risk** ⭐
     - **Systemic immunity only** (no mucosal IgA)
     - Used in US, developed countries

**India**: Polio-free since 2014 ⭐⭐⭐ (last case 2011)
- Pulse Polio Programme (OPV mass campaigns)

---

### OTHER ENTEROVIRUSES ⭐⭐⭐

**Coxsackievirus A**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Herpangina**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - Fever + **vesicular lesions on soft palate, uvula, tonsils**
  - Young children
  - Self-limited (3-7 days)
- **Hand-foot-and-mouth disease (HFMD)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Vesicles on palms, soles, oral mucosa** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - Caused by **Coxsackie A16**, **Enterovirus 71** ⭐
  - Children <5 years
  - Self-limited

**Coxsackievirus B**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Myocarditis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (dilated cardiomyopathy, heart failure)
- **Pericarditis** (chest pain, friction rub)
- **Pleurodynia** (Bornholm disease): Severe pleuritic chest/abdominal pain
- Neonatal severe disease

**Echovirus**: Aseptic meningitis, exanthems

## MUMPS, MEASLES, RUBELLA (MMR) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

### MUMPS ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Virology**: **Paramyxovirus**, ssRNA ⭐

**Transmission**: Respiratory droplets, saliva

**Clinical**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Parotitis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (bilateral parotid swelling, painful)
  - **"Chipmunk facies"**
  - Obscures angle of mandible
- **Complications**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Orchitis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (post-pubertal males, 20-30%)
    - Usually unilateral
    - Infertility rare
  - **Aseptic meningitis** (10%)
  - **Pancreatitis**, **Oophoritis**, **Deafness** (rare)

**Diagnosis**: Clinical, serology (IgM), RT-PCR

**Treatment**: Supportive

**Prevention**: **MMR vaccine** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (live attenuated)

---

### MEASLES (Rubeola) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Virology**: **Paramyxovirus**, ssRNA ⭐

**Transmission**: **Highly contagious** (airborne, droplets) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **R0 = 12-18** (most contagious human virus)

**Clinical**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Prodrome** (3-4 days): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **3 C's**: **Cough, Coryza, Conjunctivitis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - High fever
  - **Koplik spots** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (white spots on buccal mucosa opposite molars - pathognomonic)
    - Appear 2-3 days before rash
    - Disappear as rash appears
- **Rash** (day 3-4): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Maculopapular, erythematous**
  - **Starts at hairline/face → spreads downward** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (cephalocaudal)
  - Becomes confluent
  - Fades in same order (leaves brownish discoloration, desquamation)

**Complications**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Pneumonia** (most common cause of death) ⭐⭐⭐
- **Otitis media**
- **Diarrhea** (leading cause of death in developing countries)
- **Encephalitis** (1:1000): ⭐⭐⭐
  - Acute (within 2 weeks)
  - High mortality/morbidity
- **Subacute sclerosing panencephalitis (SSPE)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - Rare (1:10,000)
  - **7-10 years after measles** ⭐⭐⭐
  - Progressive neurodegeneration (personality changes, myoclonic jerks, dementia)
  - Uniformly fatal
- **Immunosuppression** (transient, weeks-months) → secondary infections

**Diagnosis**:
- Clinical (Koplik spots pathognomonic)
- Serology (IgM), RT-PCR

**Treatment**: Supportive
- **Vitamin A supplementation** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (reduces mortality/complications in children)
  - WHO recommendation: All children with measles

**Prevention**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **MMR vaccine** (live attenuated) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - 2 doses: 12-15 months, 4-6 years
  - **>95% effective**
- **Post-exposure prophylaxis**: ⭐⭐⭐
  - MMR vaccine within 72 hours OR
  - **Immunoglobulin** within 6 days (immunocompromised, infants)

---

### RUBELLA (German Measles) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Virology**: **Togavirus** (Rubivirus), ssRNA ⭐

**Transmission**: Respiratory droplets

**Clinical** (postnatal):
- **Mild or asymptomatic** (50%) ⭐
- **Prodrome**: Low-grade fever, malaise, lymphadenopathy
- **Rash**: ⭐⭐⭐
  - **Maculopapular, pink**
  - **Starts on face → spreads downward** (like measles but faster - 24h)
  - Milder, shorter duration (3 days - "3-day measles")
- **Lymphadenopathy**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Posterior auricular, suboccipital, posterior cervical** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Pathognomonic feature** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Arthralgia/arthritis** (adults, esp. women)

**Congenital Rubella Syndrome (CRS)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Risk**: Infection in **first trimester** (highest risk) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **<11 weeks**: 90% risk of defects
- **11-12 weeks**: 33%
- **13-14 weeks**: 11%
- **>20 weeks**: Rare

**Classic Triad**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
1. **Cardiac defects** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (PDA, pulmonary stenosis)
2. **Cataracts** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
3. **Deafness** (sensorineural) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Other Features**:
- **"Blueberry muffin" rash** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (purpuric skin lesions - extramedullary hematopoiesis)
- Microcephaly, intellectual disability
- Growth retardation (IUGR)
- Hepatosplenomegaly, thrombocytopenia
- **Chronic rubella infection** (virus shed for months-years)

**Diagnosis**:
- Serology: IgM (acute), IgG (immunity)
- RT-PCR
- **Check rubella immunity in all pregnant women** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Treatment**: Supportive (no specific antiviral)

**Prevention**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **MMR vaccine** (live attenuated) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Vaccinate all women of childbearing age** if non-immune ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Contraindicated in pregnancy** (live vaccine)
- **Avoid pregnancy × 4 weeks after vaccination**

## PARVOVIRUS B19 ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Virology**: **Parvoviridae**, **ssDNA** ⭐ (smallest DNA virus)

**Tropism**: **Erythroid progenitor cells** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Transmission**: Respiratory droplets, vertical, blood products

---

### CLINICAL SYNDROMES ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**1. Erythema Infectiosum (Fifth Disease)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Children** (most common presentation)
- **"Slapped cheek" rash** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (bright red facial erythema)
- **Lacy, reticular rash** on trunk/extremities ⭐⭐⭐
- Mild fever, malaise
- **Non-contagious once rash appears** ⭐⭐⭐
- Self-limited

**2. Arthropathy**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Adults** (esp. women)
- **Symmetric polyarthritis** (hands, wrists, knees)
- Mimics rheumatoid arthritis
- No rash typically

**3. Transient Aplastic Crisis (TAC)**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Patients with chronic hemolytic anemias** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (sickle cell, hereditary spherocytosis, thalassemia)
- **Severe anemia** (Hb drop), **reticulocytopenia** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Fever, pallor, weakness
- Resolves in 7-10 days (self-limited)

**4. Pure Red Cell Aplasia**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Immunocompromised** (HIV, transplant, leukemia)
- **Chronic, persistent anemia** (vs transient in TAC)
- **Reticulocytopenia**
- Requires treatment (IVIG)

**5. Hydrops Fetalis**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Fetal infection** (maternal B19 in pregnancy) ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Virus suppresses fetal erythropoiesis → **severe fetal anemia** → **high-output heart failure** → hydrops
- **Risk highest in 2nd trimester** (13-20 weeks) ⭐⭐⭐
- Fetal death in 10% of maternal infections
- Ultrasound: Ascites, pleural/pericardial effusions, skin edema
- Treatment: **Intrauterine transfusion**

**NOT associated with congenital anomalies** ⭐⭐⭐ (unlike rubella, CMV)

---

### DIAGNOSIS ⭐⭐⭐⭐

- **Serology**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **IgM** (acute infection)
  - **IgG** (past infection/immunity)
- **PCR**: Immunocompromised (persistent viremia)
- **Bone marrow** (TAC/aplasia): Giant pronormoblasts with **viral inclusions** ⭐⭐⭐

---

### TREATMENT ⭐⭐⭐⭐

- **Supportive** (immunocompetent)
- **IVIG** ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (chronic infection in immunocompromised)
- **Transfusion** if severe anemia
- **Intrauterine transfusion** (hydrops fetalis)

## NEET PG MCQS

**Q1.** Dog bite with wound on leg. Most important immediate step?
A) Rabies vaccine B) RIG infiltration C) Wound washing D) Antibiotics

**Answer: C** - Wound washing (soap/water × 15 min) is the single most important step, reduces risk by 80%

**Q2.** Dengue patient, day 5 of illness. Platelet count 80,000. Hematocrit increased from 40% to 50%. Next step?
A) Platelet transfusion B) IV fluid resuscitation C) Discharge home D) NSAIDs

**Answer: B** - Rising Hct (≥20% increase) indicates plasma leakage (DHF) → needs IV fluids. Platelets >10,000 without bleeding don't need transfusion.

**Q3.** Child with fever, bilateral parotid swelling. Most likely complication in post-pubertal male?
A) Infertility B) Orchitis C) Pancreatitis D) Meningitis

**Answer: B** - Mumps orchitis occurs in 20-30% of post-pubertal males (usually unilateral, infertility rare)

**Q4.** Patient with sickle cell disease presents with severe anemia and reticulocyte count 0.1%. Diagnosis?
A) Aplastic anemia B) Parvovirus B19 TAC C) Hemolytic crisis D) Folate deficiency

**Answer: B** - Transient aplastic crisis from Parvovirus B19 in chronic hemolytic anemia → severe anemia + reticulocytopenia

**Q5.** Pregnant woman exposed to child with rubella. She is non-immune. Risk to fetus highest at:
A) <11 weeks B) 15 weeks C) 25 weeks D) 35 weeks

**Answer: A** - CRS risk highest in first trimester, especially <11 weeks (90% risk of defects)
