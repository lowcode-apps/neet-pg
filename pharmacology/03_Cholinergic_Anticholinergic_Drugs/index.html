    CHOLINERGIC & ANTICHOLINERGIC DRUGS - NEET PG Study Material¶

    ACETYLCHOLINE (ACh) - The Neurotransmitter¶

    Synthesis & Breakdown¶

    Choline + Acetyl-CoA --[ChAT]--> Acetylcholine
Acetylcholine --[AChE]--> Choline + Acetate

    ChAT: Choline acetyltransferase (synthesis enzyme) AChE: Acetylcholinesterase (breakdown enzyme)

    CHOLINERGIC RECEPTORS¶

    Classification¶

    1. Muscarinic Receptors (M1-M5) - G-protein coupled receptors - Location: All parasympathetic effector organs - Subtypes: - M1: Gastric parietal cells, CNS (memory) - M2: Heart (↓ HR, ↓ conduction) - M3: Smooth muscle, glands (contraction, secretion) - M4, M5: CNS

    2. Nicotinic Receptors (NN, NM) - Ion channel receptors - NN (Neural): Autonomic ganglia, adrenal medulla - NM (Muscle): Neuromuscular junction (skeletal muscle)

    NEET PG: M2 on heart, M3 on smooth muscle & glands

    CHOLINERGIC AGONISTS (Parasympathomimetics)¶

    Classification¶

    A. Direct-acting (receptor agonists): 1. Choline esters: Acetylcholine, Bethanechol, Carbachol, Methacholine 2. Alkaloids: Pilocarpine, Muscarine

    B. Indirect-acting (AChE inhibitors): 1. Reversible: Neostigmine, Physostigmine, Pyridostigmine, Edrophonium, Rivastigmine, Donepezil 2. Irreversible: Organophosphates (Malathion, Parathion), Echothiophate

    Direct-Acting Cholinergic Agonists¶

    BETHANECHOL¶

    Properties: - Selective M3 agonist - Not hydrolyzed by AChE (resistant) - Mainly affects GI & bladder

    Actions: - ↑ GI motility - ↑ Bladder contraction - ↑ Salivation

    Uses: - Postoperative urinary retention - Postoperative paralytic ileus - Neurogenic bladder

    NEET PG High-Yield: Bethanechol = Urinary retention, paralytic ileus

    CARBACHOL¶

    Properties: - Both muscarinic + nicotinic agonist - Not hydrolyzed by AChE

    Uses: - Glaucoma (topical - ↓ IOP)

    PILOCARPINE¶

    Properties: - Tertiary amine (crosses BBB) - Muscarinic agonist

    Actions: - Miosis (pupil constriction) - Ciliary muscle contraction (↓ IOP) - ↑ Salivation, sweating

    Uses: - Glaucoma (open-angle & emergency angle-closure) - Xerostomia (dry mouth - Sjögren's syndrome)

    Adverse effects: - Sweating, salivation - Diarrhea

    NEET PG: Pilocarpine = Glaucoma, causes miosis

    Indirect-Acting (Anticholinesterases)¶

    Reversible AChE Inhibitors¶

    NEOSTIGMINE

    Properties: - Quaternary compound (doesn't cross BBB) - Both muscarinic + nicotinic effects - Duration: 2-4 hours

    Actions: - ↑ ACh at NMJ (muscle strength) - ↑ ACh at parasympathetic sites

    Uses: 1. Myasthenia gravis (diagnosis & treatment) 2. Reversal of non-depolarizing NM blockade 3. Postoperative urinary retention/ileus

    Adverse effects: - Muscarinic effects: SLUDGE - **S**alivation - **L**acrimation - **U**rination - **D**efecation - **G**I upset - **E**mesis (vomiting) - Bradycardia - Bronchospasm

    Antidote for overdose: Atropine (muscarinic antagonist)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Neostigmine = Myasthenia gravis, reversal of NM block - Overdose antidote = Atropine

    PHYSOSTIGMINE

    Properties: - Tertiary amine (crosses BBB) - Duration: 2-4 hours

    Uses: - Glaucoma (now rarely used) - Atropine poisoning (crosses BBB to reverse CNS effects)

    NEET PG: Physostigmine crosses BBB (vs neostigmine)

    PYRIDOSTIGMINE

    Properties: - Longer duration than neostigmine (4-6 hours) - Less muscarinic side effects

    Uses: - Myasthenia gravis (chronic treatment - preferred over neostigmine)

    EDROPHONIUM

    Properties: - Very short duration (5-10 minutes) - Rapid onset

    Uses: - Tensilon test (diagnosis of myasthenia gravis) - Differentiate myasthenic crisis vs cholinergic crisis

    Tensilon Test: - Give edrophonium IV - Myasthenic crisis: Symptoms improve (need more AChE inhibitor) - Cholinergic crisis: Symptoms worsen (too much AChE inhibitor)

    NEET PG: Edrophonium = Tensilon test for MG diagnosis

    DONEPEZIL, RIVASTIGMINE, GALANTAMINE

    Uses: - Alzheimer's disease (mild to moderate) - ↑ ACh in brain (improves cognition)

    NEET PG: Donepezil = Alzheimer's disease

    Irreversible AChE Inhibitors¶

    ORGANOPHOSPHATES (Insecticides)

    Examples: Malathion, Parathion, Sarin (nerve gas)

    Mechanism: - Irreversible binding to AChE - Phosphorylation of enzyme - "Aging" occurs (cannot be reversed after 24-48 hours)

    Toxicity (Poisoning):

    Acute symptoms: - Muscarinic: SLUDGE + miosis, bradycardia, bronchospasm - Nicotinic: Muscle fasciculations → paralysis - CNS: Confusion, seizures, coma, respiratory failure

    NEET PG High-Yield: Organophosphate poisoning presentation

    Treatment: 1. Atropine (high dose, repeat) - Blocks muscarinic effects - Give until atropinization (dry mouth, dilated pupils, ↑ HR) 2. Pralidoxime (2-PAM) - Reactivates AChE (if given BEFORE aging) - Reverses nicotinic effects - Must give within 24-48 hours 3. Supportive: Ventilation, decontamination

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Organophosphate poisoning = Atropine + Pralidoxime (2-PAM) - Pralidoxime reactivates AChE (must give early)

    ANTICHOLINERGIC DRUGS (Parasympatholytics)¶

    Classification¶

    A. Muscarinic Antagonists: 1. Natural alkaloids: Atropine, Scopolamine (Hyoscine) 2. Synthetic: - GI: Dicyclomine, Propantheline, Pirenzepine - Respiratory: Ipratropium, Tiotropium - Urinary: Oxybutynin, Tolterodine - Parkinson's: Benztropine, Trihexyphenidyl

    B. Nicotinic Antagonists: 1. Ganglionic blockers: Hexamethonium, Trimethaphan 2. Neuromuscular blockers: Covered separately

    ATROPINE - Prototype¶

    Source: Atropa belladonna (Deadly nightshade)

    Mechanism: Competitive antagonist at muscarinic receptors

    Pharmacological Actions¶

    1. Eye: - Mydriasis (pupil dilation) - Cycloplegia (paralysis of accommodation) - ↑ Intraocular pressure (contraindicated in glaucoma)

    2. Cardiovascular: - Tachycardia (blocks M2 on heart) - ↑ AV conduction - May cause initial bradycardia (central effect)

    3. Respiratory: - Bronchodilation - ↓ Secretions (dry airways)

    4. GI: - ↓ Motility (antispasmodic) - ↓ Secretions - Relaxes smooth muscle

    5. Urinary: - Urinary retention (relaxes detrusor, contracts sphincter)

    6. Glands: - ↓ Salivation (dry mouth) - ↓ Sweating (hot, dry skin) - ↓ Lacrimation

    7. CNS (high doses): - Restlessness, confusion - Hallucinations, delirium

    NEET PG: Remember effects by blocking parasympathetic

    Clinical Uses¶

    1. Ophthalmic: - Mydriatic (fundoscopy) - Cycloplegic refraction - Uveitis (prevent synechiae)

    2. Cardiac: - Sinus bradycardia - AV block - Asystole (ACLS protocol)

    3. Anesthesia: - Premedication (↓ salivary & bronchial secretions) - Prevent vagal reflexes during surgery

    4. Organophosphate poisoning (antidote)

    5. GI: - Peptic ulcer (now rarely used) - IBS (antispasmodic)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Atropine = Bradycardia, organophosphate poisoning - Premedication in anesthesia

    Adverse Effects¶

    "Hot as a hare, Blind as a bat, Dry as a bone, Red as a beet, Mad as a hatter"

    • Hot: Hyperthermia (↓ sweating)
    • Blind: Mydriasis, cycloplegia
    • Dry: Dry mouth, skin (↓ secretions)
    • Red: Flushing (vasodilation)
    • Mad: CNS effects (confusion, hallucinations)

    Contraindications: - Glaucoma (↑ IOP) - Prostatic hypertrophy (urinary retention) - Paralytic ileus

    NEET PG: Learn the mnemonic for atropine toxicity

    Atropine Poisoning¶

    Symptoms: Same as adverse effects (above mnemonic)

    Treatment: - Physostigmine (crosses BBB, reverses CNS + peripheral effects) - Supportive care, cooling

    NEET PG: Physostigmine for atropine poisoning (central + peripheral)

    SCOPOLAMINE (Hyoscine)¶

    Differences from Atropine: - Stronger CNS effects (sedation, amnesia) - Less tachycardia - Antiemetic effect

    Uses: - Motion sickness (transdermal patch) - Premedication (sedation + amnesia)

    NEET PG: Scopolamine = Motion sickness

    Selective Muscarinic Antagonists¶

    IPRATROPIUM, TIOTROPIUM

    Properties: - M3 antagonist - Quaternary compounds (poor systemic absorption) - Inhaled bronchodilators

    Uses: - COPD (first-line) - Asthma (add-on therapy)

    Advantages: - Minimal systemic effects - No cardiac effects

    NEET PG: Ipratropium/Tiotropium = COPD

    PIRENZEPINE

    Properties: - Selective M1 antagonist - ↓ Gastric acid secretion

    Uses: - Peptic ulcer (now replaced by PPIs)

    OXYBUTYNIN, TOLTERODINE

    Properties: - M3 antagonist - Relax bladder smooth muscle

    Uses: - Overactive bladder (urge incontinence) - Nocturnal enuresis

    Adverse effects: - Dry mouth, constipation - Confusion (elderly)

    NEET PG: Oxybutynin = Overactive bladder

    BENZTROPINE, TRIHEXYPHENIDYL

    Uses: - Parkinson's disease (tremor, rigidity) - Drug-induced parkinsonism (antipsychotics)

    Mechanism: ↓ Cholinergic activity (restore dopamine-ACh balance)

    NEET PG: Anticholinergics for Parkinson's tremor

    NICOTINIC ANTAGONISTS¶

    Ganglionic Blockers¶

    Examples: Hexamethonium, Trimethaphan

    Mechanism: Block NN receptors at autonomic ganglia

    Effects: - ↓ BP (vasodilation) - Inhibit both sympathetic & parasympathetic

    Uses: - Hypertensive emergency (now obsolete) - Controlled hypotension (surgery)

    NEET PG: Hexamethonium = Ganglionic blocker (historical)

    High-Yield Comparisons¶

    Drug Receptor Use Key Point
    Bethanechol M3 agonist Urinary retention, ileus Resistant to AChE
    Pilocarpine M agonist Glaucoma Causes miosis
    Neostigmine AChE inhibitor Myasthenia gravis, NM reversal Quaternary (doesn't cross BBB)
    Physostigmine AChE inhibitor Atropine poisoning Tertiary (crosses BBB)
    Edrophonium AChE inhibitor Tensilon test Very short acting
    Atropine M antagonist Bradycardia, organophosphate poisoning "Hot, blind, dry, red, mad"
    Scopolamine M antagonist Motion sickness Stronger CNS effects
    Ipratropium M3 antagonist COPD Inhaled, minimal systemic

    Practice MCQs¶

    1. Drug of choice for postoperative urinary retention? Answer: Bethanechol

    2. Antidote for atropine poisoning? Answer: Physostigmine (crosses BBB)

    3. Antidote for organophosphate poisoning? Answer: Atropine + Pralidoxime (2-PAM)

    4. Tensilon test uses which drug? Answer: Edrophonium

    5. Drug for motion sickness? Answer: Scopolamine

    6. First-line inhaled bronchodilator for COPD? Answer: Tiotropium (or Ipratropium)

    7. Pralidoxime works by? Answer: Reactivating acetylcholinesterase

    8. Contraindication for atropine? Answer: Glaucoma (↑ IOP)
