    CARDIOVASCULAR DRUGS - NEET PG Study Material¶

    ANTIHYPERTENSIVE DRUGS¶

    Classification¶

    1. Diuretics
    2. Sympathoplegics (β-blockers, α-blockers, Central acting)
    3. Vasodilators (ACE-I, ARBs, CCBs, Direct vasodilators)
    4. Others

    DIURETICS¶

    Thiazide Diuretics¶

    Prototype: Hydrochlorothiazide, Chlorthalidone, Indapamide

    Site of action: Distal convoluted tubule (DCT) Mechanism: Block Na-Cl cotransporter (NCC)

    Effects: - Moderate diuresis - ↓ BP (first-line for hypertension) - ↑ Ca2+ reabsorption (prevent kidney stones)

    Uses: - Hypertension (first-line, especially elderly) - Edema (CHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome) - Nephrogenic diabetes insipidus (paradoxical effect) - Hypercalciuria (recurrent kidney stones)

    Adverse effects: - Hypokalemia (most common) - Hyponatremia - Hypercalcemia - Hyperglycemia (worsen diabetes) - Hyperuricemia (precipitate gout) - Hyperlipidemia - Hypochloremic alkalosis - Erectile dysfunction

    Contraindications: Gout, hypokalemia

    NEET PG High-Yield: - First-line for hypertension (JNC 8) - Hypercalcemia (opposite of loop diuretics) - Used in nephrogenic DI (paradoxical) - 4 Hypers: Hyperglycemia, Hyperlipidemia, Hyperuricemia, Hypercalcemia

    Loop Diuretics¶

    Drugs: Furosemide, Bumetanide, Torsemide, Ethacrynic acid

    Site: Thick ascending limb of loop of Henle (TAL) Mechanism: Block Na-K-2Cl cotransporter (NKCC2)

    Effects: - Most efficacious diuretics ("high ceiling") - ↑ Ca2+ excretion (hypocalcemia) - ↓ Renal concentrating ability

    Uses: - Acute pulmonary edema (IV furosemide - DOC) - Acute renal failure (maintain urine output) - Hypercalcemia (↑ Ca2+ excretion) - CHF, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome - Cerebral edema

    Adverse effects: - Hypokalemia (arrhythmias) - Hypocalcemia - Hypomagnesemia - Metabolic alkalosis - Ototoxicity (especially with aminoglycosides) - Hyperuricemia (gout)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - DOC for acute pulmonary edema - Most efficacious diuretics - Hypocalcemia (opposite of thiazides) - Ototoxicity (ethacrynic acid > furosemide) - Used in hypercalcemia

    Potassium-Sparing Diuretics¶

    A. Aldosterone Antagonists¶

    Drugs: Spironolactone, Eplerenone

    Site: Collecting duct Mechanism: Block aldosterone receptors

    Effects: - Mild diuresis - ↓ K+ excretion (hyperkalemia risk) - Anti-androgenic (spironolactone)

    Uses: - Primary hyperaldosteronism (Conn's syndrome) - CHF (mortality benefit - RALES trial) - Cirrhosis with ascites (DOC) - Hirsutism, acne (spironolactone - anti-androgen)

    Adverse effects: - Hyperkalemia (most serious) - Gynecomastia (spironolactone - anti-androgen) - Metabolic acidosis

    NEET PG: - Spironolactone: Mortality benefit in CHF - DOC for ascites in cirrhosis - Gynecomastia (spironolactone, NOT eplerenone)

    B. ENaC Blockers¶

    Drugs: Amiloride, Triamterene

    Mechanism: Block epithelial Na+ channels (ENaC)

    Uses: - With thiazides (prevent hypokalemia) - Lithium-induced nephrogenic DI

    NEET PG: Amiloride blocks ENaC (direct effect, not via aldosterone)

    Osmotic Diuretics¶

    Drug: Mannitol

    Mechanism: Osmotic effect (not reabsorbed)

    Uses: - Cerebral edema (↓ ICP) - Acute glaucoma (↓ intraocular pressure) - Prevent acute renal failure (during surgery, rhabdomyolysis)

    Adverse effects: - Pulmonary edema (initial ↑ plasma volume) - Hyponatremia (dilutional)

    Contraindications: Anuria, pulmonary edema, CHF

    NEET PG: - DOC for cerebral edema & acute glaucoma - Contraindicated in anuria (will worsen)

    SYMPATHOPLEGICS¶

    Beta-Blockers¶

    Classification: 1. Non-selective (β1 + β2): Propranolol, nadolol, timolol 2. Selective β1: Atenolol, Metoprolol, bisoprolol, esmolol 3. α + β blockers: Labetalol, carvedilol

    Mnemonic for selective β1: "**A**ccepted **M**etoprolol **B**isoprolol **E**smolol"

    Mechanism: Block β-adrenergic receptors - ↓ Heart rate (β1) - ↓ Contractility (β1) - ↓ Renin release (β1) - ↓ AV conduction (β1)

    Uses: - Hypertension (especially with CAD, post-MI) - Angina (↓ myocardial O2 demand) - Post-MI (↓ mortality) - CHF (bisoprolol, metoprolol, carvedilol - mortality benefit) - Arrhythmias (SVT, AF, ventricular arrhythmias) - Hyperthyroidism (symptomatic relief) - Migraine prophylaxis (propranolol) - Essential tremor (propranolol) - Glaucoma (timolol eye drops) - Variceal bleeding (propranolol - ↓ portal pressure)

    Adverse effects: - Bronchospasm (β2 blockade - avoid in asthma) - Bradycardia, AV block - Mask hypoglycemia (in diabetics) - Worsen peripheral vascular disease - Fatigue, depression - Erectile dysfunction - Rebound hypertension (abrupt withdrawal)

    Contraindications: - Asthma, COPD (non-selective) - Heart block (2nd/3rd degree) - Cardiogenic shock - Severe bradycardia

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Mortality benefit: Post-MI & CHF - Contraindicated in asthma (bronchospasm) - Selective β1: Atenolol, Metoprolol (safer in COPD) - Labetalol: α + β blocker (DOC in pregnancy) - Esmolol: Ultra-short acting (IV, for arrhythmias)

    ACE Inhibitors¶

    Drugs: Enalapril, Lisinopril, Ramipril, Captopril, Perindopril

    Mechanism: - Inhibit ACE (Angiotensin-Converting Enzyme) - Block: Angiotensin I → Angiotensin II - ↓ Degradation of bradykinin (accumulates)

    Effects: - ↓ BP (vasodilation) - ↓ Aldosterone (↓ Na+ retention, ↑ K+) - ↓ Cardiac remodeling - Renoprotective (↓ intraglomerular pressure)

    Uses: - Hypertension (first-line, especially with diabetes/CKD) - CHF (mortality benefit - reduce afterload) - Post-MI (prevent remodeling) - Diabetic nephropathy (slow progression) - Proteinuria (↓ intraglomerular pressure)

    Adverse effects: - Dry cough (10-20%) - Due to ↑ bradykinin (most common side effect) - Hyperkalemia (↓ aldosterone) - Angioedema (rare but serious - due to ↑ bradykinin) - Acute renal failure in bilateral renal artery stenosis - Teratogenic (fetal renal dysgenesis - contraindicated in pregnancy) - First-dose hypotension - Taste alteration (captopril)

    Contraindications: - Pregnancy (Category D) - Bilateral renal artery stenosis - Hyperkalemia - Angioedema (history)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Dry cough: Most common side effect (switch to ARB) - Teratogenic (avoid in pregnancy) - Renoprotective (slow diabetic nephropathy) - Contraindicated in bilateral RAS (GFR depends on efferent constriction) - Mortality benefit: CHF & post-MI

    Angiotensin Receptor Blockers (ARBs)¶

    Drugs: Losartan, Telmisartan, Valsartan, Irbesartan, Candesartan

    Mechanism: Block AT1 receptors (Angiotensin II receptors)

    Advantages over ACE-I: - No cough (do not affect bradykinin) - No angioedema (rare) - Better tolerated

    Uses: Same as ACE inhibitors - Hypertension (alternative to ACE-I) - CHF - Diabetic nephropathy - Post-MI

    Adverse effects: Similar to ACE-I (but no cough/angioedema) - Hyperkalemia - Teratogenic (contraindicated in pregnancy) - Hypotension

    NEET PG: - Alternative to ACE-I (if cough develops) - No cough or angioedema (doesn't affect bradykinin) - Losartan: Also ↓ uric acid (good for gout with HTN)

    Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs)¶

    Classification¶

    1. Dihydropyridines (vascular-selective): - Amlodipine, Nifedipine, Felodipine, Nicardipine, Nimodipine

    2. Non-dihydropyridines (cardiac-selective): - Verapamil, Diltiazem

    Mechanism: Block L-type Ca2+ channels - Dihydropyridines: Peripheral vasodilation (↓ afterload) - Verapamil/Diltiazem: ↓ Heart rate, ↓ contractility, ↓ AV conduction

    Dihydropyridines (Amlodipine, Nifedipine)¶

    Uses: - Hypertension (especially elderly, isolated systolic HTN) - Variant (Prinzmetal's) angina (coronary vasospasm) - Raynaud's phenomenon

    Adverse effects: - Reflex tachycardia (nifedipine > amlodipine) - Peripheral edema (ankle swelling) - Flushing, headache - Gingival hyperplasia

    NEET PG: - DOC for variant angina (Prinzmetal's) - Reflex tachycardia (peripheral vasodilation) - Safe in asthma (unlike β-blockers)

    Non-Dihydropyridines (Verapamil, Diltiazem)¶

    Uses: - Hypertension - Angina (stable, unstable) - Supraventricular arrhythmias (SVT, AF rate control) - Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (verapamil)

    Adverse effects: - Constipation (verapamil - most common) - Bradycardia, AV block - Worsening of heart failure (negative inotrope)

    Contraindications: - Heart block (2nd/3rd degree) - Do NOT combine with β-blockers (↑ risk of bradycardia, heart block)

    NEET PG: - Verapamil: Constipation (most common side effect) - DOC for SVT (verapamil/diltiazem) - Contraindicated with β-blockers (additive cardiac depression)

    ANTIANGINAL DRUGS¶

    Nitrates¶

    Drugs: Nitroglycerin (sublingual, IV, transdermal), Isosorbide dinitrate, Isosorbide mononitrate

    Mechanism: - Release nitric oxide (NO) - ↑ cGMP → Smooth muscle relaxation - Venodilation > Arteriodilation

    Effects: - ↓ Preload (venodilation) - ↓ myocardial O2 demand - ↓ Afterload (mild arteriodilation) - Coronary vasodilation

    Uses: - Acute angina (sublingual nitroglycerin - DOC) - Prophylaxis of angina (long-acting nitrates) - Acute MI (IV nitroglycerin) - Acute pulmonary edema (↓ preload) - Esophageal spasm

    Adverse effects: - Headache (most common - cerebral vasodilation) - Postural hypotension (reflex tachycardia) - Tolerance (with continuous use - give nitrate-free interval) - Methemoglobinemia (high doses)

    Contraindications: - Sildenafil (Viagra) - Severe hypotension (both ↑ cGMP) - Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy - Severe aortic stenosis - Right ventricular infarction (preload-dependent)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - DOC for acute angina (sublingual nitroglycerin) - Headache: Most common side effect - Tolerance: Give nitrate-free interval (10-12 hrs) - Contraindicated with sildenafil (PDE-5 inhibitor)

    HEART FAILURE DRUGS¶

    Drugs that ↓ Mortality in CHF¶

    1. ACE inhibitors (or ARBs)
    2. β-blockers (bisoprolol, carvedilol, metoprolol)
    3. Aldosterone antagonists (spironolactone, eplerenone)
    4. ARNI (Angiotensin receptor-neprilysin inhibitor - sacubitril/valsartan)
    5. SGLT2 inhibitors (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin)

    NEET PG: These 5 drug classes ↓ mortality in CHF

    Cardiac Glycosides (Digoxin)¶

    Mechanism: - Inhibits Na+/K+-ATPase pump - ↑ Intracellular Na+ → ↑ Ca2+ (via Na+/Ca2+ exchanger) - Positive inotrope (↑ contractility) - Negative chronotrope (↓ heart rate via ↑ vagal tone) - ↓ AV conduction

    Uses: - CHF with AF (rate control + inotropy) - Systolic HF (improve symptoms, no mortality benefit)

    Adverse effects: - Arrhythmias (most serious): VPCs, VT, AV block - GI: Nausea, vomiting, anorexia - CNS: Confusion, visual disturbances (yellow-green vision - xanthopsia) - Gynecomastia

    Toxicity: - Predisposing factors: Hypokalemia, hypercalcemia, hypomagnesemia, renal failure - ECG: Scooping of ST segment, T wave inversion - Treatment: Stop digoxin, Digoxin-specific Fab antibodies, correct electrolytes

    Contraindications: WPW syndrome, AV block

    NEET PG High-Yield: - No mortality benefit in CHF (improves symptoms only) - Yellow-green vision (xanthopsia) - classic toxicity - Hypokalemia worsens toxicity (competes for Na+/K+-ATPase) - Treatment of toxicity: Digoxin-specific Fab antibodies

    ANTIARRHYTHMIC DRUGS - Vaughan Williams Classification¶

    Class I: Sodium Channel Blockers¶

    IA (Moderate blockade, ↑ APD)¶

    Drugs: Quinidine, Procainamide, Disopyramide

    Uses: Atrial & ventricular arrhythmias Adverse effects: - Quinidine: Cinchonism, torsades de pointes, diarrhea - Procainamide: Drug-induced lupus, agranulocytosis

    IB (Weak blockade, ↓ APD)¶

    Drugs: Lidocaine, Mexiletine

    Uses: Ventricular arrhythmias (post-MI) Route: IV only (high first-pass metabolism) Adverse effects: CNS (seizures), cardiac depression

    NEET PG: Lidocaine for ventricular arrhythmias (IV)

    IC (Strong blockade, no change in APD)¶

    Drugs: Flecainide, Propafenone

    Uses: Supraventricular arrhythmias, AF Contraindications: Structural heart disease, post-MI (↑ mortality)

    Class II: Beta-Blockers¶

    Drugs: Propranolol, atenolol, metoprolol, esmolol

    Uses: SVT, AF (rate control), ventricular arrhythmias

    NEET PG: Esmolol - ultra-short acting (t½ = 9 min)

    Class III: Potassium Channel Blockers (↑ APD)¶

    Drugs: Amiodarone, Sotalol, Dofetilide, Ibutilide

    Amiodarone: - Most effective antiarrhythmic - Uses: All arrhythmias (atrial & ventricular), refractory VF/VT - Adverse effects: - Pulmonary fibrosis (most serious) - Thyroid dysfunction (hypo/hyperthyroidism - contains iodine) - Hepatotoxicity - Corneal deposits (reversible) - Photosensitivity (blue-gray skin) - Bradycardia

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Most effective antiarrhythmic - Pulmonary fibrosis - most serious side effect - Contains iodine (thyroid dysfunction)

    Class IV: Calcium Channel Blockers¶

    Drugs: Verapamil, Diltiazem

    Uses: SVT, AF (rate control)

    Others (Not in Vaughan Williams)¶

    Adenosine: - DOC for acute SVT (AVNRT, AVRT) - Ultra-short t½ (< 10 seconds) - Mechanism: Activates K+ channels, ↑ AV block - Adverse effects: Flushing, dyspnea, chest pain (transient) - Contraindicated: Asthma (bronchospasm), WPW with AF

    NEET PG: Adenosine - DOC for acute SVT (ultra-short acting)

    Practice MCQs¶

    1. First-line antihypertensive in elderly? Answer: Thiazide diuretic

    2. DOC for acute pulmonary edema? Answer: IV Furosemide

    3. Most common side effect of ACE inhibitors? Answer: Dry cough

    4. Antihypertensive contraindicated in pregnancy? Answer: ACE inhibitors, ARBs

    5. DOC for acute angina? Answer: Sublingual nitroglycerin

    6. Drugs with mortality benefit in CHF? Answer: ACE-I, β-blockers, Spironolactone, ARNI, SGLT2-I

    7. Most effective antiarrhythmic? Answer: Amiodarone

    8. DOC for acute SVT? Answer: Adenosine

    Revision Checklist¶

    • Know diuretic sites of action
    • Understand ACE-I vs ARB differences
    • Learn β-blocker contraindications
    • Know CCB classification
    • Memorize drugs with CHF mortality benefit
    • Understand digoxin toxicity
    • Learn Vaughan Williams classification
    • Know antiarrhythmic drug uses
