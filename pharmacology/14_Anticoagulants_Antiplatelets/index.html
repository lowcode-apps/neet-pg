    ANTICOAGULANTS & ANTIPLATELETS - NEET PG Study Material¶

    HEMOSTASIS - Brief Review¶

    Steps of Hemostasis¶

    1. Vascular spasm

    2. Platelet plug formation: - Adhesion (vWF, collagen) - Activation - Aggregation (fibrinogen, GPIIb/IIIa)

    3. Coagulation cascade: - Extrinsic pathway (Tissue Factor, VII) - Intrinsic pathway (XII, XI, IX, VIII) - Common pathway (X, V, II → Fibrin)

    4. Fibrinolysis (Plasmin)

    NEET PG: Hemostasis = Platelet plug + Coagulation cascade + Fibrinolysis

    CLASSIFICATION¶

    ANTICOAGULANTS (Act on Coagulation Cascade)¶

    1. Parenteral: - Heparin (Unfractionated heparin - UFH) - Low molecular weight heparins (LMWH): Enoxaparin, Dalteparin - Fondaparinux (Factor Xa inhibitor) - Direct thrombin inhibitors: Bivalirudin, Argatroban, Dabigatran (oral)

    2. Oral: - Vitamin K antagonists: Warfarin - Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs): - Direct thrombin inhibitor: Dabigatran - Direct Factor Xa inhibitors: Rivaroxaban, Apixaban, Edoxaban

    ANTIPLATELET DRUGS¶

    1. COX inhibitor: Aspirin

    2. ADP receptor (P2Y12) antagonists: - Clopidogrel, Prasugrel, Ticagrelor

    3. Phosphodiesterase inhibitors: - Dipyridamole, Cilostazol

    4. GPIIb/IIIa inhibitors: - Abciximab, Tirofiban, Eptifibatide

    THROMBOLYTICS (Fibrinolytics)¶

    • Streptokinase, Alteplase (tPA), Tenecteplase

    HEPARIN (Unfractionated Heparin - UFH)¶

    Mechanism¶

    Activates antithrombin III (AT-III): - AT-III inhibits Factor IIa (thrombin) & Factor Xa - Catalyzes the process (1000x faster)

    Effect: Inhibits coagulation (prevents clot formation/extension) - Does NOT dissolve existing clots

    NEET PG: Heparin activates AT-III → inhibits IIa (thrombin) & Xa

    Pharmacokinetics¶

    Route: IV or SC (NOT oral - large molecule, not absorbed)

    Onset: Immediate (IV)

    Monitoring: aPTT (activated partial thromboplastin time) - Target: 1.5-2.5× control (60-80 seconds)

    Half-life: Short (1-2 hours)

    Reversal: Protamine sulfate (binds heparin)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Heparin monitored by aPTT (target 1.5-2.5× control) - Antidote = Protamine sulfate

    Clinical Uses¶

    1. Acute thrombosis ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) - Pulmonary embolism (PE) - Initial treatment (bridge to warfarin)

    2. Acute coronary syndrome ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Unstable angina, NSTEMI - STEMI (with thrombolysis or PCI)

    3. Atrial fibrillation: - Acute (before cardioversion)

    4. Cardiopulmonary bypass, dialysis: - Prevent clotting in extracorporeal circuits

    5. Pregnancy ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Does NOT cross placenta (large molecule) - Anticoagulant of choice in pregnancy - For DVT/PE, prosthetic valves

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Heparin = Acute DVT/PE, ACS - Anticoagulant of choice in pregnancy (doesn't cross placenta)

    Adverse Effects¶

    1. Bleeding ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Most common serious effect - Antidote: Protamine sulfate (1 mg neutralizes 100 units heparin)

    2. Heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Type I: Early (1-2 days), mild, transient, benign - Type II ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Immune-mediated (antibodies against heparin-PF4 complex) - 5-10 days after starting heparin - Thrombocytopenia (platelet <150,000 or >50% drop) - Paradoxical THROMBOSIS (venous > arterial) - Life-threatening - Management: - Stop heparin immediately - Alternative anticoagulant: Argatroban, bivalirudin, fondaparinux - NOT warfarin (↑ thrombosis risk initially) - NOT LMWH (cross-reactivity)

    3. Osteoporosis: - Long-term use (>6 months) - ↓ Bone density

    4. Hyperkalemia: - ↓ Aldosterone secretion

    5. Hypersensitivity: - Urticaria, anaphylaxis

    NEET PG High-Yield: - HIT Type II = Immune, 5-10 days, thrombocytopenia + thrombosis - Stop heparin, use argatroban (NOT warfarin, NOT LMWH)

    Monitoring¶

    aPTT (activated partial thromboplastin time): - Intrinsic + common pathway - Target: 1.5-2.5× control

    Platelet count: - Monitor for HIT (every 2-3 days)

    NEET PG: Monitor heparin with aPTT & platelet count

    LOW MOLECULAR WEIGHT HEPARINS (LMWH)¶

    Examples: Enoxaparin, Dalteparin, Tinzaparin

    Mechanism¶

    Activate AT-III → Inhibit Factor Xa >> IIa (thrombin) - Smaller molecule → Preferentially inhibits Xa

    NEET PG: LMWH preferentially inhibits Factor Xa

    Advantages over UFH¶

    1. Predictable pharmacokinetics: - No monitoring needed (aPTT NOT reliable) - Fixed dosing (weight-based)

    2. Subcutaneous (convenient): - Once or twice daily

    3. Longer half-life (4-6 hours): - Less frequent dosing

    4. Lower risk of HIT (but still possible)

    5. Lower risk of osteoporosis

    6. **Can be used outpatient

    NEET PG: LMWH advantages = No monitoring, SC dosing, less HIT

    Uses¶

    1. DVT/PE treatment (outpatient): - Instead of UFH + warfarin bridge

    2. ACS (NSTEMI, unstable angina)

    3. DVT prophylaxis ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Postoperative (orthopedic, general surgery) - Medical patients (hospitalized)

    4. Pregnancy: - Safe (doesn't cross placenta)

    NEET PG: LMWH = DVT prophylaxis (postoperative), outpatient DVT treatment

    Monitoring¶

    Usually NOT needed

    If needed (renal failure, obesity, pregnancy): - Anti-Xa levels (NOT aPTT) - Target: 0.6-1.0 U/ml (twice daily), 1.0-2.0 U/ml (once daily)

    Reversal¶

    Partial reversal: Protamine sulfate - Less effective than for UFH (~60%)

    Complete reversal: Andexanet alfa (Factor Xa decoy)

    Contraindications¶

    Renal failure (severe): - LMWH excreted by kidneys - ↑ Accumulation → bleeding - Prefer UFH (hepatic clearance)

    NEET PG: LMWH contraindicated in severe renal failure (use UFH)

    FONDAPARINUX¶

    Mechanism: Synthetic selective Factor Xa inhibitor - Via AT-III activation

    Advantages: - Lowest risk of HIT (no cross-reactivity) - Once daily SC - No monitoring

    Uses: - DVT/PE prophylaxis & treatment - HIT (alternative anticoagulant)

    Reversal: Difficult (andexanet alfa)

    NEET PG: Fondaparinux = Lowest HIT risk, use in HIT

    WARFARIN (Vitamin K Antagonist)¶

    Mechanism¶

    Inhibits vitamin K epoxide reductase: - ↓ Reduction of vitamin K - ↓ Synthesis of clotting factors II, VII, IX, X (vitamin K-dependent) - ↓ Protein C & S (anticoagulants)

    Effect: Slow onset (2-7 days) - Existing factors must be depleted - Factor VII shortest half-life (6 hours) - affected first

    Initial effect: Procoagulant (Protein C depleted first) - Risk of skin necrosis (Protein C deficiency) - Bridge with heparin for 5 days

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Warfarin inhibits vitamin K-dependent factors (II, VII, IX, X) - Slow onset (2-7 days), bridge with heparin

    Pharmacokinetics¶

    Route: Oral

    Onset: Slow (2-7 days)

    Monitoring: PT/INR (Prothrombin Time/International Normalized Ratio) - Target INR: 2-3 (most indications), 2.5-3.5 (mechanical valves)

    Half-life: Long (40 hours)

    Metabolism: Hepatic (CYP2C9)

    NEET PG: Warfarin monitored by PT/INR (target 2-3)

    Clinical Uses¶

    1. Chronic anticoagulation ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - DVT/PE (after heparin bridge, continue 3-6 months) - Atrial fibrillation (stroke prevention) - Mechanical heart valves (lifelong) - Cardiomyopathy (mural thrombus)

    NEET PG: Warfarin = Chronic anticoagulation (AF, DVT/PE, mechanical valves)

    Adverse Effects¶

    1. Bleeding ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Most common serious effect - Reversal (depends on severity): - Minor bleeding + INR <5: ↓ Dose, monitor - INR 5-9, no bleeding: Omit dose, ↓ maintenance dose - Major bleeding or INR >9: - Stop warfarin - Vitamin K (phytonadione): - Oral (5-10 mg) - slow (12-24 hours) - IV (slower than PCC, 6-12 hours) - Prothrombin complex concentrate (PCC) or Fresh frozen plasma (FFP): - Immediate reversal (contains factors II, VII, IX, X) - PCC preferred over FFP

    2. Skin necrosis ⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Early (3-8 days) - Protein C deficiency (depleted before procoagulant factors) - Thrombosis of dermal vessels - Risk: Protein C/S deficiency, high loading dose - Prevention: Bridge with heparin, avoid loading dose

    3. Teratogenicity ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Fetal warfarin syndrome - Nasal hypoplasia, stippled epiphyses, CNS abnormalities - Contraindicated in pregnancy (especially 1st trimester) - Crosses placenta

    4. Purple toe syndrome: - Cholesterol microembolization - Rare

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Warfarin major bleeding = Stop drug + Vitamin K (IV/oral) + PCC/FFP - Skin necrosis (Protein C deficiency) - bridge with heparin - Teratogenic - contraindicated in pregnancy

    Drug Interactions (MANY)¶

    ↑ Warfarin effect (↑ INR, ↑ bleeding): - Antibiotics: Metronidazole, co-trimoxazole, macrolides - Antifungals: Fluconazole, ketoconazole - NSAIDs, aspirin (also ↑ bleeding independently) - Omeprazole - Amiodarone - Alcohol (acute intoxication) - Cranberry juice

    ↓ Warfarin effect (↓ INR, ↑ thrombosis): - Rifampin (CYP inducer) - Carbamazepine, phenytoin (CYP inducers) - Vitamin K-rich foods (green leafy vegetables) - Alcohol (chronic use)

    NEET PG: Warfarin many drug interactions (antibiotics ↑ effect, rifampin ↓ effect)

    Contraindications¶

    Absolute: - Pregnancy (teratogenic) - Active bleeding - Severe liver disease - Recent CNS/eye surgery - Hemorrhagic stroke

    Relative: - Falls risk (elderly) - Non-compliance

    DIRECT ORAL ANTICOAGULANTS (DOACs)¶

    Direct thrombin inhibitor: Dabigatran

    Direct Factor Xa inhibitors: Rivaroxaban, Apixaban, Edoxaban

    DABIGATRAN¶

    Mechanism: Direct thrombin (IIa) inhibitor - Reversible, competitive

    Route: Oral (prodrug)

    Monitoring: NOT required (predictable pharmacokinetics)

    Reversal: Idarucizumab (monoclonal antibody - specific antidote)

    NEET PG: Dabigatran = Direct thrombin inhibitor, antidote = idarucizumab

    RIVAROXABAN, APIXABAN, EDOXABAN¶

    Mechanism: Direct Factor Xa inhibitors

    Route: Oral

    Monitoring: NOT required

    Reversal: Andexanet alfa (recombinant Factor Xa decoy) - Also reverses LMWH, fondaparinux

    NEET PG: Xa inhibitors (rivaroxaban, apixaban) antidote = andexanet alfa

    Advantages of DOACs over Warfarin¶

    1. No monitoring (no INR checks)

    2. Rapid onset (hours vs days)

    3. Predictable dose-response

    4. Fewer drug interactions

    5. Fewer food interactions (no dietary restrictions)

    6. Lower risk of intracranial hemorrhage

    7. No bridging with heparin needed

    NEET PG: DOACs advantages = No monitoring, rapid onset, fewer interactions

    Uses¶

    1. Atrial fibrillation (stroke prevention) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Non-valvular AF - As effective as warfarin (some superior)

    2. DVT/PE (treatment & prevention)

    3. VTE prophylaxis (post-orthopedic surgery)

    NOT for: - Mechanical heart valves (warfarin superior, dabigatran harmful) - Severe renal failure (CrCl <30) - excreted renally

    NEET PG High-Yield: - DOACs for non-valvular AF (NOT mechanical valves) - Contraindicated in severe renal failure

    Adverse Effects¶

    1. Bleeding: - Similar to warfarin - Lower intracranial bleeding

    2. GI upset (dabigatran): - Dyspepsia common

    3. **No specific antidote (except dabigatran, rivaroxaban/apixaban): - Supportive care - Hemodialysis (dabigatran)

    Contraindications¶

    • Severe renal failure (CrCl <30)
    • Mechanical heart valves
    • Active bleeding
    • Pregnancy, breastfeeding

    ANTIPLATELET DRUGS¶

    ASPIRIN - Covered in NSAIDs¶

    Mechanism: Irreversible COX-1 inhibitor → ↓ TXA2 → ↓ Platelet aggregation

    Uses: - Acute MI, ACS - Stroke (secondary prevention) - Primary prevention (high CV risk)

    NEET PG: Aspirin = Antiplatelet (irreversible COX inhibitor)

    CLOPIDOGREL, PRASUGREL, TICAGRELOR¶

    Mechanism: P2Y12 receptor antagonists (ADP receptor) - Block ADP-induced platelet aggregation

    Clopidogrel, Prasugrel: Prodrugs (irreversible) Ticagrelor: Active drug (reversible)

    Uses¶

    1. Acute coronary syndrome ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT): Aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor - Minimum 12 months post-ACS/stent

    2. Post-PCI (percutaneous coronary intervention): - Stent thrombosis prevention

    3. Stroke (clopidogrel): - Secondary prevention (alternative to aspirin)

    4. Peripheral arterial disease

    NEET PG High-Yield: - DAPT = Aspirin + Clopidogrel/Ticagrelor (post-ACS, post-stent) - Duration: 12 months minimum

    Adverse Effects¶

    1. Bleeding

    2. Clopidogrel: - TTP (thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura) - rare

    3. Ticagrelor: - Dyspnea (10-15%) - Bradycardia

    4. Drug interaction: - PPIs (omeprazole) ↓ clopidogrel activation (CYP2C19 inhibition) - Use pantoprazole instead

    NEET PG: Omeprazole ↓ clopidogrel effect (CYP interaction)

    DIPYRIDAMOLE¶

    Mechanism: - Phosphodiesterase inhibitor → ↑ cAMP → ↓ platelet aggregation - Coronary vasodilator

    Uses: - Stroke prevention (with aspirin) - Pharmacologic stress test (coronary vasodilation)

    Adverse effect: Headache, hypotension

    NEET PG: Dipyridamole for stroke prevention, stress test

    GPIIb/IIIa INHIBITORS¶

    Examples: Abciximab, Tirofiban, Eptifibatide

    Mechanism: Block GPIIb/IIIa receptor (final common pathway) - Prevent fibrinogen binding - Most potent antiplatelet

    Route: IV only

    Uses: - High-risk ACS (with PCI) - Acute STEMI (with PCI)

    Adverse effect: Bleeding, thrombocytopenia

    NEET PG: GPIIb/IIIa inhibitors = Most potent antiplatelet, IV, for high-risk ACS

    THROMBOLYTICS (Fibrinolytics)¶

    Examples: Streptokinase, Alteplase (tPA), Tenecteplase, Reteplase

    Mechanism¶

    Activate plasminogen → plasmin: - Plasmin degrades fibrin → Dissolves clot

    NEET PG: Thrombolytics activate plasminogen → plasmin → dissolves clot

    Uses¶

    1. Acute STEMI ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Within 12 hours of symptom onset - If PCI not available within 90-120 min

    2. Acute ischemic stroke ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Alteplase (tPA) - Within 4.5 hours - After excluding hemorrhagic stroke (CT scan)

    3. Massive pulmonary embolism: - Hemodynamically unstable

    4. Acute DVT (extensive, limb-threatening)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Thrombolytics for STEMI (<12 hours), stroke (<4.5 hours) - Alteplase (tPA) for stroke

    STREPTOKINASE¶

    Source: Streptococcus (bacterial)

    Properties: - Antigenic (antibodies develop) - Cannot repeat within 6 months - 1 year - Allergic reactions common

    Cheap, widely used (developing countries)

    NEET PG: Streptokinase antigenic, cannot repeat within 6 months

    ALTEPLASE (tPA - Tissue Plasminogen Activator)¶

    Source: Recombinant human

    Properties: - Non-antigenic - Fibrin-specific (more clot-selective) - More effective than streptokinase

    Uses: - Preferred for stroke - STEMI (if available)

    Expensive

    NEET PG: Alteplase = tPA, preferred for stroke, fibrin-specific

    Adverse Effects¶

    1. Bleeding ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Most serious - Intracranial hemorrhage (1-2% in stroke) - Antidote: - Stop drug - Tranexamic acid or Aminocaproic acid (antifibrinolytics) - FFP, cryoprecipitate

    2. Reperfusion arrhythmias: - VT, VF (transient)

    3. Allergic reactions (streptokinase): - Fever, rash, anaphylaxis

    NEET PG: Thrombolytics main risk = Bleeding (intracranial hemorrhage)

    Contraindications¶

    Absolute: - Active bleeding - Recent stroke (<3 months) - Recent surgery/trauma (<2 weeks) - Intracranial pathology (tumor, AVM, aneurysm) - Hemorrhagic stroke (any time) - Aortic dissection

    Relative: - Uncontrolled hypertension (>180/110) - Recent GI bleeding - Pregnancy

    NEET PG: Thrombolytics contraindicated in active bleeding, recent surgery/stroke

    Practice MCQs¶

    1. Antidote for heparin overdose? Answer: Protamine sulfate

    2. Monitoring for heparin? Answer: aPTT (target 1.5-2.5× control)

    3. Monitoring for warfarin? Answer: PT/INR (target 2-3)

    4. Antidote for warfarin overdose with major bleeding? Answer: Vitamin K + PCC (or FFP)

    5. Anticoagulant of choice in pregnancy? Answer: Heparin or LMWH (don't cross placenta)

    6. HIT type II management? Answer: Stop heparin, use argatroban/fondaparinux

    7. Antidote for dabigatran? Answer: Idarucizumab

    8. Antidote for rivaroxaban/apixaban? Answer: Andexanet alfa

    9. DAPT (dual antiplatelet therapy) includes? Answer: Aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor (clopidogrel/ticagrelor)

    10. Thrombolytic for acute stroke? Answer: Alteplase (tPA) - within 4.5 hours

    Memory Aids¶

    Warfarin Reversal (VPF)¶

    • **V**itamin K
    • **P**CC (Prothrombin Complex Concentrate)
    • **F**FP (Fresh Frozen Plasma)

    HIT Management (SAF)¶

    • **S**top heparin
    • **A**rgatroban (alternative anticoagulant)
    • **F**ondaparinux (alternative)

    Vitamin K-Dependent Factors (1972)¶

    • Factors II, VII, IX, X (and Protein C, S)

    DOAC Antidotes¶

    • **Dab**igatran → **I**darucizumab (both start with vowels)
    • Xa inhibitors → **A**ndexanet alfa (both have 'a')

    Revision Checklist¶

    • Know heparin mechanism, monitoring (aPTT), antidote
    • Understand HIT (type II) - management
    • Learn LMWH advantages over UFH
    • Know warfarin mechanism, monitoring (INR), reversal
    • Understand warfarin adverse effects (bleeding, skin necrosis, teratogenic)
    • Learn DOAC advantages, antidotes
    • Know antiplatelet drugs (DAPT = aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor)
    • Understand thrombolytic uses (STEMI, stroke) & contraindications
    • Practice 50+ MCQs on anticoagulants & antiplatelets
