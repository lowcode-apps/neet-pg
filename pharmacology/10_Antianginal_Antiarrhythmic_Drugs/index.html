    ANTIANGINAL & ANTIARRHYTHMIC DRUGS - NEET PG Study Material¶

    PART A: ANTIANGINAL DRUGS¶

    ANGINA PECTORIS - Overview¶

    Definition: Chest pain due to myocardial ischemia (O₂ demand > supply)

    Types¶

    1. Stable angina (Exertional): - Predictable - Triggered by exertion - Relieved by rest/nitroglycerin

    2. Unstable angina (ACS): - Unpredictable - At rest or minimal exertion - Medical emergency

    3. Variant (Prinzmetal's) angina: - Due to coronary vasospasm - At rest (often early morning) - ST elevation on ECG

    NEET PG: Variant angina = Vasospasm, ST elevation

    Determinants of Myocardial O₂ Demand¶

    MVO₂ ∝ Heart rate × Contractility × Wall tension (preload + afterload)

    Strategy: ↓ HR, ↓ contractility, ↓ preload, ↓ afterload

    ORGANIC NITRATES¶

    Examples: Nitroglycerin (GTN), Isosorbide dinitrate (ISDN), Isosorbide mononitrate (ISMN)

    Mechanism¶

    NO donors → ↑ cGMP → Smooth muscle relaxation

    Effects: - Venodilation > Arteriolar dilation - ↓ Preload (↓ venous return) → ↓ MVO₂ (main mechanism) - ↓ Afterload (arteriolar dilation) - Coronary vasodilation (↑ coronary blood flow) - Redistribution of coronary flow to ischemic areas

    NEET PG: Nitrates mainly ↓ preload (venodilation)

    NITROGLYCERIN (GTN)¶

    Routes: - Sublingual (acute angina) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Oral (long-acting preparations) - IV (unstable angina, acute MI) - Transdermal patch - Buccal, lingual spray

    Sublingual GTN: - Onset: 1-3 minutes - Duration: 10-30 minutes - DOC for acute angina attack - Dose: 0.3-0.6 mg, repeat every 5 min (max 3 doses)

    NEET PG: Sublingual GTN = DOC for acute angina

    Uses¶

    1. Acute angina (sublingual)

    2. Prophylaxis (long-acting): - Stable angina - Unstable angina (IV)

    3. Acute MI (IV - ↓ preload, ↓ afterload)

    4. Acute heart failure (IV - ↓ preload)

    5. Variant angina (vasospasm)

    Adverse Effects¶

    1. Headache ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Most common - Throbbing, due to cerebral vasodilation - Tolerance develops

    2. Postural hypotension: - Venodilation - Reflex tachycardia

    3. Tolerance ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Develops with continuous use - Prevention: Nitrate-free interval (10-12 hours/day) - Remove patch at night - Asymmetric dosing (ISDN: 8 AM, 2 PM - NOT 8 PM)

    4. Methemoglobinemia: - High doses, prolonged use - Treatment: Methylene blue

    5. Drug interaction: - Sildenafil (Viagra) + Nitrates → Severe hypotension - Contraindicated together - Wait 24 hours (sildenafil) or 48 hours (tadalafil)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Nitrates most common side effect = Headache - Tolerance prevented by nitrate-free interval - Contraindicated with sildenafil (severe hypotension)

    ISOSORBIDE DINITRATE (ISDN)¶

    Routes: Oral, sublingual

    Duration: Longer than GTN (4-6 hours)

    Use: Prophylaxis of angina

    Dosing: Asymmetric (to allow nitrate-free interval) - 8 AM, 2 PM (NOT 8 PM)

    ISOSORBIDE MONONITRATE (ISMN)¶

    Active metabolite of ISDN

    Duration: Longer (once or twice daily)

    Advantage: More consistent bioavailability

    β-BLOCKERS IN ANGINA¶

    Examples: Atenolol, Metoprolol, Propranolol, Bisoprolol

    Mechanism¶

    • ↓ Heart rate (most important)
    • ↓ Contractility
    • ↓ Blood pressure
    • → ↓ MVO₂

    NEET PG: β-blockers ↓ MVO₂ by ↓ HR (main)

    Uses¶

    1. Stable angina ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - First-line (with aspirin) - Especially if post-MI, hypertension, arrhythmias

    2. Post-MI (↓ mortality)

    3. Unstable angina

    NOT useful: Variant angina (may worsen vasospasm)

    NEET PG: β-blockers first-line for stable angina, avoid in variant angina

    Adverse Effects & Contraindications¶

    Contraindications: - Variant angina (worsens vasospasm) - Asthma, COPD - Heart block - Acute heart failure

    Adverse effects: - Bradycardia, fatigue - Cold extremities - Bronchoconstriction

    CALCIUM CHANNEL BLOCKERS (CCBs) IN ANGINA¶

    Mechanism¶

    Block L-type Ca²⁺ channels: - DHP (dihydropyridines): Vascular smooth muscle - Non-DHP: Cardiac + vascular

    DIHYDROPYRIDINES (Amlodipine, Nifedipine)¶

    Mechanism: - Coronary vasodilation - Peripheral vasodilation → ↓ afterload - → ↓ MVO₂

    Uses: - Variant (Prinzmetal's) angina ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (DOC) - Stable angina (if β-blockers contraindicated) - Can combine with β-blockers (safe)

    NEET PG: DHP CCBs = DOC for variant angina

    NON-DIHYDROPYRIDINES (Verapamil, Diltiazem)¶

    Mechanism: - ↓ HR, ↓ contractility - Vasodilation - → ↓ MVO₂

    Uses: - Stable angina - Alternative to β-blockers

    Contraindication: - Do NOT combine with β-blockers (↑ heart block risk)

    NEET PG: Don't combine verapamil/diltiazem with β-blockers

    RANOLAZINE¶

    Mechanism: Inhibits late Na⁺ current in cardiac myocytes - ↓ Intracellular Ca²⁺ overload - ↓ Diastolic tension - Improves perfusion

    Uses: - Chronic stable angina (add-on therapy) - When other drugs insufficient

    Advantage: No effect on HR, BP (no hemodynamic changes)

    Adverse effects: QT prolongation

    NEET PG: Ranolazine = Add-on for chronic angina, no hemodynamic effect

    IVABRADINE¶

    Mechanism: Selective If (funny current) inhibitor in SA node - Pure heart rate reduction - No effect on contractility, conduction

    Uses: - Chronic stable angina (alternative to β-blockers) - Heart failure with reduced EF (systolic HF)

    Advantage: Can use in asthma, COPD (no bronchoconstriction)

    Adverse effects: - Bradycardia - Visual disturbances (phosphenes)

    NEET PG: Ivabradine = Pure HR reduction, safe in asthma

    TRIMETAZIDINE¶

    Mechanism: Metabolic modulator - Shifts ATP production from fatty acid → glucose oxidation - More O₂-efficient

    Uses: Chronic stable angina (add-on)

    ANTIANGINAL DRUG SUMMARY¶

    Drug Class Stable Angina Unstable Angina Variant Angina
    Nitrates ✓ (prophylaxis) ✓ (IV) ✓
    β-blockers ✓ (1st line) ✓ ✗ (avoid)
    DHP CCBs ✓ ✓ ✓ (DOC)
    Non-DHP CCBs ✓ ✓ ✓
    Aspirin ✓ ✓ ✗
    Ranolazine ✓ (add-on) - -
    Ivabradine ✓ (alternative to β-blocker) - -

    NEET PG: Know drug of choice for each type of angina

    PART B: ANTIARRHYTHMIC DRUGS¶

    VAUGHAN WILLIAMS CLASSIFICATION¶

    Class I: Na⁺ channel blockers - IA: Quinidine, Procainamide, Disopyramide - IB: Lidocaine, Mexiletine - IC: Flecainide, Propafenone

    Class II: β-blockers - Propranolol, Metoprolol, Esmolol

    Class III: K⁺ channel blockers (↑ repolarization) - Amiodarone, Sotalol, Dofetilide, Ibutilide

    Class IV: Ca²⁺ channel blockers - Verapamil, Diltiazem

    Others (Unclassified): - Adenosine - Digoxin - Atropine - Magnesium

    NEET PG: Vaughan Williams classification (memorize)

    CLASS IA ANTIARRHYTHMICS¶

    QUINIDINE¶

    Mechanism: - Na⁺ channel blocker (moderate) - K⁺ channel blocker → ↑ QT interval - Anticholinergic (vagolytic)

    Effects on ECG: - ↑ PR interval - ↑ QRS duration (Na⁺ block) - ↑ QT interval (K⁺ block)

    Uses¶

    1. Atrial fibrillation/flutter (cardioversion, maintenance)

    2. Ventricular arrhythmias (VT, VPCs)

    Note: Now rarely used (replaced by safer drugs)

    Adverse Effects¶

    1. Cinchonism ⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Tinnitus, headache, vertigo - Visual disturbances - GI upset

    2. Torsades de pointes ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Polymorphic VT - Due to QT prolongation - Life-threatening - Treatment: Magnesium sulfate, isoproterenol, pacing

    3. Thrombocytopenia

    4. Drug interactions: - ↑ Digoxin levels (↓ renal clearance)

    5. Diarrhea (most common)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Quinidine causes cinchonism (tinnitus) - Torsades de pointes (QT prolongation) treated with Mg²⁺

    PROCAINAMIDE¶

    Similar to quinidine

    Additional adverse effect: - Drug-induced lupus ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Very common (50-70% with chronic use) - Anti-histone antibodies (+) - Reversible

    NEET PG: Procainamide causes drug-induced lupus (very common)

    CLASS IB ANTIARRHYTHMICS¶

    LIDOCAINE (Lignocaine)¶

    Mechanism: - Na⁺ channel blocker (fast on-off kinetics) - Preferentially blocks ischemic/depolarized tissue - No effect on normal tissue

    Effects on ECG: - No change in QRS, QT (selective for abnormal tissue)

    Uses¶

    1. Ventricular arrhythmias ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Post-MI VT, VF - Digoxin-induced arrhythmias

    NOT effective: Atrial arrhythmias

    Route: IV only (first-pass metabolism if oral)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Lidocaine = DOC for post-MI ventricular arrhythmias - Also for digoxin-induced VT - IV only

    Adverse Effects¶

    • CNS: Seizures (high doses), confusion
    • Hypotension
    • Bradycardia

    MEXILETINE¶

    Oral analog of lidocaine

    Uses: Chronic ventricular arrhythmias

    CLASS IC ANTIARRHYTHMICS¶

    FLECAINIDE, PROPAFENONE¶

    Mechanism: Potent Na⁺ channel blockers

    Uses: - Supraventricular arrhythmias (AF, PSVT) - Only in structurally normal hearts

    Contraindication ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Post-MI or structural heart disease - CAST study: ↑ Mortality in post-MI patients

    NEET PG: Flecainide contraindicated post-MI (CAST trial)

    CLASS II: β-BLOCKERS¶

    Examples: Propranolol, Metoprolol, Esmolol

    Mechanism¶

    • ↓ SA node automaticity
    • ↓ AV conduction
    • ↓ Sympathetic drive

    Effects on ECG: - ↑ PR interval - ↓ HR

    Uses¶

    1. Supraventricular arrhythmias ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - SVT, PSVT (acute & prophylaxis) - Atrial fibrillation (rate control) - Atrial flutter (rate control)

    2. Ventricular arrhythmias: - Post-MI (prophylaxis) - Exercise-induced VT

    3. Thyrotoxicosis-induced arrhythmias

    NEET PG: β-blockers for SVT, AF rate control

    ESMOLOL¶

    Ultra-short acting β1-blocker (IV)

    Uses: - Perioperative tachycardia - Acute SVT - Thyroid storm

    NEET PG: Esmolol = Ultra-short acting, IV

    CLASS III: K⁺ CHANNEL BLOCKERS¶

    AMIODARONE - Most Important¶

    Mechanism: - K⁺ channel blocker (main) → ↑ repolarization - Also blocks: Na⁺, Ca²⁺ channels, α/β receptors (non-competitive) - Broadest spectrum antiarrhythmic

    Effects on ECG: - ↑ QT interval (but rarely causes torsades) - ↑ PR, ↑ QRS

    Uses (Broadest Spectrum)¶

    1. Ventricular arrhythmias ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Life-threatening VT, VF - Refractory VT/VF (ACLS protocol) - Most effective for ventricular arrhythmias

    2. Supraventricular arrhythmias: - Atrial fibrillation (cardioversion, maintenance) - PSVT (including WPW)

    3. Cardiac arrest (VF, pulseless VT)

    NEET PG High-Yield: Amiodarone = Broadest spectrum, most effective for VT/VF

    Pharmacokinetics¶

    Half-life: Very long (20-100 days) - Slow onset (oral - days to weeks) - Slow elimination (persists weeks after stopping) - Loading dose required

    Loading: 800-1600 mg/day × 1-2 weeks, then ↓

    NEET PG: Amiodarone long half-life (20-100 days)

    Adverse Effects (Multiple Organs)¶

    1. Pulmonary toxicity ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Most serious - Pulmonary fibrosis (irreversible) - Onset: Months to years - Monitor: Chest X-ray, PFTs, symptoms - Contraindicated in pre-existing lung disease

    2. Thyroid dysfunction ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - High iodine content (37% iodine by weight) - Hypothyroidism OR hyperthyroidism - Monitor: TSH every 6 months

    3. Hepatotoxicity: - ↑ Transaminases - Monitor: LFTs

    4. Corneal microdeposits ⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Almost universal (>90%) - Usually asymptomatic - Reversible

    5. Blue-gray skin discoloration: - Photosensitivity - Sun exposure

    6. Peripheral neuropathy, myopathy

    7. Bradycardia, AV block

    8. ↑ QT (but torsades rare)

    9. Drug interactions: - ↑ Digoxin, warfarin levels (CYP inhibition)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Amiodarone most serious = Pulmonary fibrosis - High iodine → thyroid dysfunction - Corneal deposits (common, benign) - ↑ Digoxin & warfarin levels

    Monitoring¶

    Baseline & periodic: - Pulmonary: CXR, PFTs, symptoms - Thyroid: TSH, T3, T4 - Hepatic: LFTs - Ophthalmic: Eye exam

    SOTALOL¶

    Mechanism: β-blocker + K⁺ channel blocker

    Uses: - Ventricular arrhythmias - Atrial fibrillation

    Adverse effects: - Torsades de pointes (↑ QT) - Bradycardia (β-blocker)

    NEET PG: Sotalol = β-blocker + K⁺ blocker

    DOFETILIDE, IBUTILIDE¶

    Pure K⁺ channel blockers

    Uses: - Atrial fibrillation (cardioversion) - Ibutilide: IV for acute AF conversion

    Adverse effect: Torsades de pointes (high risk)

    CLASS IV: CALCIUM CHANNEL BLOCKERS¶

    VERAPAMIL, DILTIAZEM¶

    Mechanism: - Block AV node Ca²⁺ channels - ↓ AV conduction

    Effects on ECG: - ↑ PR interval

    Uses¶

    1. Supraventricular arrhythmias ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - PSVT (acute termination) - Verapamil DOC (IV) - Atrial fibrillation/flutter (rate control)

    NOT effective: Ventricular arrhythmias

    NEET PG: Verapamil (IV) = DOC for acute PSVT termination

    Contraindications¶

    • WPW syndrome with AF ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
    • May ↑ ventricular rate (paradoxical)
    • Risk of VF
    • VT (may cause hemodynamic collapse)
    • Heart block
    • Heart failure

    NEET PG: Verapamil contraindicated in WPW with AF

    UNCLASSIFIED ANTIARRHYTHMICS¶

    ADENOSINE¶

    Mechanism: - A1 receptor agonist - ↑ K⁺ conductance → Hyperpolarization - Slows AV conduction (transient AV block)

    Pharmacokinetics: - Ultra-short half-life (<10 seconds) - Rapid IV bolus required - Metabolized by RBCs (adenosine deaminase)

    Uses¶

    1. PSVT ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - DOC for acute termination - Diagnostic (unmasking flutter waves)

    NOT effective: Atrial fibrillation, VT

    NEET PG: Adenosine = DOC for acute PSVT (ultra-short acting)

    Administration¶

    Rapid IV push (6 mg → 12 mg → 12 mg if needed) - Followed immediately by saline flush - Large vein (antecubital)

    Adverse Effects¶

    Common (but transient - seconds): - Flushing - Dyspnea, chest discomfort - Transient asystole (expected) - Bronchospasm

    Contraindications: - Asthma (bronchospasm) - Heart block (2nd/3rd degree)

    Drug interactions: - Dipyridamole: ↑ Effect (blocks adenosine uptake) - Theophylline: ↓ Effect (adenosine antagonist)

    NEET PG: Adenosine contraindicated in asthma, potentiated by dipyridamole

    DIGOXIN¶

    Uses (as antiarrhythmic): - Atrial fibrillation (rate control) - Atrial flutter (rate control) - Paroxysmal SVT

    Mechanism: ↑ Vagal tone, ↓ AV conduction

    NEET PG: Digoxin for AF rate control

    MAGNESIUM SULFATE¶

    Uses: - Torsades de pointes ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (DOC) - Digoxin-induced arrhythmias - Hypomagnesemia

    NEET PG: Mg²⁺ = DOC for torsades de pointes

    SPECIFIC ARRHYTHMIA MANAGEMENT¶

    Atrial Fibrillation¶

    Rate control: - β-blockers, CCBs (verapamil, diltiazem), digoxin

    Rhythm control (cardioversion): - Amiodarone, dofetilide - Electrical cardioversion

    Anticoagulation: Warfarin, DOACs

    NEET PG: AF = Rate control (β-blocker, CCB) or rhythm control (amiodarone)

    PSVT (Paroxysmal Supraventricular Tachycardia)¶

    Acute termination: 1. Vagal maneuvers (first-line) 2. Adenosine (DOC - IV) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ 3. Verapamil (IV) - alternative 4. β-blockers

    Prophylaxis: - β-blockers, verapamil, diltiazem

    NEET PG: PSVT = Adenosine (acute DOC)

    Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)¶

    With pulse: - Amiodarone (IV) - Lidocaine - Synchronized cardioversion

    Pulseless VT/VF (Cardiac arrest): - Defibrillation - Epinephrine - Amiodarone (or lidocaine)

    NEET PG: VT = Amiodarone (most effective)

    Torsades de Pointes¶

    Treatment: - Magnesium sulfate (IV) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Isoproterenol (↑ HR) - Temporary pacing

    Prevent: Stop offending drug, correct electrolytes

    NEET PG: Torsades = Magnesium sulfate

    Practice MCQs¶

    1. Drug of choice for acute angina attack? Answer: Sublingual nitroglycerin

    2. Drug of choice for variant (Prinzmetal's) angina? Answer: Calcium channel blockers (DHP)

    3. Most common side effect of nitrates? Answer: Headache

    4. Why is sildenafil contraindicated with nitrates? Answer: Severe hypotension

    5. Drug of choice for post-MI ventricular arrhythmias? Answer: Lidocaine (IV)

    6. Drug of choice for acute PSVT termination? Answer: Adenosine (IV)

    7. Most serious adverse effect of amiodarone? Answer: Pulmonary fibrosis

    8. Treatment for torsades de pointes? Answer: Magnesium sulfate

    9. Antiarrhythmic contraindicated in WPW with AF? Answer: Verapamil, diltiazem

    10. Antiarrhythmic causing drug-induced lupus? Answer: Procainamide

    Memory Aids¶

    Nitrate Adverse Effects (HMT)¶

    • **H**eadache (most common)
    • **M**ethemoglobinemia
    • **T**olerance

    Amiodarone Toxicities (PULTH)¶

    • **P**ulmonary fibrosis
    • **U**nderactive/overactive thyroid
    • **L**iver (hepatotoxicity)
    • **T**issue (skin, blue-gray)
    • **H**eart (bradycardia)

    PSVT Treatment (VAB)¶

    • **V**agal maneuvers
    • **A**denosine
    • **B**eta-blockers (or verapamil)
