    ANTIHYPERTENSIVE DRUGS (Detailed) - NEET PG Study Material¶

    BLOOD PRESSURE REGULATION¶

    Factors Determining BP¶

    BP = Cardiac Output (CO) × Total Peripheral Resistance (TPR)

    Cardiac Output = Heart rate × Stroke volume TPR = Vascular resistance (arteriolar tone)

    Regulatory Systems¶

    1. Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System (RAAS): 

    Renin (kidney)
    ↓
Angiotensinogen → Angiotensin I
    ↓ (ACE)
Angiotensin II
    ↓
• Vasoconstriction (↑ TPR)
• Aldosterone release (↑ Na+/H2O retention → ↑ CO)
• ADH release

    2. Sympathetic Nervous System: - ↑ HR, ↑ contractility, vasoconstriction

    3. Baroreceptor Reflex

    4. Kidney (volume regulation)

    NEET PG: Angiotensin II = Vasoconstriction + aldosterone release

    CLASSIFICATION OF ANTIHYPERTENSIVES¶

    A. Diuretics¶

    • Thiazides, Loop, K+-sparing

    B. Sympatholytics¶

    1. Centrally acting: Clonidine, Methyldopa, Moxonidine
    2. Peripherally acting:
    3. α-blockers: Prazosin, Doxazosin
    4. β-blockers: Atenolol, Metoprolol
    5. α + β blockers: Labetalol, Carvedilol
    6. Adrenergic neuron blockers: Reserpine, Guanethidine

    C. Vasodilators¶

    1. Arterial: Hydralazine, Minoxidil, Diazoxide
    2. Arterial + venous: Sodium nitroprusside

    D. Calcium Channel Blockers (CCBs)¶

    • Amlodipine, Nifedipine, Verapamil, Diltiazem

    E. RAAS Inhibitors¶

    1. ACE inhibitors: Enalapril, Lisinopril, Ramipril
    2. ARBs: Losartan, Telmisartan, Valsartan
    3. Direct renin inhibitor: Aliskiren

    F. Others¶

    • Bosentan (endothelin receptor antagonist)

    DIURETICS IN HYPERTENSION¶

    THIAZIDES - First-Line¶

    Examples: Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ), Chlorthalidone, Indapamide

    Mechanism: - Inhibit Na+-Cl- cotransporter in distal convoluted tubule - Natriuresis → ↓ Blood volume → ↓ CO - Chronic: Vasodilation (↓ TPR) - main mechanism

    Advantages: - Inexpensive - Once daily - Reduces cardiovascular events (proven) - Effective in elderly, blacks

    Adverse effects: - Hypokalemia (most common) - Hyperuricemia (gout) - Hyperglycemia (↓ insulin secretion) - Hyperlipidemia (↑ LDL, ↑ TG) - Hypercalcemia (↑ Ca²⁺ reabsorption) - Hyponatremia - Erectile dysfunction

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Thiazides first-line for essential hypertension - Cause hypokalemia, hyperuricemia, hyperglycemia - Cause hypercalcemia (vs loop → hypocalcemia)

    Thiazide Combinations¶

    Thiazide + K+-sparing diuretic: - Hydrochlorothiazide + Amiloride or + Spironolactone - Prevents hypokalemia

    Thiazide + ACE-I/ARB: - Synergistic - ACE-I prevents hypokalemia

    NEET PG: Thiazide + K+-sparing prevents hypokalemia

    CENTRALLY ACTING SYMPATHOLYTICS¶

    METHYLDOPA¶

    Mechanism: - Prodrug → Converted to α-methyl norepinephrine in CNS - Central α2 agonist (like clonidine) - ↓ Sympathetic outflow → ↓ BP

    Uses: - Hypertension in pregnancy ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Safe in pregnancy - Drug of choice for chronic HTN in pregnancy - Chronic hypertension

    Adverse effects: - Sedation, drowsiness - Dry mouth - Positive Coombs test (10-20%) - usually no hemolysis - Hemolytic anemia (rare) - Hepatotoxicity - Drug-induced lupus - Sexual dysfunction - Rebound hypertension (if stopped abruptly)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Methyldopa = DOC for chronic hypertension in pregnancy - Positive Coombs test (but hemolysis rare)

    CLONIDINE¶

    Mechanism: Central α2 agonist

    Uses: - Hypertension - Opioid withdrawal - ADHD (off-label)

    Adverse effects: - Sedation, dry mouth - Severe rebound hypertension (if stopped abruptly) - Bradycardia

    NEET PG: Clonidine rebound hypertension (taper gradually)

    PERIPHERALLY ACTING SYMPATHOLYTICS¶

    α1-Blockers¶

    Examples: Prazosin, Doxazosin, Terazosin

    Mechanism: Selective α1 antagonist → Vasodilation

    Uses: - Hypertension (NOT first-line) - BPH (benign prostatic hyperplasia)

    Adverse effects: - First-dose hypotension (severe) - Orthostatic hypotension - Reflex tachycardia (minimal)

    NEET PG: α1-blockers useful for hypertension + BPH

    β-Blockers¶

    Mechanism: - ↓ Cardiac output (β1 - heart) - ↓ Renin release (β1 - kidney)

    First-line in: - Hypertension + IHD - Hypertension + Heart failure - Hypertension + Post-MI - Hypertension + Tachyarrhythmias

    Preferred agents: - Cardioselective (β1): Atenolol, Metoprolol, Bisoprolol - α + β: Carvedilol, Labetalol

    Adverse effects: - Bradycardia, heart block - Bronchospasm (avoid in asthma) - Fatigue, depression - Masks hypoglycemia - Dyslipidemia (↑ TG, ↓ HDL)

    Contraindications: - Asthma, COPD - Heart block (2nd/3rd degree) - Acute heart failure

    NEET PG: β-blockers first-line if IHD, heart failure, post-MI

    VASODILATORS¶

    HYDRALAZINE¶

    Mechanism: Direct arterial vasodilator (exact mechanism unclear) - ↑ cGMP - ↓ TPR

    Hemodynamic effects: - ↓ BP (↓ TPR) - Reflex tachycardia (↑ HR, ↑ CO) - Fluid retention

    Uses: - Hypertensive emergency in pregnancy (eclampsia) - IV/IM - Safe in pregnancy - Heart failure (with nitrates - afterload reduction) - Severe hypertension (with β-blocker + diuretic)

    Adverse effects: - Reflex tachycardia (prevent with β-blocker) - Fluid retention (prevent with diuretic) - Headache, flushing - Drug-induced lupus ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Dose >200 mg/day - Slow acetylators at higher risk - Anti-histone antibodies (+) - Reversible (stop drug)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Hydralazine for eclampsia (IV), safe in pregnancy - Causes drug-induced lupus (slow acetylators) - Always give with β-blocker + diuretic

    MINOXIDIL¶

    Mechanism: K+ channel opener → Hyperpolarization → Vasodilation

    Most potent oral vasodilator

    Uses: - Refractory hypertension (resistant to other drugs) - Always with β-blocker + diuretic

    Adverse effects: - Severe reflex tachycardia - Severe fluid retention - Hirsutism (hair growth) - common - Topical minoxidil for androgenic alopecia - Pericardial effusion

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Minoxidil = Most potent oral vasodilator, for refractory HTN - Causes hirsutism (used topically for hair growth)

    SODIUM NITROPRUSSIDE (SNP)¶

    Mechanism: NO donor → ↑ cGMP → Vasodilation (arterial + venous)

    Route: IV only (very short-acting)

    Uses: - Hypertensive emergency ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Fastest acting - Immediate onset - Drug of choice for most hypertensive emergencies - Acute heart failure

    Monitoring: Intra-arterial BP monitoring required

    Adverse effects: - Cyanide toxicity ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - SNP → Cyanide → Thiocyanate (liver) - Prolonged infusion / renal failure → accumulation - Symptoms: Metabolic acidosis, tinnitus, confusion - Treatment: Sodium thiosulfate, hydroxocobalamin - Thiocyanate toxicity: - Prolonged use (>72 hours) - Tinnitus, blurred vision, confusion - Hypotension (excessive) - Increased ICP (cerebral vasodilation)

    Contraindications: - Increased intracranial pressure - Severe renal/hepatic impairment - Leber's optic atrophy

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Sodium nitroprusside = DOC for hypertensive emergency - Cyanide toxicity (treat with sodium thiosulfate) - Contraindicated if ↑ ICP

    DIAZOXIDE¶

    Mechanism: K+ channel opener → Vasodilation

    Route: IV bolus (not infusion)

    Uses: - Hypertensive emergency (now rarely used - replaced by SNP) - Insulinoma (inhibits insulin release)

    Adverse effects: - Hyperglycemia (inhibits insulin) - Fluid retention - Hirsutism

    NEET PG: Diazoxide causes hyperglycemia (vs thiazides)

    CALCIUM CHANNEL BLOCKERS (CCBs)¶

    Classification¶

    1. Dihydropyridines (DHP): - Amlodipine, Nifedipine, Felodipine, Nicardipine - Vascular selective (peripheral vasodilation)

    2. Non-dihydropyridines: - Verapamil (cardiac selective) - Diltiazem (cardiac + vascular)

    NEET PG: DHP (-dipine) = Vascular, non-DHP = Cardiac

    AMLODIPINE / NIFEDIPINE (DHP)¶

    Mechanism: - Block L-type Ca²⁺ channels (vascular smooth muscle) - Vasodilation → ↓ TPR → ↓ BP

    Amlodipine advantages: - Long-acting (once daily) - No reflex tachycardia (slow onset) - Well tolerated

    Nifedipine: - Short-acting (immediate release) - avoid (reflex tachycardia, MI risk) - Long-acting (SR/XL) - safer

    Uses: - Hypertension (first-line option) - Angina - Vasospastic angina (Prinzmetal's) - DOC

    Adverse effects: - Peripheral edema (most common - ankle edema) - Flushing, headache - Reflex tachycardia (short-acting nifedipine) - Gingival hyperplasia

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Amlodipine long-acting, once daily, well tolerated - DHP cause peripheral edema, gingival hyperplasia

    VERAPAMIL (Non-DHP)¶

    Mechanism: Blocks cardiac Ca²⁺ channels

    Effects: - ↓ HR (negative chronotropy) - ↓ Contractility (negative inotropy) - ↓ AV conduction - Vasodilation (less than DHP)

    Uses: - Hypertension - Angina - Supraventricular tachycardia (SVT, PSVT) - Rate control in atrial fibrillation

    Adverse effects: - Constipation (most common) - Bradycardia, AV block - Heart failure (negative inotrope) - Peripheral edema

    Contraindications: - Heart block (2nd/3rd degree) - Sick sinus syndrome - Severe heart failure - Concurrent β-blocker (↑ risk of heart block)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Verapamil for SVT, rate control in AF - Most common side effect = Constipation - Don't combine with β-blocker

    DILTIAZEM (Non-DHP)¶

    Intermediate between verapamil & DHP

    Uses: - Hypertension - Angina - Atrial fibrillation (rate control)

    Adverse effects: - Less constipation than verapamil - Bradycardia, edema

    RAAS INHIBITORS¶

    ACE INHIBITORS (ACE-I)¶

    Examples: Enalapril, Lisinopril, Ramipril, Perindopril, Captopril

    Mechanism: - Inhibit ACE (Angiotensin Converting Enzyme) - ↓ Angiotensin II → Vasodilation, ↓ aldosterone - ↑ Bradykinin → Vasodilation (but also cough)

    Effects: - ↓ BP (vasodilation, ↓ volume) - Renoprotective (↓ intraglomerular pressure) - Cardioprotective (↓ remodeling)

    Indications (First-Line)¶

    1. Hypertension ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Especially with: - Diabetes (renoprotective) - CKD (proteinuria) - Heart failure - Post-MI - LVH

    2. Heart failure (all stages)

    3. Post-MI (↓ remodeling, ↓ mortality)

    4. Diabetic nephropathy (↓ proteinuria, slow progression)

    5. CKD with proteinuria

    NEET PG High-Yield: - ACE-I first-line for HTN + diabetes, CKD, heart failure, post-MI

    Adverse Effects¶

    1. Dry cough ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Most common (10-20%) - Due to ↑ bradykinin - Switch to ARB (no cough)

    2. Hyperkalemia: - ↓ Aldosterone → K+ retention - Risk: CKD, K+-sparing diuretics, NSAIDs

    3. Angioedema ⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Rare but serious (0.1-0.5%) - Due to ↑ bradykinin - Lip, tongue, laryngeal edema - Life-threatening (airway compromise) - Absolute contraindication for future ACE-I/ARB use - More common in blacks

    4. First-dose hypotension: - Especially in volume-depleted, high-renin states

    5. Acute kidney injury: - Bilateral renal artery stenosis → ARF (contraindication) - ↑ Creatinine (acceptable <30% rise)

    6. Teratogenic ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐: - Contraindicated in pregnancy - Fetal renal dysgenesis, oligohydramnios, growth restriction - Category D (2nd/3rd trimester)

    7. Taste disturbance (captopril)

    **8. Rash, proteinuria (captopril)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - ACE-I most common side effect = Dry cough (↑ bradykinin) - Angioedema = Serious, absolute contraindication - Contraindicated in pregnancy, bilateral RAS - Cause hyperkalemia

    Contraindications¶

    Absolute: - Pregnancy - Bilateral renal artery stenosis - History of angioedema - Renal artery stenosis in solitary kidney

    Relative: - Hyperkalemia (>5.5 mEq/L) - Severe renal impairment

    NEET PG: ACE-I contraindicated in pregnancy, bilateral RAS, angioedema history

    ANGIOTENSIN RECEPTOR BLOCKERS (ARBs)¶

    Examples: Losartan, Telmisartan, Valsartan, Candesartan, Irbesartan

    Mechanism: - Block AT1 receptors (Angiotensin II type 1) - Similar effects to ACE-I - No effect on bradykinin → No cough, less angioedema

    Indications¶

    Same as ACE-I: - Hypertension (with diabetes, CKD, heart failure, post-MI) - Alternative to ACE-I if intolerant (cough, angioedema)

    Additional: - Marfan syndrome (↓ aortic root dilation)

    NEET PG: ARBs = Alternative to ACE-I (no cough)

    Advantages over ACE-I¶

    • No cough (no bradykinin effect)
    • Less angioedema (but still possible)
    • Better tolerated

    Adverse Effects¶

    Similar to ACE-I (except cough): - Hyperkalemia - Acute kidney injury (bilateral RAS) - Teratogenic (contraindicated in pregnancy) - First-dose hypotension - Angioedema (rare)

    Additional: - Losartan: ↓ Uric acid (useful in gout + HTN)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - ARBs don't cause cough (vs ACE-I) - Losartan ↓ uric acid (good for HTN + gout)

    ACE-I + ARB Combination¶

    NOT recommended: - ↑ Hyperkalemia - ↑ Hypotension - ↑ Acute kidney injury - No additional benefit

    NEET PG: Don't combine ACE-I + ARB

    DIRECT RENIN INHIBITOR¶

    ALISKIREN

    Mechanism: Inhibits renin (rate-limiting step)

    Uses: Hypertension (not widely used)

    Adverse effects: Similar to ACE-I/ARB

    NEET PG: Aliskiren = Renin inhibitor (not commonly used)

    JNC 8 GUIDELINES - First-Line Drugs¶

    For general population: 1. Thiazide diuretics 2. ACE inhibitors or ARBs 3. Calcium channel blockers 4. β-blockers (if compelling indication)

    Special populations: - Diabetes: ACE-I or ARB (first-line) - CKD: ACE-I or ARB (first-line) - Blacks: Thiazide or CCB (first-line) - Elderly: Thiazide or CCB - Pregnancy: Methyldopa, Labetalol, Nifedipine (long-acting)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - First-line for HTN: Thiazide, ACE-I/ARB, CCB - Diabetes/CKD: ACE-I/ARB first - Blacks: Thiazide or CCB

    HYPERTENSIVE EMERGENCIES¶

    Definition: Severe HTN (>180/120) with end-organ damage

    Examples: - Hypertensive encephalopathy - Acute stroke - Acute MI - Acute heart failure - Eclampsia - Aortic dissection

    Goal: ↓ BP by 25% in first hour (not to normal - risk of ischemia)

    Drugs of Choice¶

    1. Sodium nitroprusside: - DOC for most emergencies - IV infusion - Immediate onset

    2. Labetalol: - Safe in pregnancy - IV bolus or infusion - Eclampsia, pre-eclampsia

    3. Hydralazine: - Eclampsia (IV/IM) - Safe in pregnancy

    4. Nitroglycerin: - ACS (acute coronary syndrome) - Acute heart failure

    5. Esmolol: - Aortic dissection (with nitroprusside) - Ultra-short acting β-blocker

    6. Nicardipine: - CCB (IV) - Cerebrovascular events

    7. Fenoldopam: - Dopamine D1 agonist - ↓ BP, preserves renal flow

    8. Clevidipine: - Ultra-short acting CCB (IV)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Hypertensive emergency = SNP (most), labetalol/hydralazine (pregnancy), esmolol (dissection)

    DRUG COMBINATIONS¶

    Rational combinations:

    1. Diuretic + ACE-I/ARB: - Synergistic - Prevents K+ loss

    2. CCB (DHP) + β-blocker: - CCB vasodilation, β-blocker prevents reflex tachycardia

    3. ACE-I/ARB + CCB: - Synergistic - ↓ Edema from CCB

    4. Thiazide + β-blocker

    AVOID: - β-blocker + verapamil/diltiazem (↑ heart block) - ACE-I + ARB (↑ hyperkalemia, no benefit) - ACE-I/ARB + K+-sparing diuretic (hyperkalemia risk)

    NEET PG: Don't combine β-blocker + verapamil, ACE-I + ARB

    Practice MCQs¶

    1. First-line drug for essential hypertension? Answer: Thiazide, ACE-I/ARB, or CCB

    2. Antihypertensive safe in pregnancy (chronic HTN)? Answer: Methyldopa

    3. Drug of choice for hypertensive emergency in eclampsia? Answer: Labetalol or Hydralazine (IV)

    4. Most common side effect of ACE inhibitors? Answer: Dry cough

    5. Why switch from ACE-I to ARB? Answer: Intolerance (cough, angioedema)

    6. Drug causing drug-induced lupus? Answer: Hydralazine (high dose, slow acetylators)

    7. Antihypertensive causing hirsutism? Answer: Minoxidil

    8. Treatment for sodium nitroprusside cyanide toxicity? Answer: Sodium thiosulfate

    9. CCB causing constipation? Answer: Verapamil

    10. ARB that lowers uric acid? Answer: Losartan

    Memory Aids¶

    ACE-I Indications (CHAMP)¶

    • **C**KD (with proteinuria)
    • **H**eart failure
    • **A**nti-remodeling (post-MI)
    • **M**yocardial infarction
    • **P**roteinuria (diabetic nephropathy)

    ACE-I Contraindications (PREG-B)¶

    • **P**regnancy
    • **R**enal artery stenosis (bilateral)
    • **E**dema (angioedema history)
    • **G**ravida (pregnancy)
    • **B**ilateral RAS

    Hydralazine Side Effects (FLATR)¶

    • **F**luid retention
    • **L**upus
    • **A**ngina (reflex tachycardia)
    • **T**achycardia
    • **R**eflex sympathetic activation
