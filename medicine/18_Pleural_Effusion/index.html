    DEFINITION & ANATOMY¶

    Definition¶

    Pleural Effusion: Abnormal accumulation of fluid in the pleural space (between visceral and parietal pleura)

    Normal Pleural Space¶

    Anatomy: - Parietal pleura: Lines chest wall, diaphragm, mediastinum - Visceral pleura: Covers lung surface - Pleural space: Potential space (normally 10-20 μm)

    Normal Pleural Fluid: - Volume: 0.1-0.2 mL/kg (~10-20 mL total) - Production: Parietal pleura (systemic circulation) - Absorption: Visceral pleura (pulmonary circulation) + Lymphatics - Protein: <1.5 g/dL - pH: 7.60-7.64 - Glucose: Equal to serum - LDH: <50% of serum - WBC: <1000/μL (mostly mesothelial cells, macrophages)

    Starling Forces¶

    FLUID PRODUCTION (Parietal Pleura)
    ↓
Capillary hydrostatic pressure (30 mmHg)
+ Pleural space oncotic pressure (6 mmHg)
MINUS
Capillary oncotic pressure (34 mmHg)
+ Pleural space hydrostatic pressure (-5 mmHg)
    ↓
NET FILTRATION = +7 mmHg → Fluid enters pleural space
    ↓
FLUID ABSORPTION (Visceral Pleura + Lymphatics)
    ↓
BALANCE: 10-20 mL maintained

    Lymphatic Drainage: ~500 mL/day capacity (can increase 10-20x)

    CLASSIFICATION¶

    By Mechanism¶

    PLEURAL EFFUSION
│
├─ TRANSUDATIVE (↑ Hydrostatic or ↓ Oncotic pressure)
│  • CHF (MOST COMMON)
│  • Cirrhosis
│  • Nephrotic syndrome
│  • Hypoalbuminemia
│  • Peritoneal dialysis
│
└─ EXUDATIVE (↑ Capillary permeability or ↓ Lymphatic drainage)
   • Infection (Parapneumonic, TB, Empyema)
   • Malignancy
   • Pulmonary embolism
   • Connective tissue disease
   • Pancreatitis
   • Post-CABG

    Mnemonic: "CHF NEPAL" for Transudative Causes¶

    • C - CHF (Congestive Heart Failure)
    • H - Hepatic cirrhosis
    • F - Failure (renal - Nephrotic syndrome)
    • N - Nephrotic syndrome
    • E - starvation (hypoalbuminEmic)
    • P - Peritoneal dialysis
    • A - Atelectasis
    • L - constrictive pericarditis (Less common)

    Mnemonic: "CITE MePPP" for Exudative Causes¶

    • C - Cancer/malignancy
    • I - Infection (parapneumonic, TB, empyema)
    • T - Trauma (hemothorax)
    • E - Embolism (PE)
    • Me - Mesothelioma
    • P - Pancreatitis
    • P - Post-CABG syndrome
    • P - autoimmune (RA, SLE - "rheumatic Pleural")

    ETIOLOGY¶

    Frequency of Causes¶

    Cause Frequency (%) Type
    Congestive Heart Failure 40% - MOST COMMON Transudate
    Pneumonia (Parapneumonic) 20-25% Exudate
    Malignancy 15% Exudate
    Pulmonary Embolism 10% Exudate (75%) or Transudate
    Cirrhosis 5% Transudate
    Tuberculosis 3-5% (Higher in endemic areas) Exudate
    Other <5% Variable

    Transudative Causes¶

    1. Congestive Heart Failure (CHF)¶

    • MOST COMMON cause overall
    • Bilateral (70%), Right > Left (if unilateral)
    • ↑ Hydrostatic pressure (pulmonary venous congestion)

    2. Hepatic Cirrhosis¶

    • Right-sided effusion (70%)
    • Ascites usually present
    • Mechanism: ↓ Oncotic pressure + ascitic fluid movement through diaphragm

    3. Nephrotic Syndrome¶

    • Bilateral
    • ↓ Oncotic pressure (hypoalbuminemia)

    4. Hypoalbuminemia¶

    • Malnutrition, protein-losing enteropathy
    • ↓ Oncotic pressure

    Exudative Causes¶

    1. Parapneumonic Effusion¶

    • Most common exudative cause
    • Accompanies bacterial pneumonia (40-60% of pneumonias)
    • Can be uncomplicated or complicated/empyema

    2. Malignancy¶

    • Lung cancer (35%) - MOST COMMON
    • Breast cancer (25%)
    • Lymphoma (10%)
    • Ovarian cancer (5%)
    • Mesothelioma

    3. Tuberculosis¶

    • Most common cause in developing countries
    • Lymphocytic predominant
    • High ADA levels

    4. Pulmonary Embolism¶

    • 30-50% of PE patients have effusion
    • Usually small, unilateral
    • Exudate (75%) or Transudate (25%)

    5. Connective Tissue Diseases¶

    • Rheumatoid Arthritis (5% of RA patients)
    • Low glucose (<30 mg/dL)
    • Low pH (<7.20)
    • SLE (30-50% of SLE patients)
    • Sjögren's syndrome

    6. Pancreatitis¶

    • Left-sided effusion (70%)
    • High amylase in pleural fluid

    7. Post-Cardiac Injury Syndrome¶

    • Post-CABG, post-MI (Dressler syndrome)
    • Weeks to months after cardiac surgery/MI

    PATHOPHYSIOLOGY¶

    Mechanisms of Effusion Formation¶

    Mechanism Example
    ↑ Hydrostatic pressure CHF, SVC obstruction
    ↓ Oncotic pressure Hypoalbuminemia (cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome, malnutrition)
    ↑ Capillary permeability Infection, inflammation, malignancy
    ↓ Lymphatic drainage Malignancy (lymphatic obstruction), lymphangitis carcinomatosa
    Movement from peritoneum Cirrhosis (transdiaphragmatic), pancreatitis
    Rupture of vascular structure Hemothorax (trauma, aortic dissection)
    Decreased pleural pressure Atelectasis (lung collapse → ↓ pressure → fluid accumulation)

    Parapneumonic Effusion Progression¶

    BACTERIAL PNEUMONIA
    ↓
STAGE 1: EXUDATIVE (0-3 days)
• Sterile fluid, ↑ capillary permeability
• Clear, free-flowing
• pH >7.20, Glucose normal, LDH <1000
    ↓
STAGE 2: FIBRINOPURULENT (3-7 days)
• Bacterial invasion
• Turbid, fibrin deposition, loculations
• pH <7.20, Glucose <60 mg/dL, LDH >1000
• Gram stain/culture may be positive
    ↓
STAGE 3: ORGANIZING (7-28 days)
• Thick peel formation (fibrous)
• Trapped lung, empyema
• Requires drainage ± decortication

    CLINICAL FEATURES¶

    Symptoms¶

    Small Effusion (<300 mL)¶

    • Often asymptomatic
    • Detected incidentally on imaging

    Moderate-Large Effusion (>500 mL)¶

    1. Dyspnea (MOST COMMON)
    2. Exertional → Progressive to rest

    3. Severity correlates with effusion size

    4. Pleuritic Chest Pain

    5. Sharp, worse with inspiration/cough

    6. Suggests pleural inflammation (infection, PE, malignancy)

    7. Dry Cough

    8. Non-productive

    9. Due to bronchial compression

    10. Orthopnea (if massive effusion)

    Systemic Symptoms (Suggest Underlying Cause)¶

    • Fever: Infection (parapneumonic, TB, empyema)
    • Weight loss: Malignancy, TB
    • Night sweats: TB, lymphoma
    • Chest trauma: Hemothorax
    • Abdominal pain: Pancreatitis, subphrenic abscess

    Physical Examination¶

    Classical Triad of Pleural Effusion¶

    1. Dullness to Percussion
    2. MOST SENSITIVE sign
    3. Stony dull

    4. Requires >300-500 mL

    5. Decreased Breath Sounds

    6. Over effusion area

    7. Requires >300-500 mL

    8. Decreased Tactile Fremitus

    9. Fluid dampens vibrations

    Additional Signs¶

    Sign Description
    Aegophony (E→A change) Above effusion level (compressed lung)
    Tracheal shift Away from effusion (if massive, >1000 mL)
    Decreased chest expansion Ipsilateral side
    Ellis-Damoiseau sign Curved upper border of dullness (meniscus)

    Signs of Underlying Cause¶

    • CHF: JVP elevation, S3 gallop, pedal edema, hepatomegaly
    • Cirrhosis: Ascites, jaundice, spider angiomas, palmar erythema
    • Malignancy: Cachexia, lymphadenopathy, clubbing
    • Empyema: Fever, toxicity

    DIAGNOSIS¶

    Diagnostic Approach¶

    CLINICAL SUSPICION (Dyspnea, Dullness, ↓ Breath sounds)
    ↓
CHEST X-RAY (Confirm effusion)
    ↓
THORACENTESIS (Diagnostic ± Therapeutic)
    ↓
PLEURAL FLUID ANALYSIS
    ↓
Light's Criteria (Transudate vs Exudate)
    ↓
FURTHER TESTS (Based on exudate type)
• Cell count & differential
• Glucose, pH, LDH, ADA
• Gram stain, Culture (aerobic/anaerobic/AFB)
• Cytology (if malignancy suspected)
    ↓
IMAGING (If diagnosis unclear or effusion persists)
• Ultrasound (loculations, septations)
• CT Chest (underlying lung pathology)
    ↓
PLEURAL BIOPSY (If TB or malignancy suspected, fluid non-diagnostic)

    1. Chest X-Ray¶

    Findings:

    Volume CXR Finding
    <50 mL Not visible on standard PA view
    50-175 mL Blunting of costophrenic angle on lateral view
    175-500 mL Blunting of costophrenic angle on PA view
    >500 mL Meniscus sign, homogeneous opacity
    >1000 mL Complete opacification of hemithorax, mediastinal shift away

    Views: - PA & Lateral: Standard - Lateral Decubitus: Detects small effusions, confirms free-flowing vs loculated - Fluid layers out if free-flowing (≥10 mm layer confirms drainable effusion)

    Classic Appearance: - Meniscus sign (Ellis line): Curved upper border (concave upward) - Homogeneous opacity obscuring costophrenic angle

    Subpulmonic Effusion: Fluid under lung base, mimics elevated hemidiaphragm

    2. Ultrasound¶

    Advantages: - More sensitive than CXR (detects 5-50 mL) - Differentiates fluid vs consolidation - Detects loculations, septations, pleural thickening - Guides thoracentesis (safer, higher yield)

    Findings: - Anechoic (simple transudate) - Echogenic/complex: Septations (exudate, empyema, hemothorax)

    3. CT Chest (Contrast-Enhanced)¶

    Indications: - Persistent/recurrent effusion despite treatment - Suspected malignancy (evaluate lung parenchyma, pleural nodules, lymph nodes) - Empyema (loculations, pleural thickening) - Hemothorax (high attenuation) - Differentiate pleural vs parenchymal disease

    Findings: - Pleural thickening/nodularity: Malignancy, empyema - Pleural enhancement (split pleura sign): Empyema (contrast-enhanced parietal + visceral pleura separated by fluid) - Loculations: Organized effusion - Underlying lung lesion: Primary tumor, pneumonia

    4. Thoracentesis¶

    ESSENTIAL for diagnosis (except clear CHF responding to diuretics)

    Indications¶

    Diagnostic: - All new effusions of unknown etiology - Effusion ≥10 mm on lateral decubitus or ultrasound

    Therapeutic: - Large effusion causing dyspnea

    Contraindications (Relative)¶

    • Coagulopathy: INR >1.5, Platelets <50,000/μL
    • Correct before procedure OR use ultrasound guidance (safer)
    • Small effusion (<10 mm)
    • Mechanical ventilation (↑ pneumothorax risk)

    Procedure¶

    Site: 5th-6th intercostal space, midaxillary or posterior axillary line - Just above the rib (neurovascular bundle runs below rib)

    Technique: - Ultrasound-guided (preferred, ↓ complications) - Aspiration: 1-1.5 L maximum (↓ re-expansion pulmonary edema risk)

    Complications: - Pneumothorax (5-10%, <2% need chest tube) - MOST COMMON - Hemothorax, infection - Re-expansion pulmonary edema (if >1.5 L removed rapidly) - Vasovagal reaction

    5. Pleural Fluid Analysis¶

    Gross Appearance¶

    Appearance Causes
    Clear, straw-colored Transudate, early exudate
    Turbid/cloudy Exudate (high cell/protein), empyema, chylothorax
    Bloody Malignancy, PE, trauma (hemothorax), TB
    Purulent Empyema (pus)
    Milky Chylothorax (lymphatic leak, triglycerides >110 mg/dL)
    Food particles Esophageal rupture
    Foul odor Anaerobic empyema

    Bloody Effusion (Differentiate from Traumatic Tap): - Hematocrit: If pleural fluid Hct/blood Hct >50% → Hemothorax - Traumatic tap: Blood clots, clears with continued aspiration

    Light's Criteria (Transudate vs Exudate)¶

    GOLD STANDARD

    Exudate if ANY ONE of the following:

    Criterion Cutoff
    Pleural protein / Serum protein >0.5
    Pleural LDH / Serum LDH >0.6
    Pleural LDH >⅔ upper limit of normal serum LDH

    Transudate: NONE of the above criteria met

    Sensitivity: 98% (rarely misses exudate) Specificity: 80% (25% of transudates misclassified as exudates, especially CHF on diuretics)

    Mnemonic: "PL-PL-PL" (Protein-LDH-LDH, 0.5-0.6-⅔)

    Additional Tests¶

    Test Transudate Exudate Significance
    Protein <3 g/dL >3 g/dL Light's criteria
    LDH Low (<200 U/L) High (>200 U/L) Light's criteria
    Glucose Normal (>60 mg/dL) <60 mg/dL Complicated parapneumonic, empyema, RA, malignancy, TB
    pH >7.40 <7.30 Complicated parapneumonic, empyema, esophageal rupture
    Cell count <1000/μL >1000/μL Inflammatory/infectious
    RBC <10,000/μL >10,000/μL (bloody) Malignancy, PE, trauma, TB
    WBC differential - PMN >50%: Acute (parapneumonic, PE), Lymphocytes >50%: Chronic (TB, malignancy)
    Amylase Normal Elevated Pancreatitis (>serum), esophageal rupture
    Triglycerides <50 mg/dL >110 mg/dL Chylothorax (milky fluid)
    ADA (Adenosine Deaminase) <40 U/L >40 U/L TB (Sensitivity 90%, Specificity 85% in endemic areas)
    Cholesterol <45 mg/dL >45 mg/dL Exudate (less specific than Light's)

    Microbiological Tests¶

    Test Indication Yield
    Gram stain Suspected infection 40-60% (empyema)
    Bacterial culture (aerobic + anaerobic) Suspected infection 60% (parapneumonic), 20% (empyema with antibiotics)
    AFB smear Suspected TB <10% (LOW yield)
    Mycobacterial culture Suspected TB 20-40%
    GeneXpert MTB/RIF Suspected TB 40-50% (better than smear/culture)
    Fungal culture Immunocompromised Low yield

    Cytology¶

    Indication: Suspected malignancy

    Yield: - 60% sensitivity (1st sample) - 80% (2nd sample) - 90% (3rd sample)

    If negative → Pleural biopsy (VATS or image-guided)

    6. Pleural Biopsy¶

    Indications: - Suspected TB (fluid AFB negative) - Suspected malignancy (fluid cytology negative) - Undiagnosed exudative effusion

    Methods:

    Method Yield (TB) Yield (Malignancy)
    Closed pleural biopsy (Abrams/Cope needle) 60-70% 40-50%
    Image-guided biopsy (CT/US) - 80-90% (if pleural nodules visible)
    Thoracoscopy (VATS) 90-95% 90-95% (GOLD STANDARD)

    Advantages of Thoracoscopy: - Direct visualization - Targeted biopsy - Therapeutic (pleurodesis, drainage)

    SPECIFIC CAUSES¶

    1. Parapneumonic Effusion & Empyema¶

    Definitions: - Parapneumonic effusion: Any effusion associated with pneumonia - Uncomplicated parapneumonic: Sterile, will resolve with antibiotics alone - Complicated parapneumonic: Bacterial invasion, requires drainage - Empyema: Pus in pleural space (purulent fluid, positive Gram stain/culture)

    Classification (Based on Pleural Fluid)¶

    Type pH Glucose LDH Gram Stain/Culture Treatment
    Uncomplicated >7.20 >60 mg/dL <1000 U/L Negative Antibiotics alone
    Complicated <7.20 <60 mg/dL >1000 U/L Negative or Positive Antibiotics + Chest tube drainage
    Empyema <7.00 <40 mg/dL >1000 U/L Positive (or frank pus) Antibiotics + Chest tube ± Surgery

    Indications for Chest Tube Drainage¶

    Absolute: - Frank pus (empyema) - Positive Gram stain or culture

    Relative (Complicated parapneumonic): - pH <7.20 - Glucose <60 mg/dL - LDH >1000 U/L - Loculated effusion

    Treatment¶

    Antibiotics: - Cover common organisms: S. pneumoniae, S. aureus, K. pneumoniae, anaerobes - Regimen: Ceftriaxone + Metronidazole OR Pip-Tazo - Duration: 2-4 weeks (longer for empyema)

    Drainage: - Chest tube (24-28 Fr) + Suction - Fibrinolytics (tPA + DNase) - For loculated empyema - Improves drainage, ↓ need for surgery - Regimen: tPA 10 mg + DNase 5 mg via chest tube BID × 3 days

    Surgery (If medical management fails): - VATS decortication: Remove fibrous peel - Open thoracotomy: If VATS not feasible

    2. Tuberculous Pleural Effusion¶

    Characteristics: - Most common in young adults (20-40 years) - Unilateral (95%) - Exudate - Lymphocytic predominant (>50%, often >80%) - Pleural fluid AFB smear: <10% positive (LOW yield) - Pleural fluid culture: 20-40% positive (slow, 6-8 weeks)

    Diagnosis¶

    High-Yield Tests:

    Test Sensitivity Specificity
    ADA (Adenosine Deaminase) >40 U/L 90%
    Pleural biopsy (Histology: Granulomas + AFB culture) 90% 95% (BEST)
    GeneXpert MTB/RIF (Pleural fluid) 40-50% 98%
    Pleural fluid AFB smear <10% 98%

    ADA >40 U/L: Highly suggestive in TB-endemic areas

    False Positives for ADA: Empyema, lymphoma, RA (but usually ADA <100)

    Treatment¶

    Same as pulmonary TB: - 2HRZE / 4HR (6 months total) - Steroids: Controversial, may ↓ residual pleural thickening - Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day × 4-6 weeks, then taper

    Prognosis: Excellent with treatment, but 10% develop active TB later

    3. Malignant Pleural Effusion¶

    Causes: 1. Lung cancer (35%) - MOST COMMON 2. Breast cancer (25%) 3. Lymphoma (10%) 4. Ovarian cancer (5%) 5. Mesothelioma (rare, but always malignant)

    Characteristics: - Exudate - Bloody (50%) - Large volume, recurrent - Low glucose (<60 mg/dL) in 30%

    Diagnosis¶

    Pleural fluid cytology: - 60% (1st tap), 80% (2nd), 90% (3rd)

    If negative: - Pleural biopsy (image-guided if nodules, or VATS)

    Tumor markers (Low sensitivity, NOT diagnostic): - CEA, CA 15-3, CA 125

    Treatment¶

    Goals: Symptom relief (dyspnea), prevent recurrence

    Options:

    Method Indication Success Rate
    Therapeutic thoracentesis Symptomatic relief Temporary (recurs in days-weeks)
    Tunneled pleural catheter (IPC) Recurrent effusion, trapped lung, poor prognosis 70-90% symptom control
    Pleurodesis Recurrent effusion, good lung expansion Talc: 90%, Doxycycline/Bleomycin: 60-70%
    Systemic chemotherapy Chemosensitive tumors (lymphoma, SCLC, breast) Variable

    Pleurodesis: - Talc (powder or slurry) - MOST EFFECTIVE - Induces pleural inflammation → Adhesions between visceral and parietal pleura - Contraindication: Trapped lung (lung cannot expand)

    Prognosis: Median survival 3-12 months (depends on primary tumor)

    4. Chylothorax¶

    Definition: Accumulation of lymphatic fluid (chyle) in pleural space

    Causes: - Trauma/Surgery (thoracic duct injury): Esophagectomy, cardiac surgery - Malignancy: Lymphoma (MOST COMMON non-traumatic cause) - Congenital (lymphangiomatosis)

    Characteristics: - Milky/opalescent appearance - Triglycerides >110 mg/dL (DIAGNOSTIC) - Chylomicrons present (lipoprotein electrophoresis) - Lymphocyte-predominant

    Pseudochylothorax: Chronic effusion with high cholesterol (shimmering, not milky), low triglycerides

    Treatment: - Conservative (50% traumatic cases resolve): - Low-fat diet or NPO + TPN (↓ chyle production) - Chest tube drainage - Surgical (if persistent >2 weeks): - Thoracic duct ligation - Pleuroperitoneal shunt

    5. Hemothorax¶

    Definition: Blood in pleural space (Pleural fluid Hct / Blood Hct >50%)

    Causes: - Trauma (blunt/penetrating) - MOST COMMON - Iatrogenic (central line, thoracentesis) - Aortic dissection - Anticoagulation - Malignancy

    Treatment: - Chest tube drainage (32-36 Fr, large bore) - Remove blood, ↓ infection risk, monitor ongoing bleeding - Surgery (VATS or thoracotomy) if: - Initial output >1500 mL - Ongoing bleeding >200 mL/hr × 2-4 hours - Retained hemothorax (organize clot → empyema risk)

    MANAGEMENT¶

    General Principles¶

    Goals: 1. Treat underlying cause 2. Relieve symptoms (dyspnea) 3. Prevent complications (empyema, trapped lung)

    Management by Cause¶

    Cause Management
    CHF Diuretics (Furosemide), afterload reduction (ACEi, ARB), thoracentesis if severe dyspnea
    Parapneumonic (Uncomplicated) Antibiotics alone
    Parapneumonic (Complicated) / Empyema Antibiotics + Chest tube ± Fibrinolytics ± Surgery
    Tuberculosis Anti-TB therapy (2HRZE/4HR) ± Steroids
    Malignancy Pleurodesis (Talc) OR Tunneled catheter (IPC), chemotherapy
    Cirrhosis Diuretics (Spironolactone + Furosemide), salt restriction, consider TIPS if refractory
    Pulmonary Embolism Anticoagulation (effusion usually resolves)
    Chylothorax Low-fat diet / TPN, chest tube, surgery if persistent
    Hemothorax Chest tube, surgery if ongoing bleeding

    Chest Tube Insertion¶

    Indications: - Empyema (frank pus, positive culture) - Complicated parapneumonic effusion (pH <7.20, Glucose <60, LDH >1000) - Hemothorax - Large recurrent effusion (post-pleurodesis)

    Site: 5th intercostal space, mid-axillary line (triangle of safety)

    Size: - Small bore (10-14 Fr): Simple effusion - Large bore (28-32 Fr): Empyema, hemothorax

    Removal: When drainage <100-150 mL/day

    Pleurodesis¶

    Indications: - Recurrent malignant effusion - Recurrent pneumothorax

    Contraindications: - Trapped lung (lung cannot expand → pleurodesis will fail) - Life expectancy <3 months (IPC preferred)

    Agents: - Talc (powder or slurry) - MOST EFFECTIVE (90% success) - Doxycycline, Bleomycin (60-70% success)

    Procedure: - Chest tube drainage until <100-150 mL/day - Instill sclerosant via chest tube - Clamp tube × 2-4 hours (patient changes position to distribute) - Resume drainage

    Complications: Pain, fever, ARDS (rare, with talc)

    COMPLICATIONS¶

    1. Trapped Lung¶

    Definition: Lung unable to expand fully due to fibrous peel on visceral pleura

    Causes: - Chronic effusion (malignant, TB, hemothorax) - Empyema

    Diagnosis: - Persistent pneumothorax after thoracentesis - Non-expandable lung on imaging

    Management: - Tunneled pleural catheter (IPC) - Palliative drainage - Decortication (surgical removal of peel) - If symptomatic and good prognosis

    2. Empyema¶

    See above (Parapneumonic effusion section)

    3. Fibrothorax¶

    Chronic fibrous thickening of pleura → Restrictive lung disease

    Causes: Hemothorax, empyema, TB

    Management: Decortication (if symptomatic)

    4. Re-expansion Pulmonary Edema¶

    Mechanism: Rapid removal of large effusion (>1.5 L) → Capillary leak in re-expanded lung

    Presentation: Dyspnea, cough, hypoxemia, unilateral infiltrates (post-thoracentesis)

    Prevention: Limit drainage to 1-1.5 L per session

    Treatment: Supportive (oxygen, diuretics if needed)

    NEET PG HIGH-YIELD POINTS¶

    Must-Know Facts¶

    1. Most Common Cause of Pleural Effusion: CHF (40%)
    2. Bilateral, Right > Left if unilateral

    3. Transudate

    4. Most Common Exudative Cause: Parapneumonic effusion (20-25%)

    5. Light's Criteria (Exudate if ANY ONE):

    6. Pleural protein / Serum protein >0.5
    7. Pleural LDH / Serum LDH >0.6
    8. Pleural LDH >⅔ upper limit of normal serum LDH

    9. Mnemonic: "0.5 - 0.6 - ⅔"

    10. Physical Exam Triad:

    11. Dullness to percussion (MOST sensitive)
    12. Decreased breath sounds
    13. Decreased tactile fremitus

    14. Requires >300-500 mL

    15. CXR Findings:

    16. 175-500 mL: Blunting of costophrenic angle (PA view)
    17. >500 mL: Meniscus sign

    18. Lateral decubitus: Most sensitive (detects ≥10 mm layering)

    19. Complicated Parapneumonic / Empyema Indicators:

    20. pH <7.20 (MOST IMPORTANT)
    21. Glucose <60 mg/dL
    22. LDH >1000 U/L
    23. Positive Gram stain/culture OR Frank pus

    24. Treatment: Antibiotics + Chest tube drainage

    25. TB Pleural Effusion:

    26. Lymphocytic (>50%)
    27. ADA >40 U/L (90% sensitivity)
    28. Pleural biopsy: BEST test (90% sensitivity) - Granulomas + AFB culture

    29. AFB smear of fluid: <10% yield (LOW)

    30. Low Glucose in Pleural Fluid (<60 mg/dL):

    31. Complicated parapneumonic / Empyema
    32. Rheumatoid arthritis (very low, <30)
    33. Malignancy
    34. TB

    35. Mnemonic: "CREMT" - Complicated parapneumonic, RA, Empyema, Malignancy, TB

    36. High Amylase in Pleural Fluid:

    37. Pancreatitis (LEFT-sided effusion, 70%)

    38. Esophageal rupture

    39. Chylothorax:

      • Milky appearance
      • Triglycerides >110 mg/dL (DIAGNOSTIC)
      • Causes: Trauma (thoracic duct injury), Lymphoma (most common non-traumatic)

    40. Malignant Effusion:

      • Lung cancer (35%) - Most common cause
      • Bloody (50%)
      • Cytology: 60% (1st), 80% (2nd), 90% (3rd)
      • Treatment: Talc pleurodesis (90% success) OR Tunneled catheter (IPC)

    41. Hemothorax:

      • Pleural fluid Hct / Blood Hct >50%
      • Chest tube (large bore, 32-36 Fr)
      • Surgery if initial >1500 mL or ongoing >200 mL/hr

    Common Mistakes to Avoid¶

    1. Diagnosing transudate by protein alone ❌

    2. Must use Light's Criteria (3 parameters)

    3. Treating all parapneumonic effusions with chest tube ❌

    4. Uncomplicated (pH >7.20, Glucose >60) → Antibiotics alone

    5. Complicated (pH <7.20) → Antibiotics + Chest tube

    6. Relying on AFB smear for TB pleural effusion ❌

    7. AFB smear yield <10% (very low)

    8. Use ADA >40 U/L (90% sensitivity) or Pleural biopsy (BEST)

    9. Removing >1.5 L of fluid at once ❌

    10. Risk of re-expansion pulmonary edema

    11. Limit to 1-1.5 L per session

    12. Pleurodesis in trapped lung ❌

    13. Trapped lung (non-expandable) → Pleurodesis will FAIL

    14. Use Tunneled catheter (IPC) instead

    15. Forgetting to check glucose and pH in parapneumonic effusion ❌

    16. pH and Glucose determine need for drainage (NOT just Gram stain/culture)

    2. **P**rotein > 0.5, **L**DH > 0.6, **PL**eural LDH > ⅔ upper limit

    4. C**HF, **H**epatic cirrhosis, renal **F**ailure, **N**ephrotic, starvation (**E), P**eritoneal dialysis, **A**telectasis, constrictive pericarditis (**L)

    6. **C**omplicated parapneumonic, **R**A, **E**mpyema, **M**alignancy, **T**B

    PRACTICE MCQs¶

    Question 1¶

    A 65-year-old man with CHF presents with bilateral pleural effusion. Thoracentesis shows: Pleural protein 2.5 g/dL, Serum protein 6.0 g/dL, Pleural LDH 150 U/L, Serum LDH 300 U/L. What type of effusion is this?

    A. Transudate B. Exudate C. Need more tests D. Empyema

    Answer: A. Transudate

    Explanation: - Apply Light's Criteria (Exudate if ANY ONE of 3 criteria met):

    Criterion This Patient Cutoff Met?
    Pleural protein / Serum protein 2.5 / 6.0 = 0.42 >0.5 ❌
    Pleural LDH / Serum LDH 150 / 300 = 0.5 >0.6 ❌
    Pleural LDH 150 U/L >200 U/L (⅔ of 300) ❌
    • NONE of the criteria met → Transudate

    Clinical Correlation: - CHF is most common cause of transudate - Bilateral effusion consistent with CHF

    Key Point: Must apply ALL 3 Light's criteria; if NONE met → Transudate

    Question 2¶

    A 45-year-old man with pneumonia has pleural effusion. Pleural fluid analysis: pH 7.10, Glucose 30 mg/dL, LDH 1500 U/L, Gram stain negative. What is the most appropriate management?

    A. Antibiotics alone B. Antibiotics + Chest tube drainage C. Antibiotics + Repeat thoracentesis in 48 hours D. Pleurodesis

    Answer: B. Antibiotics + Chest tube drainage

    Explanation: - Diagnosis: Complicated Parapneumonic Effusion

    Parapneumonic Effusion Classification:

    Type pH Glucose LDH Treatment
    Uncomplicated >7.20 >60 <1000 Antibiotics alone
    Complicated <7.20 <60 >1000 Antibiotics + Chest tube
    Empyema <7.00 <40 >1000 Antibiotics + Chest tube ± Surgery

    This Patient: - pH 7.10 (<7.20) ✓ - Glucose 30 (<60) ✓ - LDH 1500 (>1000) ✓ - ALL criteria for complicated parapneumonic met

    Why Chest Tube Needed: - Without drainage, will progress to empyema - Antibiotics alone will NOT clear organized/complicated effusion

    Gram Stain Negative: Does NOT rule out complicated effusion - pH <7.20 is MOST IMPORTANT criterion for drainage decision

    Key Point: pH <7.20 in parapneumonic effusion → Chest tube drainage MANDATORY (regardless of Gram stain)

    Question 3¶

    A 30-year-old woman presents with unilateral pleural effusion. Pleural fluid analysis shows lymphocytic predominance (85% lymphocytes). ADA level is 65 U/L. AFB smear is negative. What is the most likely diagnosis?

    A. Parapneumonic effusion B. Tuberculous pleural effusion C. Malignant pleural effusion D. Rheumatoid pleuritis

    Answer: B. Tuberculous pleural effusion

    Explanation: - Classic Features of TB Pleural Effusion: - Unilateral ✓ - Lymphocytic predominant (>50%, often >80%) ✓ (85%) - ADA >40 U/L ✓ (65 U/L)

    ADA (Adenosine Deaminase): - >40 U/L: 90% sensitivity, 85% specificity for TB - Very high ADA (>100 U/L): Strongly suggests TB

    AFB Smear Negative: - Expected - AFB smear of pleural fluid has <10% yield (very low) - Does NOT rule out TB

    Diagnostic Approach for TB Pleural Effusion: 1. ADA >40 U/L → Highly suggestive (start treatment in endemic areas) 2. Pleural biopsy (if diagnosis uncertain): - Granulomas on histology - AFB culture of biopsy tissue (20-40% of fluid culture, 70-80% of biopsy culture positive)

    Why NOT Other Options: - Parapneumonic (A): PMN predominant (acute), ADA not typically >40 - Malignancy (C): Possible (lymphocytic), but ADA usually <40 (unless lymphoma) - RA pleuritis (D): Low glucose (<30), low pH, not typically high ADA

    Treatment: 2HRZE / 4HR (same as pulmonary TB)

    Key Point: Lymphocytic effusion + ADA >40 U/L = TB (in endemic areas)

    Question 4¶

    A patient with lung cancer has recurrent large pleural effusion causing dyspnea. Cytology is positive for adenocarcinoma. What is the most appropriate definitive management?

    A. Repeat therapeutic thoracentesis as needed B. Tunneled pleural catheter (IPC) C. Talc pleurodesis D. Decortication

    Answer: C. Talc pleurodesis

    Explanation: - Diagnosis: Malignant pleural effusion (cytology-proven)

    Management Options for Malignant Effusion:

    Method Indication Success Pros/Cons
    Therapeutic thoracentesis Temporary relief N/A Recurs in days-weeks, NOT definitive
    Talc pleurodesis Recurrent effusion, good lung expansion 90% MOST EFFECTIVE, requires hospitalization
    Tunneled catheter (IPC) Trapped lung, poor prognosis 70-90% Outpatient drainage, good for trapped lung
    Chemotherapy Chemosensitive tumors Variable Adjunct, not for immediate symptom control

    This Patient: - Recurrent, symptomatic → Needs definitive management - Lung presumably expandable (no mention of trapped lung) - Best option: Talc pleurodesis (90% success, prevents recurrence)

    Pleurodesis Contraindications: - Trapped lung (lung cannot expand → pleurodesis will fail) - Life expectancy <3 months (IPC preferred)

    Why NOT Other Options: - Repeat thoracentesis (A): NOT definitive, requires frequent procedures - IPC (B): Good option, but pleurodesis more effective if lung expandable - Decortication (D): For trapped lung/empyema, NOT routine malignant effusion

    Key Point: Recurrent malignant effusion + expandable lung → Talc pleurodesis (MOST effective)

    Question 5¶

    A 50-year-old alcoholic presents with severe abdominal pain radiating to back. CXR shows LEFT-sided pleural effusion. Pleural fluid amylase is 800 U/L (serum 300 U/L). What is the diagnosis?

    A. Parapneumonic effusion B. Pancreatitis-associated effusion C. Tuberculous effusion D. Esophageal rupture

    Answer: B. Pancreatitis-associated effusion

    Explanation: - Classic Triad: - Abdominal pain (pancreatitis) ✓ - LEFT-sided effusion (70% in pancreatitis) ✓ - High pleural fluid amylase (>serum) ✓

    Mechanism: - Pancreatic enzymes cross diaphragm via lymphatics or direct communication - Left-sided because pancreatic tail is on left

    Pleural Fluid Amylase: - Elevated (typically >serum) - Pleural/Serum amylase ratio >1

    Other Causes of High Pleural Fluid Amylase: - Esophageal rupture (but would have food particles, low pH <6.0, history of vomiting/instrumentation) - Malignancy (lung, ovarian)

    Why Pancreatitis (not Esophageal Rupture): - Clinical presentation: Abdominal pain (pancreatitis) vs vomiting/chest pain (rupture) - Alcoholic → High risk for pancreatitis - No mention of vomiting, instrumentation

    Management: - Treat pancreatitis (NPO, IV fluids, pain control) - Effusion typically resolves with treatment - Thoracentesis if large and symptomatic

    Key Point: LEFT-sided effusion + High amylase = Pancreatitis (most common) or Esophageal rupture

    Question 6¶

    A patient has pleural effusion with milky appearance. Triglyceride level is 150 mg/dL. What is the diagnosis?

    A. Empyema B. Chylothorax C. Pseudochylothorax D. Tuberculous effusion

    Answer: B. Chylothorax

    Explanation: - Chylothorax: Lymphatic fluid (chyle) in pleural space

    Diagnostic Criteria: - Milky/opalescent appearance ✓ - Triglycerides >110 mg/dL ✓ (150 mg/dL) - Chylomicrons present (lipoprotein electrophoresis)

    Triglyceride Levels: - >110 mg/dL: Diagnostic of chylothorax - 50-110 mg/dL: Indeterminate (check for chylomicrons) - <50 mg/dL: Rules out chylothorax

    Causes: - Trauma/Surgery (thoracic duct injury): Esophagectomy, cardiac surgery - Malignancy: Lymphoma (MOST COMMON non-traumatic) - Congenital (lymphangiomatosis)

    Pseudochylothorax: - Cholesterol crystals (not chylomicrons) - Shimmering appearance (not truly milky) - Low triglycerides (<50 mg/dL) - High cholesterol (>200 mg/dL) - Chronic effusion (TB, RA)

    Empyema: - Purulent (not milky) - PMN predominant, positive Gram stain/culture

    Treatment of Chylothorax: - Conservative (50% resolve): - Low-fat diet or NPO + TPN (↓ chyle production) - Chest tube drainage - Surgical (if persistent >2 weeks): - Thoracic duct ligation

    Key Point: Milky fluid + Triglycerides >110 mg/dL = Chylothorax

    Question 7¶

    A patient with empyema has chest tube draining purulent fluid. Despite 7 days of antibiotics and chest tube, fever persists and CXR shows loculated effusion. What is the next best step?

    A. Continue current management B. Add intrapleural fibrinolytics (tPA + DNase) C. Remove chest tube D. Start antifungal therapy

    Answer: B. Add intrapleural fibrinolytics (tPA + DNase)

    Explanation: - Diagnosis: Loculated empyema not responding to antibiotics + chest tube alone

    Empyema Stages: 1. Exudative (0-3 days): Sterile, free-flowing → Antibiotics 2. Fibrinopurulent (3-7 days): Bacterial invasion, loculations → Chest tube 3. Organizing (>7 days): Thick peel → May need surgery

    This Patient: Stage 2-3 (loculated, not improving)

    Intrapleural Fibrinolytics: - tPA (tissue Plasminogen Activator) + DNase (Deoxyribonuclease) - Breaks down loculations, improves drainage - MIST-2 Trial: ↓ Need for surgery, ↓ hospital stay

    Regimen: - tPA 10 mg + DNase 5 mg via chest tube - BID × 3 days (6 total doses) - Clamp tube × 1 hour after instillation, then drain

    Evidence: Superior to either agent alone

    If Fibrinolytics Fail: - VATS decortication (remove fibrous peel) - Open thoracotomy (if VATS not feasible)

    Why NOT Other Options: - Continue current (A): Not improving, need escalation - Remove chest tube (C): Incorrect, still has infection - Antifungal (D): No indication (bacterial empyema)

    Key Point: Loculated empyema not improving with chest tube → Add intrapleural fibrinolytics (tPA + DNase)

    Question 8¶

    What volume of pleural fluid is required to cause blunting of costophrenic angle on PA chest X-ray?

    A. 50-75 mL B. 175-500 mL C. 500-1000 mL D. >1000 mL

    Answer: B. 175-500 mL

    Explanation: - CXR Detection of Pleural Effusion:

    Volume Finding
    <50 mL Not visible on standard PA
    50-175 mL Blunting of costophrenic angle on lateral view
    175-500 mL Blunting of costophrenic angle on PA view
    >500 mL Meniscus sign, homogeneous opacity
    >1000 mL Complete opacification, mediastinal shift

    Most Sensitive CXR View: Lateral decubitus - Detects as little as 5-10 mL (if layers out) - Confirms free-flowing vs loculated

    Clinical Significance: - <300-500 mL: Usually asymptomatic, may not be palpable - >500 mL: Symptomatic (dyspnea), detectable on exam (dullness)

    Key Point: 175-500 mL needed for blunting of costophrenic angle on PA CXR

    Question 9¶

    A patient with RA has pleural effusion with pH 7.10, Glucose 20 mg/dL, and negative cultures. What is the most likely diagnosis?

    A. Parapneumonic effusion B. Rheumatoid pleuritis C. Tuberculous effusion D. Empyema

    Answer: B. Rheumatoid pleuritis

    Explanation: - Rheumatoid Pleuritis: Occurs in 5% of RA patients

    Characteristic Pleural Fluid Findings: - Very low glucose (<30 mg/dL, often <20) ✓ (20 mg/dL) - Very low pH (<7.20, often <7.10) ✓ (7.10) - Negative cultures ✓ - Low complement (C3, C4) - Cholesterol crystals - Lymphocytic or PMN predominance

    Mechanism: Immune complex deposition, complement consumption → Low glucose

    Other Causes of Low Glucose Pleural Fluid (<60 mg/dL): - Complicated parapneumonic / Empyema - Rheumatoid pleuritis (LOWEST glucose, <30) - Malignancy - TB - Lupus pleuritis

    Why NOT Other Options: - Parapneumonic (A): Would have clinical features of pneumonia (fever, productive cough, infiltrate on CXR) - TB (C): Low glucose possible, but usually not this low; ADA would be elevated - Empyema (D): Would have purulent fluid, positive Gram stain/culture

    Clinical Correlation: RA patient + Very low glucose (<30) + Very low pH + Negative cultures = Rheumatoid pleuritis

    Treatment: - NSAIDs, Corticosteroids - Treat underlying RA

    Key Point: RA + Very low glucose (<30) + Low pH + Negative cultures = Rheumatoid pleuritis

    Question 10¶

    A trauma patient has chest tube draining 1800 mL of blood initially. What is the most appropriate next step?

    A. Continue chest tube drainage and observe B. Urgent thoracotomy C. Remove chest tube D. Add intrapleural fibrinolytics

    Answer: B. Urgent thoracotomy

    Explanation: - Diagnosis: Hemothorax (Pleural fluid Hct/Blood Hct >50%)

    Indications for Urgent Thoracotomy (Surgery):

    Absolute: - Initial chest tube output >1500 mL ✓ (1800 mL in this case) - Ongoing bleeding >200 mL/hr × 2-4 hours - Hemodynamic instability despite resuscitation

    Rationale: - Initial drainage >1500 mL suggests major vascular injury - Requires surgical exploration and repair - Continued observation → Risk of exsanguination

    Chest Tube in Hemothorax: - Large bore (32-36 Fr) - Purposes: - Remove blood (prevent organized hemothorax) - Monitor ongoing bleeding - Re-expand lung

    If Output <1500 mL Initially and <200 mL/hr ongoing: - Continue chest tube drainage + observation - Surgery if bleeding persists or increases

    Why NOT Other Options: - Observe (A): Incorrect - massive initial output requires surgery - Remove tube (C): Incorrect - would re-accumulate blood - Fibrinolytics (D): For empyema/loculations, NOT acute hemothorax

    Key Point: Hemothorax with initial drainage >1500 mL → Urgent thoracotomy

    SUMMARY¶

    Pleural Effusion is abnormal fluid accumulation in the pleural space: - Most common cause: CHF (transudate, bilateral) - Most common exudative cause: Parapneumonic effusion

    Diagnosis: - Light's Criteria (Exudate if ANY ONE: Protein ratio >0.5, LDH ratio >0.6, Pleural LDH >⅔ ULN) - Physical exam: Dullness, ↓ breath sounds, ↓ fremitus (need >300-500 mL) - CXR: Blunting (175-500 mL on PA), Lateral decubitus most sensitive - Thoracentesis: MANDATORY for unknown etiology

    Key Fluid Findings: - pH <7.20, Glucose <60: Complicated parapneumonic → Chest tube - Lymphocytic, ADA >40: TB → Anti-TB therapy - Bloody, cytology+: Malignancy → Pleurodesis or IPC - Milky, Triglycerides >110: Chylothorax - Very low glucose (<30), RA: Rheumatoid pleuritis - High amylase, LEFT-sided: Pancreatitis

    Management: - Transudate: Treat underlying cause (diuretics for CHF) - Complicated parapneumonic: Antibiotics + Chest tube ± Fibrinolytics - Malignant: Talc pleurodesis (90% success) or IPC - Hemothorax: Chest tube; surgery if >1500 mL initially or >200 mL/hr ongoing
