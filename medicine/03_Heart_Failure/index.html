    HEART FAILURE - NEET PG Study Material¶

    Definition¶

    Heart Failure (HF): Clinical syndrome resulting from structural or functional cardiac disorder that impairs ability of ventricle to fill or eject blood.

    Classification¶

    By Ejection Fraction¶

    Type EF Characteristics
    HFrEF <40% Systolic dysfunction, dilated LV
    HFmrEF 40-49% Mid-range
    HFpEF ≥50% Diastolic dysfunction, preserved EF

    NEET PG: HFrEF = Systolic dysfunction, HFpEF = Diastolic dysfunction

    By Time Course¶

    • Acute HF: Rapid onset (hours-days)
    • Chronic HF: Gradual onset (months-years)

    By Side Affected¶

    • Left HF: Pulmonary congestion
    • Right HF: Systemic congestion
    • Biventricular: Both

    NYHA Functional Classification¶

    Class Symptoms
    I No limitation, ordinary activity does not cause symptoms
    II Slight limitation, comfortable at rest, ordinary activity causes symptoms
    III Marked limitation, less than ordinary activity causes symptoms
    IV Symptoms at rest, unable to carry out any activity

    NEET PG: NYHA classification used for symptom assessment and treatment decisions

    Etiology¶

    Left Heart Failure (Most Common)¶

    1. Ischemic heart disease (60-70%) - MOST COMMON
    2. Hypertension
    3. Valvular disease (AS, AR, MR)
    4. Dilated cardiomyopathy
    5. Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy

    Right Heart Failure¶

    1. Left heart failure (most common cause)
    2. Chronic lung disease (Cor pulmonale)
    3. Pulmonary HTN
    4. Tricuspid/Pulmonary valve disease
    5. RV infarction

    NEET PG: IHD is most common cause of HF worldwide

    Neurohormonal Activation¶

    RAAS: - ↑ Angiotensin II → Vasoconstriction, ↑ Afterload - ↑ Aldosterone → Na+ & water retention, fibrosis

    Sympathetic: - ↑ Norepinephrine → ↑ HR, vasoconstriction, myocyte toxicity

    ADH: - Water retention → Hyponatremia

    Natriuretic Peptides (Compensatory): - BNP (B-type natriuretic peptide) → Vasodilation, natriuresis - ANP (Atrial natriuretic peptide)

    NEET PG: BNP released in response to ventricular stretch

    Clinical Features¶

    Left Heart Failure - Pulmonary Congestion¶

    Symptoms: - Dyspnea (exertional → at rest) - Orthopnea: Dyspnea when lying flat (ask: how many pillows?) - Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea (PND): Sudden nighttime dyspnea - Fatigue, weakness - Cough (dry or frothy pink sputum) - Nocturia

    Signs: - S3 gallop (ventricular gallop) - Hallmark - S4 (if diastolic dysfunction) - Displaced apex (laterally and downward) - Tachycardia - Bibasilar crackles (pulmonary edema) - Pleural effusion

    NEET PG High-Yield: - S3 gallop: Most specific sign of HF - Orthopnea + PND: Classic for LVF - Laterally displaced apex: LV dilatation

    Right Heart Failure - Systemic Congestion¶

    Symptoms: - Peripheral edema (ankle/leg) - Abdominal distension (ascites) - Anorexia, nausea - RUQ pain (hepatic congestion)

    Signs: - ↑ JVP (>4 cm above sternal angle) - Hepatojugular reflux (positive) - Hepatomegaly (tender, pulsatile) - Ascites - Peripheral pitting edema - Parasternal heave (RV hypertrophy)

    NEET PG: ↑ JVP + hepatomegaly + edema = RHF triad

    Investigations¶

    ECG¶

    • Not diagnostic but identifies underlying cause
    • LVH, Q waves (prior MI), arrhythmias (AF)

    Chest X-ray¶

    Findings in Pulmonary Edema (mnemonic: ABCDE): - A**lveolar edema (bat's wing appearance) - **B-lines (Kerley B lines - interstitial edema) - **C**ardiomegaly (CTR >0.5) - **D**ilated upper lobe vessels (cephalization) - **E**ffusion (pleural)

    NEET PG: Cardiomegaly = CTR >0.5 on PA chest X-ray

    Echocardiography - MOST IMPORTANT¶

    Provides: - Ejection fraction (diagnostic, prognostic) - Chamber size, wall thickness - Regional wall motion abnormalities - Valvular function - Diastolic function

    Normal EF: 55-65% HFrEF: EF <40% HFpEF: EF ≥50%

    BNP (B-type Natriuretic Peptide)¶

    Use: Diagnosis + Prognosis

    BNP Level Interpretation
    <100 pg/mL HF unlikely
    100-400 pg/mL Possible HF
    >400 pg/mL HF likely

    NT-proBNP (alternative): - <300 pg/mL: HF unlikely - >450 pg/mL (<50 years) or >900 pg/mL (≥50 years): HF likely

    NEET PG: - BNP >100 pg/mL: Suggests HF - Elevated in: HF, renal failure, PE, AF - Low in: Obesity (false negative)

    Other Tests¶

    • CBC: Anemia (exacerbates HF)
    • Renal function: Baseline before ACE-I/diuretics
    • Electrolytes: Hyponatremia (poor prognosis)
    • Liver function: Hepatic congestion
    • TSH: Rule out thyroid dysfunction
    • Troponin: If ACS suspected
    • Coronary angiography: If IHD suspected

    Management of Chronic HFrEF¶

    Goals¶

    1. Improve symptoms
    2. Reduce hospitalizations
    3. Improve survival

    Lifestyle Modifications¶

    • Salt restriction: <2g/day
    • Fluid restriction: 1.5-2 L/day (if severe HF)
    • Weight monitoring: Daily (report gain >2kg in 3 days)
    • Exercise (cardiac rehabilitation)
    • Avoid NSAIDs, alcohol
    • Smoking cessation

    Pharmacological Therapy¶

    Drugs that IMPROVE SURVIVAL (Use in ALL patients)¶

    1. ACE Inhibitors (1st line)

    Examples: Enalapril, Ramipril, Lisinopril

    Benefits: - ↓ Mortality (20-30%) - ↓ Hospitalizations - ↓ LV remodeling

    Mechanism: Block RAAS

    Dose: Start low, titrate up

    Adverse Effects: - Cough (10-15%) - Due to bradykinin accumulation - Hypotension - Hyperkalemia - Renal dysfunction - Angioedema (rare)

    Contraindications: - Bilateral renal artery stenosis - Pregnancy - Angioedema history - K+ >5.5 mEq/L

    NEET PG: If ACE-I intolerant (due to cough) → ARB (Valsartan, Losartan)

    2. Beta-blockers (Start AFTER ACE-I)

    Agents: Carvedilol, Metoprolol succinate, Bisoprolol

    Benefits: - ↓ Mortality (30-35%) - ↓ Sudden death - ↑ EF

    Mechanism: Block sympathetic overactivity

    Important: - Start at low dose, titrate slowly - May worsen symptoms initially (start when stable) - Do NOT stop abruptly (rebound)

    Contraindications: - Bradycardia (<50 bpm) - Heart block (2nd/3rd degree) - Severe asthma - Cardiogenic shock

    NEET PG: Beta-blockers reduce mortality in HFrEF (proven: Carvedilol, Metoprolol succinate, Bisoprolol)

    3. Mineralocorticoid Receptor Antagonists (MRA)

    Agents: Spironolactone, Eplerenone

    Indications: - EF ≤35% - NYHA II-IV despite ACE-I + Beta-blocker

    Benefits: - ↓ Mortality (30%) - ↓ Hospitalization - Reduce fibrosis

    Adverse Effects: - Hyperkalemia (monitor K+) - Gynecomastia (spironolactone > eplerenone) - Renal dysfunction

    Contraindications: - K+ >5 mEq/L - Creatinine >2.5 mg/dL

    NEET PG: Spironolactone causes gynecomastia (anti-androgen effect); Eplerenone does not

    4. ARNI (Angiotensin Receptor-Neprilysin Inhibitor)

    Agent: Sacubitril/Valsartan (Entresto)

    Mechanism: - Valsartan: ARB (blocks AT1 receptor) - Sacubitril: Neprilysin inhibitor (↑ natriuretic peptides)

    Indication: HFrEF (EF ≤40%), NYHA II-IV, instead of ACE-I/ARB

    Benefits: - ↓ Mortality (better than ACE-I alone) - ↓ Hospitalization

    Important: - Do NOT use with ACE-I (risk of angioedema) - Wash-out period: 36 hours after stopping ACE-I

    NEET PG: ARNI superior to ACE-I in HFrEF, but cannot combine with ACE-I

    5. SGLT2 Inhibitors

    Agents: Dapagliflozin, Empagliflozin

    Benefits: - ↓ Mortality - ↓ HF hospitalization - Works in diabetics AND non-diabetics

    Mechanism: Inhibit SGLT2 in kidney → Glycosuria, natriuresis

    NEET PG: SGLT2i beneficial in HFrEF regardless of diabetes status

    Drugs that IMPROVE SYMPTOMS (Add as needed)¶

    1. Diuretics (Loop > Thiazide)

    Agents: Furosemide, Bumetanide, Torsemide

    Indication: Fluid overload (edema, dyspnea)

    Benefits: ↓ Symptoms, ↓ Hospitalizations (NO mortality benefit)

    Monitoring: Daily weight, electrolytes, renal function

    Adverse Effects: - Hypokalemia - Hypomagnesemia - Hyponatremia - Renal dysfunction - Ototoxicity (high doses)

    NEET PG: Diuretics improve symptoms but NOT mortality

    2. Digoxin

    Mechanism: ↑ Contractility (inhibits Na-K ATPase)

    Indications: - HFrEF with atrial fibrillation (rate control) - Persistent symptoms despite optimal therapy

    Benefits: ↓ Symptoms, ↓ Hospitalization (NO mortality benefit)

    Therapeutic level: 0.5-0.9 ng/mL (lower target in HF)

    Toxicity: Nausea, visual changes (yellow halos), arrhythmias

    NEET PG: Digoxin does NOT improve survival in HF

    3. Hydralazine + Isosorbide Dinitrate

    Indication: - African-American patients (with standard therapy) - ACE-I/ARB/ARNI intolerant

    Benefits: ↓ Mortality (in African-Americans)

    4. Ivabradine

    Mechanism: Inhibits If channel in SA node → ↓ HR

    Indication: Sinus rhythm, HR >70 bpm despite beta-blocker

    NEET PG: Ivabradine for HR control if HR remains >70 despite max beta-blocker

    Device Therapy¶

    1. ICD (Implantable Cardioverter-Defibrillator)

    Indication: - EF ≤35% despite optimal medical therapy - Prior cardiac arrest/sustained VT - Primary prevention of sudden cardiac death

    Benefits: ↓ Sudden cardiac death

    NEET PG: ICD indicated if EF ≤35% (NYHA II-III) despite 3 months optimal therapy

    2. CRT (Cardiac Resynchronization Therapy)

    Indication: - EF ≤35% - NYHA III-IV despite optimal therapy - LBBB with QRS ≥150 ms (sinus rhythm)

    Mechanism: Biventricular pacing → Synchronizes contraction

    Benefits: ↑ EF, ↓ Symptoms, ↓ Mortality

    NEET PG: CRT indicated in HFrEF + LBBB (wide QRS ≥150 ms)

    Surgical Options¶

    1. Coronary Revascularization (PCI/CABG) - If IHD underlying cause + viable myocardium

    2. Valve Repair/Replacement - If significant valvular disease

    3. LV Assist Device (LVAD) - Bridge to transplant - Destination therapy (not transplant candidate)

    4. Heart Transplantation - End-stage HF refractory to medical therapy - <65 years, no contraindications

    Management of HFpEF (Preserved EF)¶

    No proven therapies to reduce mortality

    Goals: Control symptoms, manage comorbidities

    Treatment: 1. Diuretics: For congestion 2. BP control: ACE-I/ARB, CCB 3. Rate control: If AF (beta-blocker, digoxin) 4. SGLT2 inhibitors: Emerging evidence (dapagliflozin) 5. Treat comorbidities: HTN, AF, DM, obesity, sleep apnea

    Avoid: Beta-blockers (may worsen diastolic dysfunction), high-dose diuretics

    NEET PG: HFpEF management = Symptom control + Treat comorbidities (no mortality benefit proven)

    Acute Decompensated Heart Failure (ADHF)¶

    Clinical Presentation¶

    • Acute dyspnea at rest
    • Pulmonary edema
    • ↑ JVP
    • Hypoxia

    Management¶

    Immediate: 1. Position: Sit upright (↓ preload) 2. Oxygen: Maintain SpO2 >90% 3. IV loop diuretics: Furosemide 20-40 mg IV (↑ dose if already on oral diuretics) 4. Nitroglycerin: IV infusion (vasodilator, ↓ preload/afterload)

    If severe/pulmonary edema: 5. Non-invasive ventilation: BiPAP/CPAP 6. Morphine: 2-4 mg IV (if severe dyspnea) - Use cautiously

    NEET PG: Acute HF = Upright position + O2 + IV diuretics + Nitroglycerin

    Inotropes (if cardiogenic shock, hypotension): - Dobutamine: β1 agonist (↑ contractility) - Milrinone: Phosphodiesterase inhibitor - Norepinephrine: If persistent hypotension

    Ultrafiltration: If refractory to diuretics

    Complications of Heart Failure¶

    1. Cardiogenic shock
    2. Arrhythmias: AF (most common), VT/VF
    3. Thromboembolic events: Stroke, PE (anticoagulate if AF or LV thrombus)
    4. Renal dysfunction (Cardiorenal syndrome)
    5. Hepatic dysfunction (Cardiac cirrhosis)
    6. Sudden cardiac death

    Prognosis¶

    Poor prognostic factors: - NYHA IV - Low EF (<20%) - Hyponatremia (Na <135) - High BNP - Renal dysfunction - Cachexia - Frequent hospitalizations

    NEET PG: Hyponatremia is poor prognostic marker in HF

    Practice MCQs¶

    1. Most common cause of HF worldwide? Answer: Ischemic heart disease

    2. Most specific sign of heart failure? Answer: S3 gallop

    3. Investigation of choice for HF? Answer: Echocardiography (assess EF)

    4. BNP level diagnostic of HF? Answer: >100 pg/mL (>400 highly suggestive)

    5. Drugs that reduce mortality in HFrEF? Answer: ACE-I, Beta-blockers, MRA, ARNI, SGLT2i

    6. Which beta-blocker proven in HF? Answer: Carvedilol, Metoprolol succinate, Bisoprolol

    7. Indication for ICD in HF? Answer: EF ≤35% despite 3 months optimal therapy

    8. Drug causing gynecomastia in HF? Answer: Spironolactone

    Memory Aids¶

    HFrEF Drugs (Mortality Benefit)¶

    "ACE BETA MARS" - ACE inhibitor - BETA blocker - **M**ineralocorticoid antagonist (MRA) - **A**RNI - **S**GLT2 inhibitor

    Chest X-ray in Pulmonary Edema¶

    "ABCDE" - A**lveolar edema - **B-lines (Kerley) - **C**ardiomegaly - **D**ilated upper lobe vessels - **E**ffusion

    Acute HF Management¶

    "LMNOP" - **L**oop diuretics - **M**orphine - **N**itrates - **O**xygen - **P**osition (upright)

    Revision Checklist¶

    • Know difference between HFrEF and HFpEF
    • Learn NYHA classification
    • Understand pathophysiology of HF
    • Know clinical features of LVF vs RVF
    • Learn all drugs that reduce mortality
    • Understand indications for ICD and CRT
    • Know management of acute HF
    • Memorize BNP levels
    • Learn chest X-ray findings
    • Practice 40+ MCQs on heart failure
