    Overview & Classification¶

    Definition¶

    Cardiomyopathies are diseases of the myocardium associated with mechanical and/or electrical dysfunction, usually with inappropriate ventricular hypertrophy or dilation, and often leading to cardiovascular death or heart failure.

    Classification¶

    Primary Cardiomyopathies: - Genetic: - Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM) - Arrhythmogenic RV Cardiomyopathy (ARVC) - Left Ventricular Non-compaction - Mitochondrial myopathies - Acquired: - Takotsubo (Stress) cardiomyopathy - Peripartum cardiomyopathy - Myocarditis

    Secondary Cardiomyopathies: - Ischemic (CAD) - Most common cause of DCM - Hypertensive - Valvular - Toxic (Alcohol, Chemotherapy - Doxorubicin) - Infiltrative (Amyloidosis, Sarcoidosis, Hemochromatosis) - Endocrine (Diabetes, Thyroid, Acromegaly) - Nutritional (Thiamine deficiency - Beriberi, Selenium)

    Functional Classification¶

    ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      FUNCTIONAL TYPES OF CARDIOMYOPATHY                   │
├──────────────────┬────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ 1. DILATED (DCM) │ • Dilated ventricles (LV > RV)         │
│                  │ • ↓ Systolic function (↓ EF <40%)      │
│                  │ • Thin walls                           │
│                  │ • Heart failure (HFrEF)                │
├──────────────────┼────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ 2. HYPERTROPHIC  │ • Thickened LV walls (>15 mm)          │
│    (HCM)         │ • Small LV cavity                      │

│                  │ • +/- LV outflow obstruction           │
├──────────────────┼────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ 3. RESTRICTIVE   │ • Normal/Mildly thickened walls        │

    Dilated Cardiomyopathy (DCM)¶

    Definition¶

    LV/biventricular dilation and systolic dysfunction (EF <40%) without CAD, HTN, valvular, or congenital disease.

    Etiology¶

    "ABCDEFGHI" 

    A - Alcohol (Most common reversible cause)
B - Beriberi (Thiamine deficiency)
C - Chagas disease, Cocaine, Chemotherapy (Doxorubicin)
D - Diabetes
E - Endocrine (Thyroid, Acromegaly, Pheochromocytoma)
F - Familial/Genetic (20-35%)
G - Giant cell myocarditis
H - Hemochromatosis
I - Idiopathic (25-30%) - Most common
   Ischemic (Post-MI) - Most common secondary cause
   Inflammatory (Myocarditis - Viral, Autoimmune)

    Genetic DCM: - 20-35% of cases - Autosomal dominant (most common) - Genes: TTN (Titin) - most common, LMNA, MYH7

    Peripartum Cardiomyopathy: - Last month of pregnancy or first 5 months postpartum - Risk factors: Multiparity, Advanced age, African descent - May recover (50%)

    Pathophysiology¶

    Myocardial Damage
        ↓
↓ Contractility
        ↓
↓ Stroke Volume, ↓ Ejection Fraction
        ↓
↑ End-Systolic Volume (incomplete emptying)
        ↓
LV Dilation (Eccentric hypertrophy)
        ↓
Mitral Annular Dilation → Functional MR
        ↓
↑ Wall Stress (Laplace: Stress = Pressure × Radius)
        ↓
Further ↓ Contractility, Neurohormonal activation
        ↓
HEART FAILURE (HFrEF)

    Complications: - Heart failure (systolic - HFrEF) - Arrhythmias (AF, VT) - Thromboembolism (LV thrombus) - Sudden cardiac death

    Clinical Features¶

    Symptoms: - Dyspnea (exertional → rest) - Orthopnea, PND - Fatigue, weakness - Peripheral edema - Chest pain (rare - unlike ischemic)

    Signs: - Displaced apex (laterally, downward) - S3 gallop (volume overload) - Mitral regurgitation murmur (functional) - Elevated JVP - Peripheral edema, ascites - Low pulse pressure (low CO)

    Investigations¶

    ECG: - LVH, LBBB - AF, PVCs, NSVT - Q waves (if ischemic cause)

    Chest X-ray: - Cardiomegaly (Cardiothoracic ratio >50%) - Pulmonary edema - Pleural effusions

    Echocardiography - DIAGNOSTIC: 

    ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      ECHO FINDINGS IN DCM                          │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ • Dilated LV (LVEDD >55-60 mm)                     │
│ • ↓ Ejection Fraction (<40%)                       │
│ • Global hypokinesis                               │
│ • Thin walls (unlike HCM)                          │
│ • Dilated atria                                    │
│ • Functional MR (mitral annular dilation)          │
│ • LV thrombus (if present)                         │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Cardiac MRI: - Best for assessment of myocardium - Late gadolinium enhancement (LGE): - Mid-wall pattern: Non-ischemic DCM - Subendocardial pattern: Ischemic cardiomyopathy - Quantify EF, volumes

    Coronary Angiography: - Rule out ischemic cause (most important) - Recommended in all patients with new DCM (age >40 or risk factors)

    Lab Tests: - BNP/NT-proBNP: Elevated (heart failure severity) - Thyroid function: Rule out thyroid disease - Ferritin, Transferrin saturation: Hemochromatosis - Autoantibodies: Autoimmune myocarditis (Anti-Ro, Anti-La) - Genetic testing: If family history

    Endomyocardial Biopsy: - Rarely needed - If suspected: Giant cell myocarditis, Sarcoidosis, Amyloidosis

    Management¶

    Treat Underlying Cause: - Alcohol: Abstinence - Thyroid disease: Correct hypo/hyperthyroidism - Hemochromatosis: Phlebotomy, Chelation - Myocarditis: Immunosuppression (if giant cell or autoimmune)

    Heart Failure Management (Standard HFrEF Therapy):

    "ABCD" 

    A - ACE inhibitor/ARB (or ARNI - Sacubitril/Valsartan)
    • ↓ Mortality, ↓ Hospitalization
    • Titrate to target doses

B - Beta-blocker (Carvedilol, Bisoprolol, Metoprolol succinate)
    • ↓ Mortality
    • Start low, titrate slowly

C - "C"oagulation (Anticoagulation if AF, LV thrombus)

D - Diuretics (Furosemide - for volume overload)
    • Does NOT reduce mortality

Additional:
- Aldosterone antagonist (Spironolactone, Eplerenone) if EF ≤35%
- SGLT2 inhibitor (Dapagliflozin, Empagliflozin) - New standard
- Ivabradine (if HR >70 on max β-blocker)
- Hydralazine + Isosorbide dinitrate (African Americans or if ACEI/ARB intolerant)

    Device Therapy:

    ICD (Implantable Cardioverter-Defibrillator): - Primary prevention: EF ≤35% despite 3 months optimal medical therapy - Secondary prevention: Survived cardiac arrest, Sustained VT

    CRT (Cardiac Resynchronization Therapy - Biventricular Pacing): - Indications: - EF ≤35% - LBBB with QRS ≥150 ms - NYHA class II-IV despite optimal therapy - Improves symptoms, ↓ Mortality

    Advanced Therapies: - LV Assist Device (LVAD): Bridge to transplant or destination therapy - Heart Transplant: End-stage refractory HF

    Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM)¶

    Definition¶

    Inappropriate LV hypertrophy (wall thickness ≥15 mm) in the absence of loading conditions (HTN, AS) that would account for it.

    Most common genetic cardiovascular disease (1 in 500)

    Genetics¶

    • Autosomal dominant (most cases)
    • Mutations in sarcomeric protein genes:
    • β-Myosin heavy chain (MYH7) - Most common
    • Myosin binding protein C (MYBPC3)
    • Troponin T, I
    • α-Tropomyosin

    Pathophysiology¶

    Asymmetric Septal Hypertrophy (Most common - 70%): - Septal thickness >> posterior wall - ± LV outflow tract obstruction (LVOTO)

    Mechanism of LVOTO: 

    Hypertrophied septum
        +
Systolic Anterior Motion (SAM) of mitral valve
        ↓
Narrowing of LVOT
        ↓
Dynamic Obstruction (worsens during systole)
        ↓
↑ Gradient across LVOT (>30 mmHg)

    Factors ↑ LVOTO (Worsening Gradient): - ↓ Preload: Dehydration, Valsalva, Standing - ↓ Afterload: Vasodilators - ↑ Contractility: Exercise, Inotropes

    Factors ↓ LVOTO (Improving Gradient): - ↑ Preload: Fluids, Leg raise - ↑ Afterload: Squatting, Handgrip - ↓ Contractility: β-blockers

    Diastolic Dysfunction: - Stiff, non-compliant LV (thick walls) - Impaired relaxation - ↑ Filling pressures → Dyspnea

    Myocardial Ischemia: - Imbalance: ↑ O₂ demand (massive hypertrophy), ↓ O₂ supply (small vessel disease, compression during systole) - → Angina (even without CAD)

    Arrhythmias: - AF (common - 20-25%) - VT/VF → Sudden Cardiac Death (SCD)

    Clinical Features¶

    Often Asymptomatic (discovered incidentally or family screening)

    Symptoms: - Dyspnea (exertional) - Most common symptom - Due to diastolic dysfunction (HFpEF) - Angina (exertional) - ↓ Coronary flow reserve - Syncope/Presyncope (exertional) - LVOTO worsens with exercise - Arrhythmias - Palpitations (AF, VT) - Sudden cardiac death (Often 1st presentation - young athletes)

    Signs:

    Palpation: - Double/Triple apical impulse (Forceful atrial contraction) - Systolic thrill

    Auscultation:

    ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      HCM WITH LVOTO MURMUR                         │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ • HARSH, CRESCENDO-DECRESCENDO SYSTOLIC            │
│ • Best heard at LEFT STERNAL BORDER (LLSB)         │
│ • DOES NOT radiate to carotids (unlike AS)         │
│ • ↑ With Valsalva, Standing (↓ preload)            │
│ • ↓ With Squatting, Handgrip (↑ preload/afterload)│
│ • S4 (atrial kick against stiff LV)                │
│ • Mitral regurgitation (from SAM)                  │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Key Differentiating Feature: - HCM murmur ↑ with Valsalva (LVOTO worsens) - AS murmur ↓ with Valsalva (↓ Stroke volume)

    Investigations¶

    ECG: 

    ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      ECG IN HCM                                    │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ • LVH with strain (Most common)                    │
│ • DEEP Q waves (Septal depolarization)             │
│   - Inferolateral leads (II, III, aVF, V5-V6)      │
│   - Pseudo-infarction pattern                      │
│ • Giant negative T waves (Apical HCM - Yamaguchi)  │
│ • Left atrial enlargement                          │
│ • AF, PVCs                                         │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Chest X-ray: - Often normal (LV not dilated) - May show LA enlargement

    Echocardiography - DIAGNOSTIC:

    ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      ECHO FINDINGS IN HCM                          │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ • LV wall thickness ≥15 mm (any segment)           │
│ • Asymmetric septal hypertrophy (Septum/PW >1.3)   │
│ • Small LV cavity                                  │
│ • Normal or ↑ EF (hyperdynamic)                    │
│ • Systolic Anterior Motion (SAM) of mitral valve   │
│ • LVOT gradient (Doppler):                         │
│   - <30 mmHg: Non-obstructive                      │
│   - ≥30 mmHg: Obstructive                          │
│ • Mitral regurgitation (from SAM)                  │
│ • Diastolic dysfunction                            │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Provocation: - Valsalva or Amyl Nitrite → ↑ Gradient (if dynamic obstruction)

    Cardiac MRI: - Gold standard for wall thickness measurement - Late gadolinium enhancement (LGE): Myocardial fibrosis (↑ SCD risk)

    Genetic Testing: - Recommended in all HCM patients - Cascade screening of family members if mutation identified

    Holter Monitoring: - Detect NSVT (↑ SCD risk)

    Exercise Testing: - Assess symptoms, BP response - Abnormal BP response (Failure to rise or drop) → ↑ SCD risk

    Risk Stratification for Sudden Cardiac Death¶

    Major Risk Factors: 1. Cardiac arrest or Sustained VT (previous) 2. Family history of SCD (1st degree relative, <40 years) 3. Unexplained syncope (Recent, especially if exertional) 4. LV wall thickness ≥30 mm 5. NSVT on Holter (Non-sustained VT) 6. Abnormal BP response to exercise (Failure to rise ≥20 mmHg or drop)

    ≥1 Major Risk Factor → Consider ICD

    HCM Risk-SCD Score: - Calculator incorporating multiple factors - Estimates 5-year SCD risk - ≥6% risk → ICD recommended

    Management¶

    Goals: 1. Relieve symptoms (dyspnea, angina) 2. Prevent sudden cardiac death

    General Measures: - Avoid dehydration - Avoid competitive sports (Sudden death risk) - Family screening (Echo, ECG, Genetic testing)

    Medical Therapy:

    For Symptoms (Dyspnea, Angina):

    β-Blockers (First-line): - Metoprolol, Atenolol, Propranolol - ↓ HR, ↓ Contractility → ↓ LVOTO, ↑ Diastolic filling time - Improve dyspnea, angina

    Calcium Channel Blockers (Verapamil, Diltiazem): - Alternative if β-blockers contraindicated - Improve diastolic filling - Caution: May cause vasodilation → ↑ LVOTO (Use if non-obstructive)

    Disopyramide: - Negative inotrope - ↓ LVOTO - Used as add-on if symptoms persist

    AVOID: - Vasodilators (↓ Afterload → ↑ LVOTO) - Diuretics (↓ Preload → ↑ LVOTO) - Use cautiously if needed - Digoxin (↑ Contractility → ↑ LVOTO) - Nitrates (↓ Preload → ↑ LVOTO)

    For Atrial Fibrillation: - Rate control: β-blockers, Verapamil - Anticoagulation: MANDATORY (High stroke risk)

    Interventional Therapy (For Refractory Symptoms with LVOTO ≥50 mmHg):

    1. Septal Myectomy (Surgical): - Gold standard - Resect hypertrophied septum - Excellent long-term results

    2. Alcohol Septal Ablation: - Percutaneous - Inject alcohol into septal perforator artery → Controlled MI → Septal thinning - Alternative if high surgical risk

    ICD (For SCD Prevention): - Indications: - Secondary prevention: Survived cardiac arrest, Sustained VT - Primary prevention: ≥1 Major risk factor OR HCM Risk-SCD ≥6%

    Restrictive Cardiomyopathy (RCM)¶

    Definition¶

    Impaired ventricular filling due to ↓ compliance (stiff myocardium) with normal/near-normal systolic function and normal wall thickness.

    Least common cardiomyopathy

    Etiology¶

    Infiltrative (Most Common): - Amyloidosis (Most common cause of RCM) - AL (Light chain) - Primary - AA (Serum A) - Secondary to chronic inflammation - ATTR (Transthyretin) - Senile, Familial - Sarcoidosis - Hemochromatosis (Iron deposition) - Glycogen storage diseases (Fabry, Gaucher)

    Non-Infiltrative: - Idiopathic - Scleroderma - Post-radiation - Diabetic cardiomyopathy

    Endomyocardial: - Endomyocardial fibrosis (Tropical - Africa, India) - Hypereosinophilic syndrome (Loeffler endocarditis) - Carcinoid heart disease

    Pathophysiology¶

    Infiltration / Fibrosis
        ↓
↓ Ventricular Compliance (Stiff myocardium)
        ↓
↓ Diastolic Filling
        ↓
↑ Filling Pressures (LA, RA)
        ↓
Atrial Dilation
        ↓
Pulmonary/Systemic Venous Congestion
        ↓
HEART FAILURE (HFpEF)

    Hemodynamics: - "Dip and Plateau" (Square root sign) on ventricular pressure tracing - Early rapid filling, then abrupt cessation

    Clinical Features¶

    Symptoms: - Dyspnea (exertional) - Fatigue - Right heart failure (Peripheral edema, ascites, hepatomegaly) - Often more prominent than left heart failure

    Signs: - Elevated JVP with prominent x and y descents - Kussmaul's sign: ↑ JVP with inspiration (impaired RV filling) - S3, S4 gallops - Hepatomegaly, ascites, peripheral edema

    Investigations¶

    ECG: - Low voltage (infiltration - Amyloidosis) - Pseudo-infarction pattern (Q waves) - AF, AV block

    Chest X-ray: - Normal/Mildly enlarged heart - Dilated atria - Pulmonary congestion

    Echocardiography:

    ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      ECHO IN RCM                                   │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ • Normal/Mildly thickened LV walls                 │
│ • Normal/Near-normal EF                            │
│ • Dilated atria (LA >> LV)                         │
│ • Restrictive filling pattern (Doppler)            │
│   - ↑ E/A ratio (>2)                               │
│   - Short deceleration time (<150 ms)              │
│ • Granular sparkling myocardium (Amyloidosis)      │
│ • Thickened valves, IVS (Amyloidosis)              │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Cardiac MRI: - Late gadolinium enhancement: Diffuse subendocardial (Amyloidosis)

    Cardiac Catheterization: - Dip-and-plateau (Square root sign) - Equalization of pressures: RA ≈ RV diastolic ≈ LA ≈ LV diastolic

    Endomyocardial Biopsy: - Definitive diagnosis for infiltrative causes - Amyloid: Congo red stain (Apple-green birefringence under polarized light) - Sarcoidosis: Non-caseating granulomas - Hemochromatosis: Prussian blue stain (iron)

    Specific Tests: - Amyloidosis: - Serum/Urine protein electrophoresis (SPEP/UPEP) - Serum free light chains - Bone marrow biopsy (AL amyloid) - Technetium-99m pyrophosphate scan (ATTR amyloid) - Hemochromatosis: ↑ Ferritin, ↑ Transferrin saturation, Genetic testing (HFE gene) - Sarcoidosis: ↑ ACE, Tissue biopsy

    Management¶

    Treat Underlying Cause: - AL Amyloidosis: Chemotherapy (Melphalan + Dexamethasone), Stem cell transplant - ATTR Amyloidosis: Tafamidis (TTR stabilizer) - Hemochromatosis: Phlebotomy, Iron chelation (Deferasirox) - Sarcoidosis: Corticosteroids

    Heart Failure Management: - Diuretics: Cautious (need high filling pressures) - Digoxin: Contraindicated in amyloidosis (binds to amyloid fibrils → toxicity) - β-blockers, ACE inhibitors: Often poorly tolerated (↓ Preload → ↓ CO)

    Anticoagulation: - If AF (high thromboembolic risk - especially amyloidosis)

    Advanced Therapy: - Heart transplant (for selected patients)

    Arrhythmogenic Right Ventricular Cardiomyopathy (ARVC)¶

    Definition¶

    Fibrofatty replacement of RV myocardium → RV dysfunction + Ventricular arrhythmias

    Genetic disorder (Autosomal dominant with incomplete penetrance)

    Genetics¶

    • Desmosomal protein mutations:
    • Plakophilin-2 (PKP2) - Most common
    • Desmoglein-2, Desmocollin-2
    • "Disease of desmosomes"

    Pathophysiology¶

    Desmosome dysfunction
        ↓
Loss of cell-cell adhesion
        ↓
Myocyte apoptosis
        ↓
Fibrofatty replacement (RV >> LV)
        ↓
RV Dilation, Dysfunction
        ↓
Arrhythmogenic substrate
        ↓
VT (LBBB morphology), VF
        ↓
SUDDEN CARDIAC DEATH (Young athletes)

    Clinical Features¶

    Often Asymptomatic until arrhythmia

    Symptoms: - Palpitations (VT) - Syncope/Presyncope (VT/VF) - Sudden cardiac death (First presentation - young athletes) - Heart failure (Late - RV dysfunction)

    Arrhythmias: - VT with LBBB morphology (Origin from RV) - VF

    Triggers: - Exercise (↑ Catecholamines)

    Investigations¶

    ECG: 

    ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      ECG IN ARVC                                   │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ • Epsilon wave (Terminal notch in QRS - V1-V3)     │
│   (Hallmark - Delayed RV activation)               │
│ • T wave inversion in V1-V3 (RV strain)            │
│ • Prolonged S wave upstroke (>55 ms in V1-V3)      │
│ • RBBB                                             │
│ • VT with LBBB morphology (RV origin)              │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Echocardiography: - RV dilation, dysfunction - RV wall motion abnormalities (akinesia, dyskinesia) - Aneurysms (RV free wall, Apex, Infundibulum)

    Cardiac MRI - GOLD STANDARD: - Fibrofatty replacement of RV - RV dilation, dysfunction - Wall thinning

    Endomyocardial Biopsy: - <50% myocytes replaced by fibrofatty tissue (diagnostic) - Risk of perforation (RV thin)

    Genetic Testing: - Confirm diagnosis - Family screening

    Management¶

    Lifestyle Modifications: - Avoid competitive sports, strenuous exercise (Trigger for VT/SCD)

    Medical: - β-blockers: ↓ Arrhythmias, ↓ Disease progression - Antiarrhythmics: Sotalol, Amiodarone (if recurrent VT)

    ICD: - Indicated for: - Cardiac arrest survivor (Secondary prevention) - Sustained VT (Secondary prevention) - High risk (Extensive RV involvement, LV involvement, Syncope, NSVT, Family history of SCD)

    Catheter Ablation: - For recurrent VT despite ICD/drugs

    Heart Transplant: - End-stage biventricular failure

    Takotsubo Cardiomyopathy¶

    Definition¶

    Transient LV dysfunction (apical ballooning) following emotional or physical stress, mimicking acute MI but without obstructive CAD.

    "Broken Heart Syndrome" or "Stress Cardiomyopathy"

    Epidemiology¶

    • Postmenopausal women (90%)
    • Triggered by emotional stress (Death of loved one, natural disaster) or physical stress (Surgery, illness)

    Pathophysiology¶

    Severe Stress (Emotional/Physical)
        ↓
↑ Catecholamines (Surge)
        ↓
Catecholamine-induced myocardial stunning
        ↓
Apical akinesia/dyskinesia (Apical ballooning)
        ↓
Basal hyperkinesis
        ↓
Transient LV dysfunction (↓ EF)
        ↓
Recovery (days to weeks)

    Mechanism: Direct catecholamine toxicity, Microvascular dysfunction, Coronary spasm

    Clinical Features¶

    Presentation: - Chest pain, dyspnea (Mimics acute MI) - ECG changes (ST elevation, T inversion) - ↑ Troponin (Mild elevation) - Recent stressor (emotional/physical)

    Often Indistinguishable from STEMI at presentation

    Investigations¶

    ECG: - ST elevation (Anterior leads) or T wave inversion - QTc prolongation

    Cardiac Biomarkers: - Troponin: Mildly elevated (usually <10x ULN) - BNP: Elevated

    Echocardiography:

    ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      ECHO IN TAKOTSUBO                             │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ • Apical akinesia/dyskinesia (Apical ballooning)   │
│   "Octopus pot" shape (Takotsubo = octopus trap)   │
│ • Basal hyperkinesis                               │
│ • ↓ EF (usually 20-40%)                            │
│ • Resolves within weeks                            │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Coronary Angiography - DIAGNOSTIC: - Normal or Non-obstructive coronary arteries - Differentiates from STEMI (which has occluded artery)

    Ventriculography: - Apical ballooning (Takotsubo shape)

    Management¶

    Acute: - Supportive care - ACE inhibitors, β-blockers (standard HF therapy) - Diuretics if pulmonary edema - Anticoagulation (if LV thrombus)

    Complications: - Cardiogenic shock (Rare - 5-10%) - LV thrombus (Apical akinesia) - Mitral regurgitation (Functional) - LVOT obstruction (Basal hyperkinesis)

    Prognosis: - Excellent - Full recovery in days to weeks (most patients) - Mortality: 1-2% (in-hospital) - Recurrence: 5-10%

    NEET PG High-Yield Points¶

    🔥 MUST KNOW¶

    1. Dilated Cardiomyopathy (DCM):
    2. Most common cardiomyopathy (Overall)
    3. Causes: Idiopathic (most common), Ischemic, Alcohol, Viral myocarditis
    4. Echo: Dilated LV, ↓ EF (<40%), Thin walls

    5. Treatment: Standard HFrEF (ACEI/ARB, β-blocker, Diuretic, Spironolactone), ICD if EF ≤35%

    6. Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM):

    7. Most common genetic cardiovascular disease (1 in 500)
    8. Autosomal dominant (β-Myosin heavy chain gene)
    9. Asymmetric septal hypertrophy (Most common type)
    10. LV wall ≥15 mm
    11. LVOTO: ↑ with Valsalva, Standing; ↓ with Squatting, Handgrip
    12. Murmur: ↑ with Valsalva (unlike AS)
    13. ECG: LVH, Deep Q waves (pseudo-infarction)
    14. Echo: SAM (Systolic Anterior Motion), LVOT gradient
    15. SCD risk factors: Cardiac arrest, Family history SCD, Syncope, LV ≥30 mm, NSVT

    16. Treatment: β-blockers (1st-line), Avoid vasodilators/diuretics, ICD if high risk

    17. Restrictive Cardiomyopathy (RCM):

    18. Least common cardiomyopathy
    19. Most common cause: Amyloidosis
    20. Echo: Normal/Mildly thick walls, Normal EF, Dilated atria, Granular sparkling (amyloid)
    21. Catheterization: Dip-and-plateau (Square root sign)

    22. Biopsy: Congo red → Apple-green birefringence (Amyloidosis)

    23. ARVC:

    24. Fibrofatty replacement of RV
    25. Genetic: Desmosomal proteins (PKP2)
    26. ECG: Epsilon wave (Hallmark), T inversion V1-V3
    27. VT with LBBB morphology (RV origin)
    28. SCD in young athletes

    29. Treatment: β-blockers, Avoid sports, ICD

    30. Takotsubo:

    31. Postmenopausal women after emotional stress
    32. Mimics STEMI (Chest pain, ST elevation, ↑ Troponin)
    33. Angiography: Normal coronaries (Differentiates from MI)
    34. Echo: Apical ballooning (Octopus pot shape)
    35. Excellent prognosis - Full recovery

    Practice Questions¶

    Question 1¶

    A 25-year-old athlete presents with syncope during exercise. Echocardiography shows asymmetric septal hypertrophy with LV wall thickness of 22 mm and SAM of the mitral valve. What maneuver will INCREASE the intensity of the murmur?

    A) Squatting B) Handgrip C) Valsalva maneuver D) Leg raise

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: C) Valsalva maneuver** **Explanation:** - **Diagnosis:** Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy (HCM) with LVOT obstruction - **Murmur increases with ↓ preload or ↓ afterload:** - **Valsalva** (↓ venous return → ↓ LV volume → ↑ LVOTO) - **Standing** (↓ venous return) - **Nitrates** (vasodilation) - **Murmur decreases with ↑ preload or ↑ afterload:** - **Squatting** (↑ venous return + ↑ afterload) - **Handgrip** (↑ afterload) - **Leg raise** (↑ venous return) **Key Differentiator:** - **HCM murmur ↑ with Valsalva** (LVOTO worsens) - **AS murmur ↓ with Valsalva** (↓ Stroke volume)

    Question 2¶

    A 60-year-old male with history of chronic alcohol use presents with dyspnea. Echo shows dilated LV with EF 25%. Coronary angiography is normal. What is the most appropriate long-term therapy?

    A) Alcohol septal ablation B) ACE inhibitor + Beta-blocker + Diuretic C) Calcium channel blocker + Disopyramide D) Observation only

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: B) ACE inhibitor + Beta-blocker + Diuretic** **Explanation:** - **Diagnosis:** Dilated Cardiomyopathy (Alcoholic) - HFrEF - **Treatment:** Standard HFrEF therapy **"ABCD" for HFrEF:** - **A:** ACE inhibitor/ARB (or ARNI) - **B:** Beta-blocker - **D:** Diuretic (for volume overload) - **Additional:** Aldosterone antagonist (Spironolactone) if EF ≤35%, SGLT2 inhibitor **Device Therapy:** - **ICD** if EF ≤35% despite 3 months optimal medical therapy **Why not others:** - Alcohol septal ablation: For HCM with LVOTO - CCB + Disopyramide: For HCM - Observation: WRONG - needs treatment

    Question 3¶

    Which finding on ECG is MOST specific for Arrhythmogenic RV Cardiomyopathy (ARVC)?

    A) LBBB B) Epsilon wave in V1-V3 C) Delta wave D) U wave

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: B) Epsilon wave in V1-V3** **Explanation:** - **Epsilon wave** is **HALLMARK** of ARVC - **Terminal notch** in QRS complex in V1-V3 - Represents **delayed RV activation** (Fibrofatty replacement) **Other ECG findings in ARVC:** - **T wave inversion** in V1-V3 (RV strain) - Prolonged S wave upstroke (>55 ms in V1-V3) - VT with **LBBB morphology** (RV origin) **Why not others:** - LBBB: Non-specific (DCM, HCM, etc.) - Delta wave: Wolff-Parkinson-White syndrome - U wave: Hypokalemia, Hypothermia
