    DEFINITION & CLASSIFICATION¶

    Lung Cancer: Malignant tumor arising from the respiratory epithelium (bronchi, bronchioles, alveoli)

    Classification¶

    LUNG CANCER
│
├─ NON-SMALL CELL LUNG CANCER (NSCLC) - 85%
│  │
│  ├─ ADENOCARCINOMA (40%) - MOST COMMON OVERALL
│  │  • Peripheral location
│  │  • Non-smokers, women
│  │  • Glandular differentiation
│  │  • Lepidic, acinar, papillary, solid subtypes
│  │
│  ├─ SQUAMOUS CELL CARCINOMA (SCC) (25-30%)
│  │  • Central location (hilar)
│  │  • STRONG association with smoking
│  │  • Keratin pearls, intercellular bridges
│  │  • Cavitation common
│  │  • Hypercalcemia (PTHrP)
│  │
│  └─ LARGE CELL CARCINOMA (10-15%)
│     • Poorly differentiated
│     • Large cells, prominent nucleoli
│     • Diagnosis of exclusion
│
└─ SMALL CELL LUNG CANCER (SCLC) - 15%
   • Central location
   • STRONGEST association with smoking
   • Neuroendocrine origin
   • Rapid growth, early metastasis
   • Paraneoplastic syndromes (SIADH, Cushing's, LEMS)
   • Small cells with scant cytoplasm, nuclear molding
   • "Oat cell" appearance

    Mnemonic: "SALL" for Lung Cancer Types¶

    • S - Small Cell (15%, worst prognosis)
    • A - Adenocarcinoma (40%, MOST COMMON)
    • L - Large Cell (10-15%)
    • L - squamous (Large) cell (25-30%, was most common before, now 2nd)

    EPIDEMIOLOGY¶

    Global Burden¶

    • Most common cancer worldwide (both sexes combined)
    • Leading cause of cancer death worldwide
    • Accounts for ~18% of all cancer deaths

    India¶

    • Most common cancer in males (age-adjusted)
    • 3rd-4th most common in females (after breast, cervix)
    • Increasing incidence due to smoking, pollution

    Demographics¶

    Parameter Details
    Age Peak incidence: 60-70 years (rare <40 years)
    Sex Male > Female (historically 3:1, now narrowing to 2:1 due to ↑ smoking in women)
    Smoking 85-90% of lung cancers are smoking-related

    Trends¶

    • Adenocarcinoma incidence increasing (now MOST COMMON)
    • More common in non-smokers, women
    • Associated with "light" cigarettes (deeper inhalation → peripheral deposition)
    • Squamous cell incidence decreasing
    • Previously most common
    • Strongly linked to heavy smoking

    RISK FACTORS¶

    Major Risk Factors¶

    1. Cigarette Smoking¶

    MOST IMPORTANT risk factor (85-90% of cases)

    Parameter Risk
    Pack-years (Packs per day) × (Years smoked)
    Risk increase 20-fold in heavy smokers (>20 pack-years)
    Dose-response ↑ Duration and intensity → ↑ Risk
    Type Squamous cell and SCLC - STRONGEST association
    Adenocarcinoma - Weaker but still strong association
    Secondhand smoke 20-30% ↑ risk
    Cessation Risk decreases over 10-15 years but never returns to baseline

    Pack-Year Calculation: - 1 pack-year = 1 pack/day × 1 year - Example: 2 packs/day × 20 years = 40 pack-years

    2. Radon Exposure¶

    • 2nd most common cause of lung cancer (after smoking)
    • Radioactive gas from uranium decay
    • Accumulates in basements, mines
    • Synergistic with smoking (multiplicative risk)

    3. Occupational Exposures¶

    Carcinogen Occupation Associated Type
    Asbestos Shipbuilding, construction, insulation Adenocarcinoma, Mesothelioma
    Arsenic Pesticides, smelting All types
    Chromium Welding, electroplating Squamous, SCLC
    Nickel Refining, electroplating Squamous, SCLC
    Silica Mining, sandblasting All types
    Bis(chloromethyl) ether Chemical industry SCLC

    Asbestos + Smoking: Multiplicative risk (50-90x ↑ vs non-smokers)

    4. Other Risk Factors¶

    • Air pollution: Particulate matter (PM2.5)
    • Radiation: Thoracic radiation (breast cancer, lymphoma treatment)
    • Lung diseases: COPD, pulmonary fibrosis, TB (scar carcinoma)
    • Family history: First-degree relative with lung cancer (2x risk)
    • Genetic: Polymorphisms in CYP genes, DNA repair genes

    Non-Smoking-Related Lung Cancer¶

    10-15% of lung cancers occur in never-smokers

    Risk Factors: - Female sex - Asian ethnicity - EGFR mutations (somatic, not inherited) - Environmental tobacco smoke (ETS) - Radon, occupational exposures - Cooking fumes (indoor air pollution)

    Type: Predominantly Adenocarcinoma (>80%)

    PATHOPHYSIOLOGY¶

    Carcinogenesis Pathway¶

    CARCINOGEN EXPOSURE (Tobacco smoke, Radon, Asbestos)
           ↓
    GENETIC MUTATIONS
    • Oncogene activation: KRAS, EGFR, ALK, ROS1, BRAF
    • Tumor suppressor inactivation: TP53, RB, CDKN2A
           ↓
    DYSPLASIA → CARCINOMA IN SITU
           ↓
    INVASIVE CARCINOMA
           ↓
    LOCAL INVASION
    • Chest wall, mediastinum, diaphragm
    • Pleural effusion (malignant)
           ↓
    LYMPHATIC SPREAD
    • Hilar → Mediastinal → Supraclavicular nodes
           ↓
    HEMATOGENOUS SPREAD
    • Brain, Bone, Liver, Adrenal glands

    Molecular Subtypes (NSCLC)¶

    Targetable Driver Mutations (Predominantly in Adenocarcinoma, non-smokers):

    Mutation Frequency (Adenoca) Target Therapy Notes
    EGFR 15-30% (Asia: 50%) Erlotinib, Gefitinib, Osimertinib Most common in Asian non-smokers, women
    ALK 3-5% Crizotinib, Alectinib, Brigatinib Young, minimal/never-smokers, signet ring cells
    ROS1 1-2% Crizotinib Similar to ALK
    BRAF (V600E) 1-3% Dabrafenib + Trametinib Melanoma drug repurposed
    MET (exon 14 skipping) 3-4% Capmatinib, Tepotinib Elderly, sarcomatoid histology
    KRAS (G12C) 25-30% (mostly smokers) Sotorasib, Adagrasib Historically "undruggable", now targetable
    RET 1-2% Selpercatinib, Pralsetinib Also in MTC
    NTRK <1% Larotrectinib, Entrectinib Pan-cancer target

    Key Point: EGFR and ALK are the MOST CLINICALLY IMPORTANT due to frequency and efficacy of targeted therapy

    PD-L1 Expression (Immunotherapy Biomarker): - High (≥50%): First-line pembrolizumab monotherapy - 1-49%: Pembrolizumab + chemotherapy - <1%: Chemotherapy alone (or other immunotherapy combinations)

    CLINICAL FEATURES¶

    Symptoms¶

    Local/Thoracic Symptoms (50-60% at presentation)¶

    1. Persistent Cough (50-75%)
    2. Most common symptom
    3. New cough in smoker or change in chronic cough

    4. Dry or productive

    5. Hemoptysis (25-50%)

    6. Blood-streaked sputum

    7. Massive hemoptysis is rare (<5%)

    8. Dyspnea (25-40%)

    9. Airway obstruction, atelectasis, pleural effusion

    10. Chest Pain (20-40%)

    11. Dull, aching (tumor invasion)

    12. Pleuritic (pleural involvement)

    13. Wheezing/Stridor

    14. Endobronchial obstruction

    Constitutional Symptoms (30-40%)¶

    • Weight loss (SCLC > NSCLC)
    • Fatigue, anorexia
    • Fever (post-obstructive pneumonia, paraneoplastic)

    Symptoms from Intrathoracic Extension¶

    Structure Involved Syndrome Clinical Features
    Recurrent laryngeal nerve (Left more common) Vocal cord paralysis Hoarseness (ask patient to say "E")
    Phrenic nerve Hemidiaphragm paralysis Dyspnea, ↑ elevated hemidiaphragm on CXR
    Esophagus Dysphagia Difficulty swallowing, odynophagia
    Sympathetic chain (C8-T2) Horner Syndrome Ptosis, Miosis, Anhidrosis (ipsilateral face)
    Brachial plexus (C8-T1) Pancoast Syndrome Shoulder pain → arm pain (ulnar distribution), Horner syndrome, rib erosion
    Superior vena cava (SVC) SVC Syndrome Facial/upper extremity edema, distended neck veins, dyspnea, cyanosis, collateral veins on chest
    Pericardium Malignant pericardial effusion Dyspnea, tamponade (rare)
    Pleura Malignant pleural effusion Dyspnea, dullness on percussion

    Metastatic Symptoms (30-40% at presentation)¶

    Site Frequency Symptoms
    Brain 20-40% Headache, seizures, focal neurological deficits, personality changes
    Bone 30-40% Bone pain (back, hip, rib), pathological fractures
    Liver 30-50% RUQ pain, hepatomegaly (usually asymptomatic)
    Adrenal 20-30% Usually asymptomatic (bilateral involvement → adrenal insufficiency)
    Lung (contralateral) 10-20% May be synchronous primary or metastasis

    SCLC: 60-70% have metastases at presentation (vs 40% in NSCLC)

    Asymptomatic Presentation¶

    • 10-15% detected incidentally on imaging (CXR, CT)
    • Increasing with screening programs

    Physical Examination¶

    Finding Significance
    Clubbing Chronic hypoxemia, HPOA
    Lymphadenopathy Supraclavicular (especially left = Virchow's node), cervical, axillary
    Wheezing/Stridor Endobronchial obstruction
    Pleural effusion Dullness, ↓ breath sounds, ↓ vocal fremitus
    SVC syndrome Facial plethora, distended neck/chest veins, edema
    Horner syndrome Ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis (Pancoast tumor)
    Hepatomegaly Liver metastases (hard, nodular)
    Bone tenderness Bone metastases
    Focal neurological signs Brain metastases

    DIAGNOSIS¶

    Diagnostic Approach¶

    CLINICAL SUSPICION
(Persistent cough, hemoptysis, weight loss, smoking history)
           ↓
    IMAGING (Chest X-Ray → CT Chest)
           ↓
    TISSUE DIAGNOSIS (Biopsy)
    • Bronchoscopy (central lesions)
    • CT-guided biopsy (peripheral lesions)
    • Lymph node biopsy (mediastinal staging)
           ↓
    STAGING (TNM)
    • CT Chest/Abdomen
    • PET-CT
    • Brain MRI
    • Bone scan (if symptomatic)
           ↓
    MOLECULAR TESTING (NSCLC, non-squamous)
    • EGFR, ALK, ROS1, BRAF, PD-L1

    1. Chest X-Ray¶

    Initial test in most patients

    Findings: - Lung nodule/mass (>3 cm = mass) - Solitary pulmonary nodule (SPN): Single, round, <3 cm - Hilar/mediastinal lymphadenopathy - Atelectasis/collapse (obstruction) - Pleural effusion - Cavitation (Squamous > others) - Rib erosion (Pancoast tumor)

    Limitations: - Small nodules (<1 cm) may be missed - Central lesions hidden by mediastinum/heart

    2. CT Chest (Contrast-Enhanced)¶

    Gold standard for detection and characterization

    Provides: - Size, location, characteristics of primary tumor - Lymph node involvement (hilar, mediastinal) - Chest wall/mediastinal invasion - Pleural involvement - Additional nodules (multifocal disease)

    Nodule Characteristics Suggestive of Malignancy: - Size >8-10 mm - Spiculated/irregular margins (corona radiata sign) - Growth on serial imaging (doubling time 30-400 days) - Upper lobe location - Solid (vs ground-glass)

    3. PET-CT (Fluorodeoxyglucose-FDG)¶

    Indications: - Staging (detect metastases) - Differentiate benign vs malignant nodules (if indeterminate on CT) - Assess treatment response

    Principle: Cancer cells have ↑ glucose uptake (FDG avid)

    Sensitivity: 85-95% for lesions >1 cm Specificity: 70-85% (False positives: Infection, inflammation, granulomas)

    False Negatives: - Bronchoalveolar carcinoma (lepidic-predominant adenocarcinoma) - Small lesions (<8 mm) - Well-differentiated tumors - Hyperglycemia (↓ tumor uptake)

    4. Tissue Diagnosis (MANDATORY before treatment)¶

    A. Bronchoscopy¶

    Indications: - Central lesions (endobronchial) - Accessible hilar/mediastinal lymph nodes (EBUS)

    Techniques: 1. Endobronchial Biopsy: Direct visualization and biopsy 2. Transbronchial Biopsy: Through bronchial wall (peripheral lesions) 3. Bronchial Brushing/Washing: Cytology 4. Endobronchial Ultrasound (EBUS): Guide biopsy of mediastinal lymph nodes (N staging)

    Yield: - Central lesions: 80-90% - Peripheral lesions: 50-70%

    B. CT-Guided Transthoracic Needle Biopsy (TTNA)¶

    Indications: - Peripheral lesions not accessible by bronchoscopy - Staging (adrenal, liver, bone metastases)

    Yield: 80-95%

    Risk: Pneumothorax (15-30%, <5% require chest tube)

    C. Surgical Biopsy¶

    Indications: - Non-diagnostic bronchoscopy/TTNA - Diagnostic uncertainty - Concomitant treatment (e.g., resection of SPN)

    Methods: - Video-Assisted Thoracoscopic Surgery (VATS): Wedge resection - Mediastinoscopy: Mediastinal lymph node biopsy (paratracheal, subcarinal)

    D. Other Biopsy Sites¶

    • Supraclavicular lymph node: Fine-needle aspiration (FNA) or excisional biopsy
    • Pleural fluid: Thoracentesis (cytology) ± Pleural biopsy
    • Bone marrow: If SCLC with cytopenias

    5. Molecular/Genetic Testing¶

    WHO: All non-squamous NSCLC (especially adenocarcinoma) WHEN: At diagnosis (stage IIIB/IV, recurrent disease)

    Essential Tests: 1. EGFR mutations (Exons 18-21) - Activating mutations: Exon 19 deletion, L858R (Exon 21) - Best response to TKIs - Resistance mutation: T790M (Exon 20) - Use 3rd-gen TKI (Osimertinib)

    1. ALK rearrangement
    2. IHC or FISH

    3. EML4-ALK fusion (most common)

    4. ROS1 rearrangement

    5. IHC or FISH

    6. PD-L1 expression (Immunohistochemistry)

    7. Tumor Proportion Score (TPS): % of tumor cells expressing PD-L1
    8. ≥50%: High expression → Pembrolizumab monotherapy
    9. 1-49%: Combination therapy
    10. <1%: Chemotherapy

    Additional Tests (if negative for above or specific indications): - BRAF V600E mutation - MET exon 14 skipping - RET rearrangement - NTRK fusion - KRAS G12C mutation

    Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS): Can test multiple genes simultaneously

    6. Staging Investigations¶

    Test Purpose
    CT Chest + Abdomen (Contrast) Lung, mediastinum, liver, adrenal metastases
    PET-CT Distant metastases (especially bone), mediastinal lymph nodes
    Brain MRI (Contrast) ALL small cell, Stage III/IV NSCLC (brain mets common)
    Bone scan If bone pain or elevated ALP (less sensitive than PET-CT)
    Mediastinoscopy/EBUS Pathological confirmation of mediastinal nodes (if PET-positive)

    7. Pulmonary Function Tests (PFTs)¶

    Indication: Preoperative assessment (determine operability)

    Criteria for Operability: - Post-operative predicted FEV₁ >40% (or >1 L) - Post-operative predicted DLCO >40%

    If marginal lung function: - Cardiopulmonary exercise testing (VO₂ max >15 mL/kg/min)

    STAGING¶

    TNM Staging (8th Edition, 2017)¶

    T (Primary Tumor)¶

    Stage Descriptor
    TX Cannot be assessed, or tumor proven by cytology but not seen on imaging/bronchoscopy
    T0 No evidence of primary tumor
    Tis Carcinoma in situ (Squamous or Adenocarcinoma in situ)
    T1 ≤3 cm, surrounded by lung/visceral pleura, no bronchoscopic invasion proximal to lobar bronchus
    T1a ≤1 cm (minimally invasive adenocarcinoma if ≤3 cm with ≤5 mm invasion)
    T1b >1 to ≤2 cm
    T1c >2 to ≤3 cm
    T2 >3 to ≤5 cm, OR involves main bronchus (not carina), OR invades visceral pleura, OR atelectasis/pneumonitis extending to hilum
    T2a >3 to ≤4 cm
    T2b >4 to ≤5 cm
    T3 >5 to ≤7 cm, OR separate nodule(s) in same lobe, OR invades chest wall, phrenic nerve, parietal pericardium
    T4 >7 cm, OR invades diaphragm, mediastinum, heart, great vessels, trachea, RLN, esophagus, vertebral body, carina, OR separate nodule(s) in different ipsilateral lobe

    N (Regional Lymph Nodes)¶

    Stage Descriptor
    NX Cannot be assessed
    N0 No regional lymph node metastasis
    N1 Ipsilateral peribronchial and/or hilar nodes, intrapulmonary nodes
    N2 Ipsilateral mediastinal and/or subcarinal nodes
    N3 Contralateral mediastinal/hilar nodes, ipsilateral/contralateral scalene/supraclavicular nodes

    M (Distant Metastasis)¶

    Stage Descriptor
    M0 No distant metastasis
    M1a Separate nodule(s) in contralateral lobe, OR pleural/pericardial nodules, OR malignant pleural/pericardial effusion
    M1b Single extrathoracic metastasis (in single organ)
    M1c Multiple extrathoracic metastases (single or multiple organs)

    Stage Grouping (NSCLC)¶

    Stage TNM 5-Year Survival
    IA1 T1a N0 M0 90%
    IA2 T1b N0 M0 85%
    IA3 T1c N0 M0 80%
    IB T2a N0 M0 73%
    IIA T2b N0 M0 65%
    IIB T1-2 N1 M0, T3 N0 M0 56%
    IIIA T1-2 N2 M0, T3-4 N1 M0, T4 N0 M0 36%
    IIIB T3-4 N2 M0 26%
    IIIC T1-4 N3 M0 13%
    IVA Any T, Any N, M1a/M1b 10%
    IVB Any T, Any N, M1c 0%

    SCLC Staging (Simplified)¶

    Traditional (Still used clinically):

    Stage Definition Treatment
    Limited Stage Confined to one hemithorax + regional lymph nodes (can fit in one radiation port) Chemo + Radiation (Curative intent)
    Extensive Stage Beyond one hemithorax (contralateral lung, distant metastases, malignant pleural/pericardial effusion) Chemotherapy (Palliative)

    TNM Staging (Now preferred): Same as NSCLC (for uniform reporting)

    NON-SMALL CELL LUNG CANCER (NSCLC)¶

    Adenocarcinoma¶

    Most common lung cancer (40%)

    Characteristics¶

    Feature Details
    Location Peripheral (subpleural)
    Smoking association Moderate (but most common in never-smokers)
    Demographics Women, non-smokers, younger age
    Growth Slower than SCLC
    Metastasis Moderate rate
    Molecular mutations EGFR (15-30%, Asia 50%), ALK (3-5%), ROS1 (1-2%)
    Histology Glandular differentiation, mucin production
    Subtypes Lepidic, Acinar, Papillary, Solid, Mucinous

    Special Subtypes¶

    1. Lepidic-Predominant Adenocarcinoma (formerly Bronchoalveolar Carcinoma) - Growth along alveolar walls (lepidic pattern) - Ground-glass opacity on CT - Low FDG uptake on PET (may be PET-negative) - Better prognosis

    2. Invasive Mucinous Adenocarcinoma - Abundant mucin production - Consolidation on imaging (mimics pneumonia) - KRAS mutations common

    Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC)¶

    Second most common (25-30%)

    Characteristics¶

    Feature Details
    Location Central (proximal bronchi, hilar)
    Smoking association STRONGEST (rare in never-smokers)
    Demographics Men, heavy smokers
    Growth Moderate
    Metastasis Later than adenocarcinoma
    Molecular mutations Rare (EGFR, ALK uncommon)
    Histology Keratin pearls, intercellular bridges, keratinization
    Cavitation Common (25-30%) - Tumor outgrows blood supply → Necrosis

    Paraneoplastic Syndromes¶

    • Hypercalcemia: PTHrP (Parathyroid Hormone-related Peptide) - MOST COMMON
    • Hypertrophic Pulmonary Osteoarthropathy (HPOA)

    Large Cell Carcinoma¶

    Least common NSCLC (10-15%)

    Characteristics¶

    • Poorly differentiated
    • Diagnosis of exclusion: No glandular (adeno) or squamous differentiation on light microscopy
    • Large cells with prominent nucleoli
    • Peripheral location
    • Rapid growth, early metastasis
    • Worst prognosis among NSCLC

    Subtypes¶

    • Large Cell Neuroendocrine Carcinoma (LCNEC): Aggressive, neuroendocrine markers positive

    SMALL CELL LUNG CANCER (SCLC)¶

    Characteristics¶

    Feature Details
    Frequency 15% of lung cancers
    Location Central (hilar, mediastinal)
    Smoking association STRONGEST (>95% are smokers)
    Cell origin Neuroendocrine (Kulchitsky cells)
    Growth rate VERY RAPID (doubling time 30 days)
    Metastasis EARLY and WIDESPREAD (60-70% have metastases at diagnosis)
    Histology Small cells with scant cytoplasm, nuclear molding, "salt-and-pepper" chromatin, "Oat cell" appearance
    Immunohistochemistry Chromogranin+, Synaptophysin+, CD56+, TTF-1+
    Prognosis WORST (Median survival 6-12 months if untreated)

    Staging¶

    Limited Stage (30%): - Confined to one hemithorax + regional lymph nodes - Can fit in one radiation port - Median survival: 15-20 months (with treatment)

    Extensive Stage (70%): - Beyond one hemithorax - Distant metastases (Brain, liver, bone, adrenal, bone marrow) - Malignant pleural/pericardial effusion - Median survival: 8-12 months (with treatment)

    Common Metastatic Sites (at Diagnosis)¶

    • Liver (50%)
    • Brain (40%) - Prophylactic Cranial Irradiation (PCI) given after response to prevent brain mets
    • Bone (30%)
    • Adrenal (20%)
    • Bone marrow (20%)

    Paraneoplastic Syndromes (SCLC >> NSCLC)¶

    Syndrome Mechanism Clinical Features Diagnostic Test
    SIADH (10-15%) - MOST COMMON Ectopic ADH production Hyponatremia (<130 mEq/L), euvolemic, urine osmolality >100 mOsm/kg Serum Na+, Urine osmolality
    Cushing Syndrome (2-5%) Ectopic ACTH production Hyperglycemia, hypokalemia, alkalosis, muscle weakness, edema (NO cushingoid features due to rapid onset) ↑ Cortisol, ↑ ACTH, no dexamethasone suppression
    Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (LEMS) (1-3%) Autoantibodies to presynaptic Ca²⁺ channels (VGCC) at NMJ Proximal muscle weakness (legs > arms), IMPROVES with repetitive use, ↓ DTRs, autonomic dysfunction (dry mouth, erectile dysfunction) Anti-VGCC antibodies, incremental response on EMG (opposite of myasthenia)

    TREATMENT¶

    General Principles¶

    Treatment depends on: 1. Histology (NSCLC vs SCLC) 2. Stage (I-IV for NSCLC; Limited vs Extensive for SCLC) 3. Molecular markers (EGFR, ALK, ROS1, PD-L1 for NSCLC) 4. Performance Status (ECOG 0-4) 5. Lung function (FEV₁, DLCO for surgery)

    NSCLC Treatment by Stage¶

    NSCLC TREATMENT ALGORITHM
│
├─ STAGE I-II (T1-2, N0-1)
│  └─ SURGICAL RESECTION (Lobectomy preferred, Pneumonectomy if needed)
│     ± Adjuvant Chemotherapy (if IB-II, tumor >4 cm)
│
├─ STAGE III
│  ├─ IIIA (Resectable)
│  │  └─ Neoadjuvant Chemo/Chemoradiation → Surgery → Adjuvant therapy
│  │
│  ├─ IIIA (Unresectable) / IIIB / IIIC
│  │  └─ Concurrent Chemoradiation → Consolidation Immunotherapy (Durvalumab)
│  │
│  └─ Pancoast Tumor (T3-4 N0-1)
│     └─ Neoadjuvant Chemoradiation → Surgery
│
└─ STAGE IV (Metastatic)
   ├─ DRIVER MUTATION POSITIVE (EGFR/ALK/ROS1/BRAF/MET/RET/NTRK)
   │  └─ TARGETED THERAPY (First-line)
   │     • EGFR+: Osimertinib (1st-line)
   │     • ALK+: Alectinib, Brigatinib
   │     • ROS1+: Crizotinib, Entrectinib
   │     • BRAF V600E+: Dabrafenib + Trametinib
   │
   ├─ DRIVER MUTATION NEGATIVE, PD-L1 ≥50%
   │  └─ Pembrolizumab (Immunotherapy monotherapy)
   │
   └─ DRIVER MUTATION NEGATIVE, PD-L1 <50%
      └─ Chemotherapy ± Immunotherapy
         • Platinum doublet (Cisplatin/Carboplatin + Pemetrexed/Paclitaxel)
         • + Pembrolizumab (if PD-L1 ≥1%)

    Surgical Resection (NSCLC)¶

    Indications: Stage I-II, selected IIIA

    Criteria: - Resectable tumor (No T4 invasion, N2 resectable) - Adequate lung function (Post-op FEV₁ >40% or >1 L) - No distant metastases (M0) - Good performance status (ECOG 0-1)

    Procedures: - Lobectomy: Standard of care (removes entire lobe) - Lowest recurrence rate - Segmentectomy/Wedge resection: For small tumors (<2 cm) or poor lung function - Pneumonectomy: If tumor involves main bronchus/central location (higher morbidity/mortality)

    Adjuvant Chemotherapy (After Surgery): - Stage IB (>4 cm), II, IIIA: Platinum-based doublet × 4 cycles - Improves 5-year survival by 5-10%

    Radiation Therapy (NSCLC)¶

    Indications: 1. Curative Intent: - Stage I-II (medically inoperable): Stereotactic Body Radiotherapy (SBRT) / Cyberknife - High-dose, few fractions (3-5), excellent local control - Stage III (unresectable): Concurrent chemoradiation 2. Palliative: - Bone metastases (pain relief) - Brain metastases - SVC obstruction - Hemoptysis, bronchial obstruction

    Consolidation Immunotherapy (After Chemoradiation for Stage III): - Durvalumab (Anti-PD-L1) × 12 months - Improves progression-free survival

    Chemotherapy (NSCLC)¶

    Regimens (Platinum-based doublet):

    Histology Preferred Regimen
    Non-squamous Cisplatin/Carboplatin + Pemetrexed
    Squamous Cisplatin/Carboplatin + Paclitaxel/Gemcitabine

    Note: Pemetrexed contraindicated in squamous cell carcinoma (ineffective)

    Duration: 4-6 cycles

    Targeted Therapy (NSCLC)¶

    ONLY if driver mutation positive

    Mutation First-Line TKI Mechanism Notes
    EGFR (Exon 19 del, L858R) Osimertinib (3rd-gen) EGFR TKI ↓ Brain mets, overcomes T790M resistance
    Erlotinib, Gefitinib (1st-gen) Used if osimertinib unavailable
    EGFR T790M (resistance) Osimertinib After progression on 1st/2nd-gen TKIs
    ALK Alectinib, Brigatinib ALK TKI Better CNS penetration than crizotinib
    Crizotinib (1st-gen)
    ROS1 Crizotinib, Entrectinib ROS1 TKI
    BRAF V600E Dabrafenib + Trametinib BRAF + MEK inhibitors Combination required
    MET exon 14 Capmatinib, Tepotinib MET TKI

    Advantages over Chemotherapy: - Higher response rates (60-80% vs 30-40%) - Longer progression-free survival - Better quality of life (oral, fewer side effects)

    Immunotherapy (NSCLC)¶

    Checkpoint Inhibitors (Block PD-1/PD-L1 or CTLA-4 → Restore T-cell activity)

    Drug Target Indication
    Pembrolizumab Anti-PD-1 First-line (PD-L1 ≥50% monotherapy, or ≥1% with chemo)
    Nivolumab Anti-PD-1 Second-line (after platinum chemo)
    Atezolizumab Anti-PD-L1 First-line (with chemo) or second-line
    Durvalumab Anti-PD-L1 Consolidation after chemoradiation (Stage III)

    Selection Criteria: - PD-L1 ≥50%: Pembrolizumab monotherapy (first-line) - PD-L1 1-49%: Chemotherapy + Pembrolizumab - PD-L1 <1%: Chemotherapy (consider immunotherapy combinations)

    Contraindication: EGFR/ALK-positive (use TKIs first, immunotherapy after resistance)

    SCREENING¶

    U.S. Preventive Services Task Force (USPSTF) Recommendations¶

    Who to Screen: - Age 50-80 years (updated 2021, was 55-80) - ≥20 pack-year smoking history - Current smoker OR quit within past 15 years - Good overall health (life expectancy >10 years)

    Screening Test: Low-Dose CT Chest (LDCT) annually

    Benefits: - 20% reduction in lung cancer mortality (National Lung Screening Trial) - Detects cancers at earlier stage (more resectable)

    Harms: - False positives (95% of nodules are benign) → Anxiety, unnecessary procedures - Radiation exposure (low, but cumulative) - Overdiagnosis (indolent cancers that would never harm)

    Note: CXR and sputum cytology are NOT effective for screening

    Nodule Management (Fleischner Society Guidelines)¶

    For nodules detected on CT:

    Size Solid Nodule Ground-Glass Nodule
    <6 mm No routine follow-up (unless high risk) No routine follow-up
    6-8 mm CT at 6-12 months, then annually × 1-2 years CT at 6-12 months, then annually × 2-4 years
    >8 mm CT at 3 months, PET-CT, or biopsy CT at 3 months, then annually until 5 years

    Spiculated nodule >8 mm: Consider PET-CT or biopsy (high suspicion for malignancy)

    PRACTICE MCQs¶

    Question 1¶

    A 60-year-old male smoker (40 pack-years) presents with persistent cough, hemoptysis, and weight loss. CT chest shows a 5 cm central mass with mediastinal lymphadenopathy. Biopsy shows small cells with scant cytoplasm and nuclear molding. Immunohistochemistry is positive for chromogranin and synaptophysin. What is the most appropriate next step in staging?

    A. PET-CT only B. Brain MRI + CT abdomen C. Mediastinoscopy D. Bone scan

    Answer: B. Brain MRI + CT abdomen

    Explanation: - Diagnosis: Small Cell Lung Cancer (SCLC) - Small cells, scant cytoplasm, nuclear molding - Chromogranin+ and Synaptophysin+ (neuroendocrine markers)

    Staging SCLC: - Brain MRI (MANDATORY in all SCLC) - 40% have brain metastases at diagnosis - Asymptomatic brain mets common - CT Chest/Abdomen (detect liver, adrenal mets) - Bone scan or PET-CT (detect bone mets)

    Limited vs Extensive Stage: - Limited: One hemithorax + regional nodes (fit in one radiation port) - Extensive: Beyond one hemithorax, distant mets

    Why NOT Other Options?: - PET-CT only: Misses some brain mets (MRI more sensitive for brain) - Mediastinoscopy: Not needed for SCLC (rarely surgical, chemoradiation/chemo is treatment) - Bone scan: Less sensitive than PET-CT (but PET or bone scan can be done, but brain MRI is must)

    Key Point: ALL SCLC patients get Brain MRI + CT Chest/Abdomen (or PET-CT) for staging

    Question 2¶

    A 55-year-old non-smoker woman with adenocarcinoma of the lung (Stage IV) has EGFR exon 19 deletion on molecular testing. What is the most appropriate first-line treatment?

    A. Cisplatin + Pemetrexed B. Pembrolizumab monotherapy C. Osimertinib D. Carboplatin + Paclitaxel + Pembrolizumab

    Answer: C. Osimertinib

    Explanation: - Diagnosis: EGFR-mutant adenocarcinoma - Exon 19 deletion (activating mutation, TKI-sensitive)

    Treatment Paradigm: - EGFR-mutant (Exon 19 del or L858R) → Osimertinib (3rd-gen EGFR TKI) - FIRST-LINE - Alternatives: Erlotinib, Gefitinib (1st-gen), Afatinib (2nd-gen) - Osimertinib preferred: Better CNS penetration, overcomes T790M resistance

    Response: - 60-80% response rate (vs 30-40% with chemotherapy) - Longer PFS (18-20 months vs 5-6 months with chemo)

    Why NOT Other Options?: - Chemotherapy (A, D): NOT first-line if targetable mutation present - Use after TKI resistance - Pembrolizumab (B): Immunotherapy is LESS effective in EGFR+ patients - TKI first, immunotherapy after resistance

    Key Point: EGFR-mutant NSCLC → Osimertinib (Targeted therapy, NOT chemotherapy or immunotherapy first)

    Question 3¶

    A patient with squamous cell carcinoma of the lung presents with serum calcium 13 mg/dL (normal 8.5-10.5), phosphate 2.0 mg/dL, and PTH <10 pg/mL (low). What is the mechanism of hypercalcemia?

    A. Bone metastases B. Ectopic PTH production C. PTH-related peptide (PTHrP) D. Vitamin D excess

    Answer: C. PTH-related peptide (PTHrP)

    Explanation: - Diagnosis: Humoral Hypercalcemia of Malignancy due to PTHrP

    Mechanism: - Squamous Cell Carcinoma → Produces PTHrP - PTHrP mimics PTH action: - ↑ Bone resorption (osteoclast activation) - ↑ Renal calcium reabsorption - ↓ Renal phosphate reabsorption

    Laboratory Findings: - ↑ Calcium (13 mg/dL) - ↓ Phosphate (PTHrP → Renal phosphate wasting) - ↓ PTH (Suppressed by hypercalcemia) - ↑ PTHrP (if measured)

    Why NOT Other Options?: - Bone metastases (A): Would have ↑ calcium, but mechanism is local osteolysis (PTHrP is humoral/systemic) - Ectopic PTH (B): Very rare (PTHrP is more common) - Vitamin D excess (D): Would have ↑ calcium, ↑ phosphate, ↓ PTH (not seen here)

    Treatment: - IV fluids (0.9% saline 200-300 mL/hr) - Bisphosphonates (Zoledronic acid 4 mg IV) - Calcitonin (rapid onset, short duration) - Treat tumor (most important)

    Key Point: Squamous Cell Carcinoma + Hypercalcemia + ↓ Phosphate + ↓ PTH = PTHrP-mediated

    Question 4¶

    A 65-year-old man with SCLC (Limited Stage) completes concurrent chemoradiation with good response. What is the most appropriate next step to improve survival?

    A. Adjuvant chemotherapy B. Prophylactic Cranial Irradiation (PCI) C. Maintenance immunotherapy D. Observation

    Answer: B. Prophylactic Cranial Irradiation (PCI)

    Explanation: - Limited-Stage SCLC Treatment: 1. Concurrent chemoradiation (Cisplatin + Etoposide + Thoracic radiation) 2. If response → Prophylactic Cranial Irradiation (PCI)

    PCI Rationale: - Brain is common metastatic site (40% eventually develop brain mets) - SCLC has high propensity for CNS spread - PCI reduces brain metastases by 50% - Improves 3-year survival from 15% → 21%

    Dose: 25 Gy in 10 fractions

    Side Effects: - Cognitive decline (especially in older patients) - Fatigue, alopecia

    Controversial in Extensive Stage: PCI may ↓ brain mets but no clear survival benefit

    Why NOT Other Options?: - Adjuvant chemotherapy (A): Chemo already given concurrently (4-6 cycles) - Maintenance immunotherapy (C): Under investigation, not standard (though Durvalumab shows promise) - Observation (D): Misses opportunity to prevent brain mets

    Key Point: Limited-Stage SCLC + Good response to chemoradiation → PCI (Reduces brain mets, improves survival)

    Question 5¶

    A 50-year-old woman (never-smoker) has stage IV lung adenocarcinoma with PD-L1 expression of 60%. Molecular testing shows no EGFR, ALK, or ROS1 mutations. What is the most appropriate first-line treatment?

    A. Cisplatin + Pemetrexed B. Pembrolizumab monotherapy C. Pembrolizumab + Chemotherapy D. Osimertinib

    Answer: B. Pembrolizumab monotherapy

    Explanation: - PD-L1 ≥50% + No driver mutations (EGFR/ALK/ROS1 negative) → Pembrolizumab monotherapy

    Evidence: - KEYNOTE-024 Trial: PD-L1 ≥50%, Pembrolizumab vs Chemotherapy - Higher response rate, Longer PFS, Better OS - Pembrolizumab monotherapy is superior to chemotherapy

    Treatment Algorithm for Stage IV NSCLC: 1. Test for driver mutations (EGFR, ALK, ROS1, BRAF, etc.) - If positive → Targeted therapy (TKI) 2. If negative → Check PD-L1: - PD-L1 ≥50% → Pembrolizumab monotherapy - PD-L1 1-49% → Chemotherapy + Pembrolizumab - PD-L1 <1% → Chemotherapy ± Immunotherapy combinations

    Why NOT Other Options?: - Chemotherapy alone (A): Inferior to Pembrolizumab monotherapy when PD-L1 ≥50% - Pembrolizumab + Chemotherapy (C): No added benefit over pembrolizumab alone when PD-L1 ≥50% (but can be used) - Osimertinib (D): Only for EGFR-mutant patients (this patient is EGFR-negative)

    Key Point: PD-L1 ≥50% + No driver mutations → Pembrolizumab monotherapy (First-line)

    Question 6¶

    A patient with lung cancer presents with proximal muscle weakness that improves with repetitive use, decreased deep tendon reflexes, and dry mouth. EMG shows incremental response to rapid repetitive stimulation. What is the diagnosis and associated tumor type?

    A. Myasthenia Gravis; Thymoma B. Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome; Small Cell Lung Cancer C. Dermatomyositis; Adenocarcinoma D. Guillain-Barré Syndrome; Squamous Cell Carcinoma

    Answer: B. Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome; Small Cell Lung Cancer

    Explanation: - Diagnosis: Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome (LEMS)

    Clinical Features: - Proximal muscle weakness (legs > arms) - IMPROVES with repetitive use/exercise (KEY DIFFERENTIATOR) - ↓ Deep tendon reflexes - Autonomic dysfunction: Dry mouth, erectile dysfunction, constipation

    EMG Findings: - Incremental (facilitated) response: ↑ Amplitude with rapid repetitive stimulation (>10 Hz) - Opposite of Myasthenia Gravis (decremental response)

    Mechanism: - Autoantibodies to presynaptic Voltage-Gated Calcium Channels (VGCC) - ↓ ACh release at NMJ - Repetitive use → ↑ Intracellular Ca²⁺ → Partially compensates

    Associated Tumor: - Small Cell Lung Cancer (60% of LEMS cases) - Other: Lymphoma, ovarian cancer

    Diagnosis: - Anti-VGCC antibodies (P/Q-type calcium channels) - EMG: Incremental response

    Treatment: - Treat underlying tumor (most important) - 3,4-Diaminopyridine (3,4-DAP): ↑ ACh release (blocks K+ channels) - IVIG, Plasmapheresis (if severe) - Pyridostigmine (AChE inhibitor, less effective than in MG)

    Comparison with Myasthenia Gravis:

    Feature LEMS Myasthenia Gravis
    Muscle weakness Proximal (legs > arms) Ocular, bulbar, then limb
    Effect of use IMPROVES WORSENS
    DTRs Decreased Normal
    Autonomic Yes (dry mouth, etc.) No
    Antibodies Anti-VGCC Anti-AChR, Anti-MuSK
    EMG Incremental response Decremental response
    Associated tumor SCLC Thymoma
    Edrophonium test Minimal response Positive

    Key Point: Weakness that IMPROVES with use + SCLC = Lambert-Eaton Myasthenic Syndrome

    Question 7¶

    What is the most common cause of SVC (Superior Vena Cava) syndrome?

    A. Lung cancer B. Lymphoma C. Mediastinal fibrosis D. Thrombosis from central venous catheter

    Answer: A. Lung cancer

    Explanation: - Most common cause of SVC syndrome: Lung cancer (60-80%) - SCLC > NSCLC (SCLC is central, rapid growth) - Right-sided tumors more common (SVC on right side)

    Other Causes: - Lymphoma (10-15%) - 2nd most common - Metastatic cancer - Mediastinal fibrosis (histoplasmosis, radiation) - Thrombosis (central lines, pacemakers)

    Clinical Features: - Facial and upper extremity edema (bilateral) - Distended neck and chest wall veins (collaterals) - Dyspnea, cough - Facial plethora, cyanosis - Headache (worse when bending forward, lying down)

    Diagnosis: - CT Chest with Contrast: Identifies obstruction, underlying cause - Tissue diagnosis (biopsy) before treatment (UNLESS life-threatening)

    Treatment:

    Cause Treatment
    SCLC Chemotherapy ± Radiation (Very responsive, rapid relief in days)
    NSCLC Radiation therapy, Stent (palliative)
    Lymphoma Chemotherapy (Very responsive)
    Thrombosis Anticoagulation, Thrombolysis, Stent

    Emergency Management (Life-threatening symptoms: stridor, altered mental status): - Head elevation - Oxygen - Corticosteroids (↓ edema) - Radiation (if malignancy) - Endovascular stent (immediate relief)

    Key Point: Lung cancer (especially SCLC) is the most common cause of SVC syndrome

    Question 8¶

    A 70-year-old smoker undergoes Low-Dose CT screening and is found to have a 1.2 cm solid pulmonary nodule in the right upper lobe with spiculated margins. What is the most appropriate next step?

    A. Repeat CT in 12 months B. PET-CT scan C. CT-guided biopsy D. Observation

    Answer: B. PET-CT scan

    Explanation: - Solid nodule >8-10 mm with spiculated margins → High suspicion for malignancy

    Features Suggesting Malignancy: - Size >8-10 mm ✓ (1.2 cm = 12 mm) - Spiculated (irregular) margins ✓ (corona radiata sign) - Upper lobe location - Solid (vs ground-glass) - Smoking history ✓

    Management Approach:

    Nodule Size Risk Features Management
    <6 mm Low risk No routine follow-up (unless high-risk patient)
    6-8 mm Low risk CT at 6-12 months, then annually
    >8 mm High risk (spiculated, smoker, upper lobe) PET-CT or Biopsy

    PET-CT (in this case): - Differentiate benign vs malignant (FDG-avid = malignant) - Staging (if malignant, detect metastases)

    Next Steps: - PET-positive → Biopsy (tissue diagnosis) → Staging → Treatment - PET-negative → Follow-up CT (may still be early cancer or lepidic adenocarcinoma)

    Why NOT Other Options?: - Repeat CT in 12 months (A): Too long a delay for high-risk nodule - CT-guided biopsy (C): Can be done, but PET-CT helps assess malignant probability and stage before invasive procedure - Observation (D): Inappropriate for high-risk nodule

    Key Point: Solid nodule >8 mm with spiculated margins → PET-CT (assess malignancy, stage) → Biopsy

    Question 9¶

    Which chemotherapy agent should NOT be used in squamous cell carcinoma of the lung?

    A. Cisplatin B. Carboplatin C. Pemetrexed D. Paclitaxel

    Answer: C. Pemetrexed

    Explanation: - Pemetrexed is CONTRAINDICATED in Squamous Cell Carcinoma

    Mechanism: - Pemetrexed: Antifolate agent (inhibits thymidylate synthase, DHFR, GAART) - Squamous cells have HIGH thymidylate synthase expression → Resistant to pemetrexed

    Clinical Trial Evidence: - Non-squamous NSCLC: Pemetrexed shows benefit - Squamous NSCLC: Pemetrexed shows NO benefit and may be inferior

    Chemotherapy Regimens:

    Histology Preferred Regimen
    Non-squamous (Adenocarcinoma, Large Cell) Cisplatin/Carboplatin + Pemetrexed
    Squamous Cell Cisplatin/Carboplatin + Paclitaxel/Gemcitabine

    Maintenance Therapy: - Non-squamous: Pemetrexed maintenance (after induction) - Squamous: NO pemetrexed (use other agents or observation)

    Why Other Options Are Used: - Cisplatin/Carboplatin (A, B): Platinum backbone for BOTH squamous and non-squamous - Paclitaxel (D): Used in squamous cell carcinoma (with platinum)

    Key Point: DO NOT use Pemetrexed in Squamous Cell Carcinoma (ineffective, contraindicated)

    Question 10¶

    A 62-year-old man with stage IA (T1bN0M0) adenocarcinoma of the lung undergoes lobectomy with complete resection. Tumor size is 1.8 cm. What is the most appropriate next step?

    A. Adjuvant chemotherapy B. Adjuvant radiation C. Adjuvant immunotherapy D. Observation

    Answer: D. Observation

    Explanation: - Stage IA1-IA3 (T1a-c N0 M0, tumor ≤3 cm) → Observation after complete resection

    Adjuvant Chemotherapy Indications (NSCLC): - Stage IB (>4 cm tumor) - Stage II (T3 N0 or any T N1) - Stage III (any T N2)

    This Patient: - Stage IA2 (T1b: >1 to ≤2 cm, N0, M0) - Tumor 1.8 cm (<2 cm, <4 cm) - NO adjuvant chemotherapy indicated

    Adjuvant Therapy Summary:

    Stage Tumor Size Adjuvant Therapy
    IA ≤3 cm, N0 Observation
    IB >3-4 cm, N0 Observation OR Chemotherapy (controversial)
    IB >4 cm, N0 Adjuvant Chemotherapy (4 cycles platinum doublet)
    II-IIIA Any size, N1-2 Adjuvant Chemotherapy (± Immunotherapy if N1-2)

    Adjuvant Radiation: Generally NOT recommended after complete resection (except N2 disease, incomplete resection)

    Adjuvant Immunotherapy (Atezolizumab): Approved for Stage II-IIIA with PD-L1 ≥1% (after chemotherapy)

    Key Point: Stage IA NSCLC (≤3 cm, N0) after complete resection → Observation (NO adjuvant therapy)

    SUMMARY¶

    Lung Cancer is the leading cause of cancer death worldwide: - Most common type: Adenocarcinoma (40%, peripheral, non-smokers) - Strongest smoking association: Squamous and SCLC - Worst prognosis: SCLC (rapid growth, early metastasis)

    Diagnosis: - HRCT → Biopsy (bronchoscopy/CT-guided) → Staging (PET-CT, Brain MRI for SCLC/advanced NSCLC) - Molecular testing (NSCLC): EGFR, ALK, ROS1, PD-L1

    Treatment: - NSCLC: - Early (I-II): Surgery ± Adjuvant chemo - Locally Advanced (III): Chemoradiation → Durvalumab - Metastatic (IV): - Driver mutation+ → Targeted therapy (EGFR: Osimertinib, ALK: Alectinib) - PD-L1 ≥50% → Pembrolizumab - Otherwise → Chemo ± Immunotherapy - SCLC: - Limited: Chemoradiation → PCI - Extensive: Chemotherapy

    Paraneoplastic Syndromes: - Squamous: Hypercalcemia (PTHrP) - SCLC: SIADH, Ectopic ACTH, LEMS

    Screening: Low-Dose CT for age 50-80, ≥20 pack-years, current/quit within 15 years
