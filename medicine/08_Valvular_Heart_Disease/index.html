    Overview¶

    Normal Valve Areas¶

    Mitral valve:      4-6 cm²
Aortic valve:      3-4 cm²
Tricuspid valve:   7-9 cm²
Pulmonary valve:   5-6 cm²

    Classification¶

    • Stenosis: Narrowed orifice → Obstruction to forward flow
    • Regurgitation (Insufficiency): Incompetent valve → Backward flow
    • Mixed: Stenosis + Regurgitation

    Mitral Stenosis¶

    Definition¶

    Narrowing of mitral valve orifice → Obstruction to LV filling

    Severity: - Mild: Valve area 1.5-2.5 cm² - Moderate: 1.0-1.5 cm² - Severe: <1 cm² - Critical: <0.6 cm²

    Etiology¶

    Rheumatic Heart Disease (RHD) - 99% of cases (Most common cause)

    Other Rare Causes: - Congenital (Parachute mitral valve) - Calcific (elderly) - Carcinoid - SLE (Libman-Sacks endocarditis) - Rheumatoid arthritis

    Key Point: Almost all MS is rheumatic in origin

    Pathophysiology¶

    Rheumatic Changes: 1. Commissural fusion 2. Leaflet thickening and fibrosis 3. Chordal fusion and shortening 4. Calcification (chronic)

    Result: "Fish-mouth" or "buttonhole" orifice

    Hemodynamic Consequences:

    Normal MVA: 4-6 cm²
        ↓
Stenotic MVA: <2 cm²
        ↓
↑ LA Pressure (to maintain CO)
        ↓
↑ Pulmonary Venous Pressure
        ↓
↑ Pulmonary Capillary Pressure (>25 mmHg)
        ↓
PULMONARY EDEMA (dyspnea)
        ↓
Chronic ↑ Pulmonary Pressure
        ↓
PULMONARY HYPERTENSION
        ↓
RV Failure → Tricuspid Regurgitation

    Critical Gradient: - Normal: Minimal gradient across MV - MS: LA-LV gradient ≥10 mmHg → Symptoms

    Factors ↑ Gradient (Worsen Symptoms): - ↑ Heart rate (↓ diastolic filling time) - Most important - ↑ Cardiac output (Exercise, pregnancy, fever) - Atrial fibrillation (loss of atrial kick, rapid rate)

    Clinical Features¶

    Symptoms:

    Early (Mild MS): - Asymptomatic for years (latent period: 10-20 years after ARF)

    Progressive: - Dyspnea on exertion (DOE) - Most common symptom - Orthopnea - Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea (PND) - Fatigue

    Advanced (Severe MS): - Hemoptysis (3 types): - Pulmonary edema: Pink frothy sputum - Pulmonary venous rupture: Frank hemoptysis (rupture of bronchial veins) - Pulmonary infarction: From AF embolism - Hoarseness (Ortner's syndrome - LA compression of left recurrent laryngeal nerve) - Dysphagia (LA compression of esophagus) - Right heart failure: Edema, ascites, hepatomegaly

    Complications: - Atrial fibrillation (40-80%) - LA dilation - Systemic embolism (10-20%) - LA thrombus (especially with AF) - Infective endocarditis (Rare in pure MS) - Pulmonary hypertension - RV failure

    Signs:

    General: - Mitral facies: Malar flush (pinkish-purple discoloration of cheeks) - Low-volume pulse (low CO)

    Palpation: - Tapping apex beat (palpable S1) - Diastolic thrill at apex (if severe) - RV heave (if pulmonary HTN)

    Auscultation - KEY FINDINGS:

    Murmur: 

    ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      MITRAL STENOSIS MURMUR                        │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ • LOW-PITCHED, RUMBLING, MID-DIASTOLIC             │
│ • Best heard at APEX                               │
│ • With BELL of stethoscope                         │
│ • In LEFT LATERAL DECUBITUS position               │
│ • Accentuated by EXERCISE (post-exercise auscult.) │
│ • Preceded by OPENING SNAP (OS)                    │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Additional Sounds: - Loud S1 (increased closing force - pliable leaflets) - Soft S1: If heavily calcified (rigid leaflets) - Opening Snap (OS): High-pitched sound in early diastole - Timing: S2 - OS interval SHORT → Severe MS - S2 - OS >80 ms: Mild - S2 - OS <60 ms: Severe - Graham Steell murmur: High-pitched, early diastolic (Pulmonary regurgitation from severe pulmonary HTN)

    Mnemonic for MS Auscultation: "Loud S1, Opening Snap, Rumbling Diastolic Murmur"

    Investigations¶

    ECG: - P mitrale: Broad, notched P wave in lead II (>120 ms) - LA enlargement - AF (40-80% in severe MS) - RVH (if pulmonary HTN) - R/S ratio >1 in V1

    Chest X-ray: 

    ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      CHEST X-RAY IN MITRAL STENOSIS                │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ PA View:                                           │
│ • LA enlargement (Straightening of left heart      │
│   border, Double density, Splaying of carina >90°) │
│ • Pulmonary venous congestion (Upper lobe diversion)│
│ • Kerley B lines (Interstitial edema)              │
│ • Pulmonary edema (Bat's wing)                     │
│ • Pulmonary artery enlargement                     │
│                                                    │
│ Lateral View:                                      │
│ • LA pushes esophagus backwards (Barium swallow)   │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Echocardiography - GOLD STANDARD:

    2D Echo: - Valve area (Planimetry) - Mean gradient (Doppler) - LA size - Thrombus in LA/LAA (TEE more sensitive) - Pulmonary artery pressure - RV function

    Wilkins Score (For PBMV suitability): - Assesses: Leaflet mobility, Thickening, Calcification, Subvalvular thickening - Score 0-4 for each (Total: 0-16) - Score ≤8: Suitable for PBMV - Score >8: Consider surgery

    Cardiac Catheterization: - Rarely needed (echo sufficient) - If echo non-diagnostic or discrepant - Measure: LA-LV gradient, Valve area, Pulmonary pressures

    Management¶

    Medical:¶

    1. Diuretics: - Furosemide - Reduce pulmonary congestion - First-line for symptoms

    2. Rate Control (if AF): - Beta-blockers (Metoprolol, Bisoprolol) - DOC - Calcium channel blockers (Diltiazem, Verapamil) - Digoxin (if HF present) - Goal: Resting HR 60-80 bpm (prolong diastolic filling time)

    3. Anticoagulation: - Warfarin (INR 2-3) if: - AF (paroxysmal or permanent) - MANDATORY - LA thrombus - Prior embolism - LA size >55 mm (consider) - DOACs NOT recommended in MS (especially severe or AF with MS)

    4. Infective Endocarditis Prophylaxis: - NOT routinely recommended (per AHA 2007 guidelines)

    5. Rheumatic Fever Prophylaxis: - If history of ARF

    Interventional/Surgical:¶

    Indications for Intervention: - Symptomatic (NYHA class II-IV) with: - Moderate-severe MS (MVA <1.5 cm² and/or mean gradient >5 mmHg)

    ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      TREATMENT OPTIONS FOR MITRAL STENOSIS                 │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                            │
│ 1. PBMV (Percutaneous Balloon Mitral Valvuloplasty)        │
│    • DOC for suitable anatomy                              │
│    • Wilkins score ≤8                                      │
│    • No/Minimal MR                                         │
│    • No LA thrombus (TEE to rule out)                      │
│    • Non-calcified valve                                   │
│    • Success: MVA increases to 1.5-2 cm²                   │
│    • Complications: MR (severe - 2-10%), Tamponade, Stroke │
│                                                            │
│ 2. Open Mitral Commissurotomy (OMC)                        │
│    • If unsuitable for PBMV                                │
│    • Wilkins score >8 but pliable valve                    │
│    • Young patient, no calcification                       │
│    • Best long-term results                                │
│                                                            │
│ 3. Mitral Valve Replacement (MVR)                          │
│    • Heavily calcified valve                               │
│    • Severe MR                                             │
│    • Failed PBMV/OMC                                       │
│    • Prosthetic valve: Mechanical or Bioprosthetic         │
│                                                            │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Special Situations:

    Pregnancy: - MS poorly tolerated (↑ CO, ↑ HR) - If symptomatic despite medical therapy → PBMV (safe in 2nd/3rd trimester)

    Asymptomatic Severe MS: - Consider intervention if: - Pulmonary HTN (PA systolic >50 mmHg at rest or >60 mmHg with exercise) - New-onset AF - Planning pregnancy

    Mitral Regurgitation¶

    Definition¶

    Incomplete closure of mitral valve → Backflow from LV to LA during systole

    Severity (Regurgitant Fraction): - Mild: <30% - Moderate: 30-50% - Severe: >50%

    Etiology¶

    Acute MR: - Papillary muscle rupture (Post-MI) - Most common cause of acute MR - Chordae tendineae rupture (Trauma, IE, MVP) - Infective endocarditis (Leaflet perforation) - Acute rheumatic fever (Rare)

    Chronic MR:

    Primary (Organic) - Valve/Apparatus Abnormality: - Mitral valve prolapse (Most common in developed countries) - Rheumatic heart disease (Most common in developing countries) - Infective endocarditis - Degenerative (Myxomatous, Barlow's disease) - Ischemic (Papillary muscle dysfunction) - Congenital (Cleft mitral valve) - Connective tissue disorders (Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos)

    Secondary (Functional) - LV Dysfunction: - Dilated cardiomyopathy (LV dilation → Mitral annular dilation) - Ischemic cardiomyopathy

    Pathophysiology¶

    Acute MR: 

    Sudden MR
    ↓
↑ LA Pressure (normal-sized, non-compliant LA)
    ↓
↑ Pulmonary Venous/Capillary Pressure
    ↓
ACUTE PULMONARY EDEMA
    ↓
↓ Forward Cardiac Output
    ↓
CARDIOGENIC SHOCK
    Medical Emergency - Requires urgent surgery

    Chronic MR: 

    Chronic MR
    ↓
↑ LA Volume (compensated - LA dilates)
    ↓
LV Volume Overload (↑ Preload)
    ↓
LV Eccentric Hypertrophy (LV dilation)
    ↓
Compensated Phase (Asymptomatic - years)
    ↓
LV Dysfunction (↓ EF)
    ↓
Decompensated HF, Pulmonary HTN, AF

    Key Hemodynamics: - LV: Volume overload, ↑ Preload, ↓ Afterload (regurgitant pathway) - LA: Dilates (v wave prominent)

    Clinical Features¶

    Symptoms:

    Acute MR: - Sudden-onset severe dyspnea - Pulmonary edema - Cardiogenic shock

    Chronic MR: - Asymptomatic for years (compensated phase) - Dyspnea (progressive) - Fatigue (low forward CO) - Palpitations (AF) - Orthopnea, PND (late)

    Signs:

    Palpation: - Hyperdynamic, displaced apex (LV dilation) - Systolic thrill at apex (severe MR)

    Auscultation:

    ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      MITRAL REGURGITATION MURMUR                   │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ • HIGH-PITCHED, BLOWING, HOLOSYSTOLIC              │
│ • Best heard at APEX                               │
│ • Radiates to AXILLA (or left infrascapular area)  │
│ • Band-shaped (same intensity throughout systole)  │
│ • Soft S1 (incomplete coaptation)                  │
│ • Loud P2 (if pulmonary HTN)                       │
│ • S3 (LV volume overload)                          │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Acute MR: May have soft murmur (acute LA pressure rise → small LA-LV gradient)

    Investigations¶

    ECG: - P mitrale, AF (LA enlargement) - LVH (chronic MR)

    Chest X-ray: - LA enlargement - LV enlargement (chronic) - Pulmonary edema (acute)

    Echocardiography: - Severity: Color Doppler (regurgitant jet area, vena contracta) - Mechanism: Leaflet, chordae, annular abnormalities - LV size and function (EF, dimensions) - LA size

    Cardiac MRI: - Quantify regurgitant volume/fraction

    Cardiac Catheterization: - V wave in PCWP tracing (LA pressure)

    Management¶

    Acute Severe MR: - Medical Emergency - ICU, intubation if pulmonary edema - Vasodilators: Nitroprusside (↓ Afterload → ↑ Forward flow) - Diuretics: Furosemide - Inotropes: Dobutamine (if cardiogenic shock) - IABP (Intraaortic balloon pump) - Bridge to surgery - Urgent surgical repair/replacement

    Chronic MR:

    Medical: - Asymptomatic, normal LV function: - Observation (serial echo every 6-12 months) - No role for prophylactic vasodilators (if normal BP, EF)

    • Symptomatic or LV dysfunction:
    • Diuretics (pulmonary congestion)
    • ACE inhibitors/ARBs (LV remodeling, afterload reduction)
    • Beta-blockers (if HF)
    • Anticoagulation (if AF)
    • Definitive: Surgery

    Surgical:

    Indications: 1. Symptomatic severe MR (NYHA ≥II) 2. Asymptomatic severe MR with: - LV dysfunction (EF ≤60% or LVESD ≥40 mm) - AF - Pulmonary HTN (PA systolic >50 mmHg)

    Options: - Mitral valve repair (Preferred - better outcomes, no anticoagulation) - Annuloplasty, chordal repair, leaflet resection - Mitral valve replacement (If repair not feasible)

    Aortic Stenosis¶

    Definition¶

    Narrowing of aortic valve orifice → Obstruction to LV outflow

    Severity: - Mild: Valve area 1.5-2.0 cm², Mean gradient <25 mmHg - Moderate: 1.0-1.5 cm², 25-40 mmHg - Severe: <1.0 cm², >40 mmHg - Critical: <0.6 cm², >50 mmHg

    Etiology¶

    Age-Dependent:

    <70 years: - Bicuspid aortic valve (Most common) - Congenital (1-2% population) - Premature degeneration/calcification

    >70 years: - Senile degenerative calcific AS (Most common) - Age-related wear-and-tear, calcification

    Any Age: - Rheumatic heart disease (Always with mitral involvement)

    Rare: - Congenital (Unicuspid, Quadricuspid) - Familial hypercholesterolemia (early calcification)

    Pathophysiology¶

    Aortic Stenosis
    ↓
↑ LV Afterload (Pressure Overload)
    ↓
LV Concentric Hypertrophy (Thick walls, small cavity)
    ↓
Compensated Phase (Normal EF, Asymptomatic - years)
    ↓
↑ LV Wall Stress, ↑ O₂ Demand, ↓ Coronary Perfusion
    ↓
↓ Coronary Flow Reserve
    ↓
ANGINA (even without CAD)
    ↓
↓ Cardiac Output (Fixed)
    ↓
SYNCOPE (exertional - unable to ↑ CO)
    ↓
LV Systolic Dysfunction (Decompensation)
    ↓
HEART FAILURE (↓ EF)

    Classic Triad (Late Findings): 1. Angina 2. Syncope (exertional) 3. Dyspnea (Heart failure)

    Prognosis after Symptoms: - Angina: 50% mortality at 5 years - Syncope: 50% mortality at 3 years - Heart Failure: 50% mortality at 2 years - Sudden death: 5-15% (usually after symptoms)

    Clinical Features¶

    Symptoms: - Asymptomatic for decades (long latent period - 30-50 years) - Classic Triad: 1. Angina (exertional) - ↓ Coronary reserve 2. Syncope (exertional) - Fixed CO, vasodilation in muscles 3. Dyspnea (exertional → rest) - Heart failure

    Signs:

    Peripheral: - Pulsus parvus et tardus: Small, slow-rising pulse (delayed peak) - Narrow pulse pressure - Sustained, non-displaced apex (LVH)

    Auscultation:

    ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      AORTIC STENOSIS MURMUR                        │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ • HARSH, CRESCENDO-DECRESCENDO (Ejection) SYSTOLIC │
│ • Best heard at RIGHT UPPER STERNAL BORDER (RUSB)  │
│   (2nd right intercostal space)                    │
│ • Radiates to CAROTIDS                             │
│ • Increases with leg raise, decreases with Valsalva│
│ • Soft/Absent A2 (immobile valve)                  │
│ • S4 (LVH with diastolic dysfunction)              │
│ • Paradoxical splitting of S2 (severe AS)          │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Gallavardin phenomenon: High-frequency AS murmur radiates to apex (mimics MR)

    Investigations¶

    ECG: - LVH with strain (ST depression, T inversion in I, aVL, V5-V6) - LAE (LV diastolic dysfunction) - LBBB (late)

    Chest X-ray: - Normal cardiac silhouette (compensated) - LV enlargement (decompensated) - Aortic valve calcification - Post-stenotic dilation of ascending aorta

    Echocardiography - GOLD STANDARD: - Valve area (Continuity equation) - Mean gradient - Peak velocity - LV wall thickness, EF - Valve morphology (Bicuspid, calcification)

    Cardiac Catheterization: - Coronary angiography: Before surgery (if age >40 men, >50 women, or risk factors) - Measure gradient (if echo non-diagnostic)

    Exercise Testing: - CONTRAINDICATED in symptomatic severe AS - Consider in asymptomatic severe AS (supervised, to assess symptoms)

    Management¶

    Medical: - No medical therapy prevents progression or delays need for surgery - Avoid: Vasodilators (may cause hypotension - fixed CO) - Symptomatic HF: - Diuretics (cautious - need high filling pressures) - ACE inhibitors (low dose, cautious)

    Surgical - DEFINITIVE:

    Indications: 1. Symptomatic severe AS (Any symptom - angina, syncope, dyspnea) 2. Asymptomatic severe AS with: - EF <50% - Undergoing other cardiac surgery (CABG, other valve) - Abnormal exercise test (symptoms, hypotension) - Very severe AS (Vmax >5 m/s, mean gradient >60 mmHg)

    Options: - Surgical Aortic Valve Replacement (SAVR): - Mechanical valve (Younger, no contraindication to anticoagulation) - Bioprosthetic valve (Elderly >65-70, contraindication to anticoagulation)

    • Transcatheter Aortic Valve Replacement (TAVR):
    • Indications: High surgical risk, elderly (>75-80), severe comorbidities
    • Via femoral artery (or transapical)

    Balloon Aortic Valvuloplasty (BAV): - Palliative only (high restenosis rate) - Bridge to SAVR/TAVR - Contraindication to surgery

    Aortic Regurgitation¶

    Definition¶

    Incompetent aortic valve → Backflow from aorta to LV during diastole

    Etiology¶

    Acute AR: - Infective endocarditis (Leaflet perforation) - Most common - Aortic dissection (Type A) - Trauma

    Chronic AR:

    Valve Abnormality: - Bicuspid aortic valve - Rheumatic heart disease (Always with other valve involvement) - Infective endocarditis (Healed) - Degenerative (Senile) - Myxomatous (Marfan, Ehlers-Danlos) - Rheumatoid arthritis, SLE

    Aortic Root Abnormality: - Hypertension (Most common overall) - Marfan syndrome (Aortic root dilation) - Ankylosing spondylitis (Aortitis) - Takayasu arteritis, Giant cell arteritis - Syphilitic aortitis (Historical) - Aortic dissection (Chronic)

    Pathophysiology¶

    Acute AR: 

    Sudden AR
    ↓
LV Volume Overload (normal-sized, non-compliant LV)
    ↓
↑ LVEDP
    ↓
↓ Coronary Perfusion (diastolic pressure low)
    ↓
ACUTE PULMONARY EDEMA + CARDIOGENIC SHOCK

    Chronic AR: 

    Chronic AR
    ↓
LV Volume Overload (Both ↑ Preload and ↑ Afterload)
    ↓
LV Eccentric Hypertrophy (Largest heart in cardiology)
    ↓
Compensated Phase (High stroke volume - wide pulse pressure)
    ↓
LV Dysfunction
    ↓
HEART FAILURE

    Clinical Features¶

    Symptoms:

    Acute AR: - Sudden dyspnea, pulmonary edema - Cardiogenic shock

    Chronic AR: - Asymptomatic for years (decades) - Exertional dyspnea - Orthopnea, PND - Angina (↓ Diastolic BP → ↓ Coronary perfusion) - Palpitations (Large stroke volume)

    Signs:

    Wide Pulse Pressure (High SBP, Low DBP): - SBP >160, DBP <50 mmHg (severe AR)

    Eponymous Signs (Peripheral Signs of Chronic AR):

    ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      PERIPHERAL SIGNS OF AORTIC REGURGITATION          │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ • Corrigan's pulse: Water-hammer pulse (bounding)      │
│ • de Musset's sign: Head bobbing with heartbeat        │
│ • Quincke's sign: Capillary pulsations (nail bed)      │
│ • Traube's sign: "Pistol shot" sounds over femoral    │
│ • Duroziez's sign: To-and-fro murmur over femoral     │
│                    (with compression)                   │
│ • Hill's sign: Popliteal SBP >20 mmHg higher than      │
│                brachial (severe AR)                     │
│ • Müller's sign: Uvula pulsations                      │
│ • Rosenbach's sign: Liver pulsations                   │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Auscultation:

    ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      AORTIC REGURGITATION MURMUR                   │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ • HIGH-PITCHED, BLOWING, EARLY DIASTOLIC           │
│ • Best heard at LEFT STERNAL BORDER (3rd-4th ICS)  │
│ • Patient sitting, leaning forward, in expiration  │
│ • Radiates to apex                                 │
│ • Longer murmur → More severe AR                   │
│ • Austin Flint murmur: Mid-diastolic rumble at apex│
│   (Regurgitant jet hits anterior mitral leaflet)   │
│ • S3 (LV volume overload)                          │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Acute AR: - Soft murmur (Early closure of mitral valve due to ↑ LVEDP) - Soft S1

    Investigations¶

    ECG: - LVH with strain (chronic AR)

    Chest X-ray: - LV enlargement ("Boot-shaped" heart) - Aortic root dilation

    Echocardiography: - Severity: Color Doppler (jet width, vena contracta) - LV size and function - Aortic root size - Early mitral valve closure (acute AR)

    Cardiac MRI: - Quantify regurgitant volume

    Management¶

    Acute Severe AR: - Medical Emergency - Vasodilators: Nitroprusside (↓ Afterload → ↓ Regurgitant volume) - Inotropes: Dobutamine - AVOID β-blockers (prolong diastole → ↑ Regurgitation) - Urgent surgical replacement

    Chronic AR:

    Medical: - Asymptomatic, normal LV function: - Vasodilators (Nifedipine, ACE inhibitors) - May delay progression - Serial echo every 6-12 months

    • Symptomatic or LV dysfunction:
    • Diuretics, ACE inhibitors (HF)
    • Definitive: Surgery

    Surgical:

    Indications: 1. Symptomatic severe AR 2. Asymptomatic severe AR with: - EF <50% - LVESD >50 mm or LVEDD >70 mm - Undergoing other cardiac surgery

    Options: - Aortic valve replacement (Mechanical or Bioprosthetic) - Valve repair (If feasible - bicuspid, prolapse) - Aortic root replacement (If root dilation >50-55 mm)

    Tricuspid & Pulmonary Valve Disease¶

    Tricuspid Regurgitation (TR)¶

    Causes: - Functional (Secondary to RV dilation) - Most common - Pulmonary HTN from left heart disease (MS, LV failure) - Organic: - Rheumatic (with mitral/aortic) - Infective endocarditis (IVDU) - Carcinoid, Ebstein's anomaly

    Clinical Features: - Pulsatile liver (systolic pulsations in JVP - cv wave) - Prominent v wave in JVP - Holosystolic murmur at left lower sternal border (increases with inspiration - Carvallo's sign) - S3 (RV) - Peripheral edema, ascites

    Management: - Treat underlying cause (Pulmonary HTN, RV failure) - Diuretics - Surgery: If severe and undergoing left-sided valve surgery

    Tricuspid Stenosis (TS)¶

    Causes: - Rheumatic (Always with mitral stenosis)

    Clinical Features: - Giant a wave in JVP - Mid-diastolic murmur at left lower sternal border (increases with inspiration) - Hepatomegaly, ascites (worse than MS alone)

    Management: - Balloon valvuloplasty or Surgical repair/replacement

    Pulmonary Stenosis (PS)¶

    Causes: - Congenital (Most common) - Carcinoid, Rheumatic (rare)

    Clinical Features: - Ejection systolic murmur at left upper sternal border - Wide splitting of S2 (delayed P2) - RVH

    Management: - Balloon valvuloplasty (DOC)

    Pulmonary Regurgitation (PR)¶

    Causes: - Idiopathic dilation of pulmonary artery - Pulmonary HTN (Graham Steell murmur) - Post-valvuloplasty, Tetralogy of Fallot repair

    Clinical Features: - Early diastolic murmur at left upper sternal border (increases with inspiration)

    Management: - Usually well tolerated - Surgery if severe with RV dysfunction

    Prosthetic Valves¶

    Types¶

    Mechanical Valves: - Bileaflet (St. Jude, Carbomedics) - Most common - Tilting disc (Bjork-Shiley) - Ball-and-cage (Starr-Edwards) - Obsolete

    Advantages: - Durable (Lasts 20-30 years)

    Disadvantages: - Requires lifelong anticoagulation (Warfarin, INR 2.5-3.5) - Thromboembolism risk - Prosthetic valve endocarditis - Hemolysis

    Bioprosthetic Valves: - Porcine (pig valve) - Bovine pericardial

    Advantages: - No anticoagulation (or only 3-6 months)

    Disadvantages: - Limited durability (10-15 years) - Structural deterioration (calcification)

    Indications for Mechanical vs Bioprosthetic¶

    Mechanical: - Age <60-65 years (aortic), <65-70 (mitral) - Already on anticoagulation (AF, prior VTE) - No contraindication to anticoagulation

    Bioprosthetic: - Age >65-70 years - Contraindication to anticoagulation - Desire to avoid anticoagulation (Women of childbearing age - Warfarin teratogenic) - Limited life expectancy

    Complications¶

    1. Thromboembolism: - Mechanical > Bioprosthetic - Mitral > Aortic - Prevention: Warfarin (INR 2.5-3.5 for mechanical)

    2. Prosthetic Valve Endocarditis: - Early (<12 months): S. epidermidis (Coagulase-negative Staph) - Late (>12 months): Similar to native valve (S. viridans, S. aureus)

    3. Paravalvular Leak: - Regurgitation around valve (not through) - Causes: Infection, suture dehiscence - → Hemolysis, HF

    4. Structural Deterioration: - Bioprosthetic (calcification, tear) - Usually after 10-15 years

    5. Hemolysis: - Mechanical valves - Usually mild (↑ LDH, ↓ Haptoglobin)

    6. Pannus Formation: - Fibrous tissue overgrowth → Stenosis

    Anticoagulation¶

    Mechanical Valves: - Warfarin (lifelong) - INR target: - 2.5-3.5: Mechanical mitral, Older generation valves - 2.0-3.0: Mechanical aortic (bileaflet, no other risk factors)

    Bioprosthetic Valves: - Warfarin for 3-6 months (until endothelialization) - Then aspirin (or stop if no other indication)

    Pregnancy: - Warfarin teratogenic (1st trimester) - Options: - Switch to UFH or LMWH in 1st trimester - Warfarin in 2nd/3rd trimester (safer) - or UFH/LMWH throughout (less effective - ↑ valve thrombosis risk)

    NEET PG High-Yield Points¶

    🔥 MUST KNOW¶

    1. Mitral Stenosis:
    2. 99% Rheumatic in origin
    3. Most common RHD lesion
    4. Murmur: Low-pitched, rumbling, mid-diastolic at apex
    5. Loud S1 + Opening Snap
    6. S2-OS interval SHORT → Severe MS

    7. Treatment: PBMV (DOC if Wilkins ≤8), OMC, MVR

    8. Mitral Regurgitation:

    9. Acute: Papillary muscle rupture (post-MI) - Emergency
    10. Chronic: MVP (developed), RHD (developing)
    11. Murmur: High-pitched, blowing, holosystolic at apex → Axilla

    12. Treatment: Repair > Replacement

    13. Aortic Stenosis:

    14. <70y: Bicuspid AV, >70y: Senile calcific
    15. Classic Triad: Angina, Syncope, Dyspnea
    16. Prognosis: Angina (5y), Syncope (3y), HF (2y) - 50% mortality
    17. Murmur: Harsh, crescendo-decrescendo systolic at RUSB → Carotids
    18. Pulse: Parvus et tardus (small, slow-rising)

    19. Treatment: SAVR or TAVR (if symptomatic or EF <50%)

    20. Aortic Regurgitation:

    21. Acute: IE, Aortic dissection - Emergency
    22. Chronic: HTN (most common), Bicuspid AV, Marfan
    23. Murmur: High-pitched, blowing, early diastolic at left sternal border
    24. Austin Flint murmur: Mid-diastolic rumble at apex
    25. Wide pulse pressure (High SBP, Low DBP)
    26. Peripheral signs: Corrigan's, de Musset's, Quincke's, etc.

    27. Treatment: Vasodilators (chronic), Surgery if symptomatic/EF <50%/LVESD >50mm

    28. Prosthetic Valves:

    29. Mechanical: Lifelong anticoagulation (Warfarin INR 2.5-3.5)
    30. Bioprosthetic: No anticoagulation (or 3-6 months)
    31. Age <65: Mechanical, >65: Bioprosthetic

    Practice Questions¶

    Question 1¶

    A 40-year-old female with dyspnea on exertion has a low-pitched, rumbling, mid-diastolic murmur at the apex with a loud S1 and an opening snap. Chest X-ray shows LA enlargement and Kerley B lines. What is the most likely diagnosis?

    A) Mitral regurgitation B) Mitral stenosis C) Aortic stenosis D) Tricuspid stenosis

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: B) Mitral stenosis** **Explanation:** - **Classic MS findings:** - **Low-pitched, rumbling, mid-diastolic murmur at apex** (Hallmark) - **Loud S1** (increased closing force - pliable leaflets) - **Opening snap** (High-pitched sound in early diastole) - **LA enlargement** (on CXR) - **Kerley B lines** (Interstitial edema from pulmonary venous congestion) - **99% of MS is Rheumatic** in origin **Other findings:** - Orthopnea, PND, hemoptysis - AF, systemic embolism - Right heart failure (late)

    Question 2¶

    A 60-year-old male presents with exertional syncope and chest pain. On examination, pulse is small and slow-rising. A harsh, crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur is heard at the right upper sternal border radiating to the carotids. What is the most appropriate management if he is symptomatic with severe stenosis?

    A) Medical therapy with vasodilators B) Percutaneous balloon valvuloplasty C) Aortic valve replacement D) Observation with serial echocardiography

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: C) Aortic valve replacement** **Explanation:** - **Diagnosis:** Severe symptomatic aortic stenosis - **Classic triad:** Angina + Syncope + Dyspnea - **Pulse:** Parvus et tardus (small, slow-rising) - **Murmur:** Harsh, systolic at RUSB → Carotids - **Symptomatic severe AS** is an **absolute indication for surgery** - **Aortic valve replacement** (SAVR or TAVR) **Prognosis without surgery:** - **50% mortality** at 2-5 years after symptoms develop **Why not others:** - **Vasodilators:** Contraindicated (may cause hypotension) - **Balloon valvuloplasty:** High restenosis, only palliative - **Observation:** WRONG - symptomatic severe AS requires surgery

    Question 3¶

    A patient with chronic aortic regurgitation has a high-pitched, blowing, early diastolic murmur at the left sternal border and a mid-diastolic rumble at the apex. What is the apical murmur called?

    A) Carey Coombs murmur B) Graham Steell murmur C) Austin Flint murmur D) Still's murmur

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: C) Austin Flint murmur** **Explanation:** - **Austin Flint murmur:** Mid-diastolic rumble at apex in AR - **Mechanism:** Regurgitant jet from AR hits anterior mitral leaflet → Functional MS **Differentials:** - **Carey Coombs murmur:** Mid-diastolic rumble in acute rheumatic fever (mitral valvulitis) - **Graham Steell murmur:** Early diastolic murmur at LUSB (Pulmonary regurgitation from severe pulmonary HTN) - **Still's murmur:** Innocent systolic murmur in children **AR findings:** - Early diastolic murmur at left sternal border - Wide pulse pressure - Peripheral signs (Corrigan's, Quincke's, etc.) - Austin Flint murmur (if severe)
