    Definition and Classification¶

    Anatomic Classification¶

    Upper Gastrointestinal Bleeding (UGIB): - Proximal to ligament of Treitz (duodenojejunal junction) - Includes: - Esophagus - Stomach - Duodenum (first 3-4 portions)

    Lower Gastrointestinal Bleeding (LGIB): - Distal to ligament of Treitz - Includes: - Small bowel (jejunum, ileum) - Colon - Rectum, anus

    Obscure GI Bleeding: - Source not identified on upper endoscopy and colonoscopy - Usually small bowel (jejunum, ileum)

    Clinical Presentation¶

    Hematemesis: - Vomiting blood (red or coffee-ground) - Indicates UGIB (above ligament of Treitz) - Coffee-ground: Blood exposed to gastric acid (oxidized hemoglobin)

    Melena: - Black, tarry, foul-smelling stool - Indicates UPPER GI source (usually) - Requires ≥50-100 mL blood in upper GI tract - Blood breakdown by intestinal bacteria → black color - Can occur with proximal colon bleeding (if slow transit) - Requires ≥14 hours transit time for black color

    Hematochezia: - Bright red blood per rectum - Usually LOWER GI source (colon, rectum) - Can be UPPER GI if: - Massive, rapid bleeding (>1 L) with rapid transit - Associated with hemodynamic instability

    Occult Bleeding: - Positive fecal occult blood test (FOBT) OR - Iron deficiency anemia (no visible bleeding) - Requires evaluation (usually colonoscopy + EGD)

    Epidemiology¶

    Upper GI Bleeding: - Incidence: 100-200/100,000 annually - More common than lower GI bleeding (2-4:1) - Mortality: 5-10% (higher than LGIB) - Risk factors: - NSAIDs, aspirin, anticoagulants - H. pylori - Cirrhosis (varices)

    Lower GI Bleeding: - Incidence: 20-30/100,000 annually - More common in elderly - Mortality: 2-4% (lower than UGIB) - Most common cause: - <50 years: Hemorrhoids, anal fissures, IBD - >50 years: Diverticulosis, angiodysplasia, colorectal cancer

    Upper GI Bleeding¶

    Etiology¶

    Common Causes (Mnemonic: "VAGUE"): - **V**arices (esophageal, gastric) - **A**rteriovenous malformations (angiodysplasia) - **G**astric/duodenal ulcers - **U**pper GI malignancy - **E**sophagitis, erosions (Mallory-Weiss)

    1. Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD) - 40-50%¶

    Most common cause of UGIB

    Location: - Duodenal ulcer (DU): More common than gastric ulcer - Gastric ulcer (GU)

    Etiology: - H. pylori (50-70%) - NSAIDs (20-30%) - Stress (ICU patients) - Zollinger-Ellison syndrome

    Presentation: - Hematemesis (coffee-ground or fresh blood) - Melena - Epigastric pain (may be absent if NSAIDs)

    Diagnosis: - Upper endoscopy (EGD): - Identifies ulcer - Forrest Classification (predicts rebleeding risk)

    Forrest Classification:

    Class Description Rebleeding Risk Management
    Ia Spurting arterial bleeding 90% Urgent endoscopic therapy
    Ib Oozing bleeding 50% Endoscopic therapy
    IIa Visible vessel (non-bleeding) 40-50% Endoscopic therapy
    IIb Adherent clot 20-30% Endoscopic therapy (irrigate clot)
    IIc Flat pigmented spot <10% Medical therapy (no endoscopic Rx)
    III Clean base <5% Medical therapy

    High-risk features (Ia, Ib, IIa, IIb): Require endoscopic therapy

    Endoscopic Therapy: - Dual therapy (combination improves hemostasis): - Epinephrine injection (1:10,000) PLUS - Mechanical (hemoclip) OR Thermal (electrocoagulation, heater probe)

    • Epinephrine alone: NOT recommended (higher rebleeding)

    Medical Management: - High-dose PPI: - IV bolus: Pantoprazole 80 mg OR Esomeprazole 80 mg - Continuous infusion: 8 mg/hour × 72 hours (after endoscopy if high-risk stigmata) - THEN Oral PPI BID × 14 days → Daily

    • H. pylori eradication: If positive (prevents recurrence)

    • Discontinue NSAIDs: Switch to acetaminophen or COX-2 inhibitor + PPI

    Rebleeding: - Occurs in 10-20% within 72 hours - Repeat endoscopy (attempt hemostasis) - If fails: Surgery or Angiography with embolization

    2. Esophageal Varices - 10-20%¶

    Portal Hypertension → Portosystemic collaterals → Varices

    Location: - Lower esophagus (most common site) - Gastric fundus (gastric varices)

    Presentation: - Massive hematemesis (large volume, fresh blood) - Melena - Hemodynamic instability (often) - Stigmata of chronic liver disease: - Jaundice, ascites, spider angiomata, palmar erythema

    Diagnosis: - Upper endoscopy (diagnostic + therapeutic) - Actively bleeding varices OR "White nipple" sign (fibrin plug)

    Management:

    1. Resuscitation: - IV access (2 large-bore IVs) - Crystalloid: Cautious (avoid over-resuscitation → ↑ portal pressure) - Blood products: - Target Hgb 7-9 g/dL (restrictive strategy) - Platelets: If <50,000/μL - FFP: If INR >1.5-2.0 (caution - volume overload) - Cryoprecipitate: If fibrinogen <100 mg/dL

    2. Pharmacotherapy: - Octreotide (somatostatin analog): - 50 μg IV bolus → 50 μg/hour continuous infusion × 2-5 days - Mechanism: Splanchnic vasoconstriction → ↓ portal pressure - Start immediately (before endoscopy)

    3. Antibiotics (CRITICAL): - Ceftriaxone 1 g IV daily × 7 days - Reduces mortality by 30% (prevents spontaneous bacterial peritonitis, bacteremia) - Start in ALL cirrhotic patients with GI bleeding

    4. Endoscopic Therapy: - Esophageal Varices: - Endoscopic Variceal Ligation (EVL) - PREFERRED - Rubber bands applied to varices - Lower rebleeding, fewer complications than sclerotherapy - Sclerotherapy (ethanolamine, sodium morrhuate): If EVL not feasible

    • Gastric Varices:
    • Cyanoacrylate glue injection (not available in US)
    • Transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt (TIPS) if refractory

    5. Rescue Therapy (if endoscopic therapy fails):

    Balloon Tamponade: - Sengstaken-Blakemore tube (esophageal + gastric balloons) - Minnesota tube (esophageal + gastric balloons + esophageal suction) - Mechanism: Direct compression of varices - Temporary (<24 hours - ischemia/necrosis risk) - Bridge to definitive therapy (TIPS, surgery) - Complications: Aspiration, esophageal perforation

    TIPS (Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt): - Indications: - Refractory variceal bleeding (failed endoscopic + medical therapy) - Recurrent bleeding despite endoscopic therapy - Mechanism: Creates shunt between hepatic vein and portal vein → ↓ portal pressure - Complications: - Hepatic encephalopathy (30-50%) - Shunt stenosis/occlusion - Heart failure (↑ preload)

    6. Secondary Prophylaxis (prevent rebleeding): - Non-selective beta-blocker (propranolol or carvedilol): - Propranolol: Target HR 55-60 bpm OR 25% reduction - Mechanism: ↓ portal pressure (↓ cardiac output + splanchnic vasoconstriction) - EVL: Repeat sessions every 2-4 weeks until variceal obliteration

    Primary Prophylaxis (never bled): - Screen ALL cirrhotic patients with endoscopy at diagnosis - If medium-large varices: - Non-selective beta-blocker (propranolol, carvedilol, nadolol) OR - EVL

    3. Mallory-Weiss Tear - 5-10%¶

    Mucosal tear at gastroesophageal junction

    Etiology: - Forceful vomiting/retching (most common) - Alcohol use, bulimia - Violent coughing, straining

    Presentation: - Hematemesis after episode of non-bloody vomiting - Self-limited in 90% (stops spontaneously) - Minimal hemodynamic compromise (usually)

    Diagnosis: - Endoscopy: Linear mucosal tear (usually <2 cm) at GE junction

    Management: - Observation (most cases - self-limited) - Endoscopic therapy: If actively bleeding (rare) - Epinephrine injection, hemoclip, thermal coagulation

    Boerhaave Syndrome (differential): - Full-thickness esophageal perforation (transmural) - Severe chest pain, subcutaneous emphysema, pneumomediastinum - Emergency: Surgical repair

    4. Esophagitis - 5-10%¶

    Causes: - GERD (reflux esophagitis) - Medications: NSAIDs, bisphosphonates, doxycycline, potassium chloride - Infectious: Candida, CMV, HSV (immunocompromised) - Radiation - Caustic ingestion

    Presentation: - Hematemesis (usually minor) - Melena - Dysphagia, odynophagia, heartburn

    Diagnosis: - Endoscopy: Erythema, erosions, ulceration

    Management: - PPI (high-dose) - Treat underlying cause: - Candida: Fluconazole - CMV: Ganciclovir - HSV: Acyclovir

    5. Gastric Varices¶

    • Less common than esophageal varices
    • Higher bleeding risk per varix
    • Management:
    • Cyanoacrylate glue (not available in US)
    • TIPS (if refractory)
    • Balloon-occluded retrograde transvenous obliteration (BRTO)

    6. Dieulafoy's Lesion¶

    Aberrant submucosal artery that erodes through mucosa

    Characteristics: - Pinpoint mucosal defect (<3 mm) - Large-caliber artery (1-3 mm) - disproportionate to defect - No surrounding ulcer

    Location: - Proximal stomach (lesser curve) - 75% - Can occur anywhere in GI tract

    Presentation: - Massive, intermittent hematemesis - Difficult to diagnose (small lesion, intermittent bleeding)

    Diagnosis: - Endoscopy (may require multiple attempts) - Angiography (if endoscopy fails)

    Management: - Endoscopic therapy: - Hemoclip (preferred) - Band ligation - Thermal coagulation + epinephrine - Angiography with embolization: If endoscopy fails - Surgery: Last resort

    7. Angiodysplasia (Vascular Ectasia)¶

    Dilated, tortuous blood vessels in mucosa/submucosa

    Location: - Cecum, ascending colon (most common) - LGIB - Stomach, duodenum - UGIB

    Risk Factors: - Elderly (>60 years) - Chronic renal failure (common) - Aortic stenosis (Heyde syndrome) - Von Willebrand disease

    Presentation: - Occult bleeding (iron deficiency anemia) - most common - Melena or hematochezia (if active)

    Diagnosis: - Colonoscopy: Small (2-10 mm) red, flat lesions - Angiography: "Early filling vein" (if actively bleeding)

    Management: - Endoscopic therapy: Thermal coagulation (APC - argon plasma coagulation) - Treat aortic stenosis (if present - may resolve bleeding) - Estrogen-progesterone (rarely used)

    8. Gastric Antral Vascular Ectasia (GAVE)¶

    "Watermelon Stomach"

    Appearance: - Red stripes (ectatic vessels) radiating from pylorus to antrum - Resembles watermelon stripes

    Associations: - Cirrhosis (portal hypertension) - Chronic renal failure - Systemic sclerosis - Autoimmune disease

    Presentation: - Chronic occult bleeding (iron deficiency anemia)

    Diagnosis: - Endoscopy: Characteristic watermelon appearance

    Management: - Argon plasma coagulation (APC): Endoscopic thermal therapy - May require multiple sessions - Iron supplementation

    9. Portal Hypertensive Gastropathy¶

    Gastric mucosal changes due to portal hypertension

    Appearance: - "Mosaic" or "snakeskin" pattern (endoscopy) - Mucosal edema, friability

    Presentation: - Chronic occult bleeding (more common than acute)

    Management: - Non-selective beta-blocker (propranolol) - TIPS: If refractory

    10. Upper GI Malignancy¶

    Gastric Cancer, Esophageal Cancer

    Presentation: - Hematemesis, melena - Weight loss, dysphagia, anorexia

    Diagnosis: - Endoscopy with biopsy

    Management: - Endoscopic therapy: For acute bleeding (temporary) - Oncologic treatment: Surgery, chemotherapy, radiation

    11. Aortoenteric Fistula¶

    Abnormal connection between aorta and GI tract (usually duodenum)

    Etiology: - Aortic graft (synthetic graft infection) - most common - Aortic aneurysm erosion - Radiation

    Presentation: - "Herald bleed": Small self-limited bleed (hours to days before) - Massive exsanguination (follows herald bleed) - Hemodynamic collapse

    Diagnosis: - High index of suspicion (history of aortic surgery) - CT angiography: Graft-enteric fistula, perigraft gas, fluid - Upper endoscopy: Duodenal erosion, graft visible

    Management: - Emergency surgery: Graft removal + revascularization (extra-anatomic bypass) - Mortality: 40-70% (even with surgery)

    Upper GI Bleeding Management Summary¶

    Acute Management Protocol:

    1. Resuscitation (ABCs): - Airway: Intubation if massive hematemesis (protect airway) - IV access: 2 large-bore IVs (16-18 gauge) - Crystalloid: Isotonic saline or Ringer's lactate - Blood transfusion: - Hgb target: 7-9 g/dL (restrictive strategy) - Liberal transfusion (Hgb >10) → worse outcomes (↑ rebleeding, ↑ mortality) - Exception: Active ischemia (MI) - target Hgb 10

    2. Risk Stratification: - Glasgow-Blatchford Score (GBS): Identifies low-risk (can discharge) - Rockall Score: Predicts mortality (pre-endoscopy + post-endoscopy)

    3. Medications: - PPI: - IV bolus (pantoprazole 80 mg or esomeprazole 80 mg) - Infusion (8 mg/hr × 72 hrs) if high-risk stigmata on endoscopy - THEN oral PPI BID × 14 days

    • Prokinetic (Erythromycin):
    • 250 mg IV given 30-120 minutes before endoscopy
    • Improves visualization (↑ gastric emptying)

    • Consider if suspected large clot burden

    • Octreotide: If suspected variceal bleeding (50 μg bolus → 50 μg/hr)

    • Antibiotics: If cirrhosis (ceftriaxone 1 g IV daily)

    4. Endoscopy: - Timing: - Urgent (<12-24 hours): Hemodynamically stable - Emergent (<12 hours): Hemodynamically unstable, suspected varices, high-risk features

    • Therapeutic endoscopy: For high-risk stigmata (Forrest Ia-IIb)

    5. Post-Endoscopy: - High-risk stigmata: - High-dose PPI infusion × 72 hours - Resume PPI BID × 14 days → daily

    • H. pylori testing: Biopsy, breath test, or stool antigen

    • If positive: Eradicate

    • Discontinue NSAIDs/anticoagulants: Reassess need

    • If NSAID required: Use COX-2 inhibitor + PPI

    6. Rebleeding: - Repeat endoscopy - If fails: Angiography with embolization OR Surgery

    Lower GI Bleeding¶

    Etiology¶

    Common Causes by Age:

    <50 Years: - Hemorrhoids, anal fissures (most common) - IBD (UC, Crohn's) - Infectious colitis - Meckel's diverticulum (pediatric)

    >50 Years: - Diverticulosis (40-50%) - Angiodysplasia (20-30%) - Colorectal cancer/polyps (10-20%) - Ischemic colitis (10%) - Hemorrhoids

    1. Diverticulosis - 40-50%¶

    Most common cause of LGIB in elderly

    Pathophysiology: - Diverticula: Outpouchings of colonic mucosa through muscularis - Bleeding: Vasa recta rupture at diverticular dome - Painless (unlike diverticulitis)

    Location: - Left colon (sigmoid) - most common site for diverticula - RIGHT colon (cecum, ascending) - more likely to bleed (20-30% of bleeding)

    Presentation: - Acute, painless hematochezia (large volume) - Hemodynamically significant (in severe cases) - Self-limited in 80% (stops spontaneously)

    Diagnosis: - Colonoscopy: - Identify diverticula (NOT actively bleeding) - Active bleeding visualized in <20% (intermittent) - Stigmata: Visible vessel, adherent clot

    • CT Angiography (if actively bleeding):
    • Requires bleeding rate ≥0.3 mL/min

    • Shows contrast extravasation

    • Nuclear Medicine Scan (Tagged RBC Scan):

    • Detects bleeding rate ≥0.1 mL/min (more sensitive than angiography)
    • Localizes bleeding site (helps guide surgery)

    • Does NOT allow therapeutic intervention

    • Mesenteric Angiography:

    • Requires bleeding rate ≥0.5-1 mL/min
    • Therapeutic: Embolization possible

    Management:

    Resuscitation: - IV access, crystalloid, blood transfusion (Hgb target 7-9)

    If Hemodynamically Stable: - Colonoscopy (after bowel prep if possible): - Endoscopic therapy: Epinephrine injection, hemoclip, thermal coagulation - If no active bleeding seen: Observation

    If Hemodynamically Unstable / Massive Bleeding: - Resuscitate - CTA or Angiography: - Angiography: Embolization (if bleeding site identified) - Surgery: If refractory (segmental colectomy)

    If Bleeding Stops: - Colonoscopy (within 24-48 hours): - Rule out malignancy, polyps - Identify diverticula

    Rebleeding: - 25% within 4 years - Repeat colonoscopy or surgery (if recurrent)

    2. Angiodysplasia - 20-30%¶

    Covered above (can cause UGIB or LGIB)

    Most Common Location for LGIB: - Cecum, ascending colon (right-sided)

    Presentation: - Occult bleeding (iron deficiency anemia) - most common - Intermittent hematochezia

    Diagnosis: - Colonoscopy: Small red flat lesions - Angiography: Early filling vein

    Management: - Endoscopic therapy: APC (argon plasma coagulation), bipolar coagulation

    3. Ischemic Colitis - 10%¶

    Inadequate blood flow to colon

    Risk Factors: - Elderly (>60 years) - Atherosclerosis, hypotension, dehydration - Aortic surgery - Medications: NSAIDs, cocaine, vasopressors

    Location: - "Watershed" areas (poor collateral circulation): - Splenic flexure (SMA-IMA junction) - Rectosigmoid junction (IMA-internal iliac junction) - Left colon more common

    Presentation: - Sudden abdominal pain (left lower quadrant) - Bloody diarrhea (within 24 hours) - Urgency - Mild (most cases - self-limited)

    Diagnosis: - Colonoscopy: - Segmental erythema, edema, ulceration - "Thumbprinting" (submucosal hemorrhage/edema) - Spares rectum (dual blood supply)

    • CT scan:
    • Bowel wall thickening (segmental)
    • "Thumbprinting"
    • Target sign (mucosal/submucosal edema)

    Management: - Supportive (most cases): - NPO, IV fluids - Broad-spectrum antibiotics (prevent bacterial translocation) - Discontinue vasoconstricting meds (NSAIDs, cocaine)

    • Serial exams: Monitor for peritonitis, sepsis

    • Surgery: If:

    • Peritonitis (perforation)
    • Gangrene
    • Persistent symptoms (stricture)

    Prognosis: - 90% resolve spontaneously - Chronic colitis or stricture in 10-20%

    4. Colorectal Cancer / Polyps¶

    Presentation: - Change in bowel habits - Hematochezia (intermittent, small amounts) - Weight loss - Iron deficiency anemia (right-sided colon cancer)

    Diagnosis: - Colonoscopy with biopsy (gold standard)

    Management: - Surgical resection (curative) - Polypectomy (if polyp)

    5. Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD)¶

    Ulcerative Colitis, Crohn's Disease

    Presentation: - Bloody diarrhea - Abdominal pain, urgency, tenesmus - Chronic, relapsing course

    Diagnosis: - Colonoscopy with biopsy

    Management: - Treat underlying IBD (5-ASA, steroids, immunomodulators, biologics) - Severe bleeding: Rare (may require surgery)

    6. Infectious Colitis¶

    Bacterial (Shigella, Campylobacter, Salmonella, C. diff, E. coli O157:H7): - Bloody diarrhea, fever - Diagnosis: Stool culture, C. diff toxin - Management: Antibiotics (if indicated), supportive

    E. histolytica (Amoebic dysentery): - Bloody diarrhea (developing countries, travelers) - Diagnosis: Stool O&P, serology - Management: Metronidazole + paromomycin

    7. Hemorrhoids¶

    Most common cause of bright red rectal bleeding (overall)

    Types: - Internal: Above dentate line (painless) - External: Below dentate line (painful if thrombosed)

    Presentation: - Bright red blood on toilet paper, dripping into bowl - After bowel movement - Small amounts - NO melena (no upper GI contact)

    Diagnosis: - Anoscopy: Visualize hemorrhoids - Colonoscopy: If >50 years or alarm symptoms (rule out cancer)

    Management: - Conservative: - High-fiber diet, stool softeners - Sitz baths - Topical hydrocortisone

    • Procedures (if conservative fails):
    • Rubber band ligation
    • Infrared coagulation

    • Sclerotherapy

    • Surgery (hemorrhoidectomy): If refractory

    8. Anal Fissure¶

    Linear tear in anal mucosa (usually posterior midline)

    Presentation: - Severe pain during and after defecation - Bright red blood on toilet paper - Constipation (fear of pain → avoid defecation)

    Diagnosis: - Inspection: Visible tear (posterior midline) - Sentinel tag (skin tag at fissure base)

    Management: - Conservative: - High-fiber diet, stool softeners - Nitroglycerin ointment (0.2-0.4% topical) - relaxes sphincter - Calcium channel blocker (diltiazem 2% topical) - Sitz baths

    • Surgery (lateral internal sphincterotomy): If refractory

    9. Meckel's Diverticulum¶

    Most common congenital GI anomaly (2% of population)

    "Rule of 2s": - 2% of population - 2 feet from ileocecal valve - 2 inches long - 2 types of ectopic mucosa (gastric, pancreatic) - 2% symptomatic - Presents <2 years (usually) - M:F = 2:1

    Presentation: - Painless hematochezia (brick-red or maroon) - Children/young adults (<30 years) - Ectopic gastric mucosa → acid secretion → ulceration of ileal mucosa → bleeding

    Diagnosis: - Technetium-99m pertechnetate scan ("Meckel's scan"): - Pertechnetate taken up by gastric mucosa - Sensitivity: 85-90%

    Management: - Surgical resection (segmental ileal resection)

    10. Radiation Proctitis/Colitis¶

    History of pelvic radiation (prostate, cervical, rectal cancer)

    Presentation: - Chronic rectal bleeding (months to years after radiation) - Diarrhea, tenesmus

    Diagnosis: - Colonoscopy: Telangiectasias, friable mucosa

    Management: - Endoscopic therapy: APC (argon plasma coagulation) - Sucralfate enemas - Hyperbaric oxygen (if refractory)

    Lower GI Bleeding Management Summary¶

    Acute Management Protocol:

    1. Resuscitation: - IV access, crystalloid, blood transfusion (Hgb target 7-9)

    2. Nasogastric Lavage (controversial): - If massive bleeding → consider NG tube to rule out UGIB - Bilious, non-bloody aspirate → likely LGIB - Bloody aspirate → UGIB (proceed to EGD) - Note: Negative NG aspirate does NOT rule out UGIB (duodenal bleeding)

    3. Risk Stratification: - Hemodynamic stability - Comorbidities (CAD, CKD) - Ongoing bleeding vs resolved

    4. Diagnostic Approach:

    If Hemodynamically Stable: - Colonoscopy (within 24 hours after bowel prep): - First-line diagnostic and therapeutic - Bowel prep: Polyethylene glycol (PEG) - 4-6 L over 3-4 hours - Identifies source in 70-80% - Allows therapeutic intervention

    If Hemodynamically Unstable / Massive Bleeding: - Resuscitate - Consider EGD first (rule out brisk UGIB) - CT Angiography: - Non-invasive - Requires bleeding rate ≥0.3 mL/min - Identifies extravasation site - Angiography: - If CTA positive OR ongoing bleeding - Therapeutic: Embolization - Requires bleeding rate ≥0.5 mL/min

    If Bleeding Stopped: - Colonoscopy (within 24-48 hours): - Identify source (diverticula, angiodysplasia, mass) - Rule out malignancy

    5. Endoscopic Therapy: - Epinephrine injection - Hemoclips - Thermal coagulation (APC, bipolar)

    6. Angiography with Embolization: - If endoscopy fails OR patient too unstable for colonoscopy - Complication: Bowel ischemia (3-5%)

    7. Surgery: - Indications: - Massive, ongoing bleeding (uncontrolled) - Hemodynamic instability despite resuscitation - >6 units PRBC in 24 hours - Recurrent bleeding

    • Procedure:
    • Segmental colectomy (if bleeding site localized)
    • Subtotal colectomy (if bleeding site unknown - last resort)

    Obscure GI Bleeding¶

    Definition¶

    • Source not identified on EGD and colonoscopy
    • Persistent or recurrent bleeding

    Classification¶

    Obscure-Overt: - Visible bleeding (melena, hematochezia) - Source not found

    Obscure-Occult: - No visible bleeding - Iron deficiency anemia OR positive FOBT

    Etiology¶

    Small Bowel (75%): - Angiodysplasia (40%) - most common - Small bowel tumors (10%): GIST, lymphoma, carcinoid - Crohn's disease - Meckel's diverticulum - Aortoenteric fistula - NSAID enteropathy

    Missed Lesions: - Gastric antral vascular ectasia (GAVE) - Cameron erosions (hiatal hernia) - Dieulafoy's lesion

    Diagnostic Approach¶

    1. Repeat EGD and Colonoscopy: - Miss rate: 10-20% - Especially if inadequate bowel prep

    2. Video Capsule Endoscopy (VCE): - First-line for small bowel evaluation - Wireless camera swallowed, images transmitted - Visualizes entire small bowel - Diagnostic yield: 60-70%

    Contraindications: - Known stricture (capsule retention risk) - Suspected obstruction - Pacemaker/ICD (relative)

    Pre-screening: - Patency capsule (dissolves if retained) OR - CT/MR enterography (rule out stricture)

    3. Deep Enteroscopy (if VCE identifies lesion):

    Types: - Double-balloon enteroscopy - Single-balloon enteroscopy - Spiral enteroscopy

    Allows: - Visualization + Biopsy + Therapy (can't with VCE)

    4. CT/MR Enterography: - Small bowel imaging - Identifies masses, Crohn's disease, strictures

    5. Angiography: - If active bleeding (rate ≥0.5 mL/min) - Provocative angiography: Heparin, tPA (rarely done)

    6. Intraoperative Enteroscopy: - Last resort - Surgeon advances endoscope through entire small bowel

    Management¶

    Treat Underlying Cause: - Angiodysplasia: APC (via enteroscopy) - Tumor: Resection - Crohn's: Medical therapy - Meckel's: Surgical resection

    Empiric Therapy (if no source found): - Iron supplementation (if anemia) - Discontinue NSAIDs/anticoagulants (if possible) - Octreotide: For angiodysplasia (controversial) - Thalidomide: Angiodysplasia (limited evidence)

    Initial Assessment and Resuscitation¶

    Airway, Breathing, Circulation (ABCs)¶

    Airway: - Intubation if: - Massive hematemesis (protect airway from aspiration) - Altered mental status - Hemodynamic instability requiring aggressive resuscitation

    Breathing: - Supplemental oxygen - Monitor oxygen saturation

    Circulation:

    IV Access: - 2 large-bore peripheral IVs (16-18 gauge) - Central venous access if difficult peripheral access

    Fluid Resuscitation: - Crystalloid: Isotonic saline or Ringer's lactate - Initial bolus: 500-1000 mL (reassess)

    Blood Transfusion: - Hgb target: 7-9 g/dL (restrictive strategy) - Randomized trials show restrictive better than liberal (Hgb >10): - ↓ Rebleeding - ↓ Mortality - Exception: Active coronary ischemia (target Hgb 10)

    • Platelets: If <50,000/μL (target >50,000 for procedures)

    • FFP: If INR >1.5-2.0 AND active bleeding

    • Prothrombin Complex Concentrate (PCC):

    • Reversal of warfarin (faster than FFP)

    • Dosing: Based on INR and weight

    • Cryoprecipitate: If fibrinogen <100 mg/dL

    Hemodynamic Monitoring: - Vital signs: HR, BP (orthostatic vitals if stable) - Urine output: Target >0.5 mL/kg/hr - Lactate: Marker of tissue perfusion

    Initial Laboratory Studies¶

    Complete Blood Count (CBC): - Hemoglobin/Hematocrit: May be falsely normal initially (no hemodilution yet) - Platelets: Thrombocytopenia (cirrhosis, DIC)

    Coagulation Studies: - PT/INR: Warfarin, liver disease - aPTT: Heparin - Fibrinogen: DIC, massive transfusion

    Comprehensive Metabolic Panel (CMP): - BUN/Creatinine: - BUN:Cr ratio >30: Suggests UGIB (blood digested → urea) - Creatinine: Assess renal function (pre-contrast)

    Liver Function Tests: - If suspected cirrhosis, variceal bleeding

    Type and Cross-Match: - 4-6 units PRBC (massive bleeding)

    Risk Stratification¶

    Glasgow-Blatchford Score (GBS)¶

    Identifies LOW-RISK patients who can be discharged (no endoscopy needed)

    Components: - BUN - Hemoglobin - Systolic BP - Heart rate - Melena, syncope - Hepatic disease, heart failure

    Score 0: Safe for outpatient management (no endoscopy, discharge)

    Score ≥1: Requires admission and endoscopy

    Rockall Score¶

    Predicts mortality (both pre-endoscopy and post-endoscopy)

    Pre-Endoscopy Components: - Age - Shock (HR, BP) - Comorbidities (heart failure, IHD, renal failure, liver failure, malignancy)

    Post-Endoscopy Components (add to above): - Diagnosis (Mallory-Weiss = low risk, malignancy = high risk) - Stigmata of recent hemorrhage (clean base vs active bleeding)

    Score: - 0-2: Low risk (<5% mortality) - ≥8: High risk (>40% mortality)

    Endoscopic Management¶

    Techniques¶

    1. Injection Therapy:

    Epinephrine (1:10,000): - Mechanism: Vasoconstriction + tamponade effect - MUST combine with another modality (mechanical or thermal) - Epinephrine alone: Higher rebleeding rate

    Sclerosants (ethanolamine, sodium morrhuate): - For varices (esophageal variceal sclerotherapy) - Largely replaced by band ligation

    2. Mechanical Therapy:

    Hemoclips (Endoclips): - Mechanical compression of vessel - Effective for: - Peptic ulcer bleeding (visible vessel) - Dieulafoy's lesion - Mallory-Weiss tear - Post-polypectomy bleeding

    Endoscopic Variceal Ligation (EVL): - Rubber bands applied to varices - Preferred over sclerotherapy (lower rebleeding, fewer complications)

    Endoscopic Suturing, Clips (Over-the-scope clips - OTSC): - Larger, more robust clips - For larger defects, Dieulafoy's lesion

    3. Thermal Therapy:

    Electrocoagulation (Bipolar, Monopolar): - Mechanism: Heat-induced coagulation - Effective for: - Peptic ulcer (visible vessel, oozing) - Angiodysplasia

    Heater Probe: - Direct thermal contact - Coagulation + tamponade

    Argon Plasma Coagulation (APC): - Non-contact thermal therapy (ionized argon gas) - Best for: - Angiodysplasia - GAVE (watermelon stomach) - Radiation proctitis - Superficial treatment (2-3 mm depth)

    4. Topical Hemostatic Agents:

    Hemostatic Powders (TC-325, EndoClot): - Sprayed onto bleeding site - Forms mechanical barrier - Temporary (bridge to definitive therapy) - Useful for diffuse bleeding (gastric varices, malignancy)

    High-Yield Points¶

    UGIB vs LGIB Differentiation¶

    UGIB Clues: - Hematemesis (definitive) - Melena (usually) - BUN:Cr ratio >30 (blood digested in upper GI) - NG aspirate bloody or coffee-ground

    LGIB Clues: - Hematochezia (usually) - NG aspirate bilious, non-bloody (suggests LGIB) - NOTE: Brisk UGIB can present with hematochezia

    Key Management Principles¶

    1. Restrictive Transfusion: - Hgb target 7-9 g/dL (better outcomes) - Exception: Active MI (target 10)

    2. PPI in UGIB: - IV bolus: Before endoscopy - High-dose infusion (8 mg/hr × 72 hrs): If high-risk stigmata (Forrest Ia-IIb) - Oral BID × 14 days → daily

    3. Variceal Bleeding Essentials: - Octreotide: Start immediately (before endoscopy) - Antibiotics (ceftriaxone): ALL cirrhotic patients with GI bleeding (↓ mortality) - Endoscopic therapy: EVL preferred - TIPS: Rescue therapy (if endoscopic fails)

    4. Forrest Classification: - Ia-IIb: High-risk → Endoscopic therapy required - IIc, III: Low-risk → Medical therapy only

    5. Lower GI Bleeding: - Diverticulosis: Most common cause (>50 years) - Right colon bleeds more (despite left-sided diverticula) - Self-limited 80% - Colonoscopy: First-line (if stable) - CTA/Angiography: If unstable or massive bleeding

    Specific Lesion Pearls¶

    Dieulafoy's Lesion: - Aberrant artery through pinpoint mucosal defect - Massive bleeding from tiny lesion - Difficult to find (intermittent bleeding) - Management: Hemoclip (preferred)

    GAVE (Watermelon Stomach): - Red stripes radiating from pylorus - Chronic occult bleeding (iron deficiency anemia) - Management: APC (multiple sessions)

    Aortoenteric Fistula: - Herald bleed → massive exsanguination - History: Aortic graft surgery - Emergency surgery (high mortality)

    Angiodysplasia: - Cecum/ascending colon (most common) - Associations: Chronic kidney disease, aortic stenosis (Heyde syndrome) - Management: APC

    Meckel's Diverticulum: - Rule of 2s - Painless hematochezia (children/young adults) - Diagnosis: Tc-99m pertechnetate scan - Management: Surgical resection

    Ischemic Colitis: - Elderly, sudden LLQ pain + bloody diarrhea - Splenic flexure (watershed) - Segmental on colonoscopy (spares rectum) - Management: Supportive (90% resolve)

    Endoscopic Therapy Pearls¶

    Peptic Ulcer: - Dual therapy (epinephrine + mechanical/thermal) superior to monotherapy - Never use epinephrine alone

    Varices: - EVL > Sclerotherapy (lower rebleeding, safer) - Octreotide + antibiotics before endoscopy

    Angiodysplasia: - APC (best modality - non-contact, superficial)

    Hemoclips: - Best for: Dieulafoy's, visible vessel (peptic ulcer), Mallory-Weiss

    Practice MCQs¶

    Question 1¶

    A 55-year-old man with cirrhosis presents with massive hematemesis. After resuscitation, which medication should be started IMMEDIATELY before endoscopy?

    A) Pantoprazole IV bolus B) Octreotide infusion C) Erythromycin IV D) Vitamin K

    Answer: B) Octreotide infusion

    Explanation: - Suspected variceal bleeding (cirrhosis + massive hematemesis): - Octreotide: Start immediately (before endoscopy) - 50 μg IV bolus → 50 μg/hour continuous infusion - Mechanism: Splanchnic vasoconstriction → ↓ portal pressure - Reduces bleeding, improves endoscopic success

    • Also give:

    • Ceftriaxone 1 g IV daily (antibiotics reduce mortality by 30%)

    • PPI: Less relevant for variceal bleeding (no role in acute variceal management)

    • Erythromycin: Prokinetic (improves gastric emptying for better visualization)

    • Useful if suspected large clot burden
    • NOT specific for varices

    Question 2¶

    A 65-year-old man undergoes EGD for melena. A duodenal ulcer is visualized with a visible vessel (non-bleeding). According to Forrest classification, what is the rebleeding risk and appropriate management?

    A) Forrest IIc; <10% rebleeding; medical therapy only B) Forrest IIa; 40-50% rebleeding; endoscopic therapy required C) Forrest IIb; 20-30% rebleeding; endoscopic therapy required D) Forrest III; <5% rebleeding; medical therapy only

    Answer: B) Forrest IIa; 40-50% rebleeding; endoscopic therapy required

    Explanation: - Forrest Classification:

    Class Description Rebleeding Risk Management
    Ia Spurting arterial bleeding 90% Endoscopic therapy
    Ib Oozing bleeding 50% Endoscopic therapy
    IIa Visible vessel (non-bleeding) 40-50% Endoscopic therapy
    IIb Adherent clot 20-30% Endoscopic therapy
    IIc Flat pigmented spot <10% Medical therapy
    III Clean base <5% Medical therapy

    • Visible vessel (Forrest IIa): HIGH-RISK → Requires endoscopic therapy

    • Endoscopic therapy:

    • Dual therapy: Epinephrine injection + (Hemoclip OR Thermal coagulation)

    • Post-procedure:

    • High-dose PPI infusion (8 mg/hr × 72 hours)

    Question 3¶

    A 70-year-old woman presents with acute, painless hematochezia (large volume). She is hemodynamically stable. Colonoscopy after bowel prep shows multiple diverticula in the sigmoid colon, but no active bleeding is visualized. What is the MOST appropriate next step?

    A) Discharge with outpatient follow-up B) Observation in hospital C) Angiography with embolization D) Surgical consult for sigmoid colectomy

    Answer: B) Observation in hospital

    Explanation: - Diverticular bleeding: - Self-limited in 80% (stops spontaneously) - Colonoscopy identifies diverticula but often NO active bleeding seen (intermittent)

    • If bleeding stopped AND hemodynamically stable:
    • Observation in hospital
    • Monitor for rebleeding

    • Repeat Hgb in 6-8 hours

    • Discharge: Premature (risk of rebleeding)

    • Angiography: Only if:

    • Active bleeding (hemodynamic instability)

    • Rebleeding during observation

    • Surgery: Only if:

    • Massive, ongoing bleeding (uncontrolled)
    • Recurrent bleeding (multiple episodes)
    • Requires >6 units PRBC in 24 hours

    Question 4¶

    A 25-year-old man with no significant past medical history presents with sudden onset of hematemesis after an episode of forceful vomiting following alcohol intoxication. EGD shows a 1.5 cm linear mucosal tear at the gastroesophageal junction with no active bleeding. What is the diagnosis and appropriate management?

    A) Boerhaave syndrome; emergent surgery B) Esophageal varices; endoscopic band ligation C) Mallory-Weiss tear; observation D) Esophagitis; high-dose PPI

    Answer: C) Mallory-Weiss tear; observation

    Explanation: - Mallory-Weiss tear: - Mucosal tear at gastroesophageal junction - Etiology: Forceful vomiting/retching (alcohol, bulimia) - Presentation: Hematemesis after non-bloody vomiting - EGD: Linear tear (usually <2 cm)

    • Management:
    • 90% self-limited (stop spontaneously)
    • Observation if no active bleeding

    • Endoscopic therapy (epinephrine, hemoclip, thermal) if actively bleeding (rare)

    • Boerhaave syndrome:

    • Full-thickness esophageal perforation (transmural)
    • Severe chest pain, subcutaneous emphysema, pneumomediastinum
    • Emergency: Surgical repair

    Question 5¶

    A 45-year-old man with known aortic stenosis presents with chronic fatigue and is found to have iron deficiency anemia (Hgb 8 g/dL). EGD and colonoscopy are normal. What is the MOST likely diagnosis and next diagnostic step?

    A) Gastric cancer; repeat EGD with biopsies B) Angiodysplasia; video capsule endoscopy C) Peptic ulcer disease; H. pylori testing D) Celiac disease; duodenal biopsies

    Answer: B) Angiodysplasia; video capsule endoscopy

    Explanation: - Heyde Syndrome: - Aortic stenosis + GI bleeding (angiodysplasia) - Mechanism: vWF degradation (high shear stress) → bleeding diathesis + angiodysplasia

    • Angiodysplasia:
    • Occult bleeding → Iron deficiency anemia (most common presentation)
    • Location: Cecum/ascending colon (LGIB) OR stomach/duodenum (UGIB)

    • May be missed on initial endoscopy (small, flat lesions)

    • Obscure GI bleeding (negative EGD + colonoscopy):

    • Next step: Video capsule endoscopy (VCE)
      • Evaluates small bowel (jejunum, ileum)
      • Diagnostic yield: 60-70%

    • Pre-screening: Patency capsule or CT enterography (rule out stricture)

    • If VCE identifies lesion:

    • Deep enteroscopy (balloon-assisted) for therapy

    • Treatment of angiodysplasia:

    • Endoscopic therapy: APC (argon plasma coagulation)
    • Treat aortic stenosis (valve replacement may resolve bleeding)

    Question 6¶

    A 60-year-old woman presents with sudden onset of left lower quadrant pain followed by bloody diarrhea. She has a history of hypertension and takes NSAIDs for arthritis. Colonoscopy shows segmental erythema, edema, and ulceration in the descending colon and splenic flexure, with normal rectum. What is the diagnosis?

    A) Ulcerative colitis B) Ischemic colitis C) Infectious colitis D) Diverticulitis

    Answer: B) Ischemic colitis

    Explanation: - Ischemic colitis: - Elderly with vascular risk factors (hypertension) - Risk factors: Atherosclerosis, NSAIDs, hypotension, dehydration

    • Presentation:
    • Sudden abdominal pain (LLQ)

    • Bloody diarrhea (within 24 hours)

    • Location:

    • "Watershed" areas:
      • Splenic flexure (SMA-IMA junction)
      • Rectosigmoid junction (IMA-internal iliac)

    • Left colon more common

    • Colonoscopy:

    • Segmental erythema, edema, ulceration
    • "Thumbprinting" (submucosal hemorrhage/edema)

    • Spares rectum (dual blood supply from IMA + internal iliac)

    • Management:

    • Supportive: NPO, IV fluids, antibiotics
    • Discontinue NSAIDs

    • Surgery if peritonitis, gangrene, persistent symptoms

    • Prognosis: 90% resolve spontaneously

    • UC differentiation:

    • Continuous inflammation from rectum (NOT segmental)
    • ALWAYS involves rectum
    • Chronic, relapsing course

    Question 7¶

    A 15-year-old boy presents with painless, maroon-colored stools. He has no abdominal pain and is hemodynamically stable. Which diagnostic test is MOST specific for identifying the likely diagnosis?

    A) Colonoscopy B) Upper endoscopy C) Technetium-99m pertechnetate scan D) CT angiography

    Answer: C) Technetium-99m pertechnetate scan

    Explanation: - Meckel's diverticulum: - Most common congenital GI anomaly (2% of population) - Rule of 2s: - 2% of population - 2 feet from ileocecal valve - 2 inches long - 2 types of ectopic mucosa (gastric, pancreatic) - Presents <2 years (usually) - M:F = 2:1

    • Presentation:
    • Painless hematochezia (brick-red or maroon)
    • Children/young adults (<30 years)

    • Ectopic gastric mucosa → acid secretion → ulceration of adjacent ileal mucosa → bleeding

    • Diagnosis:

    • Technetium-99m pertechnetate scan ("Meckel's scan"):

      • Pertechnetate taken up by gastric mucosa (ectopic)
      • Sensitivity: 85-90%
      • MOST specific test

    • Management:

    • Surgical resection (segmental ileal resection)

    • Other tests:

    • Colonoscopy: May show blood from ileum, but can't visualize Meckel's (small bowel)
    • EGD: Won't reach ileum
    • CTA: May show if actively bleeding, but less specific

    Question 8¶

    A 58-year-old man who underwent abdominal aortic aneurysm repair with synthetic graft 3 years ago presents with a small episode of hematemesis that resolves spontaneously. Two days later, he develops massive hematemesis and hemodynamic collapse. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?

    A) Peptic ulcer disease B) Esophageal varices C) Aortoenteric fistula D) Mallory-Weiss tear

    Answer: C) Aortoenteric fistula

    Explanation: - Aortoenteric fistula: - Abnormal connection between aorta (or aortic graft) and GI tract (usually duodenum)

    • Etiology:
    • Aortic graft (synthetic graft infection) - most common
    • Aortic aneurysm erosion

    • Radiation

    • Classic Presentation:

    • "Herald bleed": Small, self-limited bleeding episode (hours to days before)
    • Massive exsanguination (follows herald bleed)

    • Hemodynamic collapse

    • Diagnosis:

    • High index of suspicion (history of aortic surgery - KEY)
    • CT angiography: Graft-enteric fistula, perigraft gas/fluid

    • Upper endoscopy: Duodenal erosion, graft visible

    • Management:

    • Emergency surgery:
      • Graft removal
      • Extra-anatomic bypass (axillobifemoral)

    • Mortality: 40-70% (even with surgery)

    • Differentiation:

    • PUD: No herald bleed pattern, no graft history
    • Varices: Stigmata of liver disease
    • Mallory-Weiss: Vomiting history, usually self-limited

    Question 9¶

    A 68-year-old woman with chronic kidney disease presents with recurrent episodes of hematochezia. Colonoscopy shows multiple small, red, flat lesions in the cecum and ascending colon. What is the MOST appropriate endoscopic treatment?

    A) Hemoclip application B) Epinephrine injection C) Argon plasma coagulation (APC) D) Endoscopic mucosal resection

    Answer: C) Argon plasma coagulation (APC)

    Explanation: - Angiodysplasia: - Dilated, tortuous blood vessels in mucosa/submucosa - Location: Cecum, ascending colon (right-sided) - most common for LGIB - Risk factors: Elderly, chronic kidney disease, aortic stenosis

    • Presentation:
    • Occult bleeding (iron deficiency anemia) - most common

    • Intermittent hematochezia

    • Colonoscopy:

    • Small (2-10 mm) red, flat lesions

    • Endoscopic Treatment:

    • Argon plasma coagulation (APC) - BEST

      • Non-contact thermal therapy
      • Superficial coagulation (2-3 mm depth)
      • Ideal for: Angiodysplasia, GAVE, radiation proctitis

    • Other modalities:

    • Bipolar electrocoagulation: Alternative thermal therapy
    • Hemoclips: NOT ideal (lesions flat, small, multiple)
    • Epinephrine: Temporary, requires adjunct therapy
    • EMR: For polyps/masses (NOT angiodysplasia)

    Question 10¶

    A 50-year-old man with cirrhosis and a history of variceal bleeding (treated with EVL 1 year ago) is found to have medium-sized esophageal varices on surveillance endoscopy. He has never been on beta-blockers. What is the MOST appropriate management for secondary prophylaxis?

    A) Repeat EVL every 2-4 weeks until obliteration B) Start propranolol (target HR 55-60 bpm) C) Combination: propranolol + repeat EVL D) TIPS procedure

    Answer: C) Combination: propranolol + repeat EVL

    Explanation: - Secondary prophylaxis (prevent rebleeding in patient with prior variceal bleed):

    Options: 1. Non-selective beta-blocker (propranolol, carvedilol, nadolol): - Target HR: 55-60 bpm OR 25% reduction from baseline - Mechanism: ↓ portal pressure (↓ cardiac output + splanchnic vasoconstriction)

    1. EVL (Endoscopic Variceal Ligation):

    2. Repeat sessions every 2-4 weeks until variceal obliteration

    3. Combination (beta-blocker + EVL):

    4. BEST for secondary prophylaxis

    5. Additive effect: ↓ rebleeding rate

    6. This patient:

    7. Prior variceal bleed (1 year ago - treated with EVL)
    8. Medium-sized varices on surveillance
    9. Never on beta-blocker → Should start NOW

    10. Combination therapy: Propranolol + repeat EVL (best outcome)

    11. TIPS:

    12. Reserved for refractory variceal bleeding (failed endoscopic + medical therapy)

    13. NOT first-line for secondary prophylaxis

    14. Primary prophylaxis (never bled):

    15. If medium-large varices: Beta-blocker OR EVL (either, not necessarily both)

    Summary¶

    Gastrointestinal Bleeding is a common medical emergency requiring systematic evaluation and management:

    Upper GI Bleeding: - Most common cause: Peptic ulcer disease (40-50%) - Forrest Ia-IIb: High-risk → Endoscopic therapy required - Variceal bleeding: Octreotide + antibiotics + EVL - PPI: IV bolus → high-dose infusion (if high-risk stigmata)

    Lower GI Bleeding: - Most common (>50 years): Diverticulosis - Colonoscopy: First-line (if stable) - Angiodysplasia: APC therapy - Ischemic colitis: Supportive (90% resolve)

    Key Principles: - Restrictive transfusion (Hgb target 7-9) - Endoscopic therapy: Dual therapy (epinephrine + mechanical/thermal) for peptic ulcers - Obscure bleeding: Video capsule endoscopy
