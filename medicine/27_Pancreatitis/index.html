    Acute Pancreatitis¶

    Definition¶

    • Acute inflammatory process of the pancreas
    • Reversible (if uncomplicated)
    • Systemic inflammatory response variable

    Etiology¶

    Mnemonic: "I GET SMASHED": - I: Idiopathic - G: Gallstones (40-50% - most common) - E: Ethanol/Alcohol (30-40%) - T: Trauma - S: Steroids - M: Mumps (and other infections) - A: Autoimmune - S: Scorpion sting, snake bite - H: Hyperlipidemia (triglycerides >1000 mg/dL), Hypercalcemia - E: ERCP - D: Drugs

    Common Causes:

    1. Gallstones (40-50%) - Most common overall: - Mechanism: Stone impaction at ampulla of Vater → obstruction → reflux of bile → pancreatic injury - Risk factors: Female, obesity, rapid weight loss - Small stones (<5 mm) more likely to cause pancreatitis (pass into ampulla)

    2. Alcohol (30-40%) - Most common in men: - Mechanism: Direct acinar cell toxicity, increased pancreatic secretions, premature enzyme activation - Typically: Years of heavy drinking (but can occur after single binge) - Dose-dependent: >50 g/day × 5+ years

    3. Hypertriglyceridemia: - Triglycerides >1000 mg/dL (risk increases, pancreatitis rare if <500) - Mechanism: Lipolysis → free fatty acids → pancreatic injury - Causes: Genetic (familial hypertriglyceridemia), poorly controlled diabetes, alcohol, medications (estrogen, retinoids)

    4. Hypercalcemia: - Hyperparathyroidism (most common cause of hypercalcemia) - Mechanism: Calcium deposition in pancreatic duct, premature enzyme activation - Any cause of hypercalcemia can trigger

    5. Medications: - Common: Azathioprine, 6-mercaptopurine, valproic acid, sulfonamides, furosemide, thiazides, pentamidine, L-asparaginase - Mechanism: Idiosyncratic (not dose-dependent)

    6. ERCP (Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography): - Post-ERCP pancreatitis: 3-5% of procedures - Risk factors: Young age, female, prior pancreatitis, SOD (sphincter of Oddi dysfunction) - Prevention: Rectal indomethacin, pancreatic duct stenting

    7. Trauma: - Blunt abdominal trauma (MVA - steering wheel injury) - Iatrogenic: Post-surgical

    8. Autoimmune: - IgG4-related disease (Type 1 AIP) - Diffuse pancreatic enlargement, elevated IgG4 - Responds to steroids

    9. Genetic: - Hereditary pancreatitis: PRSS1 mutation (cationic trypsinogen) - Cystic fibrosis: CFTR mutation

    10. Anatomic: - Pancreas divisum (most common pancreatic anomaly - 5-10% population) - Minor papilla drains majority of pancreas - May predispose to pancreatitis (controversial) - Ampullary tumor

    11. Vascular: - Ischemia: Hypotension, vasculitis, atheroembolic

    12. Infectious: - Viral: Mumps, Coxsackie, CMV, HIV - Parasitic: Ascaris (bile duct obstruction)

    13. Idiopathic (10-20%): - Consider: - Occult microlithiasis (biliary sludge) - Occult alcohol use - Genetic mutations (if recurrent)

    Pathophysiology¶

    Normal Pancreas: - Acinar cells produce digestive enzymes in inactive form (zymogens) - Trypsinogen → Trypsin (activated in duodenum by enterokinase) - Trypsin activates other enzymes (chymotrypsinogen, proelastase, phospholipase A)

    Acute Pancreatitis Cascade: 

    Inciting Event (gallstone, alcohol, etc.)
         ↓
Premature intracellular trypsinogen activation
         ↓
Trypsin activates other enzymes (elastase, phospholipase A)
         ↓
Autodigestion of pancreas
         ↓
Inflammation, edema, necrosis
         ↓
Systemic inflammatory response (cytokine release)
         ↓
Capillary leak, third-spacing, organ dysfunction

    Forms: - Interstitial Edematous Pancreatitis (80-90%): - Pancreatic edema, inflammation - NO necrosis - Mild, self-limited

    • Necrotizing Pancreatitis (10-20%):
    • Pancreatic parenchymal necrosis and/or peripancreatic fat necrosis
    • More severe
    • Higher complication rate

    Clinical Features¶

    Symptoms:

    Abdominal Pain (>95%): - Severe, constant epigastric pain - Radiates to back (50%) - Sudden onset (gallstones) or after binge drinking - Relieved by sitting up/leaning forward - Worsened by supine position, eating

    Nausea, Vomiting (80-90%): - Persistent, severe

    Physical Examination:

    Mild-Moderate: - Epigastric tenderness - Abdominal distension (ileus) - Hypoactive bowel sounds - Tachycardia, low-grade fever

    Severe: - Hypotension, tachycardia (hypovolemia, SIRS) - Abdominal guarding (peritonitis) - Decreased breath sounds (pleural effusion, ARDS) - Altered mental status (severe SIRS, hypoperfusion)

    Rare Signs (retroperitoneal hemorrhage): - Cullen's sign: Periumbilical ecchymosis (intraperitoneal bleeding) - Grey Turner's sign: Flank ecchymosis (retroperitoneal bleeding) - Both present in <3%, indicate severe necrotizing pancreatitis

    Diagnosis¶

    Revised Atlanta Classification (2012):

    Acute Pancreatitis Diagnosis requires 2 of 3: 1. Abdominal pain consistent with pancreatitis (epigastric, radiating to back) 2. Serum lipase (or amylase) ≥3× upper limit of normal 3. Imaging (CT, MRI, ultrasound) consistent with pancreatitis

    Laboratory¶

    Pancreatic Enzymes:

    Lipase: - Preferred over amylase (more sensitive and specific) - Elevated 4-8 hours, peaks 24 hours, normalizes 8-14 days - ≥3× ULN diagnostic

    Amylase: - Less specific (also elevated in: intestinal perforation, salivary gland disease, ectopic pregnancy, diabetic ketoacidosis, renal failure) - Rises/falls faster than lipase (normalizes 3-5 days)

    Degree of elevation does NOT correlate with severity

    Other Labs: - CBC: Hemoconcentration (Hct >44% predicts severe), leukocytosis - CMP: - Hyperglycemia (insulin deficiency, stress) - Hypocalcemia (<8 mg/dL - saponification with fat necrosis, poor prognostic sign) - Elevated BUN, creatinine (prerenal AKI from hypovolemia) - LFTs: - ALT >150 U/L: 95% PPV for gallstone pancreatitis - Elevated bilirubin, ALP (biliary obstruction) - Triglycerides: Check if >1000 (lipemic serum) - Calcium: Hypercalcemia (parathyroid-related) - Arterial Blood Gas: Hypoxemia, metabolic acidosis (severe)

    Imaging¶

    Not required for diagnosis (if clinical + labs sufficient)

    Indications for Imaging: - Uncertain diagnosis - No improvement after 48-72 hours - Clinical deterioration - Assess complications (necrosis, fluid collections, pseudocyst)

    Ultrasound (Abdominal): - First-line for suspected gallstone pancreatitis - Detects: Gallstones, bile duct dilation, gallbladder wall thickening - Limited pancreatic visualization (bowel gas)

    CT Abdomen/Pelvis with IV Contrast (Pancreatic Protocol): - BEST for assessing severity and complications - Timing: - Initial: Not routinely needed - Delayed (48-72 hours): Better delineates necrosis - Findings: - Pancreatic enlargement, edema - Peripancreatic fat stranding - Fluid collections - Pancreatic necrosis: Non-enhancing areas (lack of contrast uptake) - CT Severity Index (CTSI): Predicts severity

    CT Severity Index (Balthazar Score):

    Grade Findings Points
    A Normal pancreas 0
    B Pancreatic enlargement 1
    C Inflammation + peripancreatic fat 2
    D Single fluid collection 3
    E Multiple/extensive collections 4

    Necrosis: - None: 0 - <30%: +2 - 30-50%: +4 - >50%: +6

    Total CTSI: 0-10 (Higher score = worse prognosis)

    MRI/MRCP: - Best for: Biliary anatomy, pancreatic duct, fluid collections - MRCP: Non-invasive (vs ERCP) - Less commonly used than CT

    Severity Assessment¶

    Revised Atlanta Classification (2012):

    Mild: - NO organ failure - NO local or systemic complications - Usually resolves in <1 week

    Moderately Severe: - Transient organ failure (<48 hours) OR - Local complications (fluid collection, necrosis) OR - Exacerbation of comorbidities

    Severe: - Persistent organ failure (>48 hours) - Single or multiple organ failure - High mortality (15-30%)

    Organ Failure (Modified Marshall Score):

    System 0 1 2 3 4
    Respiratory (PaO₂/FiO₂) >400 301-400 201-300 101-200 ≤101
    Renal (Creatinine mg/dL) <1.4 1.4-1.8 1.9-3.6 3.6-4.9 >4.9
    Cardiovascular (SBP mmHg) >90 <90, fluid responsive <90, pH <7.3 <90, pH <7.2 <90, pH <7.1

    Score ≥2 in any system = Organ failure

    Bedside Index for Severity in Acute Pancreatitis (BISAP) Score:

    Assessed at 24 hours (1 point each): - B: BUN >25 mg/dL - I: Impaired mental status (GCS <15) - S: SIRS (≥2 SIRS criteria) - A: Age >60 years - P: Pleural effusion on imaging

    Score: - 0-2: Low mortality (<2%) - 3-5: High mortality (>15%)

    SIRS Criteria (≥2 = SIRS): - Temperature <36°C or >38°C - Heart rate >90 bpm - Respiratory rate >20/min or PaCO₂ <32 mmHg - WBC <4,000 or >12,000 or >10% bands

    Ranson's Criteria:

    At Admission (1 point each): - Age >55 years - WBC >16,000/mm³ - Glucose >200 mg/dL - LDH >350 U/L - AST >250 U/L

    At 48 Hours (1 point each): - Hematocrit drop >10% - BUN rise >5 mg/dL - Calcium <8 mg/dL - PaO₂ <60 mmHg - Base deficit >4 mEq/L - Fluid sequestration >6 L

    Score: - 0-2: Mortality <1% - 3-4: Mortality 15% - 5-6: Mortality 40% - ≥7: Mortality nearly 100%

    Limited use (requires 48 hours)

    Management¶

    Principles: 1. Supportive care (no specific treatment) 2. Aggressive fluid resuscitation 3. Pain control 4. Nutritional support 5. Treat underlying cause 6. Prevent/manage complications

    1. Fluid Resuscitation (MOST IMPORTANT)¶

    Early aggressive hydration (first 12-24 hours): - Goal-directed fluid therapy - Isotonic crystalloid (Ringer's lactate preferred): - Initial: 15-20 mL/kg bolus - Maintenance: 250-500 mL/hour (titrate to endpoints)

    Endpoints: - Urine output >0.5 mL/kg/hr - Heart rate <120 bpm - MAP 65-85 mmHg - Hematocrit: Maintain 35-44% (avoid hemoconcentration)

    Rationale: Third-spacing causes massive fluid shifts → hypovolemia, SIRS

    Avoid over-resuscitation: Pulmonary edema, abdominal compartment syndrome

    2. Analgesia¶

    Pain is severe: - Opioids: Morphine, hydromorphone, fentanyl - No evidence that morphine worsens pancreatitis (old myth about Sphincter of Oddi spasm) - Adequate pain control improves outcomes

    3. Nutritional Support¶

    Mild Pancreatitis: - NPO initially (if severe nausea/vomiting) - Resume oral feeding when pain improves and nausea resolves (no need to wait for lipase normalization) - Low-fat diet initially, advance as tolerated

    Severe Pancreatitis: - Enteral nutrition (within 24-72 hours): - Preferred over parenteral (TPN) - Reduces infection, mortality, cost - Route: Nasogastric OR nasojejunal (equivalent outcomes) - TPN: ONLY if enteral not tolerated (ileus, intolerance)

    4. Antibiotics¶

    NOT routinely indicated: - NO prophylactic antibiotics in sterile necrosis (no benefit, increases resistance)

    Indications: - Infected necrosis (gas in necrosis on CT, positive FNA culture) - Extrapancreatic infection (cholangitis, pneumonia, UTI)

    Empiric for infected necrosis: - Carbapenems (imipenem, meropenem) OR - Fluoroquinolone + Metronidazole - Penetrate pancreatic tissue

    5. Management of Gallstone Pancreatitis¶

    Cholecystectomy: - Same admission (after resolution of pancreatitis, usually within 48-72 hours) - Prevents recurrent pancreatitis (15-30% if no cholecystectomy) - Safe once inflammation subsides - If severe: Delay until inflammation resolves (weeks), but before discharge

    ERCP: - Indications: - Acute cholangitis (URGENT - within 24 hours) - Biliary obstruction (persistent jaundice, dilated CBD) - NOT routinely indicated in gallstone pancreatitis without cholangitis/obstruction

    6. Other Causes¶

    Hypertriglyceridemia-Induced: - Insulin infusion + Dextrose (if glucose <250 mg/dL) - Activates lipoprotein lipase → ↓ triglycerides - Plasmapheresis: If refractory (TG >1000 despite insulin) - Long-term: Fibrates, omega-3 fatty acids, dietary restriction

    Hypercalcemia: - IV fluids - Bisphosphonates (if severe) - Treat underlying cause (hyperparathyroidism)

    Alcohol: - Abstinence counseling - Thiamine, nutritional support

    Complications¶

    Early Complications (<1 week):¶

    1. Systemic Inflammatory Response Syndrome (SIRS): - Capillary leak, third-spacing - Hypotension, tachycardia

    2. Organ Failure: - Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS): - Most common cause of death in first week - Bilateral infiltrates, hypoxemia - Mechanical ventilation if severe - Acute Kidney Injury (AKI): - Prerenal (hypovolemia) → ATN - Cardiovascular: Shock, arrhythmias

    3. Gastrointestinal: - Ileus (common) - GI bleeding: Stress ulcers, variceal bleeding (splenic vein thrombosis)

    4. Metabolic: - Hypocalcemia (<8 mg/dL): Saponification with fat necrosis - Hyperglycemia: Insulin deficiency (transient or permanent diabetes)

    Local Complications:¶

    1. Acute Peripancreatic Fluid Collections (APFC): - <4 weeks from onset - No defined wall (homogeneous fluid) - Associated with interstitial pancreatitis - Management: Observation (most resolve spontaneously)

    2. Pancreatic Pseudocyst: - >4 weeks from onset - Mature, defined wall (fibrous, granulation tissue) - Homogeneous fluid (no solid debris) - Arises from APFC (if persists >4 weeks)

    Presentation: - Asymptomatic (incidental) OR - Persistent pain, early satiety, nausea - Palpable epigastric mass

    Complications: - Infection (fever, leukocytosis) - Rupture (peritonitis) - Hemorrhage (erosion into vessel) - Biliary/gastric outlet obstruction

    Diagnosis: - CT or MRI: Well-defined, round, fluid-filled

    Management: - Asymptomatic, <6 cm: Observation (40-50% resolve spontaneously) - Symptomatic or complications: - Endoscopic drainage (transpapillary or transmural - EUS-guided) - Percutaneous drainage (if infected, immature wall) - Surgical cystogastrostomy (if endoscopy fails)

    3. Acute Necrotic Collection (ANC): - <4 weeks from onset - Heterogeneous (fluid + necrotic debris) - Associated with necrotizing pancreatitis - No defined wall

    4. Walled-Off Necrosis (WON): - >4 weeks from onset - Mature, defined wall - Heterogeneous (fluid + solid necrotic tissue) - Arises from ANC (if persists >4 weeks)

    Infected Necrosis:

    Definition: - Bacterial infection of pancreatic necrosis - Occurs in 30-40% of necrotizing pancreatitis - Typically 2-4 weeks after onset

    Presentation: - Persistent fever, leukocytosis - Sepsis, multi-organ failure - Clinical deterioration after initial improvement

    Diagnosis: - CT: Gas within necrosis (pathognomonic) - Fine-needle aspiration (FNA): Gram stain, culture (if no gas on CT)

    Management:

    Antibiotics: - Carbapenem (imipenem, meropenem) OR - Fluoroquinolone + Metronidazole

    Drainage/Debridement (if deterioration despite antibiotics): - Delayed intervention preferred (≥4 weeks - allow WON formation, easier to drain)

    Step-Up Approach (preferred): 1. Percutaneous/endoscopic drainage first 2. Minimally invasive necrosectomy if drainage fails 3. Open necrosectomy (last resort)

    Direct open necrosectomy: Higher morbidity/mortality (avoid if possible)

    5. Splanchnic Vein Thrombosis: - Splenic vein thrombosis (most common) - → Left-sided portal hypertension → Gastric varices - Portal vein, superior mesenteric vein thrombosis

    Diagnosis: CT angiography or Doppler ultrasound

    Management: - Anticoagulation (if symptomatic or extensive) - Splenectomy (if bleeding gastric varices)

    6. Pancreatic Ascites / Pleural Effusion: - Pancreatic duct disruption → leakage of pancreatic juice - High amylase in ascitic fluid or pleural fluid

    Management: - ERCP with pancreatic duct stenting - Octreotide (reduce pancreatic secretions) - Surgical repair (if ERCP fails)

    Prognosis¶

    Mild Pancreatitis: - Mortality <1% - Resolves in days to 1 week

    Severe Pancreatitis: - Mortality 15-30% - Early deaths (<1 week): SIRS, multi-organ failure - Late deaths (>1 week): Infected necrosis, sepsis

    Predictors of Severity: - Persistent organ failure (>48 hours) - Pancreatic necrosis (especially >30%) - Infected necrosis - BISAP ≥3, Ranson's ≥3, APACHE II >8

    Chronic Pancreatitis¶

    Definition¶

    • Irreversible destruction of pancreatic parenchyma
    • Fibrosis, chronic inflammation
    • Exocrine and endocrine dysfunction

    Etiology¶

    Mnemonic: "TIGAR-O": - T: Toxic-metabolic (Alcohol #1, smoking, hypercalcemia) - I: Idiopathic - G: Genetic (PRSS1, CFTR, SPINK1) - A: Autoimmune (IgG4-related) - R: Recurrent acute pancreatitis - O: Obstructive (stricture, tumor, pancreas divisum)

    Most Common Cause: Alcohol (70-80%) - Chronic, heavy use: >5 drinks/day × 5+ years - Dose-dependent - Smoking is independent risk factor (accelerates progression)

    Tropical Pancreatitis: - Endemic in India, Africa, Brazil - Young age, malnutrition, cassava consumption - Ductal stones, diabetes

    Pathophysiology¶

    Proposed Mechanisms:

    1. Toxic-Metabolic (Alcohol): - Direct acinar injury - Increased protein secretion → protein plugs → ductal obstruction → stone formation - Oxidative stress

    2. Ductal Obstruction: - Stones, strictures, tumors - Increased intraductal pressure → acinar atrophy, fibrosis

    3. Oxidative Stress: - Free radicals → acinar cell injury → inflammation → fibrosis

    Progression: 

    Chronic Injury (alcohol, etc.)
       ↓
Recurrent acute inflammation
       ↓
Fibrosis (irreversible)
       ↓
Exocrine insufficiency (↓ lipase, protease, amylase)
       ↓
Endocrine insufficiency (diabetes - "pancreatogenic diabetes")

    Clinical Features¶

    Pain (Most common symptom - 80-90%): - Chronic, recurrent epigastric pain - Radiates to back - Worse after eating (postprandial) - Relieved by leaning forward - May decrease over time (as gland "burns out" - fibrosis replaces parenchyma)

    Exocrine Insufficiency (Malabsorption): - Occurs when >90% function lost - Steatorrhea: Bulky, greasy, foul-smelling stools - Weight loss - Fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (A, D, E, K)

    Endocrine Insufficiency: - Diabetes mellitus ("Type 3c" - pancreatogenic): - Insulin AND glucagon deficiency - Brittle (prone to hypoglycemia - no glucagon response) - Low insulin requirement (vs Type ½) - Occurs in 30-80% over time

    Physical Examination: - Often unremarkable - Epigastric tenderness - Cachexia (malabsorption) - Jaundice (if biliary stricture)

    Diagnosis¶

    Clinical: - History: Chronic alcohol use, recurrent pancreatitis, pain, steatorrhea, diabetes

    Laboratory: - Pancreatic enzymes (amylase, lipase): - Often NORMAL (acinar atrophy) - Not useful for diagnosis - Fecal elastase-1: <200 μg/g (exocrine insufficiency) - Glucose: Elevated (diabetes) - Fat-soluble vitamins: Deficiency

    Imaging:

    CT Abdomen: - Pancreatic atrophy - Ductal dilation (>4 mm) - Calcifications (PATHOGNOMONIC - 30-50%) - Intraductal stones - Parenchymal calcifications - Pseudocysts

    MRI/MRCP: - Better ductal detail than CT - "Chain of lakes" appearance (dilated main duct with strictures)

    Endoscopic Ultrasound (EUS): - Most sensitive for early chronic pancreatitis - Parenchymal changes: Hyperechoic foci, stranding, lobularity - Ductal changes: Irregularity, dilation, stones, strictures

    ERCP: - Gold standard for ductal anatomy - "Chain of lakes", strictures, stones - Therapeutic (stone removal, stricture dilation, stenting) - Rarely used diagnostically (invasive - MRCP/EUS preferred)

    Function Tests (Rarely done): - Secretin stimulation test: Measure bicarbonate secretion (↓ in chronic pancreatitis)

    Complications¶

    1. Pain (Chronic, debilitating): - Impacts quality of life

    2. Exocrine Insufficiency: - Steatorrhea, malnutrition, vitamin deficiency

    3. Endocrine Insufficiency: - Diabetes (30-80%)

    4. Pancreatic Pseudocyst: - Common (20-40%) - Asymptomatic OR pain, early satiety, obstruction

    5. Biliary Stricture: - Common bile duct compression (fibrosis, pseudocyst) - Painless jaundice, cholangitis - Management: ERCP with stenting, surgery

    6. Duodenal Obstruction: - Gastric outlet obstruction - Fibrosis, pseudocyst

    7. Splenic Vein Thrombosis: - Left-sided portal hypertension → gastric varices

    8. Pancreatic Cancer: - Increased risk (5-15× general population) - Difficult to distinguish from chronic pancreatitis (both cause mass, ductal obstruction) - Consider if: New-onset pain, weight loss, CA 19-9 elevation

    Management¶

    Goals: 1. Pain control 2. Manage exocrine insufficiency 3. Manage endocrine insufficiency 4. Treat complications

    1. Pain Management¶

    Lifestyle Modifications: - Alcohol abstinence (CRITICAL) - Smoking cessation - Small, frequent, low-fat meals

    Medications:

    Analgesics: - Start with acetaminophen, NSAIDs - Opioids: Often required (high addiction risk - careful monitoring) - Tramadol, oxycodone, morphine

    Pancreatic Enzyme Supplementation: - Controversial for pain (may reduce secretion via negative feedback) - Non-enteric coated formulations (enteric-coated inactive in duodenum) - Limited evidence

    Antioxidants: - Controversial (limited evidence)

    Pregabalin/Gabapentin: - Neuropathic pain component

    Endoscopic Therapy: - ERCP: - Pancreatic duct stone removal (lithotripsy if large) - Stricture dilation + stenting - Helps if ductal obstruction (30-60% pain relief)

    Celiac Plexus Block: - EUS-guided or CT-guided - Temporary pain relief (weeks to months)

    Surgery: - Indications: Refractory pain despite medical/endoscopic therapy - Procedures: - Lateral pancreaticojejunostomy (Puestow procedure): - Dilated duct (>7 mm) - Drain duct into jejunum - Pancreatic resection: - Whipple (pancreaticoduodenectomy) - head disease - Distal pancreatectomy - tail disease - Total pancreatectomy (last resort - causes brittle diabetes)

    2. Exocrine Insufficiency¶

    Pancreatic Enzyme Replacement Therapy (PERT): - Indications: Steatorrhea, malnutrition, weight loss - Formulation: Enteric-coated (protect from gastric acid) - Dosing: - 40,000-50,000 units lipase with each meal - 20,000-25,000 units with snacks - Take with meals

    Adjuncts: - Proton pump inhibitor (PPI): If inadequate response (↑ duodenal pH for enzyme activity)

    Fat-Soluble Vitamins: - Supplement A, D, E, K

    Diet: - High-protein, high-calorie - Low-fat if severe steatorrhea despite enzymes - Small, frequent meals

    3. Endocrine Insufficiency (Diabetes)¶

    Diabetes Management: - Insulin therapy (usually required) - Brittle diabetes (prone to hypoglycemia - no glucagon) - Caution with sulfonylureas (hypoglycemia risk) - Frequent glucose monitoring

    4. Complications¶

    Pseudocyst: - Asymptomatic, <6 cm: Observation - Symptomatic: Endoscopic drainage (EUS-guided), surgical drainage

    Biliary Stricture: - ERCP with stenting (short-term) - Surgery (hepaticojejunostomy) if long-term obstruction

    Pancreatic Cancer Surveillance: - No established guidelines - High index of suspicion if new symptoms, CA 19-9 elevation

    Prognosis¶

    Mortality: - 50% 10-year survival - Higher mortality than general population

    Causes of Death: - Complications of chronic pancreatitis (infection, bleeding, malnutrition) - Pancreatic cancer - Complications of alcohol (cirrhosis, malnutrition)

    Quality of Life: - Chronic pain impairs quality of life - Malabsorption, diabetes

    Alcohol Abstinence: - Improves pain in 50% - Slows progression - Does NOT reverse fibrosis

    High-Yield Points¶

    Acute Pancreatitis¶

    Diagnosis (2 of 3): 1. Pain consistent with pancreatitis 2. Lipase ≥3× ULN (preferred over amylase) 3. Imaging consistent

    Most Common Causes: - Gallstones (40-50%) - overall #1 - Alcohol (30-40%) - #1 in men - Hypertriglyceridemia (>1000 mg/dL)

    Severity: - Mild: No organ failure, no complications - Severe: Persistent organ failure (>48 hours)

    Management: - Aggressive fluid resuscitation (MOST IMPORTANT - Ringer's lactate 250-500 mL/hr) - Early enteral nutrition (severe - within 24-72 hours) - NO prophylactic antibiotics (sterile necrosis) - Gallstone pancreatitis: Cholecystectomy same admission - ERCP: ONLY if cholangitis or biliary obstruction (NOT routine)

    Complications: - ARDS: Most common cause of death (first week) - Infected necrosis: 30-40% of necrotizing pancreatitis (2-4 weeks) - CT: Gas in necrosis - Antibiotics + Delayed drainage (step-up approach) - Pseudocyst: >4 weeks, defined wall, homogeneous fluid - Asymptomatic <6 cm: Observe - Symptomatic: Endoscopic drainage

    Cullen's Sign (periumbilical) and Grey Turner's Sign (flank): Retroperitoneal hemorrhage (rare, severe necrotizing)

    ALT >150 U/L: 95% PPV for gallstone pancreatitis

    Chronic Pancreatitis¶

    Most Common Cause: Alcohol (70-80%)

    Pathognomonic Finding: Pancreatic calcifications on CT

    Clinical Triad: 1. Chronic pain (80-90%) 2. Steatorrhea (exocrine insufficiency - when >90% function lost) 3. Diabetes (endocrine insufficiency - 30-80%)

    Diagnosis: - CT: Calcifications, ductal dilation, atrophy - MRCP: "Chain of lakes" (dilated duct with strictures) - EUS: Most sensitive (early disease) - Fecal elastase: <200 μg/g (exocrine insufficiency)

    Management: - Pain: Abstinence, analgesics (opioids often needed), ERCP (if ductal obstruction), surgery (refractory) - Exocrine insufficiency: PERT (40,000-50,000 units lipase with meals) + PPI - Diabetes: Insulin (brittle - prone to hypoglycemia)

    Complications: - Pseudocyst (20-40%) - Biliary stricture (CBD compression) - Pancreatic cancer (5-15× increased risk)

    Key Differences: Acute vs Chronic Pancreatitis¶

    Feature Acute Chronic
    Pathology Inflammation, edema (reversible) Fibrosis (irreversible)
    Lipase/Amylase Elevated (≥3× ULN) Normal or mildly elevated
    Calcifications Absent Present (pathognomonic)
    Pain Severe, acute Chronic, recurrent (may "burn out")
    Exocrine Insufficiency Rare Common (>90% loss)
    Treatment Supportive, fluids Pain control, PERT, insulin

    Practice MCQs¶

    Question 1¶

    A 45-year-old woman presents with severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, nausea, and vomiting for 12 hours. Labs: Lipase 1200 U/L (normal <60), ALT 280 U/L, AST 190 U/L, bilirubin 2.1 mg/dL. Ultrasound shows gallstones. What is the MOST appropriate next step after initial stabilization and pain control?

    A) Immediate cholecystectomy B) Immediate ERCP C) Cholecystectomy during same admission after pancreatitis resolves D) Discharge with outpatient cholecystectomy in 6 weeks

    Answer: C) Cholecystectomy during same admission after pancreatitis resolves

    Explanation: - Gallstone pancreatitis: - Lipase ≥3× ULN ✓ - ALT >150 U/L → 95% PPV for gallstones ✓ (this patient 280) - Gallstones on ultrasound ✓

    • Management:
    • Cholecystectomy same admission (after pancreatitis resolves - usually 48-72 hours)

    • Prevents recurrent pancreatitis (15-30% if no cholecystectomy)

    • ERCP indications:

    • Acute cholangitis (URGENT)
    • Biliary obstruction (persistent jaundice, dilated CBD)

    • NOT routine in gallstone pancreatitis

    • Immediate cholecystectomy: Not during acute inflammation (wait for resolution)

    • Delayed cholecystectomy (outpatient): Contraindicated (high recurrence risk)

    Question 2¶

    A 55-year-old man with acute pancreatitis secondary to alcohol is admitted to the ICU. On day 3, he develops persistent hypotension (SBP 80 mmHg despite fluids) and hypoxemia (PaO₂/FiO₂ 150). What is his pancreatitis severity classification?

    A) Mild B) Moderately severe C) Severe D) Cannot determine without CT

    Answer: C) Severe

    Explanation: - Revised Atlanta Classification:

    Severe Acute Pancreatitis: - Persistent organ failure (>48 hours)

    Organ Failure (Modified Marshall Score ≥2): - Respiratory: PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio (this patient 150 → score ≥2) - Cardiovascular: Hypotension (SBP <90 mmHg)

    • This patient:
    • Day 3 (>48 hours) ✓
    • Respiratory failure (PaO₂/FiO₂ 150) ✓
    • Cardiovascular failure (hypotension) ✓

    • → Severe pancreatitis

    • Mild: No organ failure, no complications

    • Moderately Severe: Transient organ failure (<48 hours) OR local complications

    • Severe: Persistent organ failure (>48 hours)

    • Mortality: Severe pancreatitis 15-30%

    Question 3¶

    A 60-year-old man with severe acute pancreatitis has been NPO for 5 days in the ICU. He is hemodynamically stable but still has significant pain. What is the MOST appropriate nutritional management?

    A) Continue NPO until pain resolves and lipase normalizes B) Start nasogastric enteral nutrition C) Start total parenteral nutrition (TPN) D) Wait for oral diet tolerance

    Answer: B) Start nasogastric enteral nutrition

    Explanation: - Severe Acute Pancreatitis - Nutrition:

    Enteral Nutrition (within 24-72 hours): - Preferred over TPN - Benefits: - ↓ Infection - ↓ Mortality - ↓ Cost - Preserves gut barrier function

    • Route: Nasogastric OR Nasojejunal (equivalent outcomes)

    • NG feeding safe (no need for post-pyloric)

    • TPN: ONLY if enteral not tolerated (ileus, intolerance)

    • Mild pancreatitis:

    • Resume oral feeding when pain improves and nausea resolves
    • No need to wait for lipase normalization

    Question 4¶

    A 50-year-old man with necrotizing pancreatitis develops fever (39°C) and leukocytosis (WBC 18,000) on day 14. CT shows a 6 cm area of pancreatic necrosis with gas bubbles within it. What is the diagnosis and MOST appropriate management?

    A) Sterile necrosis; observation B) Infected necrosis; immediate open necrosectomy C) Infected necrosis; antibiotics + delayed minimally invasive drainage (step-up approach) D) Pancreatic abscess; broad-spectrum antibiotics only

    Answer: C) Infected necrosis; antibiotics + delayed minimally invasive drainage (step-up approach)

    Explanation: - Infected Pancreatic Necrosis: - Day 14 (typically 2-4 weeks) ✓ - Fever, leukocytosis (sepsis) ✓ - CT: Gas within necrosis (pathognomonic) ✓

    • Management:

    1. Antibiotics: - Carbapenem (imipenem, meropenem) OR - Fluoroquinolone + Metronidazole

    2. Drainage/Debridement: - Step-Up Approach (preferred): 1. Percutaneous or endoscopic drainage (first) 2. Minimally invasive necrosectomy (if drainage fails) 3. Open necrosectomy (last resort)

    • Delayed intervention (≥4 weeks) preferred:
    • Allows walled-off necrosis (WON) formation

    • Easier to drain, better outcomes

    • Immediate open necrosectomy: Higher mortality (avoid)

    Question 5¶

    A 35-year-old man with a history of recurrent acute pancreatitis presents with a 6-week history of persistent epigastric pain. CT shows a 4 cm well-defined, round, fluid-filled lesion in the pancreatic tail with a mature wall. What is the diagnosis and appropriate management?

    A) Acute peripancreatic fluid collection; observation B) Pancreatic pseudocyst; immediate surgical drainage C) Pancreatic pseudocyst; observation (likely to resolve) D) Walled-off necrosis; antibiotics

    Answer: C) Pancreatic pseudocyst; observation (likely to resolve)

    Explanation: - Pancreatic Pseudocyst: - >4 weeks from pancreatitis onset ✓ (6 weeks) - Mature, defined wall ✓ - Homogeneous fluid (no solid debris) ✓ - Round, well-defined ✓

    • Management:
    • Asymptomatic, <6 cm: Observation (40-50% resolve spontaneously) ✓

    • Symptomatic or complications: Drainage (endoscopic preferred)

    • Acute Peripancreatic Fluid Collection (APFC):

    • <4 weeks (NOT this patient - 6 weeks)

    • No defined wall

    • Walled-Off Necrosis (WON):

    • >4 weeks
    • Heterogeneous (fluid + solid necrotic debris)
    • NOT homogeneous fluid (like this case)

    Question 6¶

    A 52-year-old man with chronic alcohol use presents with chronic epigastric pain, steatorrhea, and weight loss. CT abdomen shows pancreatic atrophy and multiple calcifications. Fecal elastase is 120 μg/g. What is the MOST appropriate treatment for his steatorrhea?

    A) Low-fat diet only B) Pancreatic enzyme replacement 40,000 units lipase with meals C) High-dose PPI therapy D) Octreotide injections

    Answer: B) Pancreatic enzyme replacement 40,000 units lipase with meals

    Explanation: - Chronic Pancreatitis with Exocrine Insufficiency: - Chronic alcohol use ✓ - Calcifications on CT (pathognomonic) ✓ - Steatorrhea ✓ - Fecal elastase <200 μg/g (exocrine insufficiency) ✓ (this patient 120)

    • Management of Exocrine Insufficiency:

    Pancreatic Enzyme Replacement Therapy (PERT): - 40,000-50,000 units lipase with each meal - 20,000-25,000 units with snacks - Enteric-coated formulation (protect from gastric acid) - Take with meals

    Adjuncts: - PPI: If inadequate response (↑ duodenal pH) - Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K)

    • Low-fat diet: May help but NOT sufficient alone
    • Octreotide: NOT for exocrine insufficiency (reduces secretions - not replacement)

    Question 7¶

    A 48-year-old woman presents with severe epigastric pain, nausea, and vomiting. Labs: Lipase 450 U/L, triglycerides 2800 mg/dL, lipemic serum. What is the MOST appropriate initial management in addition to aggressive fluid resuscitation?

    A) Plasmapheresis B) Gemfibrozil C) Insulin infusion + dextrose D) Omega-3 fatty acids

    Answer: C) Insulin infusion + dextrose

    Explanation: - Hypertriglyceridemia-Induced Pancreatitis: - Lipase elevated ✓ - Triglycerides >1000 mg/dL ✓ (this patient 2800) - Lipemic serum (milky appearance) ✓

    • Management:

    Acute: - Insulin infusion + Dextrose (if glucose <250 mg/dL): - Activates lipoprotein lipase → ↓ triglycerides rapidly - First-line

    • Plasmapheresis:
    • If refractory (TG >1000 despite insulin)
    • Labor-intensive, expensive

    Long-term prevention: - Fibrates (gemfibrozil, fenofibrate) - Omega-3 fatty acids (high-dose) - Dietary fat restriction

    Question 8¶

    A 65-year-old man with chronic pancreatitis develops painless jaundice. Labs: Total bilirubin 8 mg/dL, direct bilirubin 6 mg/dL, ALP 450 U/L, lipase 85 U/L. MRCP shows distal common bile duct stricture with upstream dilation. What is the MOST likely complication?

    A) Pancreatic cancer B) Choledocholithiasis C) Biliary stricture from chronic pancreatitis D) Primary sclerosing cholangitis

    Answer: C) Biliary stricture from chronic pancreatitis

    Explanation: - Chronic Pancreatitis Complication - Biliary Stricture: - Painless jaundice ✓ - Cholestatic pattern (elevated bilirubin, ALP) ✓ - MRCP: Distal CBD stricture ✓

    • Mechanism:
    • Fibrosis or pseudocyst compressing common bile duct

    • Usually distal CBD (passes through pancreatic head)

    • Management:

    • ERCP with stenting (short-term)

    • Surgery (hepaticojejunostomy) if long-term obstruction

    • Pancreatic cancer:

    • Consider in chronic pancreatitis patients (increased risk)
    • Differentiate with CA 19-9, EUS with biopsy

    • This patient: No mass mentioned, chronic pancreatitis known

    • Choledocholithiasis:

    • Filling defect (stone) on MRCP (NOT stricture)

    Question 9¶

    A 42-year-old man with chronic pancreatitis and diabetes is started on insulin therapy. He experiences frequent episodes of severe hypoglycemia (glucose <40 mg/dL) despite low insulin doses. What is the explanation?

    A) Excessive insulin dosing B) Type 3c diabetes with glucagon deficiency (brittle diabetes) C) Insulin resistance D) Factitious hypoglycemia

    Answer: B) Type 3c diabetes with glucagon deficiency (brittle diabetes)

    Explanation: - Pancreatogenic Diabetes (Type 3c): - Chronic pancreatitis → Destruction of islet cells - Insulin deficiency (beta cells) - Glucagon deficiency (alpha cells)

    • Brittle Diabetes:
    • Prone to hypoglycemia
    • No glucagon response to counteract insulin
    • Low insulin requirement (vs Type ½)

    • Frequent glucose fluctuations

    • Management:

    • Insulin therapy required
    • Frequent glucose monitoring
    • Caution with sulfonylureas (↑ hypoglycemia risk)
    • Glucagon kit for severe hypoglycemia

    Question 10¶

    A 38-year-old man presents with severe epigastric pain. Labs: Lipase 2000 U/L, calcium 12.5 mg/dL, PTH 185 pg/mL (elevated). What is the MOST likely underlying cause of his pancreatitis?

    A) Gallstones B) Alcohol C) Hypercalcemia (hyperparathyroidism) D) Hypertriglyceridemia

    Answer: C) Hypercalcemia (hyperparathyroidism)

    Explanation: - Hypercalcemia-Induced Pancreatitis: - Acute pancreatitis (lipase 2000) ✓ - Hypercalcemia (calcium 12.5 mg/dL, normal 8.5-10.5) ✓ - Elevated PTH → Primary hyperparathyroidism ✓

    • Mechanism:
    • Calcium deposition in pancreatic duct

    • Premature trypsinogen activation

    • Management:

    • Acute:
      • IV fluids
      • Bisphosphonates (if severe hypercalcemia)

    • Definitive: Parathyroidectomy (if hyperparathyroidism confirmed)

    • Any cause of hypercalcemia can cause pancreatitis:

    • Primary hyperparathyroidism (most common)
    • Malignancy
    • Vitamin D intoxication
    • Granulomatous disease

    Summary¶

    Acute Pancreatitis: - Diagnosis: 2 of 3 (pain + lipase ≥3× ULN + imaging) - Causes: Gallstones (#1 overall), Alcohol (#1 men), Hypertriglyceridemia (>1000) - Management: Aggressive fluids (Ringer's lactate), early enteral nutrition (severe), NO prophylactic antibiotics - Gallstone pancreatitis: Cholecystectomy same admission; ERCP only if cholangitis/obstruction - Complications: ARDS (early death), infected necrosis (late), pseudocyst (>4 weeks)

    Chronic Pancreatitis: - Cause: Alcohol (70-80%) - Pathognomonic: Pancreatic calcifications - Triad: Pain + Steatorrhea (exocrine insufficiency) + Diabetes (endocrine insufficiency) - Management: Abstinence, PERT (40,000-50,000 units lipase with meals), insulin (brittle diabetes)
