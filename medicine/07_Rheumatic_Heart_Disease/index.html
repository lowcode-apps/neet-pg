    Definition & Overview¶

    Definition¶

    Rheumatic Heart Disease (RHD) is chronic valvular heart disease resulting from Acute Rheumatic Fever (ARF), a delayed autoimmune complication of Group A Streptococcal (GAS) pharyngitis.

    Epidemiology¶

    • Most common cause of acquired heart disease in children and young adults (worldwide)
    • Most common cause of mitral stenosis (developing countries)
    • Prevalence: Low in developed countries (improved hygiene, antibiotics)
    • Prevalence: High in developing countries (India, Sub-Saharan Africa, South America)

    Etiology & Pathogenesis¶

    Etiology¶

    Group A β-Hemolytic Streptococcus (Streptococcus pyogenes)

    Key Points: - Only pharyngitis leads to ARF (NOT skin infections/pyoderma) - M-protein serotypes: M1, M3, M5, M18 (rheumatogenic strains) - Not all strains cause ARF

    Pathogenesis¶

    Mechanism: Molecular mimicry (Autoimmune)

    ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│         PATHOGENESIS OF ARF                            │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                        │
│  1. GAS Pharyngitis                                    │
│     ↓                                                  │
│  2. Immune Response to GAS antigens                    │
│     (M-protein, Hyaluronic acid capsule)               │
│     ↓                                                  │
│  3. MOLECULAR MIMICRY:                                 │
│     • GAS antigens resemble human proteins             │
│       - M-protein ≈ Cardiac myosin                     │
│       - M-protein ≈ Joint proteins                     │
│       - M-protein ≈ Basal ganglia                      │
│     ↓                                                  │
│  4. Cross-Reactive Antibodies                          │
│     • Attack heart, joints, brain                      │
│     ↓                                                  │
│  5. Autoimmune Inflammation                            │
│     ↓                                                  │
│  6. Acute Rheumatic Fever                              │
│                                                        │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Latent Period: 2-4 weeks (average 19 days) after GAS pharyngitis

    Risk Factors for ARF: - Age: 5-15 years (peak incidence) - Genetic predisposition: HLA-DR7, HLA-DR4 - Poverty, crowding - Poor hygiene - Recurrent GAS infections

    Acute Rheumatic Fever¶

    Clinical Manifestations - "JONES" Criteria¶

    5 Major + 4 Minor Criteria (Discussed in Diagnosis section)

    Major Manifestations: 1. Carditis (Pancarditis) 2. Polyarthritis (Migratory) 3. Chorea (Sydenham's chorea) 4. Erythema Marginatum 5. Subcutaneous Nodules

    1. Carditis (50-60% of ARF)¶

    Most serious manifestation (only one causing permanent damage)

    Pancarditis: Involves all 3 layers - Endocarditis: Valvulitis (Mitral > Aortic) - Myocarditis: Dilated heart, heart failure - Pericarditis: Pericardial effusion, friction rub

    Valvular Involvement: 

    Mitral valve:     75-80% (Most common)
Aortic valve:     30% (Usually with mitral)
Tricuspid:        10% (Rare alone)
Pulmonary:        <5% (Very rare)

    Acute Valvular Lesions: - Verrucae: Small vegetations along line of closure (1-2 mm) - Regurgitation (acute phase - valve edema, inflammation) - Mitral regurgitation - Aortic regurgitation - Stenosis: Develops LATER (chronic RHD) due to scarring

    Clinical Features of Carditis: - Tachycardia (out of proportion to fever) - New murmur: - Carey Coombs murmur: Mid-diastolic rumble (mitral valvulitis) - Mitral regurgitation: Holosystolic, apex → axilla - Aortic regurgitation: Early diastolic, left sternal border - Cardiomegaly - Heart failure (severe carditis) - Pericardial friction rub (pericarditis) - S3 gallop

    Aschoff Bodies (Pathognomonic): - Granulomas in myocardium - Contains Anitschkow cells (macrophages with ribbon-like chromatin - "caterpillar cells") - Aschoff giant cells (multinucleated)

    2. Polyarthritis (70-75% of ARF)¶

    Most common manifestation (but does NOT cause permanent damage)

    Characteristics: - Large joints: Knees, ankles, elbows, wrists - Migratory: Moves from one joint to another (1-2 days per joint) - Symmetric - Additive: New joints affected while old ones still symptomatic - Exquisitely painful - Red, hot, swollen, tender - Dramatic response to aspirin (within 24-48 hours) - Diagnostic clue

    Fleeting: Resolves completely without deformity (unlike rheumatoid arthritis)

    Duration: 2-3 weeks

    Note: Small joints, axial joints (spine) NOT typically involved

    3. Sydenham's Chorea (10-15% of ARF)¶

    St. Vitus' Dance - Involuntary, purposeless, rapid movements

    Characteristics: - Delayed onset: 1-6 months after GAS infection (longest latency) - Affects: Face, tongue, upper limbs (more than lower limbs) - Exacerbated by stress, disappear during sleep - Associated: Emotional lability, muscle weakness, hypotonia - More common: Females

    Signs: - Milkmaid's grip: Intermittent grip strength - Pronator sign: Hands pronate when held above head - Choreiform movements: Grimacing, tongue protrusion

    Prognosis: - Self-limited: Resolves in 3-6 months - May recur during pregnancy (Chorea gravidarum), OCP use

    Treatment: - Haloperidol or Valproic acid (if severe) - Benzathine penicillin prophylaxis

    4. Erythema Marginatum (<5% of ARF)¶

    Characteristics: - Evanescent, pink/red rash - Serpiginous (snake-like), annular (ring-shaped) with central clearing - Trunk and proximal limbs (NOT face) - Non-pruritic, non-painful - Blanches with pressure - Migratory, transient (comes and goes) - Heat-sensitive (appears with warmth)

    Pathognomonic for ARF (if present)

    5. Subcutaneous Nodules (1-5% of ARF)¶

    Characteristics: - Firm, painless, pea-sized (0.5-2 cm) - Over bony prominences, extensor surfaces - Elbows, knees, wrists, ankles - Occiput, spine - Symmetric - Mobile under skin - Last 1-2 weeks

    Associated with severe carditis

    Rare in current era

    Chronic Rheumatic Heart Disease¶

    Development¶

    • Results from recurrent episodes of ARF
    • Progressive fibrosis and scarring of valves
    • Develops years to decades after initial ARF

    Valvular Lesions¶

    Mitral Valve (Most Common):

    1. Mitral Stenosis (MS) - Most common RHD lesion - Mechanism: - Commissural fusion - Leaflet thickening, calcification - Chordal fusion and shortening - Fish-mouth or buttonhole orifice - Normal mitral valve area: 4-6 cm² - Severe MS: <1 cm²

    2. Mitral Regurgitation (MR) - Leaflet thickening, retraction - Chordal shortening - Incomplete coaptation

    3. Combined MS + MR

    Aortic Valve:

    1. Aortic Regurgitation (AR) - More common than AS - Leaflet thickening, retraction - Incomplete coaptation - Aortic root dilation

    2. Aortic Stenosis (AS) - Commissural fusion - Leaflet calcification

    3. Combined AS + AR

    Combined Lesions: - Mitral + Aortic: Common (30% of RHD) - Mitral + Aortic + Tricuspid: 10%

    Tricuspid Valve: - Usually regurgitation (functional due to RV dilation) - Rarely: Tricuspid stenosis (always with mitral disease)

    Pulmonary Valve: - Very rare

    Pathology¶

    Gross: - Thickened, fibrosed valves - Fused commissures - Shortened, thickened chordae - Fish-mouth orifice (MS) - Calcification (chronic)

    Microscopic: - Aschoff bodies (acute phase) - Pathognomonic - Anitschkow cells (macrophages) - Fibrosis (chronic)

    MacCallum's patch: - Left atrial endocardial thickening - Due to regurgitant jet in MR

    Clinical Features¶

    Acute Rheumatic Fever¶

    General: - Fever: Low to moderate (101-103°F) - Malaise, fatigue - Preceding pharyngitis (2-4 weeks before) - Age: 5-15 years (typically)

    Major Manifestations: - Carditis: Murmurs, tachycardia, cardiomegaly, HF - Polyarthritis: Migratory, large joints, exquisitely painful - Chorea: Involuntary movements (delayed onset) - Erythema marginatum: Annular rash, trunk/limbs - Subcutaneous nodules: Bony prominences

    Minor Manifestations: - Fever - Arthralgia (if arthritis not present as major) - Elevated acute phase reactants (ESR, CRP) - Prolonged PR interval (first-degree AV block)

    Chronic RHD¶

    Symptoms depend on valve lesion: - Mitral stenosis: Dyspnea, orthopnea, PND, hemoptysis, AF - Mitral regurgitation: Dyspnea, fatigue - Aortic regurgitation: Dyspnea, angina, wide pulse pressure - Aortic stenosis: Syncope, angina, dyspnea

    Detailed in: Valvular Heart Disease topic

    Diagnosis - Jones Criteria¶

    Modified Jones Criteria (2015 AHA/ACC)¶

    For Diagnosis of ACUTE RHEUMATIC FEVER:

    Requirements: 

    Evidence of GAS infection
        +
2 Major criteria, OR
1 Major + 2 Minor criteria

    Evidence of Preceding GAS Infection (MANDATORY)¶

    One of: 1. Positive throat culture or Rapid Strep antigen test 2. Elevated/Rising anti-streptococcal antibodies: - ASO (Anti-Streptolysin O) - Most common - Anti-DNase B - Anti-Hyaluronidase - Streptozyme (multiple antibodies)

    Note: - ASO peaks at 3-5 weeks, remains elevated for months - Anti-DNase B more specific (remains elevated longer) - Negative ASO does NOT rule out ARF (20% of cases)

    Major Criteria¶

    ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│         MAJOR CRITERIA ("JONES")                     │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ J - Joints (Polyarthritis - migratory, large)        │
│ O - ❤️ (Carditis - pancarditis)                      │
│ N - Nodules (Subcutaneous)                           │
│ E - Erythema marginatum                              │
│ S - Sydenham's chorea                                │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Note: 2015 revision has different criteria for low-risk vs moderate/high-risk populations:

    Low-Risk Populations (e.g., USA, Western Europe): - Polyarthritis (only)

    Moderate/High-Risk (e.g., India, Africa): - Polyarthritis OR Monoarthritis OR Polyarthralgia

    Minor Criteria¶

    Clinical: 1. Fever: ≥38.5°C (low-risk) or ≥38°C (moderate/high-risk) 2. Arthralgia: (If arthritis NOT present as major) - Polyarthralgia (low-risk) - Monoarthralgia (moderate/high-risk)

    Laboratory: 3. Elevated acute phase reactants: - ESR ≥60 mm/hr (low-risk) or ≥30 mm/hr (moderate/high-risk) - CRP ≥3 mg/dL (low-risk) or ≥1 mg/dL (moderate/high-risk)

    1. Prolonged PR interval (first-degree AV block) on ECG
    2. Age-specific values

    Special Situations¶

    1. Recurrent ARF (Patient with history of ARF/RHD): - Lower threshold: 2 Minor criteria + Evidence of GAS infection

    2. Chorea alone: - May be sufficient for diagnosis (long latency - other criteria resolved)

    3. Indolent Carditis: - May present alone (especially in chronic RHD reactivation)

    Investigations¶

    Microbiological¶

    1. Throat Culture: - Gold standard for current GAS infection - Sensitivity: 90-95% (if done properly) - May be negative by time of ARF (2-4 weeks later)

    2. Rapid Strep Antigen Test: - Quick (5-10 minutes) - Lower sensitivity than culture - High specificity

    3. Anti-Streptococcal Antibodies: - ASO (Anti-Streptolysin O): - Most widely used - Peaks at 3-5 weeks - Elevated: >200 Todd units (adults), >333 (children) - Caveat: NOT elevated after skin infections (streptolysin O inactivated by skin lipids)

    • Anti-DNase B:
    • More specific than ASO
    • Elevated after both pharyngitis AND skin infections
    • Remains elevated longer

    • Elevated: >480 units (children), >240 (adults)

    • Streptozyme:

    • Detects multiple antibodies (ASO, DNase B, Hyaluronidase, Streptokinase)
    • Screening test

    Interpretation: - Rising titer (2-fold increase) more significant than single elevated value - Negative ASO does NOT rule out ARF (20%)

    Acute Phase Reactants¶

    1. ESR (Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate): - Elevated in ARF (usually >60 mm/hr) - Non-specific - May be normal in chorea, mild carditis

    2. CRP (C-Reactive Protein): - Elevated in ARF - More sensitive than ESR - Returns to normal faster than ESR

    Note: - Normal ESR/CRP makes ARF unlikely (except isolated chorea) - Not elevated in chorea (long latency)

    Cardiac Investigations¶

    1. ECG: - PR prolongation (first-degree AV block) - Most common (40-50%) - Minor criterion (if not higher-degree block) - Second/Third-degree AV block (less common) - ST-T changes (myocarditis, pericarditis) - Arrhythmias: Atrial fibrillation (chronic MS)

    2. Echocardiography: - Mandatory in all suspected ARF - Subclinical carditis: Echo-detected valvulitis without clinical signs - Findings: - Valve regurgitation (Mitral > Aortic) - Valve thickening - Restricted motion - Pericardial effusion - LV dysfunction

    3. Chest X-ray: - Cardiomegaly (severe carditis, chronic RHD) - Pulmonary edema (heart failure) - Normal (mild carditis)

    Other Investigations¶

    CBC: - Leukocytosis (neutrophilia) - Anemia (chronic disease - chronic RHD)

    Serum Protein Electrophoresis: - ↑ α2-globulin, γ-globulin

    Management¶

    Acute Rheumatic Fever¶

    1. Eradicate GAS Infection¶

    Benzathine Penicillin G 1.2 million units IM (single dose) - DOC for GAS eradication - OR Oral Penicillin V 250 mg QID × 10 days

    Penicillin Allergy: - Azithromycin 500 mg daily × 5 days, OR - Erythromycin 250 mg QID × 10 days

    Note: Given even if throat culture negative (ARF occurs 2-4 weeks later)

    2. Anti-Inflammatory Therapy¶

    For Arthritis/Arthralgia:

    Aspirin (High-dose): - Dose: 80-100 mg/kg/day (divided q4-6h) - Duration: 4-8 weeks (taper gradually) - Response: Dramatic within 24-48 hours (diagnostic clue) - Monitoring: Salicylate levels (20-25 mg/dL therapeutic) - Side effects: Tinnitus, GI upset, hepatotoxicity

    For Carditis:

    Mild Carditis (no cardiomegaly, HF): - Aspirin (same as arthritis)

    Moderate to Severe Carditis (cardiomegaly, HF): - Prednisolone: - Dose: 2 mg/kg/day (max 60-80 mg) - Duration: 2-3 weeks, then taper over 2-3 weeks - Overlap aspirin during steroid taper - Benefit: Reduces inflammation, may prevent chronic damage (controversial)

    Heart Failure Management: - Bed rest - Diuretics: Furosemide - ACE inhibitors - Digoxin: If AF, severe MR - Avoid β-blockers initially (may worsen AV block)

    3. Symptomatic Treatment¶

    Chorea: - Mild: Reassurance, avoid stress - Severe/Disabling: - Haloperidol 0.5-2 mg BID/TID - Valproic acid 15-20 mg/kg/day (alternative) - Carbamazepine (alternative)

    General: - Bed rest (during acute phase, especially carditis) - Fever: Antipyretics (paracetamol)

    4. Secondary Prophylaxis (Most Important)¶

    Prevent recurrent ARF → Prevent chronic RHD

    See Prophylaxis section below

    Chronic RHD¶

    Medical Management: - Heart failure: Diuretics, ACE inhibitors, Digoxin - Atrial fibrillation: Rate control, anticoagulation (Warfarin) - Infective endocarditis prophylaxis (if indicated) - Continued secondary prophylaxis

    Surgical Management: - Indicated for significant valve dysfunction - Mitral stenosis: - PBMV (Percutaneous Balloon Mitral Valvuloplasty): If suitable anatomy - Open mitral commissurotomy: If not suitable for PBMV - Mitral valve replacement: If severe calcification, MR, unsuitable anatomy - Mitral/Aortic regurgitation: Valve repair/replacement - Aortic stenosis: Valve replacement

    Detailed in: Valvular Heart Disease topic

    Prophylaxis¶

    Primary Prophylaxis¶

    Goal: Prevent ARF by treating GAS pharyngitis

    Indications: - All GAS pharyngitis (especially in children, endemic areas)

    Regimen: - Benzathine Penicillin G 1.2 million units IM (single dose) - Best - OR Oral Penicillin V 250 mg TID × 10 days

    Penicillin Allergy: - Azithromycin 500 mg daily × 5 days - Erythromycin 250 mg QID × 10 days - First-generation cephalosporin (if no anaphylaxis to PCN)

    Important: Complete 10-day course (oral) - adherence critical

    Secondary Prophylaxis (Most Important)¶

    Goal: Prevent recurrent ARF → Prevent/limit chronic RHD

    Indications: - ALL patients with history of ARF (with or without carditis)

    Regimen:

    Benzathine Penicillin G: - Dose: 1.2 million units IM - Frequency: - Every 4 weeks (standard) - Every 3 weeks (high-risk areas, previous recurrence) - Route: IM (better adherence than oral)

    Oral Alternatives (if IM not feasible): - Penicillin V 250 mg PO BID, OR - Sulfadiazine 0.5 g (if <27 kg) or 1 g (if >27 kg) PO daily

    Penicillin Allergy: - Azithromycin 250 mg PO daily, OR - Erythromycin 250 mg PO BID

    Duration of Secondary Prophylaxis¶

    ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      DURATION OF SECONDARY PROPHYLAXIS                   │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                          │
│ ARF WITHOUT carditis:                                    │
│   → 5 years OR until age 21 (whichever longer)           │
│                                                          │
│ ARF WITH carditis but NO residual heart disease:         │
│   → 10 years OR until age 21 (whichever longer)          │
│                                                          │
│ ARF WITH carditis AND residual heart disease (RHD):      │
│   → 10 years OR until age 40 (whichever longer)          │
│   → Some recommend LIFELONG (especially severe RHD)      │
│                                                          │
│ Post-valve surgery:                                      │
│   → LIFELONG                                             │
│                                                          │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    High-Risk Patients (consider longer/lifelong): - Healthcare workers - Teachers (exposure to children) - Parents of young children - Living in endemic areas

    NEET PG High-Yield Points¶

    🔥 MUST KNOW¶

    1. ARF is autoimmune sequela of Group A Streptococcal (GAS) pharyngitis
    2. Only pharyngitis (NOT skin infections) causes ARF

    3. Latent period: 2-4 weeks

    4. Mechanism: Molecular mimicry (GAS antigens resemble cardiac myosin, joints, basal ganglia)

    5. Age: 5-15 years (peak)

    6. Jones Criteria for ARF Diagnosis:

    7. Evidence of GAS infection (MANDATORY)

    8. PLUS: 2 Major OR 1 Major + 2 Minor

    9. Major Criteria - "JONES":

    10. **J**oints (Polyarthritis - migratory, large)
    11. O (Heart - Carditis)
    12. **N**odules (Subcutaneous)
    13. **E**rythema marginatum

    14. **S**ydenham's chorea

    15. Minor Criteria:

    16. Fever, Arthralgia, ↑ ESR/CRP, Prolonged PR

    17. Polyarthritis:

    18. Most common manifestation (70-75%)
    19. Large joints: Knees, ankles, elbows, wrists
    20. Migratory, additive
    21. Exquisitely painful
    22. Dramatic response to aspirin (24-48 hours)

    23. No permanent damage

    24. Carditis:

    25. Most serious manifestation (only one causing permanent damage)
    26. Pancarditis: Endocarditis + Myocarditis + Pericarditis
    27. Valve: Mitral > Aortic
    28. Acute: Regurgitation (MR, AR)
    29. Carey Coombs murmur: Mid-diastolic rumble (mitral valvulitis)

    30. Aschoff bodies: Pathognomonic granulomas in myocardium (Anitschkow cells)

    31. Sydenham's Chorea:

    32. Delayed onset: 1-6 months (longest latency)
    33. Involuntary, purposeless movements
    34. Females > Males
    35. Self-limited: 3-6 months

    36. May be sole manifestation (other criteria resolved)

    37. Erythema Marginatum:

      • Annular, serpiginous, pink rash
      • Trunk/proximal limbs (NOT face)
      • Central clearing
      • Pathognomonic for ARF

    38. ASO (Anti-Streptolysin O):

      • Most common anti-streptococcal antibody test
      • Peaks: 3-5 weeks post-infection
      • Negative in 20% of ARF cases

    39. Anti-DNase B:

      • More specific than ASO
      • Elevated after pharyngitis AND skin infections (unlike ASO)

    40. ECG: PR prolongation (first-degree AV block) - most common (40-50%)

    41. Treatment of ARF:

      • Eradicate GAS: Benzathine Penicillin G 1.2 million units IM
      • Arthritis: Aspirin 80-100 mg/kg/day (dramatic response)
      • Severe carditis: Prednisolone 2 mg/kg/day
      • Chorea: Haloperidol, Valproic acid

    42. Secondary Prophylaxis (MOST IMPORTANT):

      • Regimen: Benzathine Penicillin G 1.2 million units IM every 3-4 weeks
      • Duration:
      • No carditis: 5 years or age 21
      • Carditis no RHD: 10 years or age 21
      • RHD: 10 years or age 40 (or lifelong)

    43. Chronic RHD:

      • Results from recurrent ARF
      • Most common lesion: Mitral stenosis
      • Valve involvement: Mitral (75%) > Aortic (30%) > Tricuspid (10%)

    44. Pathology:

      • Aschoff bodies: Pathognomonic (granulomas with Anitschkow cells)
      • Commissural fusion, leaflet thickening, chordal fusion
      • Fish-mouth orifice (MS)

    Clinical Scenarios¶

    Scenario 1: Child with sore throat 3 weeks ago, now fever + migratory joint pain (knees, ankles) → ARF with polyarthritis

    Scenario 2: ARF patient with dramatic response to aspirin within 24 hours → Confirms polyarthritis

    Scenario 3: ARF with involuntary movements, emotional lability starting 3 months after pharyngitis → Sydenham's chorea

    Scenario 4: ARF with annular rash on trunk, central clearing → Erythema marginatum

    Scenario 5: ARF with mid-diastolic rumble at apex → Carey Coombs murmur (mitral valvulitis)

    Scenario 6: ARF patient with elevated ASO, normal ESR/CRP → Likely isolated chorea (long latency)

    Scenario 7: Young adult with mitral stenosis (fish-mouth orifice on echo) → Chronic RHD

    Scenario 8: ARF patient → Give Benzathine Penicillin G IM every 3-4 weeks for secondary prophylaxis

    Scenario 9: ARF without carditis → Continue prophylaxis for 5 years or until age 21

    Scenario 10: ARF with carditis and RHD → Continue prophylaxis for 10 years or until age 40

    Quick Revision Checklist¶

    • ARF: Autoimmune, post-GAS pharyngitis, 2-4 weeks latency
    • Molecular mimicry mechanism
    • Jones Criteria: GAS evidence + (2 Major OR 1 Major + 2 Minor)
    • Major: JONES (Joints, O-heart, Nodules, Erythema, Sydenham's)
    • Minor: Fever, Arthralgia, ↑ ESR/CRP, PR prolongation
    • Polyarthritis: Migratory, large joints, aspirin response
    • Carditis: Pancarditis, MR/AR (acute), Carey Coombs murmur
    • Aschoff bodies: Pathognomonic
    • Chorea: Delayed (1-6 months), self-limited
    • Erythema marginatum: Annular, trunk, pathognomonic
    • ASO peaks 3-5 weeks, negative in 20%
    • Treatment: Benzathine PCN (eradicate), Aspirin (arthritis), Prednisolone (severe carditis)
    • Secondary prophylaxis: Benzathine PCN IM q3-4 weeks
    • Duration: 5y/age 21 (no carditis), 10y/age 21 (carditis no RHD), 10y/age 40 (RHD)
    • Chronic RHD: Mitral stenosis most common

    Practice Questions¶

    Question 1¶

    A 10-year-old boy presents with fever and migratory joint pain affecting knees and ankles for 1 week. He had sore throat 3 weeks ago. On examination, joints are swollen and tender. Which feature would MOST support the diagnosis of ARF?

    A) Joint pain persisting for >1 month B) Dramatic improvement within 24 hours of aspirin C) Small joint involvement (fingers, toes) D) Residual joint deformity

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: B) Dramatic improvement within 24 hours of aspirin** **Explanation:** - **ARF polyarthritis** has **characteristic dramatic response to aspirin** within 24-48 hours - This rapid response is a **diagnostic clue** for ARF **ARF Polyarthritis Features:** - **Large joints** (knees, ankles, elbows, wrists) - **Migratory** (moves from joint to joint) - **Exquisitely painful** - **Fleeting** (2-3 weeks total) - **NO residual deformity** (unlike rheumatoid arthritis) **Why not others:** - Prolonged duration (>1 month) suggests other diagnosis - Small joints NOT typically involved in ARF - NO residual deformity in ARF (complete resolution)

    Question 2¶

    A 12-year-old girl diagnosed with ARF has involuntary, purposeless movements of face and hands that worsen with stress and disappear during sleep. These symptoms appeared 4 months after sore throat. Her ESR and CRP are normal. What is the most likely manifestation?

    A) Polyarthritis B) Sydenham's chorea C) Carditis D) Erythema marginatum

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: B) Sydenham's chorea** **Explanation:** - **Sydenham's chorea** (St. Vitus' Dance) - **Characteristic features:** - **Delayed onset:** 1-6 months after GAS infection (LONGEST latency) - **Involuntary, purposeless movements** - **Worsen with stress, disappear during sleep** - **Emotional lability** - **Normal ESR/CRP** (due to long latency - acute inflammation resolved) - **May be SOLE manifestation** of ARF (other criteria resolved by then) **Treatment:** - Self-limited (3-6 months) - If severe: Haloperidol or Valproic acid **Still requires:** - Benzathine Penicillin for GAS eradication - Secondary prophylaxis

    Question 3¶

    Which histopathological finding is PATHOGNOMONIC for rheumatic carditis?

    A) Caseating granulomas B) Aschoff bodies C) Langerhans giant cells D) Reed-Sternberg cells

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: B) Aschoff bodies** **Explanation:** - **Aschoff bodies** are **PATHOGNOMONIC** for rheumatic carditis - **Granulomas in myocardium** **Components:** - **Anitschkow cells** (macrophages with ribbon-like chromatin - "caterpillar cells") - **Aschoff giant cells** (multinucleated) - Central area of fibrinoid necrosis - Lymphocytes **Other findings:** - **Caseating granulomas:** Tuberculosis - **Langerhans giant cells:** Tuberculosis, sarcoidosis - **Reed-Sternberg cells:** Hodgkin's lymphoma

    Question 4¶

    A 15-year-old diagnosed with ARF with carditis but NO residual heart disease on echo at discharge. What is the recommended duration of secondary prophylaxis?

    A) 5 years or until age 21, whichever is longer B) 10 years or until age 21, whichever is longer C) 10 years or until age 40, whichever is longer D) Lifelong

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: B) 10 years or until age 21, whichever is longer** **Explanation:** - **Duration of secondary prophylaxis:** 
    ARF WITHOUT carditis:
  → 5 years OR until age 21 (whichever longer)

ARF WITH carditis but NO residual heart disease:
  → 10 years OR until age 21 (whichever longer) ✓

ARF WITH carditis AND residual heart disease (RHD):
  → 10 years OR until age 40 (whichever longer)
  → Some recommend lifelong

Post-valve surgery:
  → LIFELONG
    **Regimen:** - **Benzathine Penicillin G 1.2 million units IM every 3-4 weeks** **Goal:** Prevent recurrent ARF → Prevent chronic RHD

    Question 5¶

    Which valve lesion is MOST common in chronic rheumatic heart disease?

    A) Mitral regurgitation B) Mitral stenosis C) Aortic stenosis D) Aortic regurgitation

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: B) Mitral stenosis** **Explanation:** - **Mitral stenosis** is the **MOST COMMON** lesion in chronic RHD - **RHD is the MOST COMMON cause** of mitral stenosis (>99%) **Chronic RHD Valve Involvement:** - **Mitral valve:** 75-80% (Most common) - **Mitral stenosis** (most common lesion) - Mitral regurgitation - Combined MS + MR - **Aortic valve:** 30% (usually with mitral) - **Aortic regurgitation** > Aortic stenosis - **Tricuspid:** 10% (rarely alone) - **Pulmonary:** <5% (very rare) **Pathology:** - Commissural fusion - Leaflet thickening, fibrosis - Chordal fusion and shortening - **Fish-mouth orifice** **Note:** - **Acute ARF** causes **regurgitation** (MR, AR) - valvulitis, edema - **Chronic RHD** causes **stenosis** (MS, AS) - scarring, fibrosis

    Question 6¶

    A patient with suspected ARF has elevated ASO titer. Which of the following is TRUE about ASO?

    A) Peaks at 1 week post-infection B) Always elevated in ARF C) Elevated after streptococcal skin infections D) More specific than Anti-DNase B

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: None of the options are correct, but if forced to choose, D is "least wrong" though still incorrect** **Explanation:** Let me correct each option: **ASO (Anti-Streptolysin O) Facts:** - **Peaks:** 3-5 weeks post-infection (NOT 1 week) - A is wrong - **Negative in 20% of ARF** (NOT always elevated) - B is wrong - **NOT elevated after skin infections** (streptolysin O inactivated by skin lipids) - C is wrong - **LESS specific** than Anti-DNase B (NOT more specific) - D is wrong **Anti-DNase B:** - **More specific** than ASO - Elevated after BOTH pharyngitis AND skin infections - Remains elevated longer **Best Answer for Question:** - If this was an actual exam, the question might be flawed - **Anti-DNase B is more specific than ASO** - But none of the options correctly describe ASO **Key Points:** - ASO peaks 3-5 weeks - 20% of ARF have normal ASO - Only elevated after pharyngitis (not skin) - Less specific than Anti-DNase B

    Question 7¶

    A child with ARF has a pink, non-pruritic rash on trunk with serpiginous borders and central clearing. What is this rash called?

    A) Osler nodes B) Janeway lesions C) Erythema marginatum D) Erythema nodosum

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: C) Erythema marginatum** **Explanation:** - **Erythema marginatum** is a **major criterion** for ARF **Characteristics:** - **Pink/red rash** - **Serpiginous** (snake-like), **annular** (ring-shaped) - **Central clearing** - **Trunk and proximal limbs** (NOT face) - **Non-pruritic, non-painful** - **Blanches with pressure** - **Evanescent** (comes and goes) - **Heat-sensitive** (appears with warmth) **PATHOGNOMONIC for ARF** (if present) **Rare** in current era (<5% of ARF) **Differentials:** - **Osler nodes:** Painful nodules on fingertips (Infective endocarditis) - **Janeway lesions:** Painless hemorrhagic macules on palms/soles (IE) - **Erythema nodosum:** Tender nodules on shins (Sarcoidosis, TB, IBD)

    Question 8¶

    What is the MOST appropriate regimen for secondary prophylaxis in a patient with history of ARF?

    A) Oral Penicillin V 250 mg once daily B) Benzathine Penicillin G 1.2 million units IM every 3-4 weeks C) Amoxicillin 500 mg TID for 10 days every month D) Aspirin 325 mg daily

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: B) Benzathine Penicillin G 1.2 million units IM every 3-4 weeks** **Explanation:** - **Secondary prophylaxis** prevents recurrent ARF → prevents chronic RHD **Preferred Regimen:** - **Benzathine Penicillin G 1.2 million units IM** - **Frequency:** Every 4 weeks (standard) or Every 3 weeks (high-risk) - **IM route preferred** (better adherence than oral) **Oral Alternatives** (if IM not feasible): - **Penicillin V 250 mg PO BID** (NOT once daily) - Sulfadiazine 1 g PO daily **Penicillin Allergy:** - Azithromycin 250 mg PO daily - Erythromycin 250 mg PO BID **Why not others:** - Oral Penicillin V: BID, not once daily (poor adherence) - Amoxicillin intermittent: Not standard regimen - Aspirin: For acute arthritis, NOT prophylaxis **Duration:** Based on carditis and RHD status (see Q4)
