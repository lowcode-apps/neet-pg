    Glomerular Diseases¶

    Definition¶

    Glomerular diseases are a heterogeneous group of disorders affecting the glomeruli, characterized by: - Structural damage to glomerular components (mesangium, basement membrane, epithelial/endothelial cells) - Functional impairment → proteinuria, hematuria, ↓ GFR - Clinical presentation as nephrotic syndrome, nephritic syndrome, or mixed patterns

    Classification¶

    By Etiology: 1. Primary glomerular diseases (idiopathic) - Minimal change disease (MCD) - Focal segmental glomerulosclerosis (FSGS) - Membranous nephropathy (MN) - IgA nephropathy - Membranoproliferative GN (MPGN)

    1. Secondary glomerular diseases
    2. Diabetic nephropathy
    3. Lupus nephritis
    4. Post-infectious GN
    5. ANCA-associated vasculitis
    6. Anti-GBM disease (Goodpasture syndrome)
    7. Amyloidosis

    By Clinical Presentation: 1. Nephrotic syndrome (proteinuria dominant) 2. Nephritic syndrome (hematuria + inflammation) 3. Mixed nephritic-nephrotic 4. Asymptomatic urinary abnormalities 5. Rapidly progressive GN (RPGN)

    Pathophysiology¶

    Normal Glomerular Filtration Barrier¶

    Three-Layer Structure: 1. Fenestrated endothelium - Prevents blood cells from filtering

    1. Glomerular basement membrane (GBM)
    2. Type IV collagen scaffold

    3. Negative charge (heparan sulfate) → repels albumin

    4. Podocytes (epithelial cells)

    5. Foot processes with slit diaphragms
    6. Nephrin protein maintains barrier

    Size and Charge Selectivity: - Molecules <20 kDa freely filtered - Albumin (69 kDa, negative charge) → normally NOT filtered - Loss of charge barrier → albuminuria

    Mechanisms of Glomerular Injury¶

    1. Immune-Mediated:

    A. Antibody-Mediated: - Anti-GBM antibodies → linear IF pattern (Goodpasture) - Immune complex deposition → granular IF pattern (SLE, IgA nephropathy, post-strep GN)

    B. Cell-Mediated: - T-cell mediated (minimal change disease)

    2. Complement-Mediated: - Alternative pathway activation (C3 glomerulopathy, MPGN) - Membrane attack complex (C5b-9) → podocyte injury

    3. Hemodynamic: - Glomerular hypertension → hyperfiltration injury (diabetic nephropathy) - RAAS activation → mesangial expansion, sclerosis

    4. Metabolic: - Hyperglycemia → AGE formation, podocyte loss (diabetes) - Hyperlipidemia → lipotoxicity

    Clinical Features¶

    Nephrotic Syndrome¶

    Diagnostic Criteria (3 cardinal features): 1. Proteinuria ≥3.5 g/day (or UPCR ≥3.5 g/g) 2. Hypoalbuminemia (<3 g/dL) 3. Edema (periorbital → dependent → anasarca)

    Additional Features: - Hyperlipidemia (↑ LDL, ↑ total cholesterol) - Lipiduria (oval fat bodies, Maltese crosses) - Hypercoagulability (loss of antithrombin III → thrombosis)

    Common Causes (by age):

    Children: - Minimal change disease (80%) - FSGS (10%) - Membranous nephropathy (rare)

    Adults: - Membranous nephropathy (30-40%) - FSGS (35%) - Minimal change disease (10-15%) - Diabetic nephropathy (most common overall cause)

    Nephritic Syndrome¶

    Clinical Features: 1. Hematuria (gross or microscopic) 2. RBC casts (pathognomonic for glomerulonephritis) 3. Proteinuria (<3.5 g/day) 4. Hypertension (salt/water retention) 5. Oliguria (↓ GFR) 6. Edema (mild to moderate) 7. Azotemia (↑ Cr)

    Classic Example: Post-streptococcal GN - 1-3 weeks after pharyngitis (GAS infection) - Tea-colored urine - ↓ C3 (normalizes in 6-8 weeks)

    Common Causes: - IgA nephropathy (most common worldwide) - Post-infectious GN - MPGN - Lupus nephritis (Class III, IV) - ANCA-associated vasculitis

    Rapidly Progressive Glomerulonephritis (RPGN)¶

    Definition: - Acute nephritis with rapid ↓ GFR (over days-weeks) - Crescent formation on biopsy (>50% glomeruli)

    Clinical: - Oliguria/anuria - Severe hypertension - Rapidly rising creatinine - Without treatment → ESRD in weeks-months

    Pathologic Classification (by IF pattern):

    Type IF Pattern Causes
    Type I Linear Anti-GBM disease (Goodpasture)
    Type II Granular Lupus nephritis, post-infectious GN, IgA nephropathy
    Type III Pauci-immune (negative) ANCA-associated vasculitis (GPA, MPA)

    Specific Glomerular Diseases¶

    1. Minimal Change Disease (MCD)¶

    Epidemiology: - Most common nephrotic syndrome in children (80%) - Adults: 10-15% of nephrotic syndrome

    Etiology: - Primary (idiopathic) - 90% - Secondary: NSAIDs, Hodgkin lymphoma, allergies

    Pathophysiology: - T-cell dysfunction → circulating factor → podocyte injury - Loss of negative charge on GBM → selective albuminuria

    Clinical: - Sudden onset nephrotic syndrome - NO hematuria, NO hypertension (unlike FSGS) - Normal renal function initially

    Diagnosis:

    Light Microscopy: Normal glomeruli (hence "minimal change")

    Electron Microscopy: Podocyte foot process effacement (diagnostic)

    Immunofluorescence: Negative (no immune deposits)

    Lab: - Selective proteinuria (mostly albumin) - Normal complement (C3, C4)

    Treatment:

    Children: - Corticosteroids (FIRST-LINE): - Prednisone 60 mg/m²/day × 4-6 weeks → taper over 6 months - Response in 90% (within 2-4 weeks)

    Definition of Response: - Remission: Proteinuria <1+ on dipstick × 3 days - Relapse: Proteinuria ≥3+ × 3 days (after remission) - Steroid-dependent: 2 relapses during taper or within 2 weeks of stopping - Steroid-resistant: No remission after 8 weeks of steroids

    Second-Line (steroid-dependent/resistant): - Cyclophosphamide - Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, cyclosporine) - Rituximab (emerging evidence)

    Adults: - Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day (max 80 mg) × 8-16 weeks - Slower response than children (may take 8-16 weeks)

    Supportive: - ACE inhibitor/ARB (↓ proteinuria) - Diuretics (edema) - Statin (hyperlipidemia) - Thromboprophylaxis if severe (albumin <2 g/dL)

    Prognosis: - Excellent in children (no progression to ESRD) - Adults: 10-20% may develop FSGS over time - Relapses common (30-50%) but steroid-responsive

    2. Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)¶

    Epidemiology: - Most common primary GN causing nephrotic syndrome in adults (35%) - Most common primary GN causing ESRD in African Americans

    Etiology:

    Primary (Idiopathic): - Podocyte injury (mutations in NPHS1, NPHS2, TRPC6)

    Secondary: - Hyperfiltration: Obesity, reduced nephron mass (single kidney, reflux nephropathy) - Viral: HIV, CMV, parvovirus B19 - Drugs: Heroin, pamidronate, interferon, anabolic steroids - Genetic: Alport syndrome

    Pathophysiology: - Podocyte injury → detachment → sclerosis - "Maladaptive" hyperfiltration in remaining nephrons → progressive FSGS

    Clinical: - Nephrotic syndrome (60-70%) - Hypertension (50%) - Microscopic hematuria (25-75%) - Renal insufficiency at presentation (20-30%)

    Diagnosis:

    Light Microscopy: - Focal: Only some glomeruli affected (<50%) - Segmental: Only part of glomerulus sclerosed - Hyaline deposits, foam cells

    Variants: - Collapsing FSGS (worst prognosis, HIV-associated) - Tip lesion (best prognosis) - Perihilar (obesity-related) - Cellular - Classic/NOS

    Electron Microscopy: Podocyte foot process effacement

    Immunofluorescence: IgM and C3 in sclerotic areas (nonspecific trapping)

    Treatment:

    Primary FSGS:

    Corticosteroids (FIRST-LINE): - Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day (max 80 mg) or alternate day × 4-6 months - Taper over 6 months if response - Response: 40-50% (slower than MCD - may take 4-6 months)

    Second-Line: - Calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus) - for steroid-resistant - Mycophenolate mofetil - Rituximab (genetic forms)

    Secondary FSGS: - Treat underlying cause - ACE/ARB (mandatory - ↓ proteinuria, ↓ progression) - NO steroids (ineffective in secondary FSGS)

    Supportive: - ACE/ARB (target BP <130/80) - Diuretics - Statins

    Prognosis: - 50% → ESRD within 10 years (primary FSGS) - Recurrence after transplant: 30-50% (especially collapsing variant)

    3. Membranous Nephropathy (MN)¶

    Epidemiology: - Most common primary GN causing nephrotic syndrome in Caucasian adults (30-40%) - Peak age: 40-60 years

    Etiology:

    Primary (70-80%): - Anti-PLA2R antibodies (70-80% of primary MN) - Anti-THSD7A antibodies (2-5%)

    Secondary (20-30%): - Malignancy: Solid tumors (lung, colon, breast) - Infections: HBV, HCV, syphilis, malaria - Autoimmune: SLE, rheumatoid arthritis - Drugs: NSAIDs, penicillamine, gold

    Pathophysiology: - In situ immune complex formation: - Antibodies bind podocyte antigens (PLA2R) - Complement activation → membrane attack complex - Subepithelial deposits + GBM thickening

    Clinical: - Nephrotic syndrome (80%) - Asymptomatic proteinuria (20%) - Microscopic hematuria (30-40%) - Hypercoagulability (renal vein thrombosis in 5-60%)

    Diagnosis:

    Light Microscopy: - Diffuse GBM thickening - "Spike and dome" appearance (silver stain) - Stages: - Stage I: Subepithelial deposits - Stage II: Spike formation - Stage III: Deposits surrounded by GBM - Stage IV: Irregular, thickened GBM

    Electron Microscopy: Subepithelial deposits (diagnostic)

    Immunofluorescence: Granular IgG and C3 along capillary walls

    Serology: Anti-PLA2R antibodies (70-80% sensitive, 95% specific for primary MN)

    Treatment:

    Risk Stratification:

    Low-Risk (proteinuria <4 g/day, normal renal function): - Conservative therapy × 6 months: - ACE/ARB (target proteinuria <1 g/day) - BP control (<130/80) - Statin - Thromboprophylaxis if albumin <2.5 g/dL - 30% spontaneous remission

    High-Risk (proteinuria >8 g/day, ↑ Cr, persistent proteinuria >6 months):

    Immunosuppression:

    Option 1: Cyclical Alkylating Agent (FIRST-LINE): - Ponticelli regimen: - Month 1, 3, 5: Methylprednisolone IV → prednisone PO - Month 2, 4, 6: Cyclophosphamide or Chlorambucil - Remission: 60-80%

    Option 2: Calcineurin Inhibitors: - Tacrolimus or Cyclosporine × 6 months - Remission: 70-80% (but higher relapse rate after stopping)

    Option 3: Rituximab (emerging): - Anti-CD20 monoclonal antibody - Effective in anti-PLA2R+ MN - Fewer side effects than cyclophosphamide

    Secondary MN: - Treat underlying cause (malignancy, infection) - Supportive care

    Prognosis: - Natural history (untreated): - 30% spontaneous remission - 30% persistent proteinuria - 40% progressive renal failure - With treatment: 60-80% remission - Recurrence after transplant: 10-50%

    4. IgA Nephropathy (Berger Disease)¶

    Epidemiology: - Most common primary GN worldwide (30-40%) - Most common in Asians - Peak age: 20-30 years, M > F (2:1)

    Etiology: - Idiopathic (primary) - Secondary: HSP (Henoch-Schönlein purpura), cirrhosis, celiac disease

    Pathophysiology: - Galactose-deficient IgA1 (Gd-IgA1) - Antibodies against Gd-IgA1 → immune complexes - Mesangial deposition → inflammation → hematuria

    Clinical:

    Classic Presentation (40-50%): - Episodic gross hematuria (tea/cola-colored) - Concurrent with URTI ("synpharyngitic") - vs Post-infectious GN (1-3 weeks AFTER infection) - Loin pain

    Other Presentations: - Asymptomatic microscopic hematuria + proteinuria (30-40%) - Nephrotic syndrome (5%) - RPGN (5-10%)

    Physical Exam: - Usually normal - ± hypertension (in advanced disease)

    Diagnosis:

    Urinalysis: - RBCs, RBC casts - Variable proteinuria (usually <3 g/day)

    Labs: - Serum IgA elevated (50% - NOT diagnostic) - Normal complement (C3, C4) - key differentiator from post-infectious GN - ↑ Cr (if advanced)

    Biopsy (DIAGNOSTIC):

    Light Microscopy: - Mesangial hypercellularity - Mesangial matrix expansion - Crescents (if RPGN)

    Immunofluorescence: Mesangial IgA deposits (DIAGNOSTIC) - Also C3, ± IgG

    Electron Microscopy: Mesangial electron-dense deposits

    Oxford MEST-C Score (prognosis): - M: Mesangial hypercellularity - E: Endocapillary proliferation - S: Segmental sclerosis - T: Tubular atrophy/interstitial fibrosis - C: Crescents

    Treatment:

    All Patients (supportive): - ACE inhibitor/ARB (MAINSTAY) - Target BP <130/80 - Target proteinuria <1 g/day - Fish oil (omega-3 fatty acids) - controversial benefit

    Indications for Immunosuppression: - Proteinuria >1 g/day despite 3-6 months of ACE/ARB - Crescents on biopsy (RPGN)

    Immunosuppression:

    Corticosteroids: - Methylprednisolone 0.5-1 g IV × 3 days → prednisone PO × 6 months - If proteinuria >1 g/day and GFR >50

    RPGN (crescents): - High-dose steroids + Cyclophosphamide - ± Plasmapheresis (if severe)

    Do NOT Use: - Immunosuppression if GFR <30 (fibrosis predominates)

    Tonsillectomy: Considered in Japan (gross hematuria with tonsillitis), controversial

    Prognosis: - 15-40% → ESRD within 20 years

    Poor Prognostic Factors: - Persistent proteinuria >1 g/day - Hypertension - ↑ Cr at presentation - Severe histology (crescents, sclerosis, interstitial fibrosis)

    Recurrence after transplant: 50% (histologic), 10-15% (graft loss)

    5. Membranoproliferative GN (MPGN)¶

    Classification (updated):

    By Etiology: 1. Immune Complex-Mediated MPGN - HCV, HBV, SLE, cryoglobulinemia - Monoclonal gammopathies

    1. Complement-Mediated MPGN (C3 glomerulopathy)
    2. C3 GN: C3 only on IF
    3. Dense deposit disease (DDD): Intramembranous dense deposits on EM

    Etiology:

    Secondary Causes (most common): - HCV (most common in adults) - Chronic infections (HBV, endocarditis, shunt nephritis) - Autoimmune: SLE, Sjögren's - Monoclonal gammopathy: MGRS (monoclonal gammopathy of renal significance)

    Primary: - Complement dysregulation (factor H, I, B mutations)

    Pathophysiology:

    Immune Complex Type: - Subendothelial deposits → endothelial/mesangial proliferation

    C3 Glomerulopathy: - Alternative pathway dysregulation → C3 deposition - C3 nephritic factor (autoantibody that stabilizes C3 convertase)

    Clinical: - Nephrotic-nephritic syndrome (mixed features) - Proteinuria (often nephrotic-range) - Hematuria (micro or gross) - Hypertension, ↓ GFR

    Diagnosis:

    Labs: - Low C3 (characteristic) - Low C4 (if classical pathway activation - immune complex type) - C3 nephritic factor (C3 glomerulopathy) - HCV antibodies (screen all patients)

    Light Microscopy: - Mesangial proliferation - GBM thickening + splitting ("tram-track" appearance) - Lobular accentuation

    Immunofluorescence: - Immune complex type: IgG, IgM, C3 - C3 GN: C3 only (dominant) - DDD: C3 only

    Electron Microscopy: - Subendothelial deposits - Mesangial interposition (mesangial cells extend into capillary wall) - DDD: Ribbon-like, highly electron-dense deposits in GBM

    Treatment:

    Secondary MPGN: - Treat underlying cause: - HCV: Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) - Infections: Antibiotics - Monoclonal gammopathy: Treat underlying disorder

    C3 Glomerulopathy: - Mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) - Eculizumab (anti-C5 antibody) - for severe cases - Steroids (controversial)

    Supportive: - ACE/ARB - BP control

    Prognosis: - 50% → ESRD within 10 years - Recurrence after transplant: High (50-100% for DDD, 20-30% for C3 GN)

    6. Post-Infectious Glomerulonephritis (PIGN)¶

    Epidemiology: - Most common: Post-streptococcal GN (PSGN) - Children 5-12 years (after pharyngitis or impetigo) - Adults: Can occur with other infections (staph, endocarditis)

    Etiology:

    Post-Streptococcal: - Group A β-hemolytic Streptococcus - Nephritogenic strains (M types 12, 4, 1)

    Other Organisms: - Staphylococcus (endocarditis, shunt nephritis) - Gram-negatives (pneumococcal pneumonia) - Viruses (HBV, HCV)

    Pathophysiology: - Immune complex deposition (streptococcal antigens + antibodies) - In situ complex formation or circulating complexes - Complement activation → glomerular inflammation

    Clinical:

    Classic Presentation: - 1-3 weeks after pharyngitis (GAS) - 3-6 weeks after skin infection (impetigo)

    Features: - Acute nephritic syndrome: - Tea/cola-colored urine (gross hematuria) - Periorbital edema (worse in AM) - Hypertension - Oliguria - RBC casts (pathognomonic)

    Physical: - Hypertension (volume overload) - Edema (periorbital, pedal) - ± signs of URTI or impetigo

    Diagnosis:

    Urinalysis: - Hematuria (gross or microscopic) - RBC casts, dysmorphic RBCs - Proteinuria (<3 g/day usually) - WBC casts

    Labs: - ↓ C3, ↓ CH50 (90%) - normalizes in 6-8 weeks - If C3 low >8 weeks → consider MPGN or C3 glomerulopathy - Normal C4 (alternative pathway activation) - ↑ ASO titer (80% after pharyngitis, 20% after skin infection) - ↑ Anti-DNase B (better for skin infections) - ↑ Cr (mild azotemia)

    Biopsy (rarely needed - diagnosis usually clinical):

    Light Microscopy: - Diffuse proliferative GN - Neutrophil infiltration - "Starry sky" appearance

    Immunofluorescence: Granular C3 and IgG ("lumpy-bumpy")

    Electron Microscopy: Subepithelial "humps" (deposits)

    Treatment:

    Supportive (MAINSTAY): - Salt/fluid restriction - Diuretics (furosemide for volume overload) - Antihypertensives (if severe HTN)

    Antibiotics: - Penicillin (if ongoing infection or carrier state) - NOT for GN itself (already immune-mediated)

    Immunosuppression: NOT indicated (self-limited)

    Dialysis: If severe AKI, volume overload, hyperkalemia

    Prognosis:

    Children: - Excellent - 95% full recovery - Microscopic hematuria may persist 1-2 years - <1% → chronic renal failure

    Adults: - Worse - 50% incomplete recovery - 10-30% → chronic kidney disease - Elderly: Higher risk of RPGN

    C3 normalizes in 6-8 weeks - if not, consider alternative diagnosis

    7. Lupus Nephritis¶

    Epidemiology: - 50-60% of SLE patients develop lupus nephritis - More common in African Americans, Hispanics - Major cause of morbidity/mortality in SLE

    Classification (ISN/RPS 2003):

    Class Name Features Treatment
    I Minimal mesangial Normal LM, mesangial deposits on IF/EM Observation
    II Mesangial proliferative Mesangial hypercellularity Observation or mild immunosuppression
    III Focal proliferative (<50% glomeruli) Segmental/global proliferation Immunosuppression
    IV Diffuse proliferative (≥50%) Most severe, most common (40%) Aggressive immunosuppression
    V Membranous Membranous features Immunosuppression if nephrotic
    VI Advanced sclerosis (>90%) End-stage Supportive/RRT

    Pathophysiology: - Immune complex deposition (anti-dsDNA antibodies) - Complement activation - Inflammatory cell infiltration

    Clinical:

    Presentation Varies by Class: - Class I-II: Asymptomatic or mild proteinuria/hematuria - Class III-IV: Nephritic or mixed syndrome - Hematuria, RBC casts - Proteinuria (often nephrotic-range) - Hypertension - ↓ GFR - Class V: Nephrotic syndrome

    Diagnosis:

    Urinalysis: - Hematuria, RBC casts - Proteinuria (quantify with 24-hr urine or UPCR)

    Labs: - ↑ Anti-dsDNA (correlates with activity) - ↓ C3, ↓ C4 (low complement - active disease) - ↑ Cr - Positive ANA, anti-Smith

    Renal Biopsy (REQUIRED for diagnosis and classification):

    Light Microscopy: - Proliferative changes (Class III-IV) - "Wire-loop" lesions (subendothelial deposits) - "Full house" IF (IgG, IgA, IgM, C3, C1q)

    Immunofluorescence: "Full house" pattern - IgG, IgA, IgM, C1q, C3

    Electron Microscopy: Subendothelial, mesangial, subepithelial deposits

    Activity vs Chronicity: - Active lesions: Proliferation, necrosis, crescents, wire loops (reversible) - Chronic lesions: Sclerosis, fibrosis, tubular atrophy (irreversible)

    Treatment:

    Class I-II: Observation or hydroxychloroquine

    Class III-IV (PROLIFERATIVE):

    Induction (6 months):

    Option 1: Mycophenolate Mofetil (PREFERRED): - MMF 2-3 g/day + Prednisone - Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day → taper - Superior to cyclophosphamide (fewer side effects)

    Option 2: Cyclophosphamide: - Low-dose IV cyclophosphamide (Euro-Lupus protocol) - 500 mg IV q2weeks × 6 doses - OR High-dose NIH protocol (500-1000 mg/m² monthly × 6)

    Plus: - High-dose steroids: Methylprednisolone 1 g IV × 3 days (if severe) → prednisone taper

    Maintenance (≥3 years): - MMF or Azathioprine - Low-dose prednisone (taper to ≤5 mg/day)

    Refractory Cases: - Rituximab (anti-CD20) - Calcineurin inhibitors - Belimumab (anti-BLyS)

    Class V (MEMBRANOUS): - If nephrotic: MMF or Calcineurin inhibitor + steroids - If non-nephrotic: Observation + ACE/ARB

    Adjunctive: - ACE/ARB (all classes - ↓ proteinuria) - Hydroxychloroquine (all SLE patients) - BP control - Statins

    Monitoring: - Urine protein, Cr - Anti-dsDNA, C3, C4 - Repeat biopsy if worsening (to assess activity vs chronicity)

    Prognosis: - Class I-II: Excellent - Class III-IV: 80-90% achieve remission with treatment - 10-30% → ESRD within 15 years - Class V: Variable (depends on nephrotic syndrome severity) - Renal survival: 80-90% at 10 years (with modern therapy)

    8. ANCA-Associated Vasculitis (AAV)¶

    Includes: 1. Granulomatosis with polyangiitis (GPA) - formerly Wegener's 2. Microscopic polyangiitis (MPA) 3. Eosinophilic granulomatosis with polyangiitis (EGPA) - formerly Churg-Strauss

    Epidemiology: - Peak age: 50-70 years - Most common cause of RPGN in older adults

    Pathophysiology: - ANCA (anti-neutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies) activate neutrophils - Neutrophil degranulation → endothelial injury → necrotizing vasculitis - Pauci-immune (minimal immune deposits on IF)

    ANCA Types: - c-ANCA (cytoplasmic): Anti-PR3 → GPA (90%) - p-ANCA (perinuclear): Anti-MPO → MPA (70%), EGPA

    Clinical Features:

    GPA (Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis): - Upper respiratory: Sinusitis, epistaxis, saddle nose deformity - Lower respiratory: Nodules, cavitations, hemoptysis - Renal: Rapidly progressive GN (80%)

    MPA (Microscopic Polyangiitis): - Renal: RPGN (90%) - Pulmonary: Pulmonary capillaritis (50%) - NO granulomas (vs GPA) - NO upper airway involvement (vs GPA)

    EGPA (Eosinophilic Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis): - Asthma (100%) - Eosinophilia (>10%) - Mononeuritis multiplex - ANCA+ in only 40%

    Renal Manifestations: - RPGN (most common) - Rapidly rising Cr - Active urinary sediment (RBCs, RBC casts) - Variable proteinuria

    Diagnosis:

    Labs: - ANCA: c-ANCA (PR3) or p-ANCA (MPO) - ↑ Cr (rapidly rising) - Normal complement (C3, C4) - key feature - Urinalysis: Hematuria, RBC casts, proteinuria

    Renal Biopsy:

    Light Microscopy: - Focal necrotizing GN - Crescent formation (>50% glomeruli) - NO immune deposits

    Immunofluorescence: Negative or pauci-immune (minimal C3, no Ig)

    Imaging (GPA): - Chest CT: Nodules, cavitations - Sinus CT: Mucosal thickening, bone destruction

    Treatment:

    Induction (severe disease with renal involvement):

    Corticosteroids + Cyclophosphamide or Rituximab:

    Option 1: Rituximab (PREFERRED): - 375 mg/m² IV weekly × 4 weeks OR - 1000 mg IV on days 1 and 15 - Non-inferior to cyclophosphamide - Preferred if: Young (fertility concerns), relapsing disease

    Option 2: Cyclophosphamide: - IV: 15 mg/kg (max 1.2 g) q2-4 weeks × 3-6 months - OR PO: 2 mg/kg/day × 3-6 months - Adjust for age, renal function

    Plus: - Methylprednisolone 1 g IV × 3 days (if severe) → Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day → taper

    Plasmapheresis: - Add if: Dialysis-dependent, pulmonary hemorrhage - 7 exchanges over 14 days - PLEX trial: No benefit in non-dialysis AAV

    Maintenance (18-24 months): - Rituximab (500 mg q6months) OR - Azathioprine (2 mg/kg/day) OR - MMF (if cannot tolerate AZA) - Low-dose prednisone (taper to ≤5 mg/day)

    Adjunctive: - PJP prophylaxis (TMP-SMX) during induction - Bone protection (calcium, vitamin D, bisphosphonate)

    Monitoring: - Serial ANCA titers (rising titer → risk of relapse) - Cr, urinalysis - CBC (cyclophosphamide toxicity)

    Prognosis: - Remission: 80-90% with treatment - Relapse: 50% within 5 years - Renal survival: 75-85% at 5 years - Without treatment: RPGN → ESRD in weeks-months

    9. Anti-GBM Disease (Goodpasture Syndrome)¶

    Definition: - Anti-GBM disease: Kidney involvement only - Goodpasture syndrome: Kidney + pulmonary hemorrhage

    Epidemiology: - Rare (1 per million) - Bimodal: 20-30 years and 60-70 years - M > F (if pulmonary involvement)

    Pathophysiology: - Autoantibodies against α3 chain of type IV collagen - Located in GBM and alveolar basement membrane - Linear IgG deposition along GBM - Complement activation → RPGN

    Triggers: - Smoking, infections, hydrocarbon exposure

    Clinical:

    Renal: - RPGN (rapidly rising Cr, oliguria) - Hematuria, RBC casts - Variable proteinuria

    Pulmonary (60-80%): - Hemoptysis (may precede renal disease) - Dyspnea, cough - Diffuse alveolar hemorrhage - CXR: Bilateral infiltrates

    Physical: - Pallor (anemia from bleeding) - Hemoptysis - Hypertension, edema (if renal failure)

    Diagnosis:

    Labs: - Anti-GBM antibodies (serum) - DIAGNOSTIC - Positive in >90% - ↑ Cr (rapidly rising) - Anemia (blood loss, hemolysis) - Normal complement (C3, C4) - Double-positive: 30% also have ANCA+ (worse prognosis)

    Urinalysis: - Hematuria, RBC casts - Proteinuria

    Imaging: - CXR/CT: Diffuse bilateral infiltrates (alveolar hemorrhage)

    Renal Biopsy:

    Light Microscopy: - Crescentic GN (>50% glomeruli) - Focal necrosis

    Immunofluorescence: Linear IgG along GBM (DIAGNOSTIC) - C3 also present - Linear pattern (vs granular in immune complex GN)

    Electron Microscopy: No electron-dense deposits

    Pulmonary Function: - ↑ DLCO (diffusion capacity) - RBCs in alveoli bind CO

    Treatment:

    Urgent Therapy (medical emergency):

    Triple Therapy:

    1. Plasmapheresis (FIRST-LINE):
    2. Daily for 14 days or until anti-GBM undetectable
    3. 4L exchanges (60 mL/kg)

    4. Removes circulating antibodies

    5. Cyclophosphamide:

    6. 2 mg/kg/day PO × 3 months

    7. Corticosteroids:

    8. Methylprednisolone 1 g IV × 3 days → Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day

    Start Immediately (do NOT wait for biopsy): - Delay → irreversible renal failure

    Maintenance: - Prednisone taper - Cyclophosphamide × 3 months → transition to azathioprine - Monitor anti-GBM antibodies (should become undetectable)

    Prognosis:

    Renal Recovery Depends on: - Cr at presentation: - Cr <5 mg/dL → 90% renal recovery - Cr 5-7 mg/dL → 80% require dialysis - Cr >7 mg/dL or dialysis-dependent → <10% recovery - % Crescents on biopsy (>50% → poor) - Oliguria (poor prognostic sign)

    Pulmonary: Usually responds well to treatment

    Renal Survival: 50% require permanent dialysis

    Relapse: Rare (<5%)

    Transplant: Safe after antibodies undetectable × 6-12 months

    10. Diabetic Nephropathy¶

    Epidemiology: - Most common cause of nephrotic syndrome and ESRD in developed countries - 40% of Type 1 DM, 30% of Type 2 DM develop diabetic nephropathy - Usually after 10-15 years of diabetes

    Risk Factors: - Poor glycemic control - Hypertension - Family history - Smoking - Hyperlipidemia

    Pathophysiology:

    Early Changes: - Glomerular hyperfiltration (↑ GFR initially) - Hypertrophy, ↑ glomerular pressure

    Progression: 1. Hyperglycemia → AGE (advanced glycation end-products) 2. Hemodynamic injury → RAAS activation, ↑ intraglomerular pressure 3. Mesangial expansion, GBM thickening 4. Podocyte loss → proteinuria 5. Nodular glomerulosclerosis (Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules) 6. Tubulointerstitial fibrosis → ↓ GFR

    Clinical Stages (Mogensen):

    Stage Name GFR Albuminuria Reversible?
    1 Hyperfiltration ↑ (>120) None Yes
    2 Silent Normal-high None Yes
    3 Microalbuminuria Normal-↓ 30-300 mg/day Partially
    4 Macroalbuminuria ↓ >300 mg/day No
    5 ESRD <15 Variable No (RRT)

    Clinical Features:

    Early (Stage 1-3): - Asymptomatic - Microalbuminuria (30-300 mg/day) - first clinical sign - Normal or ↑ GFR

    Advanced (Stage 4-5): - Nephrotic syndrome (proteinuria >3.5 g/day) - NO hematuria (if present, consider other GN) - Hypertension - ↓ GFR → uremia

    Associated Features: - Diabetic retinopathy (if absent, question diabetic nephropathy) - Neuropathy, cardiovascular disease

    Diagnosis:

    Screening (ALL diabetic patients): - Annual UACR (urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio) - Normal: <30 mg/g - Microalbuminuria: 30-300 mg/g - Macroalbuminuria: >300 mg/g - Serum Cr and eGFR

    Biopsy (NOT routinely needed):

    Indications for Biopsy: - Atypical features: - Active urinary sediment (RBCs, casts) → suggests other GN - Rapid ↓ GFR - Nephrotic syndrome without retinopathy - Short duration of diabetes (<5 years)

    Pathology:

    Light Microscopy: - Mesangial expansion - GBM thickening (>400 nm) - Nodular glomerulosclerosis (Kimmelstiel-Wilson nodules) - PATHOGNOMONIC - Arteriolar hyalinosis

    Immunofluorescence: Linear IgG and albumin along GBM (nonspecific)

    Electron Microscopy: GBM thickening, podocyte foot process effacement

    Treatment:

    Goals: - Slow progression to ESRD - ↓ Proteinuria - ↓ Cardiovascular risk

    1. Glycemic Control:

    Target: - HbA1c <7% (individualize based on patient)

    SGLT2 Inhibitors (FIRST-LINE for diabetic nephropathy): - Canagliflozin, Dapagliflozin, Empagliflozin - Benefits: - ↓ Proteinuria - ↓ CKD progression (40% risk reduction) - ↓ Cardiovascular events - ↓ HF hospitalizations - Start if: eGFR >20-25

    GLP-1 Agonists: - Liraglutide, semaglutide - ↓ Cardiovascular events, ↓ albuminuria

    2. Blood Pressure Control:

    Target: <130/80 mmHg

    ACE Inhibitor or ARB (MAINSTAY): - ALL diabetic patients with microalbuminuria or macroalbuminuria - Renoprotective (beyond BP lowering): - ↓ Intraglomerular pressure - ↓ Proteinuria (30-40%) - Slows progression to ESRD

    Dosing: - Maximize dose (even if normotensive) - Acceptable for Cr to ↑ up to 30% after starting (hemodynamic effect) - If ↑ >30% → consider renal artery stenosis

    Monitor: - Hyperkalemia (K+ >5.5 → consider stopping) - Cr

    Do NOT Combine: - ACE + ARB → AVOID (↑ hyperkalemia, AKI, no added benefit)

    3. Proteinuria Reduction:

    Non-Steroidal MRA: - Finerenone (selective mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist) - ↓ Proteinuria, ↓ CKD progression - Add to ACE/ARB + SGLT2i

    4. Lipid Management: - Statin (all diabetic patients with CKD) - Target LDL <70 mg/dL

    5. Lifestyle: - Dietary protein restriction (0.8 g/kg/day) - may slow progression - Smoking cessation - Sodium restriction (<2 g/day)

    6. Renal Replacement Therapy:

    Indications (same as other ESRD): - GFR <15, uremia, volume overload, hyperkalemia

    Transplant: - Combined pancreas-kidney transplant (best for Type 1 DM) - Kidney alone (Type 2 DM)

    Prognosis:

    Natural History (without treatment): - Microalbuminuria → macroalbuminuria (5-10 years) - Macroalbuminuria → ESRD (5-10 years) - 20-40% → ESRD within 20 years

    With Modern Therapy (ACE/ARB + SGLT2i + glycemic control): - 50% reduction in progression to ESRD - Slower GFR decline (1-2 mL/min/year vs 10-12 mL/min/year)

    Cardiovascular Disease: - Leading cause of death in diabetic nephropathy - CVD risk > ESRD risk

    Diagnostic Approach to Glomerular Disease¶

    Clinical Syndromes¶

    1. Nephrotic Syndrome: - Proteinuria ≥3.5 g/day - Hypoalbuminemia, edema, hyperlipidemia

    Differential: - Adults: Membranous nephropathy, FSGS, diabetic nephropathy - Children: Minimal change disease

    2. Nephritic Syndrome: - Hematuria, RBC casts - Hypertension, edema, oliguria - Proteinuria (<3 g/day)

    Differential: IgA nephropathy, post-infectious GN, lupus nephritis

    3. RPGN: - Acute nephritis with rapid ↓ GFR - Crescents on biopsy

    Differential (by IF pattern): - Linear: Anti-GBM disease - Granular: Lupus, IgA, post-infectious GN - Pauci-immune: ANCA-associated vasculitis

    4. Asymptomatic Urinary Abnormalities: - Isolated hematuria or proteinuria - No edema, normal BP, normal GFR

    Differential: IgA nephropathy, thin basement membrane disease, Alport syndrome

    Initial Workup¶

    All Patients: 1. Urinalysis with microscopy - RBCs, RBC casts, dysmorphic RBCs - Proteinuria - WBCs, casts

    1. Quantify proteinuria:
    2. UPCR (spot urine protein-to-creatinine ratio)

    3. 24-hour urine protein

    4. Renal function:

    5. Serum Cr, eGFR

    6. BUN

    7. Serum albumin

    8. Lipid panel

    Serologic Testing (based on clinical syndrome):

    Nephrotic Syndrome: - Serum/urine protein electrophoresis (paraprotein) - Anti-PLA2R (membranous nephropathy) - HBV, HCV, HIV serologies - Fasting glucose, HbA1c - ANA (SLE)

    Nephritic Syndrome / RPGN: - Complement (C3, C4) - Low C3 + normal C4 → IgA, MPGN, post-infectious GN - Low C3 + low C4 → SLE, cryoglobulinemia - Normal → ANCA, anti-GBM - ANCA (c-ANCA/PR3, p-ANCA/MPO) - Anti-GBM antibodies - ANA, anti-dsDNA (SLE) - ASO, anti-DNase B (post-strep GN) - Cryoglobulins

    Hematuria: - Urine culture (exclude UTI) - Urine cytology (if >40 years, smoking - exclude malignancy)

    Renal Biopsy Indications¶

    Definite Indications: - RPGN (urgent) - Nephrotic syndrome in adults - Persistent proteinuria (>1 g/day) with ↓ GFR - Suspected lupus nephritis - AKI of unknown etiology (with proteinuria/hematuria)

    Consider: - Nephrotic syndrome in children >12 years or atypical features - Isolated proteinuria >1 g/day - Isolated hematuria with ↓ GFR or proteinuria

    Contraindications (relative): - Bleeding diathesis (INR >1.5, PLT <50K) - Uncontrolled hypertension (BP >160/100) - Single kidney - Small kidneys (<9 cm) - chronic disease

    Management Principles¶

    General Measures (All Glomerular Diseases)¶

    1. Blood Pressure Control: - Target <130/80 mmHg (all CKD) - Target <125/75 mmHg (if proteinuria >1 g/day)

    2. Proteinuria Reduction: - ACE inhibitor or ARB (FIRST-LINE) - ↓ Proteinuria by 30-50% - Renoprotective independent of BP effect - Maximize dose - Sodium restriction (<2 g/day)

    3. Edema Management: - Sodium restriction (<2 g/day) - Loop diuretics (furosemide) - Albumin infusion (if severe hypoalbuminemia + diuretic resistance)

    4. Hyperlipidemia: - Statin (all nephrotic patients) - Target LDL <100 mg/dL

    5. Hypercoagulability (nephrotic syndrome): - Anticoagulation if: - Albumin <2-2.5 g/dL - Prior VTE - High-risk (membranous nephropathy, immobility) - Options: Warfarin, DOAC

    6. Infections: - Pneumococcal vaccine (nephrotic patients - loss of IgG) - Prompt treatment of infections

    7. Dietary Protein: - 0.8 g/kg/day (normal intake) - Consider restriction (0.6-0.8 g/kg) if proteinuria refractory

    8. Avoid Nephrotoxins: - NSAIDs (worsen proteinuria, ↓ GFR) - Contrast (if possible)

    Immunosuppression Principles¶

    Corticosteroids: - High-dose: Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day (max 60-80 mg) - Pulse IV: Methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg × 3 days (severe RPGN) - Taper: Slow taper over 6-12 months to ≤5 mg/day

    Side Effects: Hyperglycemia, weight gain, osteoporosis, infections, cataracts, avascular necrosis

    Prophylaxis: PJP (if prednisone >20 mg/day × >1 month), bone protection (calcium, vitamin D, bisphosphonate)

    Cyclophosphamide: - Indications: Severe lupus nephritis, ANCA vasculitis - Dose: IV 500-1000 mg/m² monthly OR PO 2 mg/kg/day - Side Effects: Bone marrow suppression, hemorrhagic cystitis, infertility, malignancy - Monitoring: CBC (nadir WBC >3000), urinalysis - Fertility preservation: Sperm banking, ovarian suppression (leuprolide)

    Calcineurin Inhibitors (Cyclosporine, Tacrolimus): - Indications: MCD, FSGS (steroid-resistant), membranous nephropathy - Mechanism: ↓ T-cell activation - Side Effects: Nephrotoxicity (dose-dependent, reversible), hypertension, hyperkalemia, neurotoxicity - Monitoring: Trough levels

    Mycophenolate Mofetil (MMF): - Indications: Lupus nephritis (FIRST-LINE), maintenance AAV - Dose: 2-3 g/day (divided BID) - Side Effects: GI intolerance, bone marrow suppression - Advantage: Fewer side effects than cyclophosphamide

    Rituximab: - Indications: ANCA vasculitis, refractory MCD/FSGS, membranous nephropathy (anti-PLA2R+) - Mechanism: Anti-CD20 → B-cell depletion - Dose: 375 mg/m² weekly × 4 OR 1000 mg days 1 and 15 - Side Effects: Infusion reactions, infections, ↓ IgG

    Azathioprine: - Indications: Maintenance therapy (after cyclophosphamide) - Dose: 2 mg/kg/day - Side Effects: Bone marrow suppression, hepatotoxicity - Monitor: CBC, LFTs

    Complications of Glomerular Diseases¶

    Acute Kidney Injury¶

    Causes: - RPGN (crescentic GN) - Volume depletion (excessive diuresis in nephrotic syndrome) - Renal vein thrombosis (hypercoagulable state) - Acute tubular necrosis (ischemic or nephrotoxic)

    Management: Identify and treat underlying cause, may require dialysis

    Chronic Kidney Disease / ESRD¶

    Risk Factors for Progression: - Persistent proteinuria (>1 g/day) - Uncontrolled hypertension - African American race - Severe histology (crescents, sclerosis, interstitial fibrosis)

    Management: CKD management, prepare for RRT

    Infections¶

    Nephrotic Syndrome: - Loss of immunoglobulins (IgG in urine) - Encapsulated organisms: S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae - Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis (esp. children)

    Prevention: - Pneumococcal vaccine (PPSV23 + PCV13) - Prompt treatment

    Immunosuppression: - Opportunistic infections: PJP, CMV, fungal - PJP prophylaxis: TMP-SMX (if prednisone >20 mg/day × >1 month)

    Thromboembolic Complications¶

    Nephrotic Syndrome → Hypercoagulable State:

    Mechanisms: - Loss of anticoagulant factors (antithrombin III, protein C, protein S) - ↑ Procoagulant factors (fibrinogen, factor V, VIII) - Platelet hyperaggregability - Hyperlipidemia, hemoconcentration

    Risk: - Renal vein thrombosis (10-40% in membranous nephropathy) - DVT, PE - Arterial thrombosis (rare)

    Clinical: - Renal vein thrombosis: Flank pain, hematuria, ↑ proteinuria, ↑ Cr, LDH - PE: Dyspnea, chest pain, hypoxia

    Diagnosis: - Renal vein thrombosis: MRI or CT venography (Doppler US less sensitive) - PE: CTPA

    Treatment: - Anticoagulation (warfarin or DOAC) - Continue until nephrotic syndrome resolves

    Prophylaxis: - Consider if albumin <2-2.5 g/dL - Definite if prior VTE or high-risk (membranous nephropathy)

    Hyperlipidemia¶

    Mechanism: - ↑ Hepatic synthesis (response to hypoalbuminemia) - ↓ Lipoprotein lipase activity

    Lab: - ↑ Total cholesterol, ↑ LDL, ↑ VLDL, ↑ triglycerides

    Treatment: - Statin (all nephrotic patients) - Target LDL <100 mg/dL

    High-Yield Points¶

    Key Differentiators¶

    Nephrotic vs Nephritic: | Feature | Nephrotic | Nephritic | |---------|-----------|-----------| | Proteinuria | ≥3.5 g/day | <3.5 g/day | | Hematuria | Absent or mild | Prominent | | RBC casts | Absent | Present | | Edema | Severe (anasarca) | Mild-moderate | | Hypertension | Variable | Common | | Hypoalbuminemia | <3 g/dL | Normal/mild | | Hyperlipidemia | Present | Absent |

    Primary Nephrotic Syndromes (by age): - Children: Minimal change disease (80%) - Adults: Membranous nephropathy (30-40%), FSGS (35%) - Overall (all ages): Diabetic nephropathy (most common)

    RPGN (by IF pattern): - Linear IF: Anti-GBM disease (Goodpasture) - Granular IF: Immune complex (lupus, IgA, post-infectious GN) - Pauci-immune (negative IF): ANCA vasculitis

    Low Complement: - Low C3, normal C4: IgA nephropathy, MPGN, post-infectious GN - Low C3, low C4: SLE, cryoglobulinemia - Normal C3, C4: ANCA vasculitis, anti-GBM disease

    Treatment Pearls¶

    MCD: - Steroids FIRST-LINE (90% response in children) - Biopsy NOT needed in children 1-10 years with typical presentation - DO biopsy if: Age <1 or >10, steroid-resistant, atypical features

    FSGS: - Distinguish primary vs secondary - Primary: Steroids ± calcineurin inhibitors - Secondary: Treat underlying cause, ACE/ARB, NO steroids

    Membranous Nephropathy: - Risk-stratify (proteinuria, renal function) - Low-risk: Conservative (ACE/ARB) × 6 months - 30% spontaneous remission - High-risk: Immunosuppression (cyclophosphamide/steroids or rituximab) - Anti-PLA2R+: Primary MN (70-80%)

    IgA Nephropathy: - Most common primary GN worldwide - Synpharyngitic hematuria (concurrent with URTI) - classic - Normal complement (vs post-infectious GN with low C3) - Treatment: ACE/ARB (mainstay), steroids if persistent proteinuria >1 g/day

    Post-Streptococcal GN: - 1-3 weeks after pharyngitis (vs IgA - concurrent) - Low C3 (normalizes in 6-8 weeks) - Treatment: Supportive only (self-limited), antibiotics if ongoing infection - Prognosis: Excellent in children (95% full recovery)

    Lupus Nephritis: - Class III-IV: Aggressive immunosuppression (MMF or cyclophosphamide + steroids) - "Full house" IF: IgG, IgA, IgM, C1q, C3 - Monitor: Anti-dsDNA, C3, C4 (correlate with activity)

    ANCA Vasculitis: - Induction: Rituximab or cyclophosphamide + steroids - Plasmapheresis: If dialysis-dependent or pulmonary hemorrhage - PJP prophylaxis: TMP-SMX during induction

    Anti-GBM Disease: - Medical emergency - treat immediately - Triple therapy: Plasmapheresis + cyclophosphamide + steroids - Linear IgG on IF (diagnostic) - Prognosis: If Cr >7 or dialysis-dependent → <10% renal recovery

    Prognostic Factors (Poor Prognosis)¶

    All GN: - Persistent proteinuria (>1 g/day) - ↑ Cr at presentation - Hypertension - Severe histology (crescents, sclerosis, interstitial fibrosis, tubular atrophy)

    Specific Diseases: - FSGS: Collapsing variant - IgA: Crescents, proteinuria >1 g/day - Membranous: Proteinuria >8 g/day - RPGN: Oliguria, >50% crescents

    Common Exam Scenarios¶

    Scenario 1: Child with sudden periorbital edema, 4+ proteinuria, NO hematuria, NO hypertension → Minimal Change Disease → Start steroids (no biopsy needed if age 1-10 years)

    Scenario 2: Adult with nephrotic syndrome, anti-PLA2R+ → Primary Membranous Nephropathy → Risk-stratify (proteinuria <4 g → conservative; >8 g → immunosuppression)

    Scenario 3: Young adult with episodic gross hematuria concurrent with URTI → IgA Nephropathy → ACE/ARB, steroids if proteinuria >1 g/day

    Scenario 4: Child with tea-colored urine 2 weeks after sore throat, low C3, ASO+ → Post-Streptococcal GN → Supportive care (self-limited), C3 normalizes in 6-8 weeks

    Scenario 5: Adult with rapidly rising Cr, hemoptysis, c-ANCA+, normal complement → Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA) → Urgent: Rituximab/cyclophosphamide + steroids ± plasmapheresis

    Scenario 6: Adult with rapidly rising Cr, hemoptysis, anti-GBM+, linear IgG on biopsy → Goodpasture Syndrome → URGENT: Plasmapheresis + cyclophosphamide + steroids

    Scenario 7: SLE patient with nephrotic-range proteinuria, low C3, low C4, anti-dsDNA+ → Lupus Nephritis → Biopsy (determine class) → Class III-IV: MMF/cyclophosphamide + steroids

    Scenario 8: African American with nephrotic syndrome, hypertension, microscopic hematuria, renal insufficiency → FSGS → Biopsy (confirm primary vs secondary) → Primary: Steroids × 4-6 months

    Scenario 9: HCV+ patient with nephrotic-nephritic syndrome, low C4, cryoglobulinemia → MPGN (Immune Complex Type) → Treat HCV with DAAs

    Scenario 10: Long-standing diabetes, proteinuria, diabetic retinopathy, NO hematuria → Diabetic Nephropathy → SGLT2 inhibitor + ACE/ARB + glycemic control (no biopsy needed)

    Practice MCQs¶

    Question 1¶

    A 25-year-old man presents with recurrent episodes of tea-colored urine occurring concurrently with upper respiratory infections. Urinalysis shows hematuria with RBC casts and 1+ proteinuria. Serum creatinine is 1.2 mg/dL. Complement levels (C3, C4) are normal. What is the most likely diagnosis?

    A. Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis B. IgA nephropathy C. Membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis D. Lupus nephritis E. Henoch-Schönlein purpura

    Answer: B. IgA nephropathy

    Explanation: - IgA nephropathy classically presents with synpharyngitic hematuria (gross hematuria occurring concurrently with URTI), distinguishing it from post-streptococcal GN which occurs 1-3 weeks AFTER infection - Normal complement levels help differentiate from post-infectious GN (low C3) and lupus (low C3, C4) - IgA nephropathy is the most common primary glomerulonephritis worldwide - Definitive diagnosis requires renal biopsy showing mesangial IgA deposits on immunofluorescence

    Why others are incorrect: - A: Post-streptococcal GN occurs 1-3 weeks after infection (not concurrent) and has low C3 - C: MPGN typically has low C3 - D: Lupus nephritis would have low C3/C4, positive ANA, other systemic features - E: HSP would have palpable purpura, abdominal pain, arthritis

    Question 2¶

    A 6-year-old boy presents with sudden onset of periorbital edema and frothy urine. Urinalysis shows 4+ proteinuria (protein 8 g/day) with NO hematuria. Blood pressure is 110/70 mmHg. Serum albumin is 2.1 g/dL. What is the most appropriate next step?

    A. Renal biopsy B. Start ACE inhibitor C. Start corticosteroids D. Check anti-PLA2R antibodies E. Measure complement levels

    Answer: C. Start corticosteroids

    Explanation: - This is minimal change disease (MCD) - most common cause of nephrotic syndrome in children (80%) - Classic presentation: Sudden nephrotic syndrome, NO hematuria, NO hypertension - Empiric steroid therapy (prednisone 60 mg/m²/day) is appropriate for children aged 1-10 years with typical presentation - biopsy NOT needed initially - Biopsy indications in children: Age <1 or >10 years, steroid resistance (no response after 8 weeks), atypical features (hematuria, hypertension, renal insufficiency) - Expected response: 90% achieve remission within 2-4 weeks

    Why others are incorrect: - A: Biopsy NOT needed in children 1-10 years with typical MCD presentation (only if atypical or steroid-resistant) - B: ACE inhibitor is adjunctive but not first-line treatment for MCD - D: Anti-PLA2R is for membranous nephropathy (rare in children) - E: Complement levels are normal in MCD (would be useful if suspecting post-infectious GN or lupus)

    Question 3¶

    A 55-year-old man with nephrotic syndrome (proteinuria 9 g/day) undergoes renal biopsy showing diffuse GBM thickening with "spike and dome" appearance on silver stain. Immunofluorescence shows granular IgG and C3 along capillary walls. Anti-PLA2R antibody is positive. What is the most appropriate initial management?

    A. Start corticosteroids immediately B. Conservative therapy with ACE inhibitor for 6 months C. Cyclophosphamide and corticosteroids (Ponticelli regimen) D. Rituximab E. Calcineurin inhibitor (tacrolimus)

    Answer: C. Cyclophosphamide and corticosteroids (Ponticelli regimen)

    Explanation: - Diagnosis: Primary membranous nephropathy (anti-PLA2R+, characteristic biopsy findings) - Risk stratification is critical: - High-risk: Proteinuria >8 g/day (this patient has 9 g/day) → warrants immunosuppression - Low-risk: Proteinuria <4 g/day, normal renal function → conservative therapy × 6 months (ACE/ARB) - First-line immunosuppression: Cyclical alkylating agent regimen (Ponticelli protocol): - Alternating months of steroids and cyclophosphamide × 6 months - Remission rate: 60-80% - Rituximab is emerging as alternative (especially for anti-PLA2R+ MN) with fewer side effects

    Why others are incorrect: - A: Steroids alone are not effective for membranous nephropathy - B: Conservative therapy appropriate for LOW-risk only (proteinuria <4 g/day); this patient is high-risk - D: Rituximab is effective but currently second-line (though increasingly used first-line in some centers) - E: Calcineurin inhibitors have higher relapse rates after discontinuation compared to cyclophosphamide

    Question 4¶

    A 28-year-old woman presents with hemoptysis and rapidly rising creatinine (now 6.5 mg/dL). Urinalysis shows RBC casts. Anti-GBM antibody is positive. Renal biopsy shows crescents in 70% of glomeruli with linear IgG on immunofluorescence. What is the most appropriate immediate management?

    A. High-dose corticosteroids alone B. Cyclophosphamide alone C. Plasmapheresis + cyclophosphamide + corticosteroids D. Rituximab + corticosteroids E. Supportive care only (self-limited disease)

    Answer: C. Plasmapheresis + cyclophosphamide + corticosteroids

    Explanation: - Diagnosis: Anti-GBM disease (Goodpasture syndrome) - kidney + lung involvement - This is a medical emergency requiring immediate triple therapy: 1. Plasmapheresis (daily × 14 days) - removes circulating anti-GBM antibodies 2. Cyclophosphamide (2 mg/kg/day) - prevents new antibody production 3. Corticosteroids (methylprednisolone 1g IV × 3 days → prednisone 1 mg/kg/day) - reduces inflammation - Do NOT wait for biopsy to start treatment - delay leads to irreversible renal failure - Prognosis: Depends on creatinine at presentation - Cr <5 mg/dL → 90% renal recovery - Cr >7 mg/dL or dialysis-dependent → <10% recovery (this patient at high risk) - Pulmonary involvement usually responds well

    Why others are incorrect: - A/B/D: Monotherapy or dual therapy insufficient for anti-GBM disease - requires all three modalities - E: This is NOT self-limited (unlike post-infectious GN) - without treatment, progresses to ESRD in weeks

    Question 5¶

    A 60-year-old man with chronic sinusitis presents with rapidly rising creatinine and hemoptysis. C-ANCA (anti-PR3) is positive. Complement levels (C3, C4) are normal. Renal biopsy shows necrotizing glomerulonephritis with crescents and negative immunofluorescence. Chest CT shows multiple pulmonary nodules with cavitations. What is the diagnosis?

    A. Goodpasture syndrome B. Granulomatosis with polyangiitis (GPA) C. Microscopic polyangiitis (MPA) D. IgA nephropathy E. Lupus nephritis

    Answer: B. Granulomatosis with polyangiitis (GPA)

    Explanation: - GPA (formerly Wegener's granulomatosis) classically involves: - Upper airway: Chronic sinusitis, epistaxis, saddle nose deformity - Lower respiratory: Pulmonary nodules with cavitations - Renal: Rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis (RPGN) - c-ANCA (anti-PR3) is positive in 90% of GPA (vs p-ANCA/anti-MPO in MPA) - Pauci-immune GN (negative IF) - distinguishes from anti-GBM (linear IF) and immune complex GN (granular IF) - Normal complement - helps differentiate from lupus (low C3/C4) - Treatment: Induction with rituximab/cyclophosphamide + steroids ± plasmapheresis (if severe)

    Why others are incorrect: - A: Goodpasture would have positive anti-GBM antibody and linear IgG on IF - C: MPA lacks upper airway involvement and granulomas on pathology; typically p-ANCA+ (though some overlap exists) - D: IgA nephropathy would have mesangial IgA deposits on IF, not pauci-immune pattern - E: Lupus would have low complement, positive ANA/anti-dsDNA, granular IF with "full house" pattern

    Question 6¶

    A 35-year-old woman with SLE presents with worsening proteinuria (5 g/day) and rising creatinine. Complement levels show low C3 and C4. Anti-dsDNA is elevated. Renal biopsy shows diffuse proliferative glomerulonephritis affecting 65% of glomeruli with "wire loop" lesions. Immunofluorescence shows IgG, IgA, IgM, C1q, and C3 ("full house"). What is the lupus nephritis class and appropriate treatment?

    A. Class II; observation B. Class III; mycophenolate mofetil + corticosteroids C. Class IV; mycophenolate mofetil + corticosteroids D. Class V; conservative management with ACE inhibitor E. Class VI; prepare for dialysis

    Answer: C. Class IV; mycophenolate mofetil + corticosteroids

    Explanation: - Class IV lupus nephritis (diffuse proliferative) - affects ≥50% of glomeruli (this patient has 65%) - Most severe and most common class requiring aggressive immunosuppression (40% of lupus nephritis) - "Full house" IF (IgG, IgA, IgM, C1q, C3) is characteristic of lupus nephritis - "Wire loop" lesions = subendothelial deposits (pathognomonic for lupus) - First-line treatment for Class III-IV: - Induction (6 months): Mycophenolate mofetil (2-3 g/day) + high-dose corticosteroids - MMF preferred over cyclophosphamide (equal efficacy, fewer side effects, better for fertility preservation) - Maintenance (≥3 years): MMF or azathioprine + low-dose prednisone

    Why others are incorrect: - A: Class II (mesangial proliferative) is mild, requires only observation/mild immunosuppression - B: Class III affects <50% of glomeruli (focal); this is diffuse (≥50%) = Class IV - D: Class V (membranous) presents with pure nephrotic syndrome without proliferative features - E: Class VI (advanced sclerosis) has >90% sclerosed glomeruli; this biopsy shows active proliferative disease

    A. Corticosteroids for 4-6 months B. Conservative therapy with ACE inhibitor only C. Cyclophosphamide D. Rituximab E. Supportive care (self-limited)

    Answer: A. Corticosteroids for 4-6 months

    Explanation: - FSGS is most common primary GN causing nephrotic syndrome in adults (35%) and most common primary GN causing ESRD in African Americans - This presentation suggests primary FSGS: Nephrotic syndrome + hypertension + hematuria + renal insufficiency - Distinguish primary vs secondary FSGS: - Primary: Nephrotic-range proteinuria, treat with immunosuppression - Secondary (obesity, reduced nephron mass, drugs): Sub-nephrotic proteinuria, treat underlying cause + ACE/ARB, NO steroids - First-line for primary FSGS: Corticosteroids - Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day (max 80 mg) or alternate day × 4-6 months - Response rate: 40-50% (slower than MCD - may take 4-6 months) - Taper over 6 months if response - Second-line (steroid-resistant): Calcineurin inhibitors (cyclosporine, tacrolimus)

    Why others are incorrect: - B: Conservative therapy alone is for SECONDARY FSGS (e.g., obesity-related); this appears to be primary FSGS - C: Cyclophosphamide not first-line for FSGS - D: Rituximab used for genetic forms or refractory cases, not first-line - E: FSGS is NOT self-limited; 50% progress to ESRD within 10 years without treatment

    A 12-year-old boy presents 2 weeks after a sore throat with tea-colored urine, periorbital edema, and hypertension (140/90). Urinalysis shows hematuria with RBC casts and 2+ proteinuria. Serum C3 is low (40 mg/dL; normal 90-180), C4 is normal. ASO titer is elevated. What is the expected course and appropriate management?

    A. Immediate plasmapheresis to prevent ESRD B. Corticosteroids for 6 months C. Supportive care with salt restriction and diuretics; C3 normalizes in 6-8 weeks D. Cyclophosphamide and steroids E. ACE inhibitor and close monitoring for progression to chronic kidney disease

    Answer: C. Supportive care with salt restriction and diuretics; C3 normalizes in 6-8 weeks

    Explanation: - Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis (PSGN): Classic presentation - 1-3 weeks after pharyngitis (GAS infection) - Acute nephritic syndrome (hematuria, RBC casts, hypertension, edema, oliguria) - Low C3, normal C4 (alternative pathway activation) - Elevated ASO titer - Self-limited disease in children (95% full recovery) - Treatment: Supportive only - Salt/fluid restriction - Diuretics (furosemide for volume overload/hypertension) - Antihypertensives if needed - Antibiotics if ongoing infection (penicillin) - but NOT for GN itself - C3 normalizes in 6-8 weeks - if not, consider alternative diagnosis (MPGN, C3 glomerulopathy) - NO role for immunosuppression (steroids, cyclophosphamide, plasmapheresis)

    Why others are incorrect: - A/B/D: Immunosuppression/plasmapheresis NOT indicated for PSGN (self-limited) - E: While ACE inhibitor may be used for BP control, emphasis should be on recognizing excellent prognosis in children (<1% develop chronic kidney disease)

    Question 9¶

    A 50-year-old man with HCV infection presents with proteinuria (4 g/day), hematuria, and low C4 (with normal C3). Serum cryoglobulins are positive. Renal biopsy shows mesangial proliferation with GBM thickening and "tram-track" appearance. What is the most appropriate treatment?

    A. Corticosteroids B. Plasmapheresis C. Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) for HCV D. Rituximab E. Mycophenolate mofetil

    Answer: C. Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) for HCV

    Explanation: - Membranoproliferative GN (MPGN) secondary to HCV infection - HCV is the most common cause of secondary MPGN in adults - Associated with cryoglobulinemia (immune complexes containing HCV antigens) - Low C4 with normal C3 suggests classical pathway activation (cryoglobulinemia pattern) - Biopsy: Mesangial proliferation, GBM thickening/"tram-track" appearance, subendothelial deposits - Treatment of secondary MPGN: Treat underlying cause - HCV: Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) - cure HCV (>95% SVR) → resolves cryoglobulinemia and improves/stabilizes renal function - Sofosbuvir/velpatasvir, glecaprevir/pibrentasvir (pan-genotypic) - Immunosuppression (rituximab, steroids) may be considered for severe/refractory cases but treating HCV is primary

    Why others are incorrect: - A: Steroids may worsen HCV viremia - B: Plasmapheresis reserved for severe cryoglobulinemic vasculitis with life-threatening manifestations - D: Rituximab can be used for severe HCV-related cryoglobulinemia but DAAs are first-line - E: MMF not indicated; treat underlying HCV

    A 68-year-old man with 25-year history of type 2 diabetes presents with nephrotic syndrome (proteinuria 5 g/day) and diabetic retinopathy. Urinalysis shows NO hematuria. Serum creatinine is 2.8 mg/dL. What is the most appropriate management to slow progression of kidney disease?

    A. Renal biopsy to confirm diagnosis B. High-dose corticosteroids C. SGLT2 inhibitor + ACE inhibitor + glycemic control (HbA1c <7%) D. Plasmapheresis E. Immediate dialysis

    Answer: C. SGLT2 inhibitor + ACE inhibitor + glycemic control (HbA1c <7%)

    Explanation: - Diabetic nephropathy: Most common cause of nephrotic syndrome and ESRD overall - Clinical diagnosis (biopsy NOT needed) if: - Long-standing diabetes (>10 years) - Nephrotic-range proteinuria - Diabetic retinopathy (strong correlation) - NO hematuria (if present, suggests other GN) - Optimal management to slow progression: 1. SGLT2 inhibitors (FIRST-LINE for diabetic nephropathy): - Canagliflozin, dapagliflozin, empagliflozin - 40% reduction in CKD progression - ↓ Proteinuria, ↓ CV events, ↓ HF hospitalizations 2. ACE inhibitor or ARB (MAINSTAY): - Renoprotective (beyond BP lowering) - ↓ Intraglomerular pressure, ↓ proteinuria - Target BP <130/80 3. Glycemic control: HbA1c <7% (individualize) 4. GLP-1 agonists: Additional renoprotective and CV benefits - Avoid: ACE + ARB combination (↑ hyperkalemia, no added benefit)

    Why others are incorrect: - A: Biopsy NOT needed with typical presentation (diabetic retinopathy, long-standing DM, no hematuria) - B: Steroids NOT indicated for diabetic nephropathy (would worsen hyperglycemia) - D: Plasmapheresis has no role in diabetic nephropathy - E: Cr 2.8 (eGFR ~25) is CKD Stage 4 - not dialysis-dependent yet (dialysis typically eGFR <15 or uremia)
