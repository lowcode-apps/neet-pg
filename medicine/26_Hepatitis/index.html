    Hepatitis (Viral & Autoimmune)¶

    Overview¶

    Definition¶

    Hepatitis: Inflammation of the liver

    Classification by Duration¶

    • Acute: <6 months
    • Chronic: >6 months

    Etiology¶

    Viral: - Hepatitis A, B, C, D, E (hepatotropic viruses) - Other: EBV, CMV, HSV

    Non-Viral: - Autoimmune hepatitis - Drug-induced (acetaminophen, isoniazid, NSAIDs) - Alcohol - Metabolic: Wilson's disease, hemochromatosis

    Laboratory Pattern¶

    Hepatocellular Injury: - AST, ALT >> ALP - AST/ALT >1000 U/L (acute viral, drug-induced)

    Cholestatic Injury: - ALP, GGT >> AST/ALT - Bilirubin elevated

    Mixed Pattern: - Both elevated

    Hepatitis A¶

    Virology¶

    • Picornavirus (RNA virus)
    • Fecal-oral transmission
    • NO chronic infection
    • NO carrier state

    Epidemiology¶

    • Endemic in developing countries (poor sanitation)
    • Outbreaks: Contaminated food/water, daycare centers
    • Groups at risk: Travelers, MSM, injection drug users, homeless

    Clinical Features¶

    Incubation Period: 2-6 weeks (average 28 days)

    Phases:

    1. Prodrome (1-2 weeks): - Flu-like symptoms: Fever, malaise, fatigue - Nausea, vomiting, anorexia - Right upper quadrant pain

    2. Icteric Phase (1-3 weeks): - Jaundice (dark urine precedes jaundice) - Pruritus - Hepatomegaly (tender) - Acholic stools (pale) - Symptoms improve when jaundice appears

    3. Recovery (weeks to months): - Gradual resolution

    Severity: - Children: Often asymptomatic or mild - Adults: Symptomatic (70% icteric) - Elderly: More severe

    NO chronic hepatitis

    Diagnosis¶

    Serologic Testing:

    Test Interpretation
    Anti-HAV IgM Acute infection (positive 5-10 days after exposure, lasts 6 months)
    Anti-HAV IgG Past infection or vaccination (lifelong immunity)
    HAV RNA Rarely done (research)

    Liver Enzymes: - AST, ALT: 1000-5000 U/L (hepatocellular pattern) - Bilirubin: Elevated (5-20 mg/dL) - ALP: Mildly elevated

    Management¶

    Supportive: - No specific antiviral therapy - Hydration, rest - Avoid alcohol, hepatotoxic drugs - Rarely progresses to acute liver failure (<0.5%)

    Hospitalization: If unable to maintain oral hydration, coagulopathy, encephalopathy

    Prevention¶

    Pre-Exposure:

    Hepatitis A Vaccine (inactivated): - 2-dose series: 0 and 6-12 months - >95% effective - Indications: - All children (age 12-23 months) - Travelers to endemic areas - Chronic liver disease - MSM, injection drug users - Homeless

    Post-Exposure Prophylaxis: - Unvaccinated exposed <2 weeks ago: - Hepatitis A vaccine (preferred) OR - Immune globulin (IG) 0.02 mL/kg IM (if age <12 months, immunocompromised, contraindication to vaccine)

    Prognosis¶

    • Self-limited
    • 100% recovery in most cases
    • Fulminant hepatitis: <0.5% (higher in elderly, chronic liver disease)
    • NO chronic infection
    • Lifelong immunity after infection

    Hepatitis B¶

    Virology¶

    • Hepadnavirus (DNA virus)
    • Partially double-stranded circular DNA
    • Reverse transcriptase (like retroviruses)

    Viral Components: - HBsAg (surface antigen): Envelope protein - HBcAg (core antigen): Nucleocapsid protein (NOT in serum) - HBeAg (e antigen): Secreted protein (marker of high replication) - HBV DNA: Direct measure of viral load

    Epidemiology¶

    Global Burden: - 296 million people chronically infected - Endemic in Sub-Saharan Africa, East Asia

    Transmission: - Parenteral: Blood, needlestick, injection drug use - Sexual: Unprotected sex - Perinatal: Mother to child (most common in endemic areas) - 90% become chronic if infected as neonate - 5-10% become chronic if infected as adult

    Natural History¶

    Acute Infection → Recovery (95% adults) OR Chronic (5% adults, 90% neonates)

    Chronic HBV Phases:

    1. Immune Tolerant: - HBeAg positive - High HBV DNA (>2 × 10⁷ IU/mL) - Normal ALT - Minimal liver damage - Common in perinatal infection

    2. Immune Active (HBeAg-Positive Chronic Hepatitis): - HBeAg positive - High HBV DNA (>2 × 10⁴ IU/mL) - Elevated ALT (hepatitis) - Active liver damage, fibrosis progression - Treat

    3. Immune Control (Inactive Carrier): - HBeAg negative, Anti-HBe positive (seroconversion) - Low/undetectable HBV DNA (<2 × 10³ IU/mL) - Normal ALT - Minimal liver damage - Best phase (low risk progression)

    4. Immune Escape (HBeAg-Negative Chronic Hepatitis): - HBeAg negative (precore/core promoter mutant) - High HBV DNA (>2 × 10⁴ IU/mL) - Elevated ALT - Active liver damage - Treat

    Clinical Features¶

    Acute Hepatitis B:

    Incubation Period: 6 weeks to 6 months (average 90 days)

    Presentation: - Asymptomatic (70% adults, >90% children) - Symptomatic (30%): - Prodrome: Fever, malaise, anorexia, nausea, RUQ pain - Icteric phase: Jaundice, dark urine, pruritus - Serum sickness-like syndrome (10%): Arthralgias, rash, fever (due to immune complex deposition)

    Severe: - Fulminant hepatic failure (0.1-0.5%)

    Chronic Hepatitis B: - Often asymptomatic for years/decades - Complications: - Cirrhosis (20-25% over 20-30 years) - Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC): Annual incidence 2-5% in cirrhosis, 0.5-1% in chronic HBV without cirrhosis

    Extrahepatic Manifestations: - Polyarteritis nodosa (PAN) - Membranous glomerulonephritis - Cryoglobulinemia (rare)

    Diagnosis¶

    Serologic Markers:

    Marker Meaning
    HBsAg Active infection (acute or chronic)
    Anti-HBs Immunity (recovery or vaccination)
    Anti-HBc IgM Acute infection
    Anti-HBc IgG Past or chronic infection
    HBeAg High viral replication, high infectivity
    Anti-HBe Low viral replication (seroconversion)
    HBV DNA Direct measure of viral load (quantitative)

    Serologic Patterns:

    1. Acute HBV: - HBsAg positive - Anti-HBc IgM positive (KEY for acute) - HBeAg positive (usually) - Anti-HBs negative

    2. Chronic HBV: - HBsAg positive (>6 months) - Anti-HBc IgG positive - Anti-HBc IgM negative - HBeAg: Positive (active) or Negative (inactive/mutant) - HBV DNA: Measure viral load

    3. Resolved HBV (Immunity): - HBsAg negative - Anti-HBs positive (protective antibody) - Anti-HBc IgG positive

    4. Vaccinated: - HBsAg negative - Anti-HBs positive - Anti-HBc negative (never infected)

    5. Window Period (Between HBsAg clearance and Anti-HBs appearance): - HBsAg negative - Anti-HBs negative - Anti-HBc IgM positive (ONLY marker during window)

    6. Occult HBV: - HBsAg negative - Anti-HBc positive - HBV DNA positive (low level in liver)

    HBV DNA Quantification: - High: >2 × 10⁷ IU/mL (immune tolerant) - Moderate: >2 × 10⁴ IU/mL (treat if elevated ALT) - Low: <2 × 10³ IU/mL (inactive carrier)

    Management¶

    Acute Hepatitis B: - Supportive care (most resolve) - Antiviral therapy: ONLY if severe/fulminant (tenofovir or entecavir)

    Chronic Hepatitis B:

    Indications for Treatment: - HBV DNA >2 × 10⁴ IU/mL (HBeAg-positive) or >2 × 10³ IU/mL (HBeAg-negative) AND - Elevated ALT AND/OR Significant fibrosis (≥F2)

    OR: - Cirrhosis (any HBV DNA level) - Acute liver failure - HCC - Immunosuppressive therapy planned - Pregnancy (if high viral load - prevent transmission)

    First-Line Antivirals (Nucleos(t)ide Analogs):

    Entecavir: - Dosing: 0.5 mg PO daily (1 mg if lamivudine-resistant) - High barrier to resistance - Potent

    Tenofovir Disoproxil Fumarate (TDF): - Dosing: 300 mg PO daily - High barrier to resistance - Potent - Preferred in pregnancy (safe)

    Tenofovir Alafenamide (TAF): - Dosing: 25 mg PO daily - Lower bone/renal toxicity than TDF - High barrier to resistance

    Avoid (high resistance rates): - Lamivudine (older, high resistance) - Adefovir (weaker, nephrotoxic)

    Pegylated Interferon-α: - Limited use (side effects, <30% response) - Finite duration (48 weeks) - Consider in young, non-cirrhotic, high ALT, low HBV DNA

    Duration: - Indefinite in most cases (cirrhosis, HBeAg-negative) - May stop after HBeAg seroconversion + undetectable HBV DNA × ≥12 months (close monitoring) - Goal: HBsAg clearance (rare - "functional cure")

    Monitoring: - HBV DNA: Every 3-6 months (goal: undetectable) - ALT: Every 3-6 months - HCC surveillance: Ultrasound ± AFP every 6 months (ALL cirrhotic + high-risk non-cirrhotic)

    Prevention¶

    Pre-Exposure:

    Hepatitis B Vaccine (Recombinant HBsAg): - 3-dose series: 0, 1, 6 months (or 0, 1-2, 4 months for some formulations) - >90% protective (Anti-HBs >10 mIU/mL) - Indications: - Universal infant/childhood vaccination - Healthcare workers - Chronic liver disease (all causes) - Chronic kidney disease, dialysis - HIV, injection drug users, MSM - Travelers to endemic areas

    • Check Anti-HBs after series if:
    • Healthcare worker, immunocompromised, dialysis
    • If Anti-HBs <10 mIU/mL: Repeat series (1-3 doses) or double dose (dialysis patients)

    Post-Exposure Prophylaxis:

    Needlestick, Sexual, Perinatal:

    Unvaccinated: - HBIG (Hepatitis B Immune Globulin) 0.06 mL/kg IM (within 24 hours, up to 7 days) - Start HBV vaccine series (separate site)

    Vaccinated: - Check Anti-HBs: - If ≥10 mIU/mL: No treatment - If <10 mIU/mL: HBIG + booster dose

    Perinatal (Infant of HBsAg-positive mother): - HBIG 0.5 mL IM within 12 hours of birth - HBV vaccine within 12 hours (separate site) - Complete vaccine series (0, 1, 6 months) - Check HBsAg and Anti-HBs at 9-12 months (assess success)

    Complications¶

    Cirrhosis: 20-25% after 20-30 years Hepatocellular Carcinoma: 2-5%/year (cirrhosis), 0.5-1%/year (chronic HBV without cirrhosis) Fulminant Hepatic Failure: 0.1-0.5% (acute)

    Hepatitis C¶

    Virology¶

    • Flavivirus (RNA virus)
    • High mutation rate → 6 major genotypes (1-6)
    • Genotype 1: Most common in US (50%)
    • Genotype 3: Associated with steatosis, faster fibrosis

    Epidemiology¶

    Global Burden: - 58 million people chronically infected

    Transmission: - Parenteral: - Injection drug use (most common in US) - Blood transfusion (before 1992) - Needlestick - Tattoos (unsterile equipment) - Sexual: Low efficiency (<5% lifetime risk in monogamous couples) - Perinatal: 5-6% if mother HCV RNA-positive - NO transmission: Casual contact, sharing food/drinks

    Natural History¶

    Acute HCV → Spontaneous clearance (15-25%) OR Chronic (75-85%)

    Chronic HCV → Cirrhosis (15-30% over 20-30 years) → HCC (1-5%/year)

    Factors accelerating fibrosis: - Alcohol - Coinfection (HBV, HIV) - Male gender - Age >40 at infection - Obesity, diabetes

    Clinical Features¶

    Acute HCV:

    Incubation Period: 2-26 weeks (average 6-7 weeks)

    Presentation: - 80% asymptomatic - 20% symptomatic: Mild flu-like illness, jaundice (rare) - Fulminant hepatitis: Very rare (<1%)

    Chronic HCV: - Often asymptomatic for decades - Fatigue (most common symptom) - Discovered incidentally: Abnormal LFTs, screening

    Extrahepatic Manifestations (Immune complex-mediated): - Mixed cryoglobulinemia (most common): - Palpable purpura, arthralgias, glomerulonephritis - Check: Cryoglobulins, Rheumatoid factor, low C4 - Membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis (MPGN) - Porphyria cutanea tarda (PCT) - Lichen planus

    Diagnosis¶

    Serologic Testing:

    1. Anti-HCV Antibody: - Screening test (ELISA) - Positive: Current or past infection - Appears 4-12 weeks after exposure (window period) - Does NOT distinguish active vs resolved infection - Remains positive after cure (unlike HBV)

    2. HCV RNA (Quantitative PCR): - Confirmatory test - Detects active infection (viremia) - Positive as early as 1-2 weeks after exposure (before anti-HCV) - Used for: - Confirm active infection - Monitor treatment response

    Diagnostic Algorithm: 

    Anti-HCV positive
     ↓
Check HCV RNA
     ↓
  HCV RNA positive → Active infection (Chronic HCV)
  HCV RNA negative → Resolved infection OR false-positive anti-HCV

    Additional Tests: - HCV genotype: Determine treatment (now less important with pan-genotypic DAAs) - Fibrosis assessment: - Non-invasive: FibroScan (elastography), FIB-4, APRI score - Liver biopsy: Rarely needed

    Liver Enzymes: - AST, ALT: Often normal or minimally elevated (unreliable for diagnosis) - Fluctuating levels

    Management¶

    All patients with HCV RNA-positive should be treated (unless limited life expectancy)

    Direct-Acting Antivirals (DAAs): - >95% cure rate (Sustained Virologic Response - SVR) - Short duration: 8-12 weeks - Well-tolerated

    Pan-Genotypic Regimens (cover all genotypes):

    1. Sofosbuvir/Velpatasvir (Epclusa): - SOF 400 mg + VEL 100 mg PO daily × 12 weeks - All genotypes, compensated cirrhosis

    2. Glecaprevir/Pibrentasvir (Mavyret): - GLE 300 mg + PIB 120 mg (3 tabs) PO daily × 8 weeks - All genotypes, no cirrhosis or compensated - Contraindicated: Decompensated cirrhosis (Child-Pugh B/C)

    3. Sofosbuvir/Velpatasvir/Voxilaprevir (Vosevi): - For treatment-experienced patients

    Treatment-Naïve, No Cirrhosis: - GLE/PIB × 8 weeks OR SOF/VEL × 12 weeks

    Compensated Cirrhosis (Child-Pugh A): - SOF/VEL × 12 weeks OR GLE/PIB × 12 weeks (some extend to 16 weeks)

    Decompensated Cirrhosis (Child-Pugh B/C): - SOF/VEL × 12 weeks + Ribavirin (if tolerated) - Avoid protease inhibitors (GLE/PIB contraindicated)

    SVR Definition: - Undetectable HCV RNA ≥12 weeks after end of treatment (SVR12) - Equivalent to cure

    Post-Treatment: - Check HCV RNA at 12 weeks (SVR12) - HCC surveillance: Continue if cirrhosis (ultrasound ± AFP every 6 months) - Fibrosis regression: Possible if non-cirrhotic - Reinfection: Possible (no immunity) - counsel on prevention

    Prevention¶

    No vaccine available

    Prevention Strategies: - Screening: - One-time screening for ALL adults ≥18 years (2020 CDC recommendation) - High-risk: Injection drug users, recipients of blood/organs before 1992, incarcerated, HIV-positive, hemodialysis - Pregnant women: Screen at each pregnancy - Harm reduction: Clean needles for injection drug users - Blood donor screening

    Post-Exposure: - No post-exposure prophylaxis available - Monitor: Check HCV RNA at 4-6 weeks, Anti-HCV at 4-6 months

    Prognosis¶

    Without Treatment: - Cirrhosis: 15-30% over 20-30 years - HCC: 1-5%/year (if cirrhosis)

    With DAA Treatment: - >95% cure (SVR) - Reduced progression to cirrhosis/HCC - Improved survival

    Hepatitis D¶

    Virology¶

    • Defective RNA virus (requires HBV for replication)
    • Needs HBsAg (uses HBV envelope)

    Epidemiology¶

    • Endemic: Mediterranean, Middle East, Central Africa, Amazon basin
    • Rare in US
    • Transmission: Same as HBV (parenteral, sexual)

    Types of Infection¶

    Coinfection: - Simultaneous HBV + HDV infection - Acute hepatitis (often more severe than HBV alone) - Usually resolves (clears HBV → clears HDV)

    Superinfection: - HDV infection in chronic HBV carrier - Accelerated progression to cirrhosis - 90% become chronic HDV - Worse prognosis

    Diagnosis¶

    Marker Coinfection Superinfection
    HBsAg Positive Positive
    Anti-HBc IgM Positive (acute HBV) Negative (chronic HBV)
    Anti-HDV IgM Positive (transient) Positive (persistent)
    HDV RNA Positive Positive (high)

    Coinfection: - Anti-HBc IgM positive (acute HBV) - Anti-HDV IgM positive (may be transient)

    Superinfection: - Anti-HBc IgM negative (chronic HBV) - Anti-HDV IgM positive (persistent) - HDV RNA high

    Management¶

    No effective treatment: - Pegylated interferon-α: Low response rate (15-25%) - Nucleos(t)ide analogs (entecavir, tenofovir): Suppress HBV but NOT HDV - Used to prevent HBV flare, but don't affect HDV

    Emerging therapies: - Bulevirtide (entry inhibitor) - approved in Europe

    Supportive care Liver transplantation: If decompensated cirrhosis

    Prevention¶

    HBV vaccination prevents HDV (since HDV requires HBV)

    Hepatitis E¶

    Virology¶

    • Hepevirus (RNA virus)
    • Fecal-oral transmission (like HAV)
    • Genotypes:
    • 1, 2: Human-only, developing countries (waterborne outbreaks)
    • 3, 4: Zoonotic (pigs), developed countries (foodborne - undercooked pork)

    Epidemiology¶

    • Endemic: South Asia, Middle East, Africa (genotypes 1, 2)
    • Sporadic: US, Europe (genotypes 3, 4 - undercooked pork/game meat)

    Clinical Features¶

    Incubation Period: 2-9 weeks (average 40 days)

    Acute Hepatitis: - Similar to HAV (self-limited) - Jaundice, nausea, RUQ pain

    High-Risk Groups: - Pregnant women (genotype 1): - 20-30% mortality in 3rd trimester (fulminant hepatic failure) - Unique to HEV - Immunocompromised: May develop chronic HEV (genotype 3) - Transplant recipients, HIV, chemotherapy

    Usually NO chronic infection (except immunocompromised with genotype 3)

    Diagnosis¶

    Serologic Testing: - Anti-HEV IgM: Acute infection - Anti-HEV IgG: Past infection - HEV RNA: Confirm active infection (especially if immunocompromised)

    Management¶

    Acute HEV: - Supportive care (self-limited in most)

    Chronic HEV (immunocompromised): - Reduce immunosuppression (if possible) - Ribavirin × 12 weeks (off-label)

    Pregnant Women: - Supportive care - High mortality risk (3rd trimester)

    Prevention¶

    Vaccine: - HEV vaccine (Hecolin): Available in China (not US/Europe)

    General Measures: - Avoid contaminated water (endemic areas) - Cook pork/game meat thoroughly

    Autoimmune Hepatitis¶

    Definition¶

    • Chronic hepatitis caused by immune-mediated destruction of hepatocytes
    • Progressive → cirrhosis if untreated

    Epidemiology¶

    • Bimodal: Young women (15-40 years), older adults (50-70 years)
    • Female predominance (70%)

    Types¶

    Type 1 (Most common - 80%): - ANA (Antinuclear Antibody) and/or Anti-SMA (Smooth Muscle Antibody) - All ages - Better response to treatment

    Type 2 (Rare - children/young adults): - Anti-LKM-1 (Liver-Kidney Microsomal antibody) - More severe, rapid progression

    Clinical Features¶

    Presentation: - Asymptomatic → Acute hepatitis → Cirrhosis - Acute presentation (40%): - Jaundice, fatigue, RUQ pain - May mimic acute viral hepatitis - Can present as acute liver failure - Chronic/Insidious (40%): - Fatigue, arthralgias - Discovered on abnormal LFTs - Cirrhosis at diagnosis (25%)

    Associated Conditions: - Other autoimmune diseases: Thyroiditis, type 1 diabetes, IBD, celiac disease, rheumatoid arthritis

    Physical Exam: - Hepatomegaly - Stigmata of chronic liver disease (if cirrhotic)

    Diagnosis¶

    Diagnostic Criteria (International AIH Group - Simplified Scoring):

    Feature Points
    ANA or SMA ≥1:40 +1
    ANA or SMA ≥1:80 +2
    Anti-LKM-1 ≥1:40 +2
    SLA/LP positive +2
    IgG >Upper limit normal +1
    IgG >1.1× ULN +2
    Liver biopsy compatible +1
    Liver biopsy typical +2
    Viral hepatitis excluded +2

    Score: - ≥6: Probable AIH - ≥7: Definite AIH

    Laboratory: - AST, ALT: Elevated (100-1000 U/L) - hepatocellular pattern - Hypergammaglobulinemia: IgG >1.5× ULN (very suggestive) - Autoantibodies: - ANA (80%) - Anti-SMA (60-70%) - Anti-LKM-1 (Type 2) - Exclude viral hepatitis: Negative HBsAg, anti-HCV

    Liver Biopsy (Required for diagnosis): - Interface hepatitis (piecemeal necrosis) - KEY - Lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate (plasma cells prominent) - Rosette formation (hepatocytes) - Fibrosis/cirrhosis (variable)

    Management¶

    Indications for Treatment: - AST/ALT >10× ULN OR - AST/ALT >5× ULN + IgG >2× ULN OR - Bridging fibrosis or cirrhosis on biopsy

    Do NOT treat if: - Inactive disease (normal transaminases, minimal histology) - Decompensated cirrhosis (treat complications, transplant)

    First-Line:

    Prednisone + Azathioprine (Standard): - Prednisone: 30-40 mg daily × 1 week, then taper to 10-20 mg daily (maintenance) - Azathioprine: 50-150 mg daily (1-2 mg/kg) - Combination preferred (lower steroid dose → fewer side effects)

    Prednisone Monotherapy: - 60 mg daily × 1 week, taper to 20 mg (maintenance) - If azathioprine contraindicated (pregnancy, TPMT deficiency, cytopenias)

    Response: - Biochemical remission: Normalization of AST, ALT, IgG (6-12 months in 80%) - Histologic remission: Resolution of inflammation (may take 18-24 months)

    Duration: - Minimum 2-3 years (until biochemical + histologic remission) - Attempt withdrawal: After remission × ≥2 years - Relapse rate: 50-90% after withdrawal - Most require lifelong therapy

    Relapse: - Restart prednisone + azathioprine

    Second-Line (if azathioprine intolerant/refractory): - Mycophenolate mofetil (MMF) 1-2 g daily - Budesonide (9 mg daily) + azathioprine (non-cirrhotic only - first-pass metabolism) - Tacrolimus, cyclosporine (refractory cases)

    Liver Transplantation: - Indications: Acute liver failure, decompensated cirrhosis, refractory disease - Recurrence post-transplant: 10-35% (continue immunosuppression)

    Prognosis¶

    Without Treatment: - Cirrhosis: 40-50% at 5 years - High mortality: 40% at 5 years

    With Treatment: - Excellent prognosis: 10-year survival >90% (if non-cirrhotic at diagnosis) - Cirrhosis: May still develop HCC (surveillance needed)

    Drug-Induced Liver Injury¶

    Overview¶

    • Common cause of acute liver failure in US (50%)
    • Acetaminophen = most common overall
    • Antibiotics = most common non-acetaminophen

    Classification¶

    Intrinsic (Dose-Dependent, Predictable): - Acetaminophen (most common) - Carbon tetrachloride, mushroom toxins

    Idiosyncratic (Unpredictable): - Immune-mediated: Isoniazid, nitrofurantoin, diclofenac - Metabolic: Valproic acid, tetracycline

    Common Drugs¶

    Hepatocellular Pattern (AST/ALT >> ALP): - Acetaminophen (dose-dependent) - Isoniazid (idiosyncratic) - Valproic acid - Amoxicillin-clavulanate - Statins (rare)

    Cholestatic Pattern (ALP >> AST/ALT): - Amoxicillin-clavulanate (most common antibiotic DILI) - Azithromycin - Oral contraceptives - Anabolic steroids

    Mixed Pattern: - Phenytoin - Sulfonamides

    Acetaminophen Hepatotoxicity¶

    Mechanism: - Toxic metabolite (NAPQI) produced by CYP2E1 - Glutathione detoxifies NAPQI - Overdose → glutathione depletion → NAPQI accumulates → hepatocyte necrosis

    Toxic Dose: - >7.5-10 g in adults (or >150 mg/kg) - Lower doses toxic if: - Chronic alcohol use (CYP2E1 induction) - Malnutrition (↓ glutathione) - Medications (inducing CYP2E1)

    Clinical Features:

    Phase 1 (0-24 hours): - Nausea, vomiting (non-specific)

    Phase 2 (24-72 hours): - RUQ pain, hepatomegaly - AST, ALT rise (may be >10,000 U/L)

    Phase 3 (72-96 hours): - Peak hepatotoxicity - Acute liver failure: Coagulopathy, encephalopathy, jaundice - AST/ALT >3000 U/L (often >10,000)

    Phase 4 (>5 days): - Recovery or death

    Diagnosis: - History of ingestion - Acetaminophen level: - Plot on Rumack-Matthew nomogram (if ingestion within 24 hours) - >150 μg/mL at 4 hours or >30 μg/mL at 12 hours = toxic - Elevated AST, ALT (often >10,000 U/L) - INR elevation (hepatic synthetic dysfunction)

    Management:

    N-Acetylcysteine (NAC) (Antidote): - Mechanism: Replenishes glutathione, detoxifies NAPQI - Indications: - Toxic level on nomogram OR - Unknown time of ingestion + acetaminophen level >10 μg/mL OR - Acute liver failure (regardless of level)

    • Most effective if started within 8 hours of ingestion

    • Still beneficial up to 24-48 hours

    • Oral NAC:

    • Loading: 140 mg/kg

    • Maintenance: 70 mg/kg q4h × 17 doses

    • IV NAC:

    • Loading: 150 mg/kg over 1 hour
    • Maintenance: 50 mg/kg over 4 hours, then 100 mg/kg over 16 hours

    Supportive Care: - Liver transplant evaluation if acute liver failure

    Prognosis: - Excellent if NAC started early (<8 hours) - Mortality: <1% if treated early, up to 20-40% if late/untreated

    Other Drug-Induced Liver Injury¶

    Isoniazid: - 10-20% asymptomatic transaminase elevation - 1% clinical hepatitis - Risk factors: Age >35, alcohol, slow acetylators - Monitor: AST, ALT monthly × 3 months, then as needed - Discontinue if AST/ALT >3× ULN with symptoms or >5× ULN asymptomatic

    Statins: - Rare (<1%) serious hepatotoxicity - Asymptomatic elevation in 1-3% (often self-limited) - Check baseline AST, ALT, then as clinically indicated - Do NOT routinely monitor

    Amoxicillin-Clavulanate: - Most common antibiotic DILI - Cholestatic pattern - Onset: 1-6 weeks after starting (may be after stopping)

    Management: - Discontinue offending drug (most important) - Supportive care - Corticosteroids: May help if immune-mediated (phenytoin, nitrofurantoin) - Liver transplant: If acute liver failure

    Acute Liver Failure¶

    Definition¶

    • Severe acute liver injury with:
    • Hepatic encephalopathy AND
    • INR ≥1.5
    • <26 weeks duration (no pre-existing liver disease)

    Classification by Timeline¶

    Hyperacute: <7 days (acetaminophen) Acute: 7-21 days Subacute: 21 days to 26 weeks

    Etiology¶

    US: - Acetaminophen (40-50%) - most common - Idiosyncratic drug reaction (10-15%) - Viral hepatitis: HBV, HAV, HEV (rare in US) - Autoimmune hepatitis (5%) - Ischemic hepatitis (shock liver) - Indeterminate (15-20%)

    Less Common: - Wilson's disease (young patients) - Budd-Chiari syndrome - Acute fatty liver of pregnancy, HELLP syndrome - Amanita phalloides (mushroom poisoning)

    Clinical Features¶

    Hepatic Encephalopathy: - Mandatory for diagnosis - Grades 1-4 (see cirrhosis section)

    Coagulopathy: - INR ≥1.5 (mandatory) - Bleeding (GI, intracranial)

    Jaundice: - Bilirubin often >20 mg/dL

    Multi-Organ Failure: - Cerebral edema (high ICP → herniation) - Acute kidney injury (50-80%) - Hypoglycemia (impaired gluconeogenesis) - Infection (50% - SBP, pneumonia, fungal) - Hemodynamic instability (vasodilation, ↓ SVR)

    Diagnosis¶

    Labs: - AST, ALT: Markedly elevated (acetaminophen >10,000; viral 1000-3000) - INR: ≥1.5 (reflects synthetic dysfunction) - Bilirubin: Elevated - Ammonia: Elevated (correlates with encephalopathy severity) - Hypoglycemia: Common

    Identify Etiology: - Acetaminophen level - Viral serologies: Anti-HAV IgM, HBsAg, Anti-HBc IgM, Anti-HCV, HCV RNA, HEV RNA - Autoimmune: ANA, anti-SMA, IgG - Wilson's disease (if age <40): - Low ceruloplasmin - High urinary copper - Alkaline phosphatase/Bilirubin ratio <4 (suggests Wilson's) - Hemolytic anemia (Coombs-negative) - Pregnancy test (women of childbearing age) - Drug/toxin screen

    Imaging: - Doppler ultrasound: Assess hepatic vein/portal vein patency (Budd-Chiari)

    Management¶

    Intensive Care Unit: - All patients with ALF should be in ICU

    Supportive Care:

    1. Hepatic Encephalopathy: - Lactulose - Avoid sedatives - Intubation if Grade 3-4 (airway protection)

    2. Cerebral Edema (leading cause of death): - ICP monitoring (if Grade 3-4 encephalopathy) - Head of bed 30° - Mannitol (if ICP >20 mmHg) - Hypertonic saline (Na 145-155 mEq/L) - Avoid hypotension (maintain CPP >50 mmHg)

    3. Coagulopathy: - DO NOT correct with FFP/platelets unless: - Active bleeding OR - Invasive procedure (e.g., ICP monitor placement) - INR is prognostic marker (correcting obscures assessment)

    4. Hypoglycemia: - Frequent glucose monitoring - D10W infusion as needed

    5. Infection: - High suspicion (impaired immunity) - Empiric antibiotics if suspected (after cultures) - Antifungal prophylaxis (fluconazole) in some centers

    6. Renal Failure: - Continuous renal replacement therapy (CRRT) (gentler on hemodynamics than hemodialysis)

    7. Hemodynamic Support: - Vasopressors (norepinephrine) if hypotensive

    Specific Therapies:

    Acetaminophen: - N-Acetylcysteine (NAC): Even if >24 hours post-ingestion (improves outcomes in ALF)

    HBV: - Entecavir or tenofovir

    Autoimmune Hepatitis: - Corticosteroids (if confirmed AIH and no sepsis/GI bleed)

    Wilson's Disease: - Liver transplantation (URGENT - high mortality) - Plasmapheresis (bridge to transplant)

    Amanita Phalloides: - Penicillin G (high-dose) + Silibinin (not available in US)

    Liver Transplantation:

    Indications (King's College Criteria):

    Acetaminophen: - pH <7.3 (after resuscitation) OR - All 3 of: - INR >6.5 - Creatinine >3.4 mg/dL - Grade 3-4 encephalopathy

    Non-Acetaminophen: - INR >6.5 OR - Any 3 of 5: - Age <10 or >40 - Etiology: Indeterminate, drug (non-acetaminophen), Wilson's, Budd-Chiari - Jaundice >7 days before encephalopathy - INR >3.5 - Bilirubin >17.5 mg/dL

    Prognosis: - Without transplant: 50-80% mortality (depends on etiology) - With transplant: 60-80% survival - Spontaneous survival: Higher in acetaminophen (60-70%) vs other causes (25-40%)

    Diagnostic Pearls¶

    Hepatitis B Serology: - Acute: HBsAg +, Anti-HBc IgM + - Chronic: HBsAg + (>6 mo), Anti-HBc IgG + - Resolved/Immune: Anti-HBs +, Anti-HBc + - Vaccinated: Anti-HBs +, Anti-HBc negative - Window period: Only Anti-HBc IgM + (HBsAg cleared, Anti-HBs not yet appeared)

    Hepatitis C: - Anti-HCV: Screening (remains + after cure) - HCV RNA: Confirms active infection (use for diagnosis)

    Autoimmune Hepatitis: - Hypergammaglobulinemia (IgG >1.5× ULN) - very suggestive - ANA/SMA (Type 1), Anti-LKM-1 (Type 2) - Interface hepatitis on biopsy - Treatment: Prednisone + azathioprine

    Treatment Pearls¶

    Chronic HBV: - First-line: Entecavir or tenofovir (high barrier to resistance) - Treat if: HBV DNA elevated + elevated ALT + significant fibrosis, OR cirrhosis - Duration: Usually indefinite - HCC surveillance: Ultrasound ± AFP every 6 months (ALL cirrhotic)

    Chronic HCV: - Cure >95% with DAAs - Pan-genotypic: SOF/VEL, GLE/PIB - Duration: 8-12 weeks - SVR12 = cure (HCV RNA undetectable 12 weeks post-treatment)

    Autoimmune Hepatitis: - Prednisone + azathioprine (first-line) - Minimum 2-3 years treatment - Relapse common after withdrawal (often require lifelong)

    Acetaminophen Overdose: - N-Acetylcysteine (most effective <8 hours, but give even if late) - Loading: 140 mg/kg PO, then 70 mg/kg q4h × 17 doses

    Special Populations¶

    Pregnancy: - HEV (genotype 1): 20-30% mortality in 3rd trimester - HBV transmission prevention: - Infant: HBIG + vaccine within 12 hours of birth - Mother: If high viral load (>200,000 IU/mL) → tenofovir in 3rd trimester

    Immunocompromised: - HEV (genotype 3): May develop chronic infection - Reactivation: HBV (check HBsAg, Anti-HBc before chemotherapy/immunosuppression)

    Acute Liver Failure¶

    Most Common Cause (US): Acetaminophen (40-50%)

    King's College Criteria (Transplant): - Acetaminophen: pH <7.3 OR (INR >6.5 + Cr >3.4 + Grade 3-4 HE) - Non-acetaminophen: INR >6.5 OR any 3 of 5 criteria

    Management: - ICU care - NAC (acetaminophen - give even if late) - Avoid correcting INR (unless bleeding/procedure) - Liver transplant evaluation (urgent)

    Practice MCQs¶

    Question 1¶

    A 25-year-old woman presents with jaundice and fatigue for 1 week. She recently returned from a camping trip. Labs: AST 2000 U/L, ALT 2500 U/L, bilirubin 8 mg/dL. Serologies: HBsAg negative, Anti-HBc IgG negative, Anti-HAV IgM positive, Anti-HCV negative. What is the diagnosis and expected outcome?

    A) Acute hepatitis A; will develop chronic hepatitis in 75% B) Acute hepatitis A; complete recovery expected C) Acute hepatitis B; will develop chronic hepatitis in 5% D) Acute hepatitis C; complete recovery expected

    Answer: B) Acute hepatitis A; complete recovery expected

    Explanation: - Acute Hepatitis A: - Anti-HAV IgM positive = acute infection - Fecal-oral transmission (contaminated water - camping)

    • Course:
    • Self-limited
    • 100% recovery (NO chronic infection)

    • Lifelong immunity (Anti-HAV IgG develops)

    • Hepatitis A:

    • NEVER becomes chronic
    • Fulminant hepatitis: Rare (<0.5%)

    Question 2¶

    A 35-year-old man has the following hepatitis B panel: HBsAg positive, Anti-HBc IgG positive, Anti-HBc IgM negative, HBeAg negative, Anti-HBe positive, HBV DNA 500 IU/mL, ALT 35 U/L. What is his hepatitis B status?

    A) Acute hepatitis B B) Chronic hepatitis B - immune active phase C) Chronic hepatitis B - inactive carrier D) Resolved hepatitis B

    Answer: C) Chronic hepatitis B - inactive carrier

    Explanation: - Chronic HBV - Inactive Carrier phase: - HBsAg positive (>6 months = chronic) - Anti-HBc IgG positive (chronic) - Anti-HBc IgM negative (NOT acute) - HBeAg negative, Anti-HBe positive (seroconversion occurred) - HBV DNA low (<2000 IU/mL) ✓ (500 IU/mL) - ALT normal ✓ (35 U/L)

    • Best phase of chronic HBV:
    • Minimal liver damage
    • Low progression risk

    • NO treatment needed (but monitor)

    • HCC surveillance: Still needed every 6 months (chronic HBV)

    • Other phases:

    • Acute: Anti-HBc IgM positive
    • Immune active: HBeAg+ OR HBeAg- with high HBV DNA + elevated ALT
    • Resolved: HBsAg negative, Anti-HBs +, Anti-HBc +

    Question 3¶

    A 50-year-old woman with chronic hepatitis C (genotype 1) is treatment-naïve with no cirrhosis. What is the MOST appropriate treatment regimen?

    A) Pegylated interferon + ribavirin × 48 weeks B) Glecaprevir/pibrentasvir × 8 weeks C) Sofosbuvir/velpatasvir × 24 weeks D) Supportive care only

    Answer: B) Glecaprevir/pibrentasvir × 8 weeks

    Explanation: - Chronic HCV - Treatment-Naïve, No Cirrhosis:

    Pan-Genotypic DAA Options: - Glecaprevir/Pibrentasvir (GLE/PIB): 8 weeks (no cirrhosis) - Sofosbuvir/Velpatasvir (SOF/VEL): 12 weeks (all patients)

    • Shortest duration: GLE/PIB × 8 weeks (if no cirrhosis)

    • Cure rate: >95% (SVR12)

    • Interferon-based therapy: Outdated (<50% cure, significant side effects, long duration)

    • If cirrhosis (Child-Pugh A):

    • SOF/VEL × 12 weeks OR GLE/PIB × 12-16 weeks

    Question 4¶

    A 30-year-old woman with chronic hepatitis B (HBsAg positive) develops acute hepatitis. Labs show: HBsAg positive, Anti-HBc IgM negative, Anti-HDV IgM positive, HDV RNA high. What type of hepatitis D infection is this?

    A) HDV coinfection B) HDV superinfection C) Chronic HDV only D) Resolved HDV

    Answer: B) HDV superinfection

    Explanation: - HDV Superinfection: - HDV infection in chronic HBV carrier - HBsAg positive (chronic HBV) ✓ - Anti-HBc IgM negative (NOT acute HBV - chronic) ✓ - Anti-HDV IgM positive (acute HDV) ✓ - HDV RNA high ✓

    • Superinfection characteristics:
    • 90% become chronic HDV
    • Accelerated progression to cirrhosis

    • Worse prognosis

    • Coinfection (vs Superinfection):

    • Simultaneous HBV + HDV
    • Anti-HBc IgM positive (acute HBV)
    • Usually resolves (clears HBV → clears HDV)

    Question 5¶

    A 28-year-old woman in her 3rd trimester of pregnancy (32 weeks gestation) who recently returned from India presents with jaundice, nausea, and RUQ pain. Labs: AST 1500 U/L, ALT 1800 U/L, bilirubin 12 mg/dL. Viral serologies: Anti-HAV IgM negative, HBsAg negative, Anti-HCV negative, Anti-HEV IgM positive. What is the expected outcome?

    A) Complete recovery (self-limited) B) High risk of fulminant hepatic failure (20-30% mortality) C) 75% chance of chronic hepatitis D) No risk to fetus

    Answer: B) High risk of fulminant hepatic failure (20-30% mortality)

    Explanation: - Hepatitis E in pregnant woman (3rd trimester): - Anti-HEV IgM positive (acute HEV) - Genotype 1 (endemic in India)

    • Pregnancy + HEV:
    • 20-30% mortality in 3rd trimester
    • Fulminant hepatic failure (unique to HEV)

    • High fetal mortality

    • Management:

    • Supportive care
    • Close monitoring for liver failure

    • Consider delivery if severe

    • Non-pregnant adults: Self-limited (like HAV)

    • Genotype 3 (US/Europe):

    • Foodborne (undercooked pork)
    • Lower risk in pregnancy
    • May become chronic in immunocompromised

    Question 6¶

    A 22-year-old woman presents with jaundice and fatigue. Labs: AST 800 U/L, ALT 900 U/L, ANA 1:320, Anti-smooth muscle antibody positive, IgG 3000 mg/dL (normal <1500). Liver biopsy shows interface hepatitis and lymphoplasmacytic infiltrate. What is the first-line treatment?

    A) Pegylated interferon B) Prednisone + azathioprine C) Tenofovir D) Sofosbuvir/velpatasvir

    Answer: B) Prednisone + azathioprine

    Explanation: - Autoimmune Hepatitis (Type 1): - Young woman (typical) - ANA positive, Anti-SMA positive - Hypergammaglobulinemia (IgG 3000 mg/dL - >2× ULN) - very suggestive - Interface hepatitis on biopsy (characteristic)

    • First-Line Treatment:
    • Prednisone + Azathioprine (combination preferred)
    • Prednisone: 30-40 mg daily → taper to 10-20 mg (maintenance)

    • Azathioprine: 50-150 mg daily (1-2 mg/kg)

    • Duration:

    • Minimum 2-3 years (until biochemical + histologic remission)

    • Most require lifelong therapy (high relapse rate after withdrawal)

    • Response: 80% achieve remission in 6-12 months

    Question 7¶

    A 45-year-old man presents with altered mental status 18 hours after intentional acetaminophen overdose (20 grams). Labs: AST 8500 U/L, ALT 9200 U/L, INR 3.5, creatinine 2.1 mg/dL, bilirubin 4 mg/dL, acetaminophen level 25 μg/mL, pH 7.32. What is the MOST appropriate management?

    A) Activated charcoal B) N-Acetylcysteine (NAC) C) Liver transplant evaluation + NAC D) Observation only (too late for antidote)

    Answer: C) Liver transplant evaluation + NAC

    Explanation: - Acetaminophen-Induced Acute Liver Failure: - Massive hepatotoxicity: AST, ALT >8000 U/L - Coagulopathy: INR 3.5 - Renal failure: Cr 2.1 - Altered mental status (hepatic encephalopathy) - pH 7.32 (metabolic acidosis)

    • Management:

    1. N-Acetylcysteine (NAC): - Give even if >24 hours post-ingestion - Beneficial in ALF (improves survival even when late) - NOT too late for antidote

    2. Liver Transplant Evaluation: - King's College Criteria (Acetaminophen): - pH <7.3 (this patient 7.32 - borderline) OR - All 3: INR >6.5, Cr >3.4, Grade 3-4 HE - This patient: Approaching criteria (INR 3.5, Cr 2.1, altered MS) - Urgent transplant evaluation warranted

    3. Supportive Care (ICU): - Manage encephalopathy, coagulopathy, renal failure

    • Activated charcoal:
    • Effective ONLY within 1-2 hours of ingestion
    • 18 hours post-ingestion = too late

    Question 8¶

    A 40-year-old man with chronic hepatitis B on entecavir presents for follow-up. Labs: HBV DNA undetectable, ALT 28 U/L. He asks when he can stop treatment. What is the appropriate response?

    A) Stop entecavir now (HBV DNA undetectable) B) Stop after 6 more months of undetectable HBV DNA C) Continue indefinitely (lifelong treatment) D) Switch to pegylated interferon × 48 weeks, then stop

    Answer: C) Continue indefinitely (lifelong treatment)

    Explanation: - Chronic HBV - Treatment Duration:

    Most patients: Indefinite treatment (lifelong) - Cirrhosis: NEVER stop (high reactivation risk) - HBeAg-negative chronic hepatitis: Indefinite - HBeAg-positive: May consider stopping after HBeAg seroconversion + undetectable HBV DNA × ≥12 months (but high relapse rate)

    • Goal: HBsAg clearance (rare - "functional cure")
    • Achieved in <10% even with long-term treatment

    • HBV DNA undetectable does NOT mean cured

    • If treatment stopped:

    • High reactivation rate (especially cirrhosis)

    • Close monitoring required (HBV DNA, ALT q3 months)

    • This patient: No indication for stopping

    • Continue entecavir indefinitely
    • Monitor HBV DNA, ALT every 6 months
    • HCC surveillance every 6 months (if cirrhotic or high-risk)

    Question 9¶

    A healthcare worker sustains a needlestick injury from an HBsAg-positive patient. The healthcare worker was vaccinated for hepatitis B 10 years ago but never had Anti-HBs checked. What is the MOST appropriate post-exposure prophylaxis?

    A) No treatment needed (vaccinated) B) HBIG only C) HBV vaccine booster only D) Check Anti-HBs; if <10 mIU/mL, give HBIG + booster

    Answer: D) Check Anti-HBs; if <10 mIU/mL, give HBIG + booster

    Explanation: - Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP) - Hepatitis B:

    Vaccinated Healthcare Worker (unknown response): - Check Anti-HBs: - If ≥10 mIU/mL: Adequate protection → No treatment - If <10 mIU/mL: Inadequate response → HBIG + Booster dose

    • Unvaccinated:
    • HBIG (within 24 hours, up to 7 days)

    • Start HBV vaccine series (0, 1, 6 months)

    • Known Responder (documented Anti-HBs ≥10 after vaccination):

    • No treatment (lifelong immunity, even if titer declines)

    • Known Non-Responder (no response after 6 doses):

    • HBIG × 2 doses (0 and 1 month)

    • Timing:

    • HBIG: Most effective within 24 hours (up to 7 days)
    • Vaccine: Can give simultaneously (separate site)

    Question 10¶

    A 55-year-old woman with chronic hepatitis C (compensated cirrhosis, Child-Pugh A) is starting DAA therapy. Which regimen is MOST appropriate?

    A) Glecaprevir/pibrentasvir × 8 weeks B) Glecaprevir/pibrentasvir × 12 weeks C) Sofosbuvir/velpatasvir × 12 weeks D) Pegylated interferon + ribavirin × 48 weeks

    Answer: C) Sofosbuvir/velpatasvir × 12 weeks

    Explanation: - Chronic HCV with Compensated Cirrhosis (Child-Pugh A):

    Treatment Options: - Sofosbuvir/Velpatasvir (SOF/VEL): 12 weeks ✓ - Safe in compensated cirrhosis - Pan-genotypic

    • Glecaprevir/Pibrentasvir (GLE/PIB):
    • Contraindicated in decompensated cirrhosis (Child-Pugh B/C)
    • May use in compensated cirrhosis (Child-Pugh A): 12-16 weeks

    • NOT 8 weeks (8 weeks only for non-cirrhotic)

    • SOF/VEL preferred in cirrhosis (better safety data)

    • Decompensated Cirrhosis (Child-Pugh B/C):

    • SOF/VEL × 12 weeks + Ribavirin (if tolerated)
    • Avoid protease inhibitors (GLE contraindicated)

    Summary¶

    Viral Hepatitis: - HAV, HEV: Fecal-oral, self-limited, NO chronic (except HEV in immunocompromised/pregnant) - HBV: Chronic in 5% adults (90% neonates), treat with entecavir/tenofovir (indefinite) - HCV: Chronic in 75-85%, cure >95% with DAAs (SOF/VEL, GLE/PIB) - HDV: Requires HBV, superinfection worse than coinfection

    Autoimmune Hepatitis: - Young women, hypergammaglobulinemia (IgG), ANA/SMA (Type 1), interface hepatitis - Treatment: Prednisone + azathioprine (lifelong in most)

    Drug-Induced Liver Injury: - Acetaminophen most common, treat with NAC (most effective <8 hours, but give even if late)

    Acute Liver Failure: - Acetaminophen #1 cause (US), King's College Criteria for transplant - ICU care, avoid correcting INR (unless bleeding/procedure)
