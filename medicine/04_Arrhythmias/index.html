    Overview¶

    Definition¶

    Arrhythmias are disorders of cardiac rhythm caused by abnormalities in impulse formation, impulse conduction, or both.

    Classification¶

    By Heart Rate: - Tachyarrhythmias: Heart rate >100 bpm - Bradyarrhythmias: Heart rate <60 bpm

    By Origin: - Supraventricular: Atria or AV node - Ventricular: Ventricles

    By Mechanism: - Re-entry: Most common (AF, VT, AVNRT) - Abnormal Automaticity: Ectopic pacemaker - Triggered Activity: EADs (Torsades), DADs (Digoxin toxicity)

    Mechanisms - "RAT"¶

    R - Re-entry (Most common)
A - Automaticity
T - Triggered activity

    Atrial Fibrillation (AF)¶

    Definition¶

    Most common sustained cardiac arrhythmia characterized by rapid, irregular atrial activation without discrete P waves.

    Epidemiology¶

    • Prevalence: 1-2% general population
    • Increases with age: 10% in >80 years
    • Risk of stroke: 5x increased

    Etiology - "PIRATES"¶

    P - Pulmonary disease (COPD, PE)
I - Ischemic heart disease (MI, CAD)
R - Rheumatic heart disease (Mitral stenosis)
A - Alcohol (Holiday heart syndrome)
T - Thyrotoxicosis
E - Embolism (Pulmonary)
S - Sepsis, Surgery

    Additional Causes: - Hypertension (Most common in developed countries) - Mitral valve disease (Most common worldwide) - Cardiomyopathies - Congenital heart disease (ASD) - Pericarditis - Sick sinus syndrome

    Classification¶

    1. Based on Duration¶

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                   AF CLASSIFICATION                      │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ First detected      → First episode documented          │
│ Paroxysmal          → Terminates within 7 days          │
│ Persistent          → Lasts >7 days                     │
│ Long-standing       → Lasts >12 months                  │
│ Permanent           → Accepted, no cardioversion        │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    2. Lone AF¶

    • AF in patients <60 years
    • No structural heart disease
    • Good prognosis

    Pathophysiology¶

    Mechanism: Multiple micro-re-entry circuits in atria

    Key Changes: 1. Atrial rate: 300-600 bpm (irregular) 2. Ventricular rate: 120-180 bpm (irregularly irregular) 3. Loss of atrial kick: ↓ Cardiac output by 20-30% 4. Atrial stasis: Thrombus formation (LAA - Left Atrial Appendage)

    Clinical Features¶

    Symptoms: - Palpitations (Most common) - Dyspnea, fatigue - Chest discomfort - Dizziness, syncope - Some patients asymptomatic (detected incidentally)

    Signs: - Irregularly irregular pulse (Hallmark) - Pulse deficit (Radial pulse < Apical rate) - Varying intensity of S1 - Absent 'a' wave in JVP

    Complications: 1. Thromboembolism (Stroke - most serious) 2. Heart failure (Tachycardia-induced) 3. Hypotension 4. Cardiomyopathy

    Investigations¶

    ECG - Diagnostic¶

    Key Features: 

    ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              AF ON ECG                               │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ 1. Absent P waves                                    │
│ 2. Fibrillatory waves (f waves)                      │
│ 3. Irregularly irregular QRS complexes               │
│ 4. Narrow QRS (unless bundle branch block)           │
│ 5. Ventricular rate: Usually 120-180 bpm             │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    AF with RVR (Rapid Ventricular Response): Rate >100 bpm

    Other Investigations¶

    • Echocardiography:
    • LA size (>4 cm - high risk)
    • LV function
    • Valvular disease

    • Thrombus detection (TEE more sensitive)

    • Thyroid function tests: Rule out thyrotoxicosis

    • Holter monitoring: Paroxysmal AF
    • Chest X-ray: Cardiomegaly, pulmonary congestion

    Management¶

    1. Rate Control vs Rhythm Control¶

    Rate Control (Preferred in most patients): - Target: Resting HR <110 bpm (lenient) or <80 bpm (strict) - First-line drugs: - Beta-blockers (Metoprolol, Bisoprolol) - DOC - Calcium channel blockers (Diltiazem, Verapamil) - Digoxin (Only in sedentary/HF patients)

    Rhythm Control (Cardioversion): - Indications: - Symptomatic despite rate control - First episode - Young patients - AF precipitated by reversible cause

    • Methods:
    • Electrical cardioversion: 200-360 J (synchronized)
    • Pharmacological: Flecainide, Propafenone, Amiodarone

    <48 hours rule: - AF duration <48 hours: Cardioversion safe (low thrombus risk) - AF duration >48 hours or unknown: - Anticoagulate 3 weeks before cardioversion - Or TEE to rule out thrombus - Continue anticoagulation 4 weeks after

    2. Anticoagulation - MOST IMPORTANT¶

    CHA₂DS₂-VASc Score (Stroke Risk):

    ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│          CHA₂DS₂-VASc SCORE                       │
├────────────────────────────────┬──────────────────┤
│ C - CHF/LV dysfunction         │      1 point     │
│ H - Hypertension               │      1 point     │
│ A₂ - Age ≥75 years             │      2 points    │
│ D - Diabetes mellitus          │      1 point     │
│ S₂ - Stroke/TIA/TE history     │      2 points    │
│ V - Vascular disease           │      1 point     │
│ A - Age 65-74 years            │      1 point     │
│ Sc - Sex category (Female)     │      1 point     │
├────────────────────────────────┴──────────────────┤
│ Maximum Score: 9                                  │
└───────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Interpretation:
Score 0 (Male) or 1 (Female): Low risk → No anticoagulation
Score 1 (Male): Consider anticoagulation
Score ≥2: Anticoagulation recommended

    HAS-BLED Score (Bleeding Risk):

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│           HAS-BLED SCORE                        │
├──────────────────────────────────┬──────────────┤
│ H - Hypertension (>160 mmHg)     │   1 point    │
│ A - Abnormal renal/liver function│   1-2 points │
│ S - Stroke history               │   1 point    │
│ B - Bleeding history             │   1 point    │
│ L - Labile INR                   │   1 point    │
│ E - Elderly (>65 years)          │   1 point    │
│ D - Drugs/Alcohol                │   1-2 points │
├──────────────────────────────────┴──────────────┤
│ Score ≥3: High bleeding risk (CAUTION)          │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Anticoagulation Options:

    1. Vitamin K Antagonists: - Warfarin: Target INR 2-3 - Requires regular monitoring

    2. DOACs (Direct Oral Anticoagulants) - Preferred: - Dabigatran: Direct thrombin inhibitor - Rivaroxaban, Apixaban, Edoxaban: Factor Xa inhibitors - Advantages: No monitoring, fixed dose, fewer drug interactions - Disadvantages: No reversal agent (except Idarucizumab for Dabigatran), cost

    DOAC Contraindications: - Severe renal impairment (CrCl <30 ml/min) - Mechanical heart valves - Mitral stenosis (Warfarin preferred)

    3. Special Situations¶

    Hemodynamically Unstable AF: - Immediate DC cardioversion - No waiting for anticoagulation

    Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) with AF: - AVOID: Digoxin, Beta-blockers, CCBs (may cause VF) - Use: Procainamide, Ibutilide, or DC cardioversion

    4. Catheter Ablation¶

    • Indications:
    • Symptomatic paroxysmal AF refractory to drugs
    • Patient preference

    • Tachycardia-induced cardiomyopathy

    • Procedure: Pulmonary vein isolation (PVI)

    • Success rate: 60-80%

    5. Surgical Options¶

    • MAZE procedure: Creates scar tissue to block abnormal circuits
    • LAA closure: Watchman device (alternative to anticoagulation)

    Ventricular Tachycardia (VT)¶

    Definition¶

    ≥3 consecutive ventricular beats at rate >100 bpm

    Classification¶

    By Duration: - Non-sustained VT (NSVT): <30 seconds - Sustained VT: ≥30 seconds or requires termination

    By Morphology: - Monomorphic VT: Same QRS morphology (single focus) - Polymorphic VT: Varying QRS morphology - Torsades de Pointes: Special type with twisting QRS

    Etiology¶

    With Structural Heart Disease (90%): - Ischemic heart disease (Post-MI scar - most common) - Cardiomyopathies (Dilated, Hypertrophic, ARVC) - Valvular heart disease - Congenital heart disease

    Without Structural Heart Disease (10%): - Idiopathic VT: - RVOT VT (Right Ventricular Outflow Tract) - Fascicular VT - Channelopathies: - Long QT syndrome - Brugada syndrome - Catecholaminergic polymorphic VT

    Reversible Causes - "HIKE" 

    H - Hypoxia
I - Ischemia/Infarction
K - K+ abnormalities (Hypo/Hyperkalemia)
E - Electrolyte imbalance, Drugs

    Pathophysiology¶

    Mechanism: - Re-entry (Most common) - around MI scar - Abnormal automaticity - Triggered activity

    Clinical Features¶

    Symptoms: - Palpitations - Dizziness, syncope - Chest pain - Dyspnea - Sudden cardiac death (if sustained)

    Signs: - Tachycardia (>100 bpm, usually 120-250) - Hypotension - Signs of poor perfusion - Cannon 'a' waves in JVP (AV dissociation) - Varying S1 intensity

    Hemodynamic Stability: - Stable: Conscious, BP maintained - Unstable: Hypotension, altered consciousness, pulmonary edema

    Investigations¶

    ECG Features of VT¶

    ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              VT ON ECG                               │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ 1. Wide QRS complex (>120 ms)                        │
│ 2. Rate >100 bpm (usually 120-250)                   │
│ 3. Regular or slightly irregular                     │
│ 4. AV dissociation (P and QRS independent)           │
│ 5. Capture beats (occasional normal QRS)             │
│ 6. Fusion beats (QRS intermediate morphology)        │
│ 7. Positive or negative concordance in chest leads   │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Brugada Criteria for VT vs SVT with Aberrancy:

    1. Absence of RS complex in all chest leads → VT
    2. R to S interval >100 ms in any chest lead → VT
    3. AV dissociation → VT
    4. Morphology criteria:
    5. RBBB pattern: R in V1 or QS/QR → VT
    6. LBBB pattern: R in V1 >30ms, Slurred S downstroke → VT

    VT is more likely if: - History of MI/structural heart disease - Age >35 years - Wide QRS (>140 ms)

    Other Investigations¶

    • Echocardiography: LV function, structural disease
    • Coronary angiography: CAD
    • Cardiac MRI: Scar, infiltrative disease, ARVC
    • Electrophysiology study: Inducibility, ablation

    Management¶

    Acute Management¶

    Algorithm: 

    ┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│         VT ACUTE MANAGEMENT                        │
├───────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                    │
│  UNSTABLE (Hypotension/AMS/Chest pain/Pulm edema) │
│               ↓                                    │
│      DC Cardioversion (Synchronized)               │
│      Energy: 100-200 J (Biphasic)                  │
│                                                    │
│  ────────────────────────────────────────────────  │
│                                                    │
│  STABLE                                            │
│               ↓                                    │
│      Correct K+, Mg2+ (Target: K>4, Mg>2)          │
│               ↓                                    │
│      Amiodarone 150 mg IV over 10 min              │
│      (or Procainamide, Sotalol)                    │
│               ↓                                    │
│      If fails → DC Cardioversion                   │
│                                                    │
└───────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    NEVER use: - Verapamil in VT → May cause cardiovascular collapse - Adenosine → Only for VT vs SVT differentiation (if truly uncertain)

    Long-term Management¶

    1. ICD (Implantable Cardioverter-Defibrillator) - MOST IMPORTANT

    Indications: - Secondary prevention: - Survived cardiac arrest (VT/VF) - Sustained VT with hemodynamic compromise

    • Primary prevention:
    • Post-MI with EF ≤30% (>40 days post-MI)
    • Non-ischemic cardiomyopathy with EF ≤35%
    • Genetic conditions (Brugada, LQTS, HCM)

    2. Anti-arrhythmic Drugs - Amiodarone: Most effective - Sotalol: Beta-blocker + Class III - Beta-blockers: Post-MI (reduce VT)

    3. Catheter Ablation - Monomorphic VT with identifiable scar - Recurrent VT despite ICD/drugs - Idiopathic VT (curative)

    4. Treat Underlying Cause - Revascularization for ischemia - Optimize heart failure management - Correct electrolytes

    Torsades de Pointes (TdP)¶

    Special type of polymorphic VT with prolonged QT

    ECG Features: - "Twisting of the points" - QRS axis rotates around baseline - Ventricular rate: 200-250 bpm - Prolonged QT interval (>500 ms)

    Causes - "ABCDE" 

    A - Anti-arrhythmics (Quinidine, Sotalol, Amiodarone)
B - Bradycardia (Heart block)
C - Congenital (Long QT syndrome)
D - Diuretics (Hypokalemia, Hypomagnesemia)
E - Erythromycin, other drugs (Antipsychotics, TCAs)

    Management: 1. Magnesium sulfate 2g IV (Even if Mg2+ normal) - DRUG OF CHOICE 2. Correct electrolytes: K+ >4.5, Mg2+ >2 3. Stop offending drugs 4. Temporary pacing (to increase HR and shorten QT) 5. Isoproterenol (if pacing not available) 6. DC cardioversion (if hemodynamically unstable)

    AVOID: Standard anti-arrhythmics (may prolong QT further)

    Heart Blocks¶

    Overview¶

    Definition: Impaired conduction from atria to ventricles through AV node/His-Purkinje system

    Classification: 1. First-degree AV block 2. Second-degree AV block - Mobitz Type I (Wenckebach) - Mobitz Type II 3. Third-degree (Complete) AV block

    First-Degree AV Block¶

    Definition: Prolonged AV conduction without dropped beats

    ECG Features: 

    ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      1° AV BLOCK ECG                         │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ • PR interval >200 ms (>5 small squares)     │
│ • Every P wave followed by QRS               │
│ • PR interval constant                       │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Causes: - Increased vagal tone (Athletes) - Drugs (Beta-blockers, CCBs, Digoxin) - Inferior MI - Myocarditis, Lyme disease - Degenerative conduction disease

    Management: - Usually no treatment required - Asymptomatic - Monitor if drugs/ischemia involved - No pacemaker needed

    Second-Degree AV Block¶

    Mobitz Type I (Wenckebach)¶

    ECG Features: 

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      MOBITZ TYPE I (WENCKEBACH)                 │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ • Progressive PR prolongation                   │
│ • Until one P wave not conducted (dropped QRS)  │
│ • Then cycle repeats                            │
│ • "Progressively longer, until dropped, repeat" │
│ • RR interval progressively shortens            │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Characteristics: - Site: AV node (above His bundle) - Usually benign - Common in inferior MI (RCA occlusion) - Athletes, during sleep

    Management: - Usually no treatment if asymptomatic - May resolve spontaneously - Temporary pacing if symptomatic (rare) - Treat underlying cause (e.g., inferior MI)

    Mobitz Type II¶

    ECG Features: 

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      MOBITZ TYPE II                             │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ • Constant PR interval                          │
│ • Sudden dropped QRS (without PR prolongation)  │
│ • "Constant, constant, dropped"                 │
│ • Wide QRS if infra-Hisian                      │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Characteristics: - Site: Below AV node (His-Purkinje system) - More serious - may progress to complete heart block - Common in anterior MI (LAD occlusion) - Wide QRS (bundle branch involvement)

    Management: - Permanent pacemaker indicated (high risk of progression) - Temporary pacing if acute (e.g., MI) - Do NOT use Atropine (may worsen block)

    Third-Degree (Complete) AV Block¶

    Definition: Complete dissociation between atria and ventricles

    ECG Features: 

    ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      COMPLETE HEART BLOCK                       │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ • P waves regular                               │
│ • QRS complexes regular                         │
│ • No relationship between P and QRS             │
│ • "P and QRS march independently"               │
│ • Ventricular rate slower (20-40 bpm)           │
│ • Wide QRS if ventricular escape                │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Escape Rhythms: - Junctional escape: 40-60 bpm, narrow QRS (if block at AV node) - Ventricular escape: 20-40 bpm, wide QRS (if block below AV node)

    Causes: - Congenital: Maternal SLE (anti-Ro antibodies) - Acquired: - Degenerative (Lev's disease, Lenègre's disease) - Ischemic: MI (inferior or anterior) - Drugs: Beta-blockers, CCBs, Digoxin - Infiltrative: Sarcoidosis, amyloidosis - Infections: Lyme disease, endocarditis - Post-cardiac surgery

    Clinical Features: - Bradycardia (20-40 bpm) - Syncope (Stokes-Adams attacks) - Fatigue, dyspnea - Heart failure - Sudden cardiac death

    Management:

    Acute: 1. Atropine 0.5 mg IV (if narrow QRS/junctional escape) 2. Temporary pacing (transcutaneous or transvenous) 3. Isoproterenol infusion (if pacing delayed)

    Chronic: - Permanent pacemaker (Definitive treatment) - Indications: - Symptomatic complete heart block - Asymptomatic with pauses >3 seconds - HR <40 bpm while awake - Congenital CHB with wide QRS, symptoms, or ventricular dysfunction

    Other Important Arrhythmias¶

    Atrial Flutter¶

    Definition: Regular atrial rhythm at 250-350 bpm with "sawtooth" pattern

    ECG Features: - Sawtooth F waves (best in II, III, aVF) - Atrial rate: 300 bpm (typically) - Variable AV block: 2:1, 3:1, 4:1 - 2:1 block → Ventricular rate 150 bpm - 3:1 block → Ventricular rate 100 bpm

    Management: - Similar to AF - Rate control: Beta-blockers, CCBs - Rhythm control: Cardioversion (lower energy needed, ~50-100 J) - Anticoagulation: Same as AF (CHA₂DS₂-VASc) - Catheter ablation: Very high success rate (>90%) - CURATIVE

    Supraventricular Tachycardia (SVT)¶

    Types: 1. AVNRT (AV Nodal Reentrant Tachycardia) - Most common (60%) 2. AVRT (AV Reentrant Tachycardia) - Accessory pathway (WPW) 3. Atrial tachycardia

    AVNRT: - Mechanism: Re-entry within AV node (slow-fast pathway) - ECG: Narrow QRS, regular, rate 150-250, pseudo-r' in V1, pseudo-s in II/III/aVF - Acute management: - Vagal maneuvers (Carotid massage, Valsalva) - First - Adenosine 6 mg IV rapid bolus → 12 mg → 12 mg - Verapamil/Diltiazem if adenosine fails - DC cardioversion if unstable - Long-term: Catheter ablation (curative)

    Adenosine: - Mechanism: Blocks AV node transiently - Dose: 6 mg → 12 mg → 12 mg (rapid IV push) - Side effects: Transient asystole, flushing, chest discomfort - Contraindications: Asthma (bronchospasm), WPW with AF - Use: Diagnostic (unmasks atrial activity) and therapeutic (terminates SVT)

    Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) Syndrome¶

    Definition: Pre-excitation syndrome with accessory pathway (Bundle of Kent)

    ECG Features (Sinus Rhythm): 

    ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      WPW ECG                                 │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ • Short PR interval (<120 ms)                │
│ • Delta wave (slurred QRS upstroke)          │
│ • Wide QRS (>120 ms)                         │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Complications: - AVRT (Orthodromic - narrow QRS; Antidromic - wide QRS) - AF with rapid ventricular response → May degenerate to VF

    Management: - Acute AVRT: - Vagal maneuvers, Adenosine (if narrow QRS) - DC cardioversion if unstable

    • AF with WPW:
    • Procainamide or Ibutilide (slow accessory pathway)
    • DC cardioversion if unstable

    • AVOID: Digoxin, Beta-blockers, CCBs, Adenosine (may accelerate conduction through accessory pathway → VF)

    • Long-term: Catheter ablation of accessory pathway (Curative)

    Premature Beats¶

    PACs (Premature Atrial Contractions): - Early P wave (different morphology) - Followed by QRS (usually narrow) - Benign - no treatment needed

    PVCs (Premature Ventricular Contractions): - Wide QRS, no preceding P wave - Compensatory pause (full pause) - Benign in structurally normal heart - Concerning if: - Frequent (>10/hour) - Multifocal - R-on-T phenomenon - Associated with structural heart disease - Management: Treat underlying cause, Beta-blockers

    Key Drug Details¶

    Amiodarone: - Most effective anti-arrhythmic - Uses: VT, AF, SVT - Side effects: "ABCDEFG" - A - Arrhythmias (Bradycardia, TdP) - B - Blue-gray skin (photosensitivity) - C - Corneal deposits - D - Deranged LFTs - E - Everything else (Neuropathy, myopathy) - F - Fibrosis (Pulmonary - most serious) - G - Goiter (Hypo/Hyperthyroidism) - Monitoring: TFTs, LFTs, PFTs, Chest X-ray

    Sotalol: - Beta-blocker + Class III - Proarrhythmic: May cause TdP (dose-dependent) - Contraindicated: CrCl <40 ml/min, QT >450 ms

    Digoxin: - Use: AF with heart failure (rate control) - Narrow therapeutic window - Toxicity: "Yellow vision," Nausea, Arrhythmias (bidirectional VT) - Precipitants: Hypokalemia, Hypercalcemia, Hypomagnesemia, Renal failure - Treatment of toxicity: Digoxin-specific Fab fragments

    NEET PG High-Yield Points¶

    🔥 MUST KNOW¶

    1. AF is the most common sustained arrhythmia

    2. CHA₂DS₂-VASc score:

    3. ≥2: Anticoagulation mandatory

    4. Score 0 (male) or 1 (female): No anticoagulation

    5. DOACs preferred over Warfarin (except mechanical valves, mitral stenosis)

    6. AF <48 hours: Cardioversion safe without prior anticoagulation

    7. VT vs SVT with aberrancy: VT more likely if history of MI, age >35, wide QRS >140 ms

    8. AV dissociation, Capture/Fusion beats → VT

    9. Torsades de Pointes treatment: Magnesium sulfate (DOC)

    10. Mobitz II requires permanent pacemaker (unlike Mobitz I)

    11. Complete heart block: Permanent pacemaker indicated

    12. WPW with AF: AVOID Digoxin, Beta-blockers, CCBs → Use Procainamide

    13. ICD indications:

      • Post-MI EF ≤30%
      • Survived cardiac arrest
      • Sustained VT with hemodynamic compromise

    14. Adenosine:

      • Dose: 6 mg → 12 mg → 12 mg (rapid IV)
      • Contraindicated in asthma, WPW with AF

    15. Amiodarone most common side effect: Pulmonary fibrosis (most serious), Thyroid dysfunction (most common)

    16. Flecainide: Only in structurally normal heart (CAST study - ↑ mortality post-MI)

    Clinical Scenarios¶

    Scenario 1: Irregularly irregular pulse → AF

    Scenario 2: Wide QRS tachycardia in post-MI patient → VT (until proven otherwise)

    Scenario 3: Young patient with palpitations, delta wave on ECG → WPW

    Scenario 4: Progressive PR prolongation, then dropped QRS in inferior MI → Mobitz I (Wenckebach)

    Scenario 5: Constant PR with sudden dropped QRS in anterior MI → Mobitz II → Needs pacemaker

    Scenario 6: Patient on Sotalol develops "twisting" QRS tachycardia → Torsades → Give Magnesium

    Scenario 7: AF patient, CHA₂DS₂-VASc = 3 → Anticoagulation mandatory

    Quick Revision Checklist¶

    • AF classification: Paroxysmal, Persistent, Permanent
    • CHA₂DS₂-VASc and HAS-BLED scores
    • DOACs: Dabigatran, Rivaroxaban, Apixaban, Edoxaban
    • VT ECG features: Wide QRS, AV dissociation, Capture/Fusion beats
    • Brugada criteria for VT vs SVT
    • Torsades management: Magnesium sulfate
    • Heart blocks: 1°, Mobitz I, Mobitz II, Complete
    • Pacemaker indications: Mobitz II, Complete heart block
    • WPW: Delta wave, avoid AV nodal blockers in AF
    • ICD indications: Post-MI EF ≤30%, cardiac arrest survivor
    • Anti-arrhythmic classification: Vaughan Williams
    • Amiodarone side effects: Pulmonary, thyroid
    • Adenosine: 6-12-12 mg, contraindicated in asthma

    Practice Questions¶

    Question 1¶

    A 65-year-old male presents with palpitations. ECG shows irregularly irregular rhythm without P waves and narrow QRS complexes at a rate of 140 bpm. He has diabetes and hypertension. What is his CHA₂DS₂-VASc score and management?

    A) Score 2, Aspirin B) Score 3, Anticoagulation with DOAC C) Score 1, No anticoagulation D) Score 4, Warfarin only

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: B) Score 3, Anticoagulation with DOAC** **Explanation:** - Diagnosis: Atrial fibrillation (irregularly irregular, no P waves) - CHA₂DS₂-VASc calculation: - C (CHF): 0 - H (Hypertension): 1 - A₂ (Age ≥75): 0 - D (Diabetes): 1 - S₂ (Stroke): 0 - V (Vascular): 0 - A (Age 65-74): 1 - Sc (Female): 0 - **Total: 3** - Score ≥2 → Anticoagulation required - DOACs preferred over Warfarin (unless contraindicated)

    Question 2¶

    A 55-year-old male with previous MI presents with wide QRS tachycardia at 180 bpm. ECG shows AV dissociation and capture beats. He is hemodynamically stable. What is the most appropriate initial management?

    A) Verapamil IV B) Adenosine 6 mg IV C) Amiodarone 150 mg IV D) Immediate DC cardioversion

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: C) Amiodarone 150 mg IV** **Explanation:** - Diagnosis: **Ventricular Tachycardia** (wide QRS, AV dissociation, capture beats, post-MI) - Patient is **stable** → Pharmacological management first - **Amiodarone 150 mg IV** is drug of choice for stable VT - Verapamil is **contraindicated** in VT (may cause cardiovascular collapse) - Adenosine not appropriate for VT - DC cardioversion reserved for unstable patients or failed medical management

    Question 3¶

    A 70-year-old female with inferior MI on day 2 develops bradycardia. ECG shows progressive PR interval prolongation followed by a dropped QRS, then the cycle repeats. Management?

    A) Permanent pacemaker B) Temporary pacing C) Observation only D) Atropine infusion

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: C) Observation only** **Explanation:** - Diagnosis: **Mobitz Type I (Wenckebach)** - progressive PR prolongation until dropped QRS - Context: **Inferior MI** (RCA occlusion affects AV node) - Mobitz I is usually **benign** and often **resolves spontaneously** - No treatment needed if asymptomatic - If symptomatic (rare): Consider temporary pacing - **No permanent pacemaker** needed (unlike Mobitz II) - Atropine may help if symptomatic, but not as infusion

    Question 4¶

    A patient on Sotalol develops palpitations. ECG shows polymorphic ventricular tachycardia with QRS complexes that appear to "twist" around the baseline. QTc is 580 ms. What is the treatment?

    A) Amiodarone IV B) Magnesium sulfate 2g IV C) Lidocaine IV D) Increase Sotalol dose

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: B) Magnesium sulfate 2g IV** **Explanation:** - Diagnosis: **Torsades de Pointes** (polymorphic VT with "twisting" QRS, prolonged QT) - Cause: Sotalol (Class III anti-arrhythmic - prolongs QT) - **Magnesium sulfate** is the **drug of choice** for Torsades - Give even if serum Mg2+ is normal - Also: - **Stop Sotalol** - Correct K+ (>4.5) and Mg2+ (>2) - Consider temporary pacing to increase HR (shortens QT) - **Avoid:** Standard anti-arrhythmics (may worsen by prolonging QT further)

    Question 5¶

    A 30-year-old athlete has routine ECG showing PR interval of 220 ms with normal QRS complexes. Every P wave is followed by a QRS. He is asymptomatic. Management?

    A) Permanent pacemaker B) EP study C) No treatment, reassurance D) Beta-blocker therapy

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: C) No treatment, reassurance** **Explanation:** - Diagnosis: **First-degree AV block** (PR >200 ms, every P followed by QRS) - **Common in athletes** (increased vagal tone) - **Asymptomatic** and **benign** - **No treatment required** - No pacemaker needed - Monitor if taking AV nodal blocking drugs or if ischemia present

    Question 6¶

    A patient with WPW syndrome develops AF with rapid ventricular rate of 220 bpm. He is hypotensive. What should be avoided?

    A) DC cardioversion B) Procainamide C) Ibutilide D) Verapamil

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: D) Verapamil** **Explanation:** - **WPW with AF** is a medical emergency (risk of VF) - **AVOID:** Digoxin, Beta-blockers, CCBs (Verapamil, Diltiazem), Adenosine - These block AV node → More conduction through accessory pathway → ↑ Ventricular rate → VF - **Appropriate treatments:** - **DC cardioversion** (if hemodynamically unstable) - **CORRECT ANSWER HERE** - **Procainamide or Ibutilide** (slow accessory pathway conduction) - This patient is **unstable (hypotensive)** → **Immediate DC cardioversion** is the treatment - Verapamil could be **fatal** in this scenario

    Question 7¶

    A 60-year-old male with dilated cardiomyopathy and EF 25% presents after syncope. Holter shows non-sustained VT. What is the most appropriate long-term management?

    A) Amiodarone only B) ICD implantation C) EP study with catheter ablation D) Beta-blocker only

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: B) ICD implantation** **Explanation:** - **Primary prevention ICD indication:** - Non-ischemic cardiomyopathy (dilated CM) with **EF ≤35%** - Symptomatic (syncope) with NSVT - ICD reduces risk of sudden cardiac death - **Amiodarone/Beta-blockers** are adjuncts but **ICD is definitive** for primary prevention - Catheter ablation has limited role in non-ischemic cardiomyopathy with diffuse disease

    Question 8¶

    A patient presents with palpitations. ECG shows narrow complex regular tachycardia at 180 bpm. Carotid sinus massage terminates the arrhythmia. What is the most likely diagnosis?

    A) Atrial fibrillation B) Atrial flutter with 2:1 block C) AVNRT D) Ventricular tachycardia

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: C) AVNRT** **Explanation:** - **AVNRT** (AV Nodal Reentrant Tachycardia): - **Most common SVT** (60%) - **Narrow QRS, regular** tachycardia - **Terminates with vagal maneuvers** (carotid massage, Valsalva) - Mechanism: Re-entry within AV node - AF is **irregular** - Atrial flutter has **sawtooth** pattern and usually doesn't terminate with vagal maneuvers (may slow temporarily) - VT has **wide QRS** and doesn't respond to vagal maneuvers
