    Definition & Overview¶

    Definition¶

    Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) is a preventable and treatable disease characterized by persistent respiratory symptoms and airflow limitation that is not fully reversible, due to airway and/or alveolar abnormalities, usually caused by significant exposure to noxious particles or gases.

    Key Features: - Chronic airflow limitation (FEV₁/FVC <0.70 post-bronchodilator) - Not fully reversible (vs Asthma - reversible) - Progressive - Preventable (Smoking cessation)

    Epidemiology¶

    • 4th leading cause of death worldwide
    • Smoking: 80-90% of cases
    • Prevalence increases with age (>40 years)
    • More common in men (Historically - gap narrowing due to ↑ women smoking)

    Umbrella Term¶

    COPD includes: 1. Chronic Bronchitis (Clinical diagnosis) 2. Emphysema (Pathological diagnosis)

    Most patients have BOTH

    Pathophysiology¶

    Risk Factors¶

    "COPD RISKS" 

    C - Cigarette smoking (MOST IMPORTANT - 80-90%)
O - Occupational exposures (Coal dust, Silica, Cadmium)
P - Pollution (Biomass fuel, Air pollution)
D - Deficiency (α₁-Antitrypsin) - Genetic

R - Recurrent respiratory infections (Childhood)
I - Indoor air pollution (Biomass fuels - Developing countries)
S - Socioeconomic status (Poverty)
K - Kyphoscoliosis (Restrictive, but can coexist)
S - Second-hand smoke

    Smoking: - Dose-dependent: Pack-years (Packs/day × Years) - Most important modifiable risk factor

    1. Chronic Bronchitis¶

    Clinical Definition: "Productive cough for ≥3 months per year for ≥2 consecutive years" (after excluding other causes)

    Pathology: - Hypertrophy of mucus-secreting glands (Reid index >0.4) - Reid index = Gland thickness / Bronchial wall thickness (Normal <0.4) - Goblet cell hyperplasia - Chronic inflammation (Neutrophils, Macrophages) - Small airway narrowing, fibrosis

    Phenotype: "Blue Bloater" - Cyanosis (Chronic hypoxemia) - Edema (Cor pulmonale) - Overweight - ↑ PaCO₂ (Hypercapnia)

    2. Emphysema¶

    Pathological Definition: "Abnormal permanent enlargement of airspaces distal to terminal bronchioles, with destruction of alveolar walls, without obvious fibrosis"

    Types (Based on Location):

    Centriacinar (Centrilobular): - Most common (95%) - Smoking-related - Upper lobes predominantly - Respiratory bronchioles affected

    Panacinar (Panlobular): - α₁-Antitrypsin deficiency - Lower lobes predominantly - Entire acinus affected

    Paraseptal: - Subpleural (Periphery) - Associated with pneumothorax (Bullae rupture)

    Pathology: - Alveolar wall destruction (Protease-antiprotease imbalance) - Loss of elastic recoil → Air trapping - ↓ Diffusing capacity (DLCO)

    Phenotype: "Pink Puffer" - Pink (Normal O₂ saturation - compensated) - Pursed-lip breathing (Prevent airway collapse) - Cachexia (Weight loss - ↑ Work of breathing) - Barrel chest (Hyperinflation) - Normal/Low PaCO₂ (Hyperventilation to maintain O₂)

    Protease-Antiprotease Imbalance (Emphysema)¶

    Normal: - Balance between proteases (Elastase, Proteinase) and antiproteases (α₁-Antitrypsin)

    Emphysema: 

    Cigarette Smoke / α₁-AT Deficiency
        ↓
↑ Neutrophils, Macrophages
        ↓
↑ Elastase, Proteases
        ↓
Overwhelm antiproteases (↓ α₁-Antitrypsin)
        ↓
Alveolar Wall Destruction
        ↓
EMPHYSEMA

    α₁-Antitrypsin Deficiency¶

    Genetics: - Autosomal codominant - Pi phenotype (Protease inhibitor) - PiMM: Normal (Most common) - PiZZ: Severe deficiency (<10-15% normal) - Homozygous - PiMZ: Heterozygous (Mild deficiency)

    Clinical Features: - Early-onset emphysema (<40 years, especially smokers) - Panacinar pattern, Lower lobe predominance - Liver cirrhosis (Protein accumulation in hepatocytes)

    Diagnosis: - Low serum α₁-Antitrypsin level (<50 mg/dL - severe) - Pi phenotyping or Genetic testing

    Management: - Smoking cessation (MOST IMPORTANT) - α₁-Antitrypsin augmentation therapy (IV infusions) - If severe deficiency + established emphysema - Lung transplant (End-stage)

    Clinical Features¶

    Symptoms¶

    Cardinal Symptoms: 1. Dyspnea (Progressive, Exertional → At rest) - Most common presenting symptom - Chronic, Progressive 2. Chronic cough (Productive - "Smoker's cough") - May precede dyspnea by years 3. Sputum production (Mucoid, Chronic) - Purulent during exacerbations

    Additional: - Wheezing (Less prominent than asthma) - Chest tightness - Fatigue, Weight loss (Advanced disease)

    Symptoms worse in: - Early morning (Mucus accumulation overnight) - Cold weather - Exacerbations

    Physical Examination¶

    Inspection: - Barrel chest (Hyperinflation - AP = Transverse diameter) - Pursed-lip breathing (Prevent airway collapse) - Use of accessory muscles (Sternocleidomastoid, Scalene) - Tripod position (Sitting, leaning forward) - Cyanosis (Chronic hypoxemia - "Blue bloater") - Cachexia (Advanced disease - "Pink puffer") - Clubbing: NOT typical (If present, think lung cancer complication)

    Palpation: - ↓ Chest expansion - ↓ Tactile fremitus (Hyperinflation)

    Percussion: - Hyperresonant (Hyperinflation) - ↓ Liver dullness (Diaphragm pushed down)

    Auscultation: - ↓ Breath sounds (Air trapping) - Prolonged expiration (Airflow obstruction) - Wheeze (Expiratory, polyphonic) - Crackles (If bronchitis or infection)

    Cor Pulmonale (Right Heart Failure): - Elevated JVP - RV heave (Parasternal lift) - Loud P2 (Pulmonary hypertension) - Hepatomegaly, Peripheral edema, Ascites

    Diagnosis¶

    Spirometry - GOLD STANDARD¶

    Essential for Diagnosis

    Criteria: 

    ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      COPD DIAGNOSTIC CRITERIA (GOLD)               │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ POST-BRONCHODILATOR FEV₁/FVC <0.70                 │
│                                                    │
│ • Confirms PERSISTENT airflow limitation           │
│ • NOT fully reversible (vs Asthma)                 │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

    Key Point: Post-bronchodilator FEV₁/FVC <0.70 (Confirms irreversible obstruction)

    Obstructive Pattern: - FEV₁/FVC <0.70 (Ratio reduced) - ↓ FEV₁ (Severity) - Normal or ↓ FVC

    Bronchodilator Response: - Minimal reversibility (<12% or <200 ml FEV₁ improvement) - Differentiates from asthma (Asthma: ≥12% AND ≥200 ml)

    Other Pulmonary Function Tests¶

    Lung Volumes: - ↑ Total Lung Capacity (TLC) (Hyperinflation) - ↑ Residual Volume (RV) (Air trapping) - ↑ Functional Residual Capacity (FRC) - ↑ RV/TLC ratio (>35%) - Air trapping

    Diffusing Capacity (DLCO): - ↓ in Emphysema (Alveolar destruction) - Normal in Chronic Bronchitis

    Chest X-ray¶

    Findings:

    Hyperinflation: - Flattened diaphragm (<1.5 cm height) - ↑ Retrosternal airspace (Lateral view - >4.5 cm) - Narrow cardiac silhouette ("Tear-drop" heart) - ↓ Vascular markings (Emphysema)

    Chronic Bronchitis: - Increased lung markings ("Dirty lungs") - Cardiomegaly (If cor pulmonale)

    Bullae: Large airspaces (>1 cm) - Emphysema

    Uses: - Rule out complications: Pneumonia, Pneumothorax, Lung cancer - Not diagnostic for COPD (Spirometry is)

    ABG (Arterial Blood Gas)¶

    Indications: - Severe COPD (FEV₁ <50%) - Acute exacerbation - Suspected respiratory failure

    Findings:

    Chronic Bronchitis ("Blue Bloater"): - Hypoxemia: ↓ PaO₂ (<60 mmHg) - Hypercapnia: ↑ PaCO₂ (>45 mmHg) - Chronic CO₂ retention - Compensated respiratory acidosis: pH normal (↑ HCO₃⁻ - Renal compensation)

    Emphysema ("Pink Puffer"): - Normal/Mild hypoxemia (Initially) - Normal/Low PaCO₂ (Hyperventilation)

    Acute Exacerbation: - Uncompensated respiratory acidosis: ↓ pH, ↑ PaCO₂

    Additional Investigations¶

    ECG: - Cor Pulmonale: RVH, P pulmonale (Tall P in II, III, aVF), RAD, RBBB

    Echocardiography: - Pulmonary hypertension (PA systolic pressure) - RV function, Tricuspid regurgitation

    6-Minute Walk Test (6MWT): - Assess functional capacity - Prognostic: <350 m → Poor prognosis

    α₁-Antitrypsin Level: - If early-onset COPD (<40 years), Minimal smoking, or Family history

    mMRC (Modified Medical Research Council) Dyspnea Scale: - 0: Dyspnea only with strenuous exercise - 1: Dyspnea when hurrying or walking up a slight hill - 2: Walks slower than people of same age due to dyspnea, or stops for breath when walking at own pace - 3: Stops for breath after walking ~100 m - 4: Too dyspneic to leave house, or dyspneic with dressing

    CAT (COPD Assessment Test): - Questionnaire (0-40 points) - <10: Low symptoms - ≥10: High symptoms

    Management¶

    Goals¶

    1. Relieve symptoms
    2. Prevent disease progression
    3. ↓ Exacerbations
    4. Improve exercise tolerance and quality of life
    5. Reduce mortality

    Non-Pharmacological¶

    1. Smoking Cessation - MOST IMPORTANT¶

    ONLY intervention that slows disease progression

    All smokers with COPD should be counseled to quit

    Methods: - Behavioral counseling - Nicotine replacement therapy (NRT - Gum, Patch, Lozenge) - Pharmacotherapy: - Varenicline (Champix) - Partial nicotine receptor agonist (Most effective) - Bupropion (Antidepressant)

    Benefits: - ↓ Rate of FEV₁ decline - ↓ Exacerbations - ↓ Mortality

    2. Vaccinations¶

    Influenza Vaccine: - Annual (All COPD patients) - ↓ Exacerbations, ↓ Hospitalizations

    Pneumococcal Vaccine: - PCV13 (Conjugate) + PPSV23 (Polysaccharide) - All COPD patients ≥65 years or younger with significant comorbidities

    COVID-19 Vaccine: - Recommended

    3. Pulmonary Rehabilitation¶

    Components: - Exercise training (Aerobic, Strength) - Education - Nutritional counseling - Psychosocial support

    Indications: - All symptomatic COPD (Group B, C, D)

    Benefits: - ↑ Exercise tolerance - ↓ Dyspnea, ↑ Quality of life - ↓ Hospitalizations - Does NOT improve FEV₁ or mortality

    4. Oxygen Therapy (Long-Term Oxygen Therapy - LTOT)¶

    Indications: - PaO₂ ≤55 mmHg (or SpO₂ ≤88%) at rest, OR - PaO₂ 56-59 mmHg with: - Evidence of cor pulmonale (Edema, P pulmonale on ECG) - OR Polycythemia (Hct >55%)

    Prescription: - ≥15 hours/day (More is better) - Flow rate: To achieve SpO₂ 88-92%

    Benefits: - ↓ Mortality (ONLY intervention proven in COPD besides smoking cessation) - ↓ Polycythemia - ↓ Pulmonary hypertension - ↑ Exercise tolerance

    AVOID high-flow oxygen (Target SpO₂ 88-92%, NOT >92%): - Risk of CO₂ retention (Hypercapnia) - Loss of hypoxic drive

    5. Surgical Options¶

    Lung Volume Reduction Surgery (LVRS): - Resection of emphysematous tissue (Upper lobe) - Indications: - Severe emphysema (FEV₁ <45%) - Hyperinflation with poor exercise capacity - Upper lobe predominant disease - Benefits: ↑ FEV₁, ↑ Exercise tolerance, ↓ Mortality (Selected patients)

    Lung Transplant: - End-stage COPD (FEV₁ <25%, Severe dyspnea) - Age <65 years

    Bullectomy: - Large bullae compressing surrounding lung

    Medications¶

    1. Short-Acting Bronchodilators - RELIEVERS¶

    Short-Acting Beta-2 Agonists (SABA): - Salbutamol, Levosalbutamol - Mechanism: Bronchodilation (Onset 5 min, Duration 4-6 hr) - Use: As-needed for acute symptoms

    Short-Acting Muscarinic Antagonists (SAMA): - Ipratropium bromide - Mechanism: Block M3 receptors → Bronchodilation (Onset 30 min, Duration 6-8 hr) - Use: As-needed or regular (QID)

    Combination: Salbutamol + Ipratropium (Combivent)

    2. Long-Acting Bronchodilators - MAINTENANCE (Mainstay)¶

    Long-Acting Beta-2 Agonists (LABA): - Formoterol, Salmeterol, Indacaterol, Olodaterol, Vilanterol - Duration: 12-24 hours - Benefits: ↑ FEV₁, ↓ Dyspnea, ↑ Quality of life, ↓ Exacerbations

    Long-Acting Muscarinic Antagonists (LAMA): - Tiotropium, Glycopyrronium, Umeclidinium, Aclidinium - Duration: 12-24 hours - Benefits: ↑ FEV₁, ↓ Dyspnea, ↓ Exacerbations (More effective than LABA)

    LAMA vs LABA: - LAMA slightly more effective in ↓ exacerbations - LAMA preferred as initial monotherapy (Group C)

    Dual Bronchodilation (LABA + LAMA): - Examples: Indacaterol/Glycopyrronium, Vilanterol/Umeclidinium - Better than monotherapy (↑ FEV₁, ↓ Exacerbations) - First-line for Group B, D

    3. Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICS) - ANTI-INFLAMMATORY¶

    Examples: - Budesonide, Fluticasone, Beclomethasone

    Indications: - NOT first-line in COPD (Unlike asthma) - Add to LABA in: - Frequent exacerbations despite LABA/LAMA - Eosinophilia (Blood eosinophils ≥300 cells/μL) - Asthma-COPD overlap

    ICS/LABA Combinations: - Budesonide/Formoterol, Fluticasone/Salmeterol, Fluticasone/Vilanterol

    Side Effects: - Pneumonia (↑ Risk - 30-70%) - Oral candidiasis, Dysphonia - Osteoporosis (Long-term)

    Triple Therapy (ICS + LABA + LAMA): - Group D with eosinophils ≥100 - Severe COPD with frequent exacerbations - Single inhaler: Fluticasone/Umeclidinium/Vilanterol (Trelegy)

    4. Phosphodiesterase-4 (PDE-4) Inhibitors¶

    Roflumilast: - Oral anti-inflammatory - Indications: - Severe COPD (FEV₁ <50%) - Chronic bronchitis phenotype - Frequent exacerbations despite LABA/LAMA or ICS/LABA

    Benefits: - ↓ Exacerbations (Moderate effect) - Small ↑ FEV₁

    Side Effects: - Diarrhea, Nausea, Weight loss (Common) - Psychiatric effects (Depression)

    5. Theophylline¶

    Mechanism: Bronchodilation (PDE inhibitor, Adenosine antagonist)

    Role in COPD: - Third-line (If inadequate response to LABA/LAMA) - Limited use (Narrow therapeutic window, Side effects, Drug interactions)

    Side Effects: - Nausea, Arrhythmias, Seizures (Toxicity)

    Monitoring: Serum levels (Therapeutic: 10-20 μg/ml)

    6. Mucolytics¶

    N-Acetylcysteine, Carbocysteine: - Mechanism: Breaks disulfide bonds in mucus - Controversial benefit - May ↓ Exacerbations in selected patients (Chronic bronchitis, frequent exacerbations)

    Acute Exacerbation¶

    Definition¶

    Acute worsening of respiratory symptoms beyond normal day-to-day variation, requiring change in medication.

    Triggers¶

    "ICE" - Most Common: 

    I - Infection (50-80%) - Viral >> Bacterial
    • Viral: Rhinovirus, Influenza, RSV
    • Bacterial: H. influenzae, S. pneumoniae, M. catarrhalis
C - Cardiac (MI, CHF)
E - Environmental (Air pollution, Cold weather)

    Other: - Non-adherence to medications - Pulmonary embolism - Pneumothorax

    30%: No identifiable cause

    Clinical Features¶

    Symptoms: - ↑ Dyspnea (Cardinal symptom) - ↑ Sputum volume - ↑ Sputum purulence (Yellow/Green) - Cough - Wheeze, Chest tightness

    Anthonisen Criteria (For Bacterial Infection): 1. ↑ Dyspnea 2. ↑ Sputum volume 3. ↑ Sputum purulence

    Type I: All 3 → Antibiotics indicated Type II: 2 of 3 → Antibiotics usually given Type III: 1 of 3 + URTI → Antibiotics may not be needed

    Severity Assessment¶

    Mild: - Managed outpatient - ↑ SABA/SAMA use

    Moderate: - Outpatient or hospitalization - Antibiotics, Steroids

    Severe: - Hospitalization - Respiratory failure, ICU

    Management of Acute Exacerbation¶

    "ABCDEFG" 

    A - Assess severity (ABG, RR, HR, Mentation)
B - Bronchodilators (SABA + SAMA)
C - Corticosteroids (Systemic)
D - Deliver oxygen (Controlled)
E - Antibiotics (if indicated)
F - Fluids, Nutrition
G - Get ready for ventilation (if needed)

    1. Oxygen Therapy - CONTROLLED¶

    Target: SpO₂ 88-92% (NOT >92%)

    Why controlled: - Type 2 respiratory failure in COPD (Chronic CO₂ retainers) - Hypoxic drive: If give high O₂ → Loss of hypoxic respiratory drive → ↑ CO₂ retention → Respiratory acidosis, CO₂ narcosis

    Delivery: - Venturi mask (Fixed FiO₂ - Preferred) - Start 24-28% O₂ - Nasal cannula: 1-2 L/min

    Monitor: ABG (15-30 min after starting O₂)

    2. Bronchodilators¶

    Nebulized: - Salbutamol 2.5-5 mg + Ipratropium 0.5 mg every 4-6 hours (Or more frequent if severe)

    Better than MDI in acute setting (Ease of use, Delivery in distressed patient)

    3. Systemic Corticosteroids¶

    Indication: All exacerbations requiring hospitalization

    Regimen: - Prednisolone 40 mg PO daily × 5 days - OR IV Methylprednisolone if unable to take oral

    Benefits: - ↓ Recovery time - ↑ Lung function - ↓ Treatment failure - ↓ Length of stay

    Duration: 5 days (Longer courses NO additional benefit)

    4. Antibiotics¶

    Indications: - Purulent sputum (Type I or II exacerbation - Anthonisen criteria) - Mechanically ventilated

    Not needed if: Likely viral (Watery sputum, URTI symptoms)

    Choice: - First-line: - Amoxicillin-Clavulanate 625 mg TID × 5-7 days - Doxycycline 100 mg BD × 5-7 days - Azithromycin 500 mg OD × 3-5 days

    • Second-line (Risk factors - Previous exacerbations, Severe COPD):
    • Respiratory fluoroquinolone (Levofloxacin, Moxifloxacin)
    • Cephalosporins (Ceftriaxone)

    Duration: 5-7 days

    5. Ventilatory Support¶

    Non-Invasive Ventilation (NIV) - BiPAP:

    Indications: - Acute hypercapnic respiratory failure (pH <7.35, PaCO₂ >45 mmHg) - Despite optimal medical therapy

    Contraindications: - Respiratory arrest - Hemodynamic instability - Impaired consciousness (GCS <8) - Facial trauma - Unable to protect airway

    Benefits: - ↓ Intubation rate - ↓ Mortality - ↓ Length of stay

    Settings: IPAP 10-15 cm H₂O, EPAP 4-5 cm H₂O (Titrate)

    Invasive Mechanical Ventilation:

    Indications: - Respiratory arrest - Failed NIV - Severe acidosis (pH <7.25) - Severe hypoxemia despite O₂ - Impaired consciousness

    Complications: Prolonged weaning, Ventilator-associated pneumonia

    Complications¶

    1. Respiratory Failure¶

    Type 2 (Hypercapnic) Respiratory Failure: - Most common in COPD - PaO₂ <60 mmHg, PaCO₂ >50 mmHg - Chronic CO₂ retention → Compensated (↑ HCO₃⁻) - Acute on chronic → Decompensated (↓ pH)

    2. Cor Pulmonale (Right Heart Failure)¶

    Pathophysiology: 

    Chronic Hypoxemia
        ↓
Hypoxic Pulmonary Vasoconstriction
        ↓
Pulmonary Hypertension
        ↓
RV Pressure Overload
        ↓
RV Hypertrophy → RV Failure
        ↓
COR PULMONALE

    Clinical Features: - Elevated JVP - RV heave (Parasternal lift) - Loud P2 (Pulmonary HTN) - Hepatomegaly (Pulsatile), Peripheral edema, Ascites - Tricuspid regurgitation murmur

    Management: - LTOT (Most important) - Diuretics (Edema) - Treat underlying COPD

    3. Polycythemia¶

    Mechanism: Chronic hypoxemia → ↑ EPO → ↑ RBC production

    Complications: ↑ Blood viscosity → Thrombosis

    Management: LTOT

    4. Pneumothorax¶

    Spontaneous: Rupture of bullae (Emphysema)

    High index of suspicion if sudden worsening of dyspnea

    5. Pneumonia¶

    Common due to impaired clearance, Smoking

    6. Lung Cancer¶

    Smoking is common risk factor for both COPD and lung cancer

    Higher incidence in COPD patients

    NEET PG High-Yield Points¶

    🔥 MUST KNOW¶

    1. COPD Definition: Persistent airflow limitation, NOT fully reversible, Progressive

    2. Diagnosis: Post-bronchodilator FEV₁/FVC <0.70

    3. Most Important Risk Factor: Cigarette smoking (80-90%)

    4. Most Important Intervention: Smoking cessation (Only one that slows progression)

    5. Chronic Bronchitis Definition: Productive cough ≥3 months/year for ≥2 years

    6. Emphysema Definition: Alveolar destruction, Loss of elastic recoil

    7. Blue Bloater vs Pink Puffer:

    8. Blue Bloater: Chronic bronchitis, Cyanosis, Edema, ↑ PaCO₂

    9. Pink Puffer: Emphysema, Pursed-lip breathing, Cachexia, Normal/↓ PaCO₂

    10. α₁-Antitrypsin Deficiency:

    11. PiZZ genotype
    12. Early-onset emphysema (<40 years)
    13. Panacinar, Lower lobe predominance

    14. Liver cirrhosis

    15. GOLD Severity:

    16. GOLD 1: FEV₁ ≥80%
    17. GOLD 2: FEV₁ 50-79%
    18. GOLD 3: FEV₁ 30-49%

    19. GOLD 4: FEV₁ <30%

    20. Mainstay of Treatment: Long-acting bronchodilators (LABA, LAMA)

    21. ICS in COPD:

      • NOT first-line (Unlike asthma)
      • Add if frequent exacerbations or eosinophilia (≥300)
      • Side effect: ↑ Pneumonia risk

    22. LTOT (Long-Term Oxygen Therapy):

      • Indications: PaO₂ ≤55 mmHg OR PaO₂ 56-59 mmHg + Cor pulmonale/Polycythemia
      • ≥15 hours/day
      • ↓ Mortality (Only pharmacological intervention besides smoking cessation)

    23. Oxygen in COPD Exacerbation:

      • Controlled: Target SpO₂ 88-92% (NOT >92%)
      • Avoid high O₂: Risk of CO₂ retention

    24. Acute Exacerbation Management:

      • Oxygen (Controlled)
      • Bronchodilators: SABA + SAMA (Nebulized)
      • Steroids: Prednisolone 40 mg × 5 days
      • Antibiotics: If purulent sputum (Amoxicillin-Clavulanate, Doxycycline)

    25. NIV (BiPAP) Indications:

      • Acute hypercapnic respiratory failure (pH <7.35, PaCO₂ >45 mmHg)
      • ↓ Intubation, ↓ Mortality

    26. Cor Pulmonale: RV failure from chronic pulmonary HTN (Chronic hypoxemia)

    Clinical Scenarios¶

    Scenario 1: Chronic smoker, productive cough, post-bronchodilator FEV₁/FVC 0.65 → COPD

    Scenario 2: COPD patient, best intervention to slow progression → Smoking cessation

    Scenario 3: COPD patient with PaO₂ 52 mmHg, cor pulmonale → Start LTOT (≥15 hr/day)

    Scenario 4: COPD exacerbation, give oxygen, target SpO₂ → 88-92% (NOT >92%)

    Scenario 5: COPD exacerbation, purulent sputum → Amoxicillin-Clavulanate + Prednisolone

    Scenario 6: COPD exacerbation, pH 7.28, PaCO₂ 55 mmHg → NIV (BiPAP)

    Scenario 7: Young patient (<40y), emphysema, lower lobes, liver cirrhosis → α₁-Antitrypsin deficiency

    Scenario 8: COPD, Blue bloater phenotype → Chronic bronchitis (Cyanosis, Edema, ↑ PaCO₂)

    Quick Revision Checklist¶

    • COPD: Post-bronchodilator FEV₁/FVC <0.70
    • Smoking: Most important risk factor and intervention (cessation)
    • Chronic bronchitis: Productive cough ≥3 mo/yr × 2 yr
    • Blue bloater vs Pink puffer
    • α₁-Antitrypsin deficiency: Early, Panacinar, Lower lobe, Liver cirrhosis
    • GOLD grades: 1-4 based on FEV₁
    • Treatment: LABA/LAMA (Mainstay), ICS (NOT first-line)
    • LTOT: PaO₂ ≤55 mmHg, ≥15 hr/day, ↓ Mortality
    • Oxygen target: SpO₂ 88-92%
    • Exacerbation: Bronchodilators, Steroids (5 days), Antibiotics (if purulent)
    • NIV: pH <7.35, PaCO₂ >45 mmHg
    • Cor pulmonale: RV failure from pulmonary HTN

    Practice Questions¶

    Question 1¶

    A 60-year-old chronic smoker has dyspnea and chronic productive cough. Spirometry shows FEV₁ 55% predicted, post-bronchodilator FEV₁/FVC 0.62. What is the diagnosis and GOLD grade?

    A) Asthma, Moderate B) COPD, GOLD 2 C) COPD, GOLD 3 D) Bronchiectasis

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: B) COPD, GOLD 2** **Explanation:** - **Diagnosis:** COPD (Post-bronchodilator FEV₁/FVC <0.70) - **GOLD Grade:** - FEV₁ 55% predicted - **GOLD 2 (Moderate):** 50% ≤ FEV₁ <80% **Why not others:** - Asthma: Would have reversibility (≥12% AND ≥200 ml) - GOLD 3: FEV₁ 30-49% - Bronchiectasis: Different clinical picture (purulent sputum, imaging findings)

    Question 2¶

    What is the ONLY intervention proven to slow disease progression in COPD?

    A) Inhaled corticosteroids B) Long-acting bronchodilators C) Smoking cessation D) Long-term oxygen therapy

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: C) Smoking cessation** **Explanation:** - **Smoking cessation** is the **ONLY intervention** that **slows the rate of FEV₁ decline** in COPD - **Most important** intervention (Both prevention and treatment) **Other interventions:** - ICS, LABA/LAMA: Improve symptoms, ↓ exacerbations (Do NOT slow disease progression) - LTOT: ↓ Mortality (Does NOT slow FEV₁ decline)

    Question 3¶

    A COPD patient has PaO₂ 52 mmHg at rest with evidence of cor pulmonale. What is the most appropriate management?

    A) Inhaled corticosteroids B) Long-term oxygen therapy ≥15 hours/day C) Pulmonary rehabilitation D) Lung transplant

    Click to reveal answer **Answer: B) Long-term oxygen therapy ≥15 hours/day** **Explanation:** - **LTOT Indications:** - **PaO₂ ≤55 mmHg** at rest (Patient has 52 mmHg) ✓ - OR PaO₂ 56-59 mmHg + Cor pulmonale or Polycythemia ✓ - **Prescription:** ≥15 hours/day (More is better - Aim for 24 hr) - **Target:** SpO₂ 88-92% **Benefits:** - **↓ Mortality** (ONLY pharmacological intervention with this benefit besides smoking cessation) - ↓ Pulmonary HTN - ↓ Polycythemia
