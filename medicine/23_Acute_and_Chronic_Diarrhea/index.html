    Definition and Classification¶

    Definition¶

    Diarrhea: - Increase in stool frequency (≥3 stools/day) AND/OR - Decrease in stool consistency (loose/watery) AND/OR - Increase in stool weight (>200 g/day)

    Duration-Based Classification¶

    Acute Diarrhea: - <14 days duration - Usually infectious - Self-limited in most cases

    Persistent Diarrhea: - 14 days to 4 weeks - May be infectious or post-infectious

    Chronic Diarrhea: - >4 weeks duration - Usually non-infectious etiology - Requires systematic evaluation

    Pathophysiology¶

    Mechanisms of Diarrhea¶

    1. Secretory Diarrhea¶

    Mechanism: - Active secretion of electrolytes (Cl⁻) into lumen - Water follows osmotically - No osmotic gap

    Characteristics: - Large volume (>1 L/day) - Watery (like "rice water") - Persists with fasting (doesn't stop) - No blood or pus - Stool osmotic gap <50 mOsm/kg

    Calculation: 

    Stool Osmotic Gap = 290 - 2([Na⁺] + [K⁺])stool

<50 mOsm/kg = Secretory
>125 mOsm/kg = Osmotic
50-125 mOsm/kg = Mixed

    Causes: - Toxins: Cholera (CT), ETEC (LT/ST), Staph aureus (enterotoxin) - Hormones: VIPoma, gastrinoma, carcinoid - Bile acids: Ileal resection, bile acid malabsorption - Laxatives: Senna, bisacodyl - Villous adenoma - Medications: Colchicine, misoprostol

    2. Osmotic Diarrhea¶

    Mechanism: - Non-absorbed solutes in lumen - Water drawn into lumen osmotically - Osmotic gap present

    Characteristics: - Stops with fasting (KEY feature) - Moderate volume - No blood - Stool osmotic gap >125 mOsm/kg

    Causes: - Carbohydrate malabsorption: - Lactose intolerance (most common) - Fructose, sorbitol - Laxatives: Magnesium, lactulose, polyethylene glycol (PEG) - Maldigestion: Pancreatic insufficiency (steatorrhea)

    3. Inflammatory Diarrhea¶

    Mechanism: - Mucosal damage → disrupted absorption + inflammation - Exudation of blood, mucus, pus

    Characteristics: - Small, frequent stools - Blood and/or mucus (dysentery) - Fever, tenesmus - Fecal leukocytes/lactoferrin POSITIVE

    Causes: - Invasive infections: - Bacteria: Shigella, Salmonella, Campylobacter, C. diff, EHEC - Parasites: Entamoeba histolytica - IBD (Crohn's, UC) - Ischemic colitis - Radiation colitis

    4. Dysmotility (Altered Transit)¶

    Mechanism: - Rapid transit → decreased absorption time - OR Bacterial overgrowth (slow transit)

    Causes: - Rapid transit: - IBS-D (diarrhea-predominant) - Post-vagotomy - Hyperthyroidism - Diabetic neuropathy

    • Slow transit (bacterial overgrowth):
    • Scleroderma
    • Diabetes
    • Small bowel diverticula

    Pathophysiology Summary Table¶

    Type Volume Fasting Effect Blood/Mucus Osmotic Gap Examples

    Osmotic Moderate STOPS Absent >125 Lactose intolerance, Laxatives

    Acute Diarrhea¶

    Epidemiology¶

    • Most common cause: Viral gastroenteritis (60-70%)
    • Self-limited: 90% resolve in <7 days
    • Mortality: Mainly in extremes of age (dehydration)

    Etiology¶

    Viral (Most Common - 60-70%)¶

    1. Norovirus: - #1 cause of epidemic gastroenteritis - Outbreaks: Cruise ships, nursing homes, restaurants - Incubation: 24-48 hours - Presentation: - Acute onset nausea, vomiting (prominent) - Watery diarrhea - Cramping, low-grade fever - Duration: 1-3 days - Diagnosis: Clinical (testing rarely needed) - Management: Supportive

    2. Rotavirus: - Most common in children <5 years (pre-vaccine) - Vaccine available: Reduced incidence - Presentation: Vomiting, watery diarrhea, fever - Diagnosis: Stool antigen - Management: Oral rehydration

    3. Adenovirus (types 40, 41): - Children (long-lasting - up to 2 weeks)

    Bacterial¶

    A. Preformed Toxin (Rapid Onset - <6 hours):

    Staphylococcus aureus: - Incubation: 1-6 hours (shortest) - Source: Mayonnaise, cream pastries, food handlers - Toxin: Heat-stable enterotoxin (preformed) - Presentation: - Severe nausea, vomiting (predominant) - Watery diarrhea - NO fever - Duration: 12-24 hours (very short) - Management: Supportive

    Bacillus cereus: - 2 syndromes: - Emetic (1-6 hrs): Fried rice, vomiting predominant (preformed toxin) - Diarrheal (8-16 hrs): Meat, vegetables (in-vivo toxin) - Management: Supportive

    B. Secretory Toxin (Watery Diarrhea):

    Vibrio cholerae: - Cholera toxin (CT): Activates adenylate cyclase → ↑ cAMP → Cl⁻ secretion - Incubation: Hours to 5 days - Presentation: - Profuse watery diarrhea ("rice-water stools") - Massive volume loss (up to 1 L/hour) - Severe dehydration (hypovolemic shock) - NO fever, NO blood - Diagnosis: - Stool microscopy: Darting motility ("shooting star") - Stool culture: TCBS agar - Management: - Aggressive fluid repletion (IV/oral) - Antibiotics: Doxycycline (reduces duration, shedding) - Alternatives: Azithromycin, ciprofloxacin

    Enterotoxigenic E. coli (ETEC): - Traveler's diarrhea (#1 bacterial cause) - Heat-labile (LT) and heat-stable (ST) toxins - Presentation: Watery diarrhea, cramping, nausea - Management: - Prevention: Avoid tap water, raw food - Treatment: Supportive; Rifaximin (if severe)

    C. Invasive/Inflammatory (Bloody Diarrhea - Dysentery):

    Campylobacter jejuni: - Most common bacterial cause in developed countries - Source: Undercooked poultry, unpasteurized milk - Incubation: 2-5 days - Presentation: - Bloody diarrhea (colitis) - Fever, cramping - Fecal leukocytes POSITIVE - Complications: - Guillain-Barré syndrome (1-2 weeks post-infection) - Reactive arthritis - Diagnosis: Stool culture - Management: - Mild: Supportive - Severe/immunocompromised: Azithromycin or fluoroquinolone

    Salmonella (non-typhoidal): - Source: Poultry, eggs, reptiles (turtles, iguanas) - Presentation: - Watery or bloody diarrhea - Fever, cramping - Complications: - Bacteremia (especially sickle cell, HIV) - Reactive arthritis - Osteomyelitis (sickle cell - Salmonella #1) - Management: - Uncomplicated: NO antibiotics (prolongs carrier state) - Severe/high-risk: Ciprofloxacin or azithromycin - High-risk: <3 months, >50 years, immunocompromised, prosthetic valves

    Shigella: - Most virulent (low inoculum - 10-100 organisms) - Fecal-oral (person-to-person) - Presentation: - Bloody, mucoid diarrhea (dysentery) - High fever - Tenesmus - Complications: - Seizures (children - Shigella dysenteriae neurotoxin) - HUS (Shigella dysenteriae type 1 - Shiga toxin) - Reactive arthritis - Diagnosis: Stool culture - Management: - Antibiotics: Fluoroquinolone or azithromycin (reduces duration, transmission)

    Enterohemorrhagic E. coli (EHEC) - E. coli O157:H7: - Shiga toxin-producing E. coli (STEC) - Source: Undercooked beef, unpasteurized juice/milk - Presentation: - Bloody diarrhea (hemorrhagic colitis) - Severe cramping - NO fever (or low-grade) - Complications: - Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (HUS): - 5-10% of cases (especially children <5 years) - Triad: Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, acute kidney injury - Presents 5-7 days after diarrhea onset - Diagnosis: - Stool culture (sorbitol-negative on MacConkey) - Shiga toxin assay (more sensitive) - Management: - Supportive ONLY - NO antibiotics (may ↑ HUS risk by releasing more toxin) - NO antimotility agents - Monitor for HUS (CBC, creatinine)

    Clostridioides difficile (C. diff): - Antibiotic-associated diarrhea - Risk factors: - Recent antibiotics (within 8 weeks): - Highest risk: Clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, cephalosporins - Age >65, hospitalization, PPI use - Presentation: - Watery diarrhea (3-15 stools/day) - Lower abdominal cramping - Fever, leukocytosis - Fecal leukocytes POSITIVE - Complications: - Fulminant colitis: Toxic megacolon, perforation, shock - Diagnosis: - Stool PCR (toxin genes) OR - EIA (toxins A/B) - less sensitive - Colonoscopy: Pseudomembranes (yellow plaques) - Management: - Discontinue offending antibiotic (if possible) - Non-severe: - Vancomycin 125 mg PO QID × 10 days OR - Fidaxomicin 200 mg PO BID × 10 days (lower recurrence) - Severe (WBC >15,000, Cr ≥1.5× baseline): - Vancomycin 125 mg PO QID × 10 days - Fulminant (hypotension, shock, ileus, megacolon): - Vancomycin 500 mg PO QID + Metronidazole 500 mg IV TID - Consider Fecal microbiota transplant (FMT) - Surgery if perforation - Recurrent (≥2 episodes): - Fidaxomicin OR FMT (90% cure rate)

    D. Vibrio parahaemolyticus, Vibrio vulnificus: - Seafood (raw oysters) - Coastal exposure - V. vulnificus: Hemorrhagic bullae (especially cirrhosis, immunocompromised)

    Parasitic¶

    Giardia lamblia: - Most common parasitic cause in US - Transmission: Contaminated water (camping, daycare) - Incubation: 1-2 weeks - Presentation: - Watery, foul-smelling diarrhea - Bloating, flatulence - Steatorrhea (fat malabsorption) - Chronic if untreated - Diagnosis: - Stool antigen (ELISA) - most sensitive - Stool microscopy: Trophozoites ("falling leaf" motility), cysts - String test (Enterotest) - Management: - Metronidazole 250 mg TID × 5-7 days OR - Tinidazole 2 g single dose (preferred - single dose)

    Entamoeba histolytica: - Endemic: Developing countries, travelers - Presentation: - Bloody diarrhea (amoebic dysentery) - Flask-shaped ulcers (colonoscopy) - Complications: - Liver abscess ("anchovy paste" aspirate) - Diagnosis: - Stool microscopy: Trophozoites with ingested RBCs - Stool antigen (E. histolytica-specific) - Management: - Metronidazole 750 mg TID × 10 days - THEN Paromomycin (luminal agent to clear cysts)

    Cryptosporidium: - Immunocompromised (HIV with CD4 <100) - Waterborne (chlorine-resistant oocysts) - Presentation: - Watery diarrhea (profuse in immunocompromised) - Self-limited in immunocompetent - Diagnosis: Stool acid-fast stain (oocysts) - Management: - Immunocompetent: Supportive - Immunocompromised: Nitazoxanide + ART (restore immunity)

    Clinical Evaluation of Acute Diarrhea¶

    Red Flags (Require Workup):¶

    • Severe dehydration
    • Bloody diarrhea (dysentery)
    • Fever ≥38.5°C (101.3°F)
    • Severe abdominal pain
    • Duration >7 days
    • Age >70 years
    • Immunocompromised
    • Recent antibiotics (C. diff)
    • Recent hospitalization
    • Outbreak (multiple cases)

    Stool Studies (if red flags present):¶

    Routine: - Stool culture: Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter - C. diff testing: If recent antibiotics - Fecal leukocytes/lactoferrin: Inflammatory diarrhea

    Additional (based on exposure): - Ova and parasites: Travel, daycare, camping - Shiga toxin assay: Bloody diarrhea, no fever - Vibrio culture: Raw seafood, coastal exposure

    When NOT to Test:¶

    • Mild, watery diarrhea
    • No red flags
    • Duration <3 days
    • Likely viral

    Management of Acute Diarrhea¶

    1. Rehydration (Cornerstone)¶

    Oral Rehydration Solution (ORS): - First-line for mild-moderate dehydration - WHO-ORS formula: - Sodium 75 mEq/L - Glucose 75 mmol/L - Potassium 20 mEq/L - Chloride, citrate

    • Mechanism: Glucose-coupled Na⁺ absorption (SGLT1 - intact even in cholera)

    Indications for IV Fluids: - Severe dehydration - Shock - Altered mental status - Inability to tolerate oral (vomiting)

    Fluid Choice: - Isotonic saline or Ringer's lactate - Large volumes may be needed (cholera: 200 mL/kg in 24 hrs)

    2. Diet¶

    • Continue regular diet (especially in children)
    • BRAT diet (bananas, rice, applesauce, toast) - low fiber, bland
    • Avoid: Dairy (lactase deficiency - transient), high-fat, caffeine, alcohol

    3. Antimotility Agents¶

    Loperamide (Imodium): - Mechanism: μ-opioid agonist (slows transit, increases absorption) - Dosing: 4 mg initial, then 2 mg after each loose stool (max 16 mg/day) - Effective for non-inflammatory diarrhea

    Contraindications: - Bloody diarrhea (invasive pathogens) - High fever - C. diff - E. coli O157:H7 (↑ HUS risk) - Toxic megacolon risk

    Bismuth Subsalicylate (Pepto-Bismol): - Mild benefit - Black stools (bismuth sulfide - benign)

    4. Antibiotics¶

    Indications: - Traveler's diarrhea (empiric) - Severe disease: High fever, bloody diarrhea, signs of sepsis - Immunocompromised - Specific pathogens: - Shigella (always) - Campylobacter (if severe) - C. diff - Giardia - Cholera

    Empiric Regimen (Traveler's Diarrhea): - Rifaximin 200 mg TID × 3 days (non-invasive) - Ciprofloxacin 500 mg BID × 3 days (if invasive - fever, blood) - Azithromycin 1 g single dose OR 500 mg daily × 3 days - Preferred in SE Asia (fluoroquinolone resistance)

    Do NOT use antibiotics: - E. coli O157:H7 (↑ HUS risk) - Uncomplicated Salmonella (prolongs carrier state)

    5. Probiotics¶

    • Lactobacillus, Saccharomyces boulardii
    • Modest benefit: Reduce duration by ~1 day
    • Best evidence: Antibiotic-associated diarrhea prevention

    Chronic Diarrhea¶

    Definition¶

    • >4 weeks duration

    Approach by Mechanism¶

    Key Initial Questions: 1. Stool characteristics: Watery, fatty (steatorrhea), bloody/mucoid 2. Volume: Large (small bowel) vs small frequent (colon) 3. Fasting effect: Stops (osmotic) vs persists (secretory) 4. Associated symptoms: Weight loss, pain, urgency

    Classification by Stool Type¶

    1. Watery Diarrhea¶

    A. Secretory:

    Clinical Features: - Large volume (>1 L/day) - Persists with fasting - No blood/mucus - Nocturnal (wakes patient from sleep)

    Stool Analysis: - Osmotic gap <50 mOsm/kg - Fecal Na⁺ >70 mEq/L

    Causes:

    Medications/Laxatives: - Stimulant laxatives: Senna, bisacodyl - Colchicine - Metformin - SSRIs - Magnesium (if osmotic gap done on supernatant - Mg not measured)

    Bile Acid Diarrhea: - Ileal resection (>100 cm): - Bile acids enter colon → secretion - Ileal disease (Crohn's): - Decreased bile acid reabsorption - Idiopathic (bile acid malabsorption): - 25-30% of chronic diarrhea - Diagnosis: - SeHCAT scan (bile acid retention - not available in US) - Therapeutic trial: Cholestyramine (bile acid sequestrant) - Treatment: - Cholestyramine 4-16 g/day

    Endocrine:

    VIPoma (Verner-Morrison Syndrome): - Vasoactive Intestinal Peptide (VIP) secreting tumor - Presentation: - Massive watery diarrhea ("pancreatic cholera") - Hypokalemia (severe) - Achlorhydria (VIP inhibits gastric acid) - WDHA Syndrome: Watery Diarrhea, Hypokalemia, Achlorhydria - Diagnosis: - Elevated VIP (>200 pg/mL) - CT/MRI: Pancreatic mass - Treatment: - Octreotide (somatostatin analog) - Surgical resection

    Carcinoid Syndrome: - Serotonin-secreting neuroendocrine tumor - Presentation: - Watery diarrhea - Flushing (face, neck) - Wheezing (bronchoconstriction) - Right-sided heart disease (tricuspid regurgitation, pulmonary stenosis) - Diagnosis: - 24-hour urine 5-HIAA (serotonin metabolite) - >25 mg/24 hr - Chromogranin A (elevated) - Somatostatin receptor scintigraphy (Octreoscan) - Treatment: - Octreotide - Surgical resection

    Gastrinoma (Zollinger-Ellison Syndrome): - Covered in PUD topic - Diarrhea + PUD (multiple, refractory ulcers)

    Hyperthyroidism: - Increased motility - Associated symptoms: Weight loss, palpitations, tremor

    Addison's Disease: - Chronic diarrhea + hyperpigmentation, hypotension

    Microscopic Colitis: - Lymphocytic or Collagenous colitis - Presentation: - Chronic watery diarrhea (secretory) - Middle-aged/elderly women - Normal colonoscopy (MUST biopsy) - Associations: - Medications: NSAIDs, PPIs, SSRIs - Celiac disease, autoimmune conditions - Diagnosis: - Colonoscopy with biopsies (random biopsies even if normal-appearing) - Histology: - Lymphocytic: Intraepithelial lymphocytes (>20/100 epithelial cells) - Collagenous: Subepithelial collagen band (>10 μm) - Treatment: - Discontinue offending drug (NSAIDs) - Budesonide 9 mg daily (first-line) - Loperamide (symptom control) - Bismuth subsalicylate

    Villous Adenoma (Large): - Secretes K⁺-rich mucus → hypokalemia

    B. Osmotic:

    Clinical Features: - Stops with fasting (KEY) - Moderate volume - Improves with removal of offending agent

    Stool Analysis: - Osmotic gap >125 mOsm/kg

    Causes:

    Carbohydrate Malabsorption:

    Lactose Intolerance: - Primary: Lactase deficiency (genetic - common in Asians, Africans, Native Americans) - Secondary: Post-gastroenteritis, celiac disease, Crohn's - Presentation: - Diarrhea, bloating, cramping after dairy - Diagnosis: - Hydrogen breath test (lactose given → H₂ production by colonic bacteria) - Trial lactose-free diet - Treatment: - Lactose-free diet - Lactase supplements (Lactaid)

    Fructose, Sorbitol: - Fruits, artificial sweeteners - Diagnosis: Trial elimination diet

    Laxatives: - Magnesium (Milk of Magnesia) - Lactulose (hepatic encephalopathy) - PEG (MiraLAX)

    Maldigestion:

    Pancreatic Insufficiency: - Covered in detail below (Fatty Diarrhea - Steatorrhea) - Osmotic component: Undigested nutrients

    2. Fatty Diarrhea (Steatorrhea)¶

    Clinical Features: - Bulky, greasy, foul-smelling stools - Float (high fat content) - Difficult to flush - Weight loss (fat-soluble vitamin deficiency)

    Quantification: - Fecal fat >7 g/day (72-hour fecal fat collection on 100 g fat diet) - Sudan stain: Quick bedside test (fat globules)

    Pathophysiology: - Maldigestion: Defective intraluminal breakdown (pancreatic/bile) - Malabsorption: Defective mucosal uptake (small bowel disease)

    A. Maldigestion (Intraluminal):

    Pancreatic Insufficiency:

    Causes: - Chronic pancreatitis (most common) - Cystic fibrosis - Pancreatic cancer (obstructing duct) - Pancreatic resection - Zollinger-Ellison (inactivates pancreatic enzymes)

    Clinical Features: - Steatorrhea (>90% exocrine function lost) - Fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (A, D, E, K) - Diabetes (endocrine insufficiency) - History of pancreatitis, alcohol

    Diagnosis: - Fecal elastase-1: <200 μg/g stool (low = pancreatic insufficiency) - Specific but low sensitivity - 72-hour fecal fat: >7 g/day (gold standard) - Secretin stimulation test: Research - Imaging: CT/MRI (pancreatic calcifications, atrophy, duct dilation)

    Treatment: - Pancreatic enzyme replacement (PERT): - Pancrelipase (Creon, Zenpep) - 40,000-50,000 units lipase with each meal - 25,000 units with snacks - Take with meals - PPI: If inadequate response (↑ duodenal pH for enzyme activity) - Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) - Monitor: Weight, stool frequency, fat-soluble vitamins

    Bile Acid Deficiency:

    Causes: - Cholestasis: PBC, PSC, biliary obstruction - Ileal resection (>100 cm) or disease - Bacterial overgrowth (deconjugates bile acids)

    Diagnosis: - Elevated bilirubin, ALP (cholestasis) - Low fecal elastase (if coexisting pancreatic insufficiency)

    Treatment: - Treat underlying cause - Medium-chain triglycerides (MCT): Don't require bile acids for absorption - Fat-soluble vitamins

    B. Malabsorption (Mucosal):

    Celiac Disease:

    Definition: - Autoimmune enteropathy triggered by gluten (wheat, barley, rye) - HLA-DQ2 (90%) or HLA-DQ8 (10%)

    Presentation: - Diarrhea, steatorrhea - Weight loss - Bloating, flatulence - Anemia (iron, folate deficiency) - Osteoporosis (vitamin D, calcium malabsorption) - Dermatitis herpetiformis: Pruritic vesicles (extensor surfaces)

    Diagnosis:

    Serology (on gluten-containing diet): - IgA anti-tissue transglutaminase (tTG): Most sensitive/specific - IgA anti-endomysial antibody (EMA): Highly specific - Total IgA: Rule out IgA deficiency (5% of celiac) - If IgA deficient: Use IgG-based tests (IgG-tTG, IgG-DGP)

    Endoscopy: - Duodenal biopsy (gold standard): - Villous atrophy - Crypt hyperplasia - Intraepithelial lymphocytosis (>25/100 epithelial cells) - Marsh Classification: Grade severity

    Additional Tests: - HLA-DQ2/DQ8: High negative predictive value (if negative, celiac unlikely) - Labs: Iron, folate, vitamin D, calcium

    Treatment: - Lifelong gluten-free diet (ONLY treatment) - Serologic follow-up: tTG should normalize in 6-12 months - Endoscopic follow-up: If symptoms persist despite diet - Complications: - Enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma (EATL): Rare (if non-adherent) - Refractory celiac disease: Consider steroids, immunosuppressants

    Tropical Sprue: - Endemic: Tropical regions (Caribbean, India, Southeast Asia) - Presentation: Steatorrhea, folate/B12 deficiency, megaloblastic anemia - Diagnosis: Duodenal biopsy (villous atrophy - similar to celiac) - Treatment: Tetracycline + Folate × 6 months

    Whipple's Disease: - Tropheryma whipplei (bacteria) - Presentation: - Steatorrhea, weight loss - Migratory arthritis (precedes GI symptoms) - Neurologic symptoms (oculomasticatory myorhythmia) - Fever, lymphadenopathy - Diagnosis: - Duodenal biopsy: PAS-positive macrophages (foamy) - PCR for T. whipplei - Treatment: Ceftriaxone × 2 weeks → TMP-SMX × 1 year

    Small Intestinal Bacterial Overgrowth (SIBO):

    Definition: - Excessive bacteria (>10⁵ CFU/mL) in small bowel

    Risk Factors: - Anatomic: Blind loops, strictures, diverticula, post-surgical (Billroth II) - Motility disorders: Scleroderma, diabetes, chronic opioids - Achlorhydria: PPI, atrophic gastritis - Immune deficiency: IgA deficiency

    Presentation: - Steatorrhea (bile acid deconjugation) - Bloating, flatulence (bacterial fermentation) - Vitamin B12 deficiency (bacteria consume B12) - Fat-soluble vitamin deficiency

    Diagnosis: - Glucose or lactulose breath test: - Early H₂ rise (within 90 minutes) = SIBO - Small bowel aspirate and culture: Gold standard (invasive, not routine) - Therapeutic trial: Response to antibiotics

    Treatment: - Antibiotics: - Rifaximin 550 mg TID × 14 days - Metronidazole or Ciprofloxacin - Rotate antibiotics if recurrent (cyclic therapy) - Treat underlying cause: Correct strictures, optimize motility - Probiotics: Controversial

    Crohn's Disease: - Terminal ileum involvement → bile acid malabsorption, B12 deficiency - Extensive small bowel disease → malabsorption

    Short Bowel Syndrome: - Extensive resection (>200 cm small bowel removed) - Presentation: Steatorrhea, malabsorption, dehydration - Management: - Nutritional support (TPN initially) - High-calorie diet, small frequent meals - Loperamide (slow transit) - Octreotide (reduce secretions) - Teduglutide (GLP-2 analog - promotes adaptation)

    3. Inflammatory Diarrhea (Bloody/Mucoid)¶

    Clinical Features: - Blood and/or mucus - Small, frequent stools - Urgency, tenesmus - Fecal leukocytes POSITIVE

    Causes:

    Inflammatory Bowel Disease (IBD): - Ulcerative Colitis - Crohn's Disease - Covered in IBD topic

    Chronic Infections (Immunocompromised): - CMV colitis (HIV, transplant) - Mycobacterium avium complex (MAC) - Chronic parasites: Entamoeba

    Ischemic Colitis: - Elderly, vascular disease - Presentation: Abrupt bloody diarrhea, LLQ pain (splenic flexure) - Diagnosis: Colonoscopy (thumbprinting, segmental distribution) - Management: Supportive (most resolve); surgery if gangrene

    Radiation Colitis: - History of pelvic radiation - Chronic bloody diarrhea - Telangiectasias (colonoscopy)

    Colon Cancer: - Change in bowel habits - Hematochezia - Weight loss, anemia

    4. Dysmotility¶

    Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS):

    Rome IV Criteria: - Recurrent abdominal pain ≥1 day/week (last 3 months) associated with ≥2: 1. Related to defecation 2. Change in stool frequency 3. Change in stool form

    Subtypes: - IBS-D (diarrhea-predominant) - IBS-C (constipation-predominant) - IBS-M (mixed)

    Diagnosis of Exclusion: - Red flags (exclude organic disease): - Age >50 (no prior screening) - Alarm symptoms: Weight loss, blood, anemia, fever, family history of colon cancer/IBD - Nocturnal symptoms

    Workup: - Minimal if no red flags: - CBC, CRP, fecal calprotectin (rule out IBD) - Celiac serology (tTG) - Thyroid function

    Management: - Lifestyle: Low-FODMAP diet, stress management - Medications: - IBS-D: - Loperamide (first-line) - Rifaximin (antibiotic - reduces bloating) - Eluxadoline (μ-opioid agonist) - Alosetron (5-HT3 antagonist - women only, ischemic colitis risk) - Antispasmodics: Dicyclomine, hyoscyamine - Antidepressants: TCAs (low-dose), SSRIs

    Hyperthyroidism: - Increased motility - Diarrhea + weight loss, palpitations, tremor - Diagnosis: Low TSH, elevated T4

    Diabetic Autonomic Neuropathy: - Nocturnal diarrhea (alternating with constipation) - Other autonomic symptoms: Orthostatic hypotension, gastroparesis

    Diagnostic Approach to Chronic Diarrhea¶

    Step 1: History and Exam¶

    Key Questions: 1. Duration: Acute vs chronic 2. Stool characteristics: Watery, fatty, bloody 3. Volume: Large (small bowel) vs small frequent (colon) 4. Timing: Nocturnal, postprandial, fasting 5. Associated symptoms: Weight loss, pain, fever 6. Diet: Dairy, sorbitol, caffeine 7. Medications: Laxatives, antibiotics, metformin, PPIs 8. Exposures: Travel, well water, raw food 9. Past history: Surgery (bowel resection), radiation 10. Family history: IBD, celiac, colon cancer

    Step 2: Initial Laboratory Studies¶

    Basic Labs: - CBC: Anemia (iron, B12, folate deficiency; IBD) - CMP: Electrolytes (hypokalemia - secretory), albumin (malnutrition) - TSH: Hyperthyroidism - Celiac serology: IgA-tTG (if watery or fatty diarrhea)

    Stool Studies: - Fecal calprotectin: IBD vs IBS (>250 suggests IBD) - Fecal leukocytes/lactoferrin: Inflammatory - Stool culture, O&P: If infectious suspected - C. diff testing: If recent antibiotics

    72-Hour Fecal Fat: - If steatorrhea suspected - >7 g/day abnormal (on 100 g fat diet)

    Stool Osmotic Gap: - If watery diarrhea: - <50: Secretory - >125: Osmotic

    Step 3: Endoscopy¶

    Colonoscopy with Biopsies: - Indications: - Age >50 (cancer screening due) - Alarm symptoms: Blood, weight loss, anemia - Persistent diarrhea without clear cause - Elevated fecal calprotectin

    • Obtain biopsies even if normal-appearing (microscopic colitis, celiac)

    Upper Endoscopy (EGD) with Duodenal Biopsies: - Indications: - Steatorrhea (celiac, Whipple's, tropical sprue, giardia) - Positive celiac serology - Unexplained malabsorption

    Step 4: Specialized Testing¶

    If Secretory Diarrhea (after excluding meds/laxatives): - Serum VIP: VIPoma - 24-hour urine 5-HIAA: Carcinoid - Serum gastrin: Gastrinoma - Serum calcitonin: Medullary thyroid cancer

    If Steatorrhea (after EGD/colonoscopy): - Fecal elastase: Pancreatic insufficiency - Secretin stimulation test: Pancreatic function (rarely done) - CT/MRCP: Pancreatic imaging

    If Osmotic Diarrhea: - Lactose hydrogen breath test: Lactose intolerance - Trial elimination: Fructose, sorbitol

    If Suspected SIBO: - Glucose/lactulose breath test

    Management¶

    General Principles¶

    1. Treat Underlying Cause (most important)

    2. Symptomatic Management:

    Antidiarrheals: - Loperamide: 2-4 mg PRN (max 16 mg/day) - Contraindicated: Bloody diarrhea, C. diff, severe IBD

    • Diphenoxylate-atropine (Lomotil): Alternative

    Bile Acid Sequestrants: - Cholestyramine 4-16 g/day (bile acid diarrhea)

    Dietary Modifications: - Lactose-free (lactose intolerance) - Gluten-free (celiac) - Low-FODMAP (IBS) - Low-fat (steatorrhea - if enzyme replacement inadequate)

    Probiotics: - Lactobacillus, Saccharomyces boulardii - Modest benefit

    Special Situations¶

    Traveler's Diarrhea¶

    Definition: - ≥3 unformed stools in 24 hours during or shortly after travel

    Etiology: - Bacterial (80%): ETEC (#1), Campylobacter, Salmonella, Shigella - Viral (10%): Norovirus - Parasitic (10%): Giardia, Cryptosporidium

    Risk Factors: - Destination: High-risk (Latin America, Africa, Asia) - Food/water: Tap water, ice, raw vegetables, street food

    Prevention: - "Boil it, cook it, peel it, or forget it" - Avoid tap water, ice, raw produce - Bismuth subsalicylate 525 mg QID (50% reduction) - Rifaximin 200 mg daily (prophylaxis - expensive)

    Management:

    Mild (1-2 stools, no symptoms): - Loperamide 4 mg initial, 2 mg after each stool (max 16 mg/day) - Bismuth subsalicylate 525 mg q30 min × 8 doses

    Moderate-Severe (≥3 stools, cramps, fever): - Antibiotics: - Rifaximin 200 mg TID × 3 days (non-invasive - no fever/blood) - Ciprofloxacin 500 mg BID × 3 days (invasive) - Azithromycin 1 g single dose (SE Asia - fluoroquinolone resistance; Campylobacter)

    • Loperamide + Antibiotic: Faster symptom relief

    When to Seek Care: - High fever, bloody diarrhea - No improvement in 48 hours - Severe dehydration

    Antibiotic-Associated Diarrhea (AAD)¶

    Definition: - Diarrhea during or within 8 weeks of antibiotic use

    Etiology: - Non-C. diff (75-80%): Altered microbiome, osmotic effect - C. diff (20-25%): Covered above

    Management: - Discontinue antibiotic (if possible) - Probiotics: Lactobacillus, S. boulardii (may reduce risk) - Test for C. diff if: - Severe/persistent (>3 days) - Fever, leukocytosis - Bloody diarrhea

    HIV-Associated Diarrhea¶

    Common Causes (by CD4 count):

    CD4 >200: - Medication side effects (ARVs) - Bacterial (Salmonella, Campylobacter, Shigella, C. diff) - IBS

    CD4 <200: - Cryptosporidium (watery, profuse) - Microsporidia - Isospora - Giardia

    CD4 <100: - CMV colitis (bloody diarrhea, ulcers on colonoscopy) - Mycobacterium avium complex (MAC): Fever, weight loss

    Diagnosis: - Stool studies: Culture, O&P (with modified acid-fast for Cryptosporidium, Isospora) - Colonoscopy: CMV (biopsy - inclusion bodies), MAC

    Management: - Antiretroviral therapy (ART): Immune reconstitution (CRITICAL) - Specific therapy: - Cryptosporidium: Nitazoxanide (if CD4 >100); ART (if CD4 <100) - CMV: Ganciclovir - MAC: Clarithromycin + Ethambutol

    Factitious Diarrhea (Laxative Abuse)¶

    Suspect: - Young women (eating disorders common) - Healthcare workers - Secretory pattern (osmotic gap <50) BUT diarrhea stops with observation - Hypokalemia, metabolic alkalosis - No weight loss (despite chronic diarrhea)

    Diagnosis: - Stool/urine laxative screen: - Bisacodyl, senna, aloe - Magnesium (elevated stool Mg²⁺ if not measured in osmotic gap) - Colonoscopy: Melanosis coli (anthracene laxatives - dark pigmentation)

    Management: - Psychiatric evaluation - Treat eating disorder

    High-Yield Points¶

    Acute Diarrhea Pearls¶

    1. Shortest incubation: Staph aureus (1-6 hours - preformed toxin)
    2. "Rice-water stools": Cholera (profuse watery - up to 1 L/hour)
    3. Most common traveler's diarrhea: ETEC
    4. Most common bacterial (developed countries): Campylobacter
    5. Post-infectious GBS: Campylobacter jejuni
    6. Most virulent (lowest inoculum): Shigella (10-100 organisms)
    7. E. coli O157:H7:
    8. NO antibiotics (↑ HUS risk)
    9. NO fever (unlike other bacterial dysentery)
    10. Sorbitol-negative
    11. C. diff:
    12. Discontinue offending antibiotic
    13. Vancomycin 125 mg PO QID (first-line)
    14. Recurrent: Fidaxomicin or FMT (90% cure)

    Chronic Diarrhea Pearls¶

    Osmotic vs Secretory: - Stops with fasting = Osmotic - Persists with fasting = Secretory - Nocturnal (wakes from sleep) = Organic (secretory, IBD) - NOT IBS

    Stool Osmotic Gap: 

    Gap = 290 - 2([Na⁺] + [K⁺])

<50 = Secretory
>125 = Osmotic

    Steatorrhea Clues: - Bulky, greasy, foul-smelling, floating - Weight loss - Fat-soluble vitamin deficiency (A, D, E, K)

    Celiac Disease: - IgA-tTG (best screening) - Check total IgA (5% deficient → use IgG-based tests) - Duodenal biopsy: Villous atrophy (gold standard) - Treatment: Gluten-free diet (lifelong) - Complication: Enteropathy-associated T-cell lymphoma

    Pancreatic Insufficiency: - Fecal elastase <200 μg/g - Steatorrhea when >90% exocrine function lost - Treatment: Pancrelipase 40,000-50,000 units lipase with meals

    Microscopic Colitis: - Normal colonoscopy (MUST biopsy) - Watery diarrhea (middle-aged/elderly women) - Associations: NSAIDs, PPIs, celiac - Histology: - Lymphocytic: Intraepithelial lymphocytes - Collagenous: Subepithelial collagen band - Treatment: Budesonide 9 mg daily

    Secretory Diarrhea Endocrine Causes: - VIPoma: WDHA (Watery Diarrhea, Hypokalemia, Achlorhydria) - Carcinoid: Diarrhea + Flushing + Wheezing + Right heart disease - Diagnosis: 24-hour urine 5-HIAA - Gastrinoma: Diarrhea + PUD (multiple, refractory ulcers)

    Bile Acid Diarrhea: - Ileal resection >100 cm or ileal Crohn's - Diagnosis: Therapeutic trial with cholestyramine - Treatment: Cholestyramine 4-16 g/day

    SIBO: - Risk factors: Blind loops, scleroderma, diabetes, PPI - Presentation: Steatorrhea, bloating, B12 deficiency - Diagnosis: Breath test (early H₂ rise) - Treatment: Rifaximin 550 mg TID × 14 days

    IBS: - Rome IV: Recurrent abdominal pain ≥1 day/week × 3 months + ≥2: - Related to defecation - Change in frequency - Change in form - Red flags (exclude IBS): - Age >50, weight loss, blood, anemia, nocturnal symptoms, family history IBD/cancer

    Management Pearls¶

    ORS: - Glucose-coupled Na⁺ absorption (SGLT1) - Works even in cholera (secretory)

    Loperamide Contraindications: - Bloody diarrhea (invasive pathogens) - C. diff - E. coli O157:H7 (↑ HUS risk) - Toxic megacolon risk

    Traveler's Diarrhea: - Prevention: "Boil, cook, peel, or forget it" - Empiric antibiotics: - Rifaximin (non-invasive - no fever/blood) - Azithromycin (SE Asia - Campylobacter) - Loperamide + antibiotic: Fastest relief

    Antibiotic Use - When NOT to Use: - E. coli O157:H7: ↑ HUS risk - Uncomplicated Salmonella: Prolongs carrier state

    Practice MCQs¶

    Question 1¶

    A 30-year-old woman develops sudden onset of severe nausea and vomiting 3 hours after eating mayonnaise-based potato salad at a picnic. She has watery diarrhea but no fever. What is the MOST likely causative organism?

    A) Salmonella B) Staphylococcus aureus C) Campylobacter jejuni D) Norovirus

    Answer: B) Staphylococcus aureus

    Explanation: - Staphylococcus aureus food poisoning: - Shortest incubation: 1-6 hours (preformed toxin) - Source: Mayonnaise, cream pastries, food handlers - Presentation: - Severe nausea, vomiting (predominant) - Watery diarrhea - NO fever - Duration: 12-24 hours (very short) - Management: Supportive

    • Other options:
    • Salmonella: Longer incubation (6-72 hrs), fever common
    • Campylobacter: Incubation 2-5 days, bloody diarrhea, fever
    • Norovirus: Incubation 24-48 hrs (longer than 3 hrs)

    Question 2¶

    A 25-year-old man returns from Mexico with 4 days of watery diarrhea (6-8 stools/day), cramping, and low-grade fever. He has no blood in stools. What is the MOST appropriate empiric treatment?

    A) Loperamide only B) Rifaximin C) Ciprofloxacin D) Metronidazole

    Answer: C) Ciprofloxacin

    Explanation: - Traveler's diarrhea with fever (suggests invasive pathogen): - Empiric antibiotics: - Ciprofloxacin 500 mg BID × 3 days (invasive - fever present) - Azithromycin 1 g single dose (alternative; preferred in SE Asia)

    • Rifaximin is for:

      • Non-invasive (NO fever, NO blood)
      • Not effective against Campylobacter, Shigella

    • Loperamide alone: Inadequate if fever present (suggests bacterial)

    • Metronidazole: For Giardia, Entamoeba (not first-line empiric)

    • Can add loperamide to antibiotic for faster symptom relief

    Question 3¶

    A 70-year-old woman with chronic watery diarrhea (8 stools/day) for 6 months undergoes colonoscopy, which appears completely normal. Random biopsies are taken. Histology shows a thickened subepithelial collagen band (15 μm). What is the diagnosis and first-line treatment?

    A) Lymphocytic colitis; budesonide B) Collagenous colitis; budesonide C) Ulcerative colitis; mesalamine D) Ischemic colitis; supportive care

    Answer: B) Collagenous colitis; budesonide

    Explanation: - Collagenous colitis (type of microscopic colitis): - Chronic watery diarrhea (secretory) - Middle-aged/elderly women - Normal colonoscopy (MUST biopsy) - Histology: Subepithelial collagen band >10 μm (this patient has 15 μm)

    • Lymphocytic colitis (other type of microscopic colitis):
    • Intraepithelial lymphocytes >20/100 epithelial cells

    • NO collagen band thickening

    • Treatment:

    • Budesonide 9 mg daily (first-line)
    • Discontinue NSAIDs/PPIs (if taking)

    • Loperamide (symptom control)

    • Associations: NSAIDs, PPIs, SSRIs, celiac disease

    Question 4¶

    A 35-year-old man with chronic pancreatitis presents with bulky, greasy, foul-smelling stools that float and are difficult to flush. Which test will MOST likely be abnormal?

    A) Stool osmotic gap B) Fecal elastase C) Fecal calprotectin D) Lactose hydrogen breath test

    Answer: B) Fecal elastase

    Explanation: - Steatorrhea (fatty diarrhea): - Bulky, greasy, foul-smelling, floating - Chronic pancreatitis → pancreatic insufficiency

    • Fecal elastase:
    • <200 μg/g = pancreatic insufficiency
    • Specific for pancreatic function

    • Will be LOW in this patient

    • Other tests:

    • 72-hour fecal fat: Gold standard for steatorrhea (>7 g/day)
    • Fecal calprotectin: Elevated in IBD (inflammatory), NOT maldigestion
    • Stool osmotic gap: For watery diarrhea (osmotic vs secretory)

    • Lactose breath test: Lactose intolerance

    • Treatment:

    • Pancrelipase (pancreatic enzyme replacement)
    • 40,000-50,000 units lipase with each meal
    • Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K)

    Question 5¶

    A 28-year-old woman with chronic watery diarrhea has extensive workup including normal colonoscopy with biopsies, normal celiac serology, and stool studies. Stool osmotic gap is calculated as 35 mOsm/kg. Diarrhea persists despite 48-hour fast. Which endocrine cause should be investigated?

    A) Hyperthyroidism B) VIPoma C) Carcinoid syndrome D) Addison's disease

    Answer: B) VIPoma

    Explanation: - Secretory diarrhea: - Osmotic gap <50 (this patient: 35) - Persists with fasting (key feature)

    • VIPoma (Verner-Morrison syndrome):
    • Vasoactive Intestinal Peptide (VIP) secreting tumor
    • WDHA Syndrome:
      • **W**atery diarrhea (massive - "pancreatic cholera")
      • **H**ypokalemia (severe)
      • **A**chlorhydria (VIP inhibits gastric acid)
    • Diagnosis:
      • Elevated VIP (>200 pg/mL)
      • CT/MRI: Pancreatic mass

    • Treatment: Octreotide, surgical resection

    • Other options:

    • Hyperthyroidism: Dysmotility (increased motility), NOT pure secretory
    • Carcinoid: Secretory diarrhea BUT also has flushing, wheezing, right heart disease
      • Diagnosis: 24-hour urine 5-HIAA

    • Addison's: Can cause chronic diarrhea, but secretory pattern less common

    • Secretory diarrhea endocrine workup:

    • Serum VIP
    • 24-hour urine 5-HIAA
    • Serum gastrin (gastrinoma)
    • Serum calcitonin (medullary thyroid cancer)

    Question 6¶

    A 40-year-old man develops diarrhea (8-10 watery stools/day), cramping, and fever while hospitalized and receiving IV ceftriaxone for pneumonia. Which test should be ordered FIRST?

    A) Stool culture B) C. difficile PCR C) Fecal calprotectin D) Colonoscopy

    Answer: B) C. difficile PCR

    Explanation: - Antibiotic-associated diarrhea: - Recent antibiotics (within 8 weeks) - Hospitalized - Watery diarrhea, cramping, fever

    • C. difficile colitis:
    • High-risk antibiotics: Clindamycin, fluoroquinolones, cephalosporins (patient on ceftriaxone)

    • Test: Stool PCR (most sensitive) OR EIA (toxins A/B)

    • Management:

    • Discontinue ceftriaxone (if possible)

    • Vancomycin 125 mg PO QID × 10 days

    • Other tests:

    • Stool culture: For other bacterial pathogens (lower yield in hospitalized, not first-line)
    • Fecal calprotectin: Inflammatory marker (not specific)
    • Colonoscopy: If diagnosis unclear or concern for severe/fulminant (pseudomembranes)

    Question 7¶

    A 22-year-old woman with chronic diarrhea, bloating, and flatulence reports symptoms worsen after eating bread and pasta. Serology shows elevated IgA anti-tissue transglutaminase. What is the NEXT best step?

    A) Start gluten-free diet immediately B) Upper endoscopy with duodenal biopsies C) HLA-DQ2/DQ8 testing D) Repeat serology in 3 months

    Answer: B) Upper endoscopy with duodenal biopsies

    Explanation: - Suspected celiac disease: - Elevated IgA-tTG (highly sensitive/specific) - Symptoms with gluten

    • Diagnosis requires:
    • Positive serology (IgA-tTG or EMA) AND

    • Duodenal biopsy confirmation (gold standard)

    • EGD with duodenal biopsies (BEFORE starting gluten-free diet):

    • Villous atrophy
    • Crypt hyperplasia

    • Intraepithelial lymphocytosis

    • MUST be done on gluten-containing diet:

    • Starting gluten-free diet before biopsy → false-negative

    • HLA-DQ2/DQ8:

    • High negative predictive value (if negative, celiac very unlikely)
    • NOT needed for diagnosis if serology positive

    • Used if diagnosis unclear

    • Treatment: Lifelong gluten-free diet (AFTER biopsy confirmation)

    Question 8¶

    A 50-year-old man with Crohn's disease who underwent 120 cm ileal resection 5 years ago presents with chronic watery diarrhea. Which medication is MOST likely to improve his symptoms?

    A) Loperamide B) Cholestyramine C) Pancrelipase D) Mesalamine

    Answer: B) Cholestyramine

    Explanation: - Ileal resection >100 cm: - Bile acid malabsorption - Bile acids enter colon → stimulate Cl⁻ secretion → watery diarrhea

    • Cholestyramine (bile acid sequestrant):
    • Binds bile acids in colon
    • Dose: 4-16 g/day

    • First-line for bile acid diarrhea

    • Loperamide: Symptomatic (slows motility) but doesn't address cause

    • Pancrelipase: For pancreatic insufficiency (steatorrhea, not bile acid diarrhea)

    • Mesalamine: NOT effective in Crohn's disease

    • If resection <100 cm:

    • Bile acid malabsorption still occurs but liver can compensate
    • If >100 cm: Bile acid pool depleted → steatorrhea (fat malabsorption)
    • May need low-fat diet + fat-soluble vitamins

    Question 9¶

    A 55-year-old woman with diabetes and scleroderma has chronic diarrhea, bloating, and weight loss. Stool shows increased fat. Fecal elastase is normal. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?

    A) Pancreatic insufficiency B) Celiac disease C) Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO) D) Bile acid malabsorption

    Answer: C) Small intestinal bacterial overgrowth (SIBO)

    Explanation: - SIBO risk factors: - Scleroderma (dysmotility - slowed transit) - Diabetes (autonomic neuropathy)

    • Presentation:
    • Steatorrhea (bile acid deconjugation by bacteria)
    • Bloating, flatulence (bacterial fermentation)

    • Weight loss (malabsorption)

    • Fecal elastase NORMAL:

    • Rules out pancreatic insufficiency

    • Diagnosis:

    • Glucose or lactulose breath test (early H₂ rise)

    • Small bowel aspirate (gold standard, invasive)

    • Treatment:

    • Rifaximin 550 mg TID × 14 days

    • Rotate antibiotics if recurrent

    • Other causes ruled out:

    • Pancreatic insufficiency: Fecal elastase would be LOW (<200)
    • Celiac: Possible, but risk factors here point to SIBO
    • Bile acid malabsorption: Unlikely with scleroderma/diabetes

    Question 10¶

    A 7-year-old child develops watery diarrhea 2 days after eating undercooked hamburger. On day 5, she develops pallor, petechiae, and decreased urine output. Labs show hemoglobin 7 g/dL, platelets 40,000/μL, creatinine 3.5 mg/dL, and schistocytes on blood smear. What is the MOST likely complication?

    A) Guillain-Barré syndrome B) Reactive arthritis C) Hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS) D) Immune thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP)

    Answer: C) Hemolytic uremic syndrome (HUS)

    Explanation: - E. coli O157:H7 (EHEC): - Source: Undercooked beef - Shiga toxin-producing E. coli (STEC)

    • Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome (HUS):

    • Classic triad:

      1. Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia (schistocytes, low Hgb)
      2. Thrombocytopenia (low platelets)
      3. Acute kidney injury (elevated Cr, oliguria)

    • Timing: 5-7 days after diarrhea onset

    • Most common in children <5 years (10-15% of O157:H7 cases)

    • Management:

    • Supportive care: Fluids, electrolytes, transfusions, dialysis
    • NO antibiotics (may worsen by releasing more toxin)
    • NO antimotility agents

    • NO platelets (unless life-threatening bleeding)

    • Prognosis:

    • Most recover with supportive care
    • 3-5% mortality

    • 10-25% develop chronic kidney disease

    • Other complications:

    • GBS: Campylobacter (not E. coli)
    • Reactive arthritis: Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter, Yersinia
    • ITP: Isolated thrombocytopenia (no hemolysis or renal failure)

    Summary¶

    Diarrhea is a common presenting symptom with diverse etiologies:

    Acute Diarrhea (<14 days): - Viral (most common): Norovirus, Rotavirus - Bacterial: Campylobacter (#1 developed countries), ETEC (#1 traveler's), Salmonella, Shigella, E. coli O157:H7, C. diff - Management: ORS (cornerstone), antibiotics (if severe/invasive), NO antibiotics for O157:H7 or uncomplicated Salmonella

    Chronic Diarrhea (>4 weeks): - Watery: - Secretory (persists with fasting, gap <50): VIPoma, carcinoid, bile acids, microscopic colitis - Osmotic (stops with fasting, gap >125): Lactose intolerance, laxatives, pancreatic insufficiency - Fatty (steatorrhea): Celiac disease, pancreatic insufficiency, SIBO - Inflammatory (bloody): IBD, ischemic colitis - Dysmotility: IBS, hyperthyroidism

    Diagnostic Approach: - History (duration, stool type, fasting effect) - Labs (CBC, celiac serology, fecal calprotectin) - Stool studies (osmotic gap, fecal fat, culture, O&P) - Endoscopy (colonoscopy with biopsies, EGD if steatorrhea)

    Key Management Principles: - Treat underlying cause - ORS (glucose-coupled Na⁺ absorption) - Avoid loperamide if bloody diarrhea, C. diff, O157:H7
