    DEFINITION¶

    Bronchiectasis is a chronic respiratory condition characterized by: - Permanent abnormal dilatation of bronchi and bronchioles - Destruction of elastic and muscular components of bronchial wall - Chronic inflammation and infection - Impaired mucociliary clearance

    Types Based on Morphology¶

    BRONCHIECTASIS TYPES (Reid Classification)
│
├─ CYLINDRICAL (Tubular)
│  • Uniform dilatation
│  • Most common type (50%)
│  • Best prognosis
│
├─ VARICOSE
│  • Irregular dilatation (beaded appearance)
│  • Alternating areas of dilatation and constriction
│  • Moderate severity
│
└─ CYSTIC (Saccular)
   • Severe dilatation forming cyst-like spaces
   • Most severe form
   • Worst prognosis
   • Associated with extensive destruction

    EPIDEMIOLOGY¶

    Prevalence¶

    • Increasing due to improved detection (HRCT)
    • More common in developing countries
    • Prevalence: 4-20 per 100,000 in developed countries

    Demographics¶

    • Female > Male (in most studies)
    • Can occur at any age
    • Onset depends on underlying cause:
    • Childhood: Cystic fibrosis, immunodeficiency, congenital disorders
    • Adulthood: Post-infectious, COPD-related

    Risk Factors¶

    • Severe respiratory infections in childhood
    • Immunodeficiency
    • Chronic aspiration
    • Smoking
    • COPD

    ETIOLOGY¶

    Classification by Etiology¶

    CAUSES OF BRONCHIECTASIS
│
├─ POST-INFECTIOUS (30-40%) - MOST COMMON
│  ├─ Bacterial: Tuberculosis (MOST COMMON WORLDWIDE)
│  │              Pertussis, Staphylococcus, Klebsiella
│  ├─ Viral: Measles, Adenovirus, Influenza
│  └─ Other: Mycoplasma, Aspergillus (ABPA)
│
├─ GENETIC/CONGENITAL (5-10%)
│  ├─ Cystic Fibrosis (MOST COMMON GENETIC CAUSE)
│  ├─ Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia (PCD)
│  │  • Kartagener Syndrome (PCD + Situs inversus + Sinusitis)
│  ├─ Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency
│  └─ Tracheobronchomegaly (Mounier-Kuhn Syndrome)
│
├─ IMMUNODEFICIENCY (5-10%)
│  ├─ Common Variable Immunodeficiency (CVID)
│  ├─ X-linked Agammaglobulinemia
│  ├─ Selective IgA Deficiency
│  ├─ HIV/AIDS
│  └─ Hypogammaglobulinemia
│
├─ ASPIRATION
│  ├─ Gastroesophageal reflux (GERD)
│  ├─ Recurrent aspiration (neurological disorders)
│  └─ Foreign body
│
├─ OBSTRUCTIVE
│  ├─ Foreign body
│  ├─ Tumor (carcinoid, bronchogenic carcinoma)
│  └─ Lymph node compression
│
├─ INFLAMMATORY/AUTOIMMUNE
│  ├─ Allergic Bronchopulmonary Aspergillosis (ABPA)
│  ├─ Inflammatory Bowel Disease (UC, Crohn's)
│  ├─ Rheumatoid Arthritis
│  └─ Sjögren's Syndrome
│
├─ OTHER
│  ├─ Yellow Nail Syndrome
│  ├─ Young's Syndrome
│  └─ Post-transplant (lung, bone marrow)
│
└─ IDIOPATHIC (30-50%)
   • No identifiable cause despite thorough evaluation

    Mnemonic: "CIGAR CABS" for Causes of Bronchiectasis¶

    • C - Cystic Fibrosis
    • I - Immunodeficiency (CVID, XLA)
    • G - GERD/Aspiration
    • A - Allergic Bronchopulmonary Aspergillosis (ABPA)
    • R - Rheumatologic (RA, Sjögren's)
    • C - Ciliary dyskinesia (Kartagener)
    • A - Alpha-1 Antitrypsin deficiency
    • B - Bacterial/TB (Post-infectious)
    • S - Sinusitis (with PCD)

    Important Specific Causes¶

    1. Tuberculosis¶

    • Most common cause worldwide (especially in endemic areas)
    • Usually affects upper lobes
    • Often unilateral
    • History of TB infection/treatment

    2. Cystic Fibrosis¶

    • Most common genetic cause
    • Autosomal recessive (CFTR gene mutation)
    • Upper lobe predominance
    • Sweat chloride test (>60 mEq/L)
    • Associated: Pancreatic insufficiency, infertility

    3. Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia (PCD)¶

    • Defective ciliary motility
    • Kartagener Syndrome (Classic triad):
    • Bronchiectasis
    • Situs inversus (50%)
    • Chronic sinusitis
    • Infertility (immotile sperm)
    • Nasal nitric oxide very low (diagnostic)

    4. Allergic Bronchopulmonary Aspergillosis (ABPA)¶

    • Hypersensitivity to Aspergillus fumigatus
    • Usually occurs in asthmatics or CF patients
    • Central bronchiectasis (proximal airways)
    • Elevated IgE, eosinophilia

    PATHOPHYSIOLOGY¶

    Vicious Cycle Hypothesis (Cole's Vicious Cycle)¶

    INITIAL INSULT
(Infection, Aspiration, Immunodeficiency, Genetic defect)
           ↓
IMPAIRED MUCOCILIARY CLEARANCE
           ↓
MUCUS STASIS in Airways
           ↓
BACTERIAL COLONIZATION
• Pseudomonas aeruginosa (MOST COMMON)
• Haemophilus influenzae
• Staphylococcus aureus
• Moraxella catarrhalis
           ↓
CHRONIC INFLAMMATION
• Neutrophil infiltration
• Release of proteases (Elastase, MMPs)
• Oxidative stress
           ↓
BRONCHIAL WALL DESTRUCTION
• Loss of elastic and muscular layers
• Permanent dilatation
           ↓
FURTHER IMPAIRED CLEARANCE
           ↓
[VICIOUS CYCLE CONTINUES]

    Pathological Changes¶

    1. Bronchial Wall:
    2. Loss of elastic tissue
    3. Destruction of smooth muscle
    4. Replacement by fibrous tissue

    5. Chronic inflammatory infiltrate

    6. Airway Lumen:

    7. Dilatation (>1.5x adjacent vessel diameter)
    8. Mucus plugging

    9. Purulent secretions

    10. Surrounding Parenchyma:

    11. Atelectasis
    12. Consolidation
    13. Fibrosis

    14. Compensatory hyperinflation

    15. Vascular Changes:

    16. Bronchial artery hypertrophy (systemic circulation)
    17. Potential for hemoptysis

    CLINICAL FEATURES¶

    Symptoms¶

    Cardinal Features¶

    1. Chronic Productive Cough (MOST COMMON)
    2. Present in >90%
    3. Daily production of purulent/mucopurulent sputum
    4. Copious sputum (>30 mL/day in severe cases)
    5. Worse in the morning (overnight accumulation)

    6. Foul-smelling if anaerobic infection

    7. Recurrent Respiratory Infections

    8. Frequent exacerbations (≥3 per year in severe disease)

    9. Pneumonia

    10. Hemoptysis (50-70%)

    11. Usually mild (blood-streaked sputum)
    12. Massive hemoptysis (>200 mL/24 hr) in 5-10%

    13. Due to bronchial artery hypertrophy/erosion

    14. Dyspnea (60-70%)

    15. Progressive with advanced disease

    16. Due to airflow obstruction, impaired gas exchange

    17. Chest Pain (20-30%)

    18. Pleuritic pain during exacerbations

    19. Constitutional Symptoms

    20. Fatigue, weight loss (severe/advanced disease)
    21. Low-grade fever (during exacerbations)

    Physical Signs¶

    1. Respiratory Examination
    2. Coarse crepitations/crackles (80-90%) - MOST COMMON SIGN
      • Localized to affected area
      • May shift with coughing
    3. Rhonchi/wheeze (40-50%)

    4. Decreased breath sounds (localized)

    5. Clubbing (30-40%)

    6. More common in severe/long-standing disease

    7. Cystic fibrosis-related bronchiectasis

    8. Cyanosis

    9. Advanced disease with respiratory failure

    10. Signs of Cor Pulmonale (Advanced disease)

    11. Elevated JVP
    12. Pedal edema
    13. Loud P2

    Clinical Severity Assessment¶

    Bronchiectasis Severity Index (BSI)¶

    Variable Points
    Age (years) 1 point per decade >40
    BMI <18.5 kg/m² 2
    FEV₁ <30% predicted 3
    FEV₁ 30-49% predicted 2
    FEV₁ 50-79% predicted 1
    Hospital admission in past 2 years 5
    Exacerbations in past year (≥3) 2
    MRC dyspnea score 5 3
    MRC dyspnea score 4 2
    MRC dyspnea score 3 1
    Colonization: P. aeruginosa 3
    Colonization: Other organisms 1
    Radiological severity >3 lobes involved 1

    Score Interpretation: - 0-4: Mild (Low mortality) - 5-8: Moderate - ≥9: Severe (High mortality)

    DIAGNOSIS¶

    Diagnostic Approach¶

    CLINICAL SUSPICION
(Chronic productive cough, recurrent infections, hemoptysis)
           ↓
    HRCT CHEST (GOLD STANDARD)
           ↓
    CONFIRMS DIAGNOSIS
           ↓
IDENTIFY UNDERLYING CAUSE
• Clinical history
• Laboratory tests
• Microbiological studies
           ↓
ASSESS SEVERITY & COMPLICATIONS

    1. High-Resolution CT (HRCT) Chest¶

    GOLD STANDARD for diagnosis

    HRCT Diagnostic Criteria¶

    Direct Signs (Diagnostic): 1. Bronchial Dilatation - Signet Ring Sign: Dilated bronchus (>1.5x) adjacent to pulmonary artery - Bronchus = ring, Artery = signet stone - MOST SPECIFIC SIGN

    1. Lack of Bronchial Tapering
    2. Bronchi visible in peripheral ⅓ of lung (normally invisible)

    3. Tram-track appearance (parallel lines)

    4. Bronchial Wall Thickening

    5. Thickened bronchial walls

    Indirect Signs: - Mucus plugging (tree-in-bud pattern) - Atelectasis - Mosaic perfusion/air trapping - Consolidation

    Distribution Patterns¶

    Cause Distribution Features
    Tuberculosis Upper lobes Unilateral/asymmetric, cavitation
    Cystic Fibrosis Upper lobes Bilateral, mucus plugging
    ABPA Central/proximal Upper-mid zones, high attenuation mucus plugs
    Aspiration Lower lobes (RLL, posterior segments) Related to gravity
    Idiopathic Lower lobes Bilateral, cylindrical

    2. Chest X-Ray¶

    Sensitivity: 50-60% (NOT sensitive, but may show): - Tram-track lines (thickened bronchial walls) - Ring shadows (dilated bronchi on end) - Increased lung markings - Atelectasis, consolidation - Cystic spaces

    Key Point: Normal CXR does NOT exclude bronchiectasis → Need HRCT

    3. Pulmonary Function Tests (PFTs)¶

    Pattern: Variable (Obstructive, Restrictive, or Mixed)

    Pattern Finding Frequency
    Obstructive ↓ FEV₁/FVC <0.70 50-60%
    Mixed ↓ FEV₁/FVC + ↓ TLC 30-40%
    Normal Early/mild disease 10-20%

    Additional Findings: - Reversibility: Partial response to bronchodilators (unlike COPD) - ↓ FEV₁: Severity correlates with disease extent - ↓ DLCO: In advanced disease

    Purpose: - Assess severity - Monitor disease progression - Evaluate response to therapy

    4. Sputum Analysis¶

    Microscopy and Culture¶

    Indications: All patients (baseline and during exacerbations)

    Common Organisms: 1. Pseudomonas aeruginosa (30-40%) - Associated with worse prognosis - More exacerbations, ↓ FEV₁, ↑ mortality 2. Haemophilus influenzae (30-40%) 3. Staphylococcus aureus (including MRSA) 4. Moraxella catarrhalis 5. Nontuberculous Mycobacteria (NTM) (10-15%) - M. avium complex, M. abscessus 6. Aspergillus species (ABPA)

    Definitions: - Colonization: Organism present without symptoms - Infection: Organism + clinical deterioration

    Acid-Fast Bacilli (AFB)¶

    Indications: All patients at diagnosis (rule out TB, NTM) - 3 sputum samples for AFB smear and culture - TB: Especially in endemic areas - NTM: May require prolonged treatment

    5. Investigations for Underlying Cause¶

    Test Purpose Positive Finding
    Serum Immunoglobulins Immunodeficiency ↓ IgG, IgA, IgM (CVID)
    Total IgE ABPA >1000 IU/mL
    Aspergillus IgE/IgG ABPA Elevated specific IgE/IgG
    Sweat Chloride Test Cystic Fibrosis >60 mEq/L (CF)
    CFTR Gene Mutation Cystic Fibrosis Positive (F508del most common)
    Nasal Nitric Oxide Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia Very low (<77 nL/min)
    Ciliary Biopsy PCD Absent dynein arms (EM)
    Alpha-1 Antitrypsin AAT deficiency Low levels, PiZZ phenotype
    Autoantibodies Autoimmune RF, ANA, Anti-CCP
    HIV Test HIV-related Positive HIV antibody/RNA

    6. Bronchoscopy¶

    Indications (NOT routine): - Localized bronchiectasis (rule out obstruction: tumor, foreign body) - Massive hemoptysis (identify bleeding site) - Atypical presentation - Poor response to treatment

    7. Other Tests¶

    • 6-Minute Walk Test: Functional capacity, desaturation
    • Arterial Blood Gas: Assess hypoxemia, hypercapnia (advanced disease)
    • Echocardiography: Pulmonary hypertension, cor pulmonale

    MANAGEMENT¶

    Goals of Treatment¶

    1. Reduce exacerbation frequency
    2. Improve quality of life
    3. Prevent disease progression
    4. Manage complications (hemoptysis, respiratory failure)

    Treatment Components¶

    BRONCHIECTASIS MANAGEMENT
│
├─ AIRWAY CLEARANCE TECHNIQUES (CORNERSTONE)
├─ ANTIBIOTICS
│  ├─ Acute exacerbations
│  ├─ Chronic suppressive (selected patients)
│  └─ Eradication (P. aeruginosa)
├─ ANTI-INFLAMMATORY
├─ BRONCHODILATORS
├─ TREAT UNDERLYING CAUSE
├─ VACCINATIONS
└─ SURGICAL (Selected cases)

    1. Airway Clearance Techniques (ACT)¶

    MOST IMPORTANT non-pharmacological intervention

    Techniques¶

    Method Description Efficacy
    Active Cycle of Breathing Breathing control + thoracic expansion + forced expiration High
    Autogenic Drainage Controlled breathing at different lung volumes High
    Postural Drainage Gravity-assisted drainage (position based on affected lobe) Moderate
    Percussion/Vibration Chest clapping/shaking Moderate
    Oscillating PEP devices Flutter valve, Acapella High
    High-Frequency Chest Wall Oscillation (HFCWO) Mechanical vest High

    Recommendation: ≥2 sessions per day (each 10-30 minutes)

    Adjuncts: - Nebulized Hypertonic Saline (3-7%) - Improves mucus hydration and clearance - Induces cough (may cause bronchospasm → give after bronchodilator) - Nebulized Mannitol - Osmotic agent, improves clearance

    2. Antibiotics¶

    A. Acute Exacerbations¶

    Definition of Exacerbation (≥3 of the following): 1. ↑ Sputum volume 2. ↑ Sputum purulence 3. ↑ Dyspnea 4. ↑ Cough 5. Fever 6. ↑ Fatigue/malaise 7. ↓ Lung function (FEV₁)

    Antibiotic Selection:

    Severity Organism Antibiotic Duration
    Mild-Moderate (Outpatient) H. influenzae, M. catarrhalis Amoxicillin-clavulanate 875/125 mg TID, OR Doxycycline 100 mg BID, OR Azithromycin 500 mg OD 14 days
    Severe or P. aeruginosa colonization P. aeruginosa Ciprofloxacin 750 mg BID (PO), OR Levofloxacin 750 mg OD 14-21 days
    Hospitalized P. aeruginosa, others IV: Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5 g q6h, OR Ceftazidime 2 g q8h, OR Meropenem 1 g q8h ± Aminoglycoside 14-21 days

    Key Points: - Longer duration than typical pneumonia (14 days minimum) - Culture-guided therapy preferred - IV therapy if severe or failed oral treatment

    B. Chronic Suppressive Antibiotics¶

    Indications (Selected patients): - ≥3 exacerbations per year despite optimal ACT, OR - Severe disease with frequent hospitalizations

    Options:

    1. Macrolide (Long-term)
    2. Azithromycin 250-500 mg 3x/week (or daily)
    3. Erythromycin 500 mg BID
    4. Mechanism: Anti-inflammatory, anti-biofilm (NOT just antibacterial)
    5. Benefit: ↓ Exacerbations by 40-50%
    6. Monitoring: Baseline ECG (QTc), LFTs, hearing test

    7. Contraindication: NTM colonization (risk of resistance)

    8. Inhaled Antibiotics

    9. Tobramycin (300 mg BID nebulized) - On/off cycles (28 days on, 28 off)
    10. Colistin (1-2 million units BID nebulized)
    11. Indications: Chronic P. aeruginosa colonization with frequent exacerbations
    12. Benefit: ↓ Bacterial load, ↓ exacerbations

    C. Pseudomonas Eradication¶

    Rationale: Early eradication may prevent chronic colonization

    Regimen (at first isolation): - Ciprofloxacin 750 mg BID PO × 2 weeks - + Inhaled Tobramycin 300 mg BID × 4 weeks

    3. Anti-Inflammatory Therapy¶

    Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICS)¶

    Indications: - Concomitant asthma or COPD - Frequent exacerbations despite other therapies - Not routinely recommended for all patients

    Example: Budesonide/Formoterol, Fluticasone/Salmeterol

    Risks: ↑ NTM risk, pneumonia

    4. Bronchodilators¶

    Indications: - Airflow obstruction (FEV₁/FVC <0.70) - Reversibility on PFTs - Dyspnea relief

    Options: - SABA: Salbutamol (as needed) - LABA: Formoterol, Salmeterol (if persistent obstruction) - LAMA: Tiotropium (if COPD overlap)

    5. Mucoactive Agents¶

    • Hypertonic Saline (3-7%): Nebulized BID, ↑ mucus clearance
    • Mannitol: Dry powder inhaler
    • DNase (Dornase alfa): ONLY in CF (may worsen non-CF bronchiectasis)

    6. Treatment of Underlying Cause¶

    Cause Treatment
    ABPA Prednisone (0.5 mg/kg/day × 2 weeks, taper over 3-6 months) + Itraconazole (200 mg BID)
    Cystic Fibrosis CFTR modulators (Elexacaftor/Tezacaftor/Ivacaftor), pancreatic enzymes
    Immunodeficiency IV Immunoglobulin (IVIG) replacement (0.4-0.6 g/kg q3-4 weeks)
    GERD PPI (Omeprazole 20-40 mg daily), lifestyle modifications
    NTM Multi-drug regimen (Azithromycin + Ethambutol + Rifampin ± Amikacin) × 12-18 months
    Aspiration Treat underlying cause (dysphagia management, NGT if needed)

    7. Vaccinations¶

    Recommended: - Influenza vaccine (annual) - Pneumococcal vaccine: - PCV20 (single dose), OR - PCV15 followed by PPSV23 (1 year later) - COVID-19 vaccine

    8. Pulmonary Rehabilitation¶

    Components: Exercise training, breathing techniques, education

    Benefits: Improves exercise capacity, QOL, reduces dyspnea

    9. Oxygen Therapy¶

    Indications: - Resting SpO₂ ≤88% - PaO₂ ≤55 mmHg

    Goal: SpO₂ ≥90%

    10. Surgical Treatment¶

    Indications (Rare, <5% of patients): - Localized bronchiectasis (1-2 lobes) with: - Recurrent infections despite medical therapy - Massive hemoptysis (recurrent) - Adequate pulmonary reserve (post-resection FEV₁ >1L)

    Procedures: - Lobectomy/Segmentectomy (curative for localized disease) - Lung transplantation (end-stage bilateral disease, CF)

    Contraindications to Surgery: - Diffuse/widespread disease - Poor lung function (FEV₁ <40%) - Severe comorbidities

    COMPLICATIONS¶

    1. Massive Hemoptysis¶

    Definition: >200-300 mL blood in 24 hours

    Mechanism: Erosion of hypertrophied bronchial arteries

    Management: 1. Resuscitation: IV fluids, blood transfusion 2. Positioning: Bleeding side down (prevent aspiration to healthy lung) 3. Reverse coagulopathy (if present) 4. Bronchoscopy: Localize bleeding site 5. Bronchial Artery Embolization (BAE): Treatment of choice - Success rate: 70-90% - Recurrence: 20-30% 6. Surgery: If BAE fails or recurrent bleeding (lobectomy)

    2. Respiratory Failure¶

    Acute-on-Chronic: - During severe exacerbations - Type II respiratory failure (hypercapnia)

    Chronic: - Advanced disease - Progressive hypoxemia

    Management: - Oxygen therapy - Non-invasive ventilation (NIV) - Mechanical ventilation (if needed) - Treat underlying exacerbation

    3. Cor Pulmonale (Pulmonary Hypertension)¶

    Pathophysiology: Chronic hypoxemia → Pulmonary vasoconstriction → RV failure

    Management: - Treat underlying disease - Oxygen therapy (most important) - Diuretics (edema) - Consider pulmonary vasodilators (limited evidence)

    4. Pneumonia¶

    Recurrent bacterial pneumonia in affected lobes

    Prevention: Vaccinations, airway clearance

    5. Pneumothorax¶

    Occurs rarely (cystic/advanced disease)

    6. Amyloidosis (AA Type)¶

    Rare complication of chronic inflammation

    NEET PG HIGH-YIELD POINTS¶

    Must-Know Facts¶

    1. Definition: Permanent abnormal dilatation of bronchi with chronic inflammation

    2. Most Common Cause:

    3. Worldwide: Tuberculosis (post-infectious)

    4. Genetic: Cystic Fibrosis

    5. Kartagener Syndrome (Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia):

    6. Triad: Bronchiectasis + Situs inversus + Chronic sinusitis
    7. Infertility (immotile sperm)

    8. Very low nasal nitric oxide

    9. HRCT Chest:

    10. GOLD STANDARD for diagnosis
    11. Signet Ring Sign: Dilated bronchus (ring) > adjacent artery (signet stone)
      • MOST SPECIFIC SIGN

    12. Broncho-arterial ratio >1.5

    13. Clinical Features:

    14. Chronic productive cough with copious sputum (MOST COMMON)
    15. Hemoptysis (50-70%)
    16. Coarse crackles (MOST COMMON SIGN)

    17. Clubbing (30-40%)

    18. Most Common Organism: Pseudomonas aeruginosa (30-40%)

    19. Associated with worse prognosis

    20. Airway Clearance Techniques: MOST IMPORTANT non-pharmacological treatment

    21. ≥2 sessions/day

    22. Antibiotics for Exacerbation:

    23. Mild-Moderate: Amoxicillin-clavulanate × 14 days
    24. P. aeruginosa: Ciprofloxacin 750 mg BID × 14-21 days

    25. Longer duration than typical pneumonia

    26. Long-term Azithromycin:

    27. ≥3 exacerbations/year despite optimal ACT

    28. Contraindication: NTM colonization (risk of resistance)

    29. Massive Hemoptysis:

      • Bronchial Artery Embolization (BAE): Treatment of choice

    Distribution Patterns (High-Yield)¶

    Cause Lobes Affected Key Feature
    Tuberculosis Upper lobes Unilateral/asymmetric, history of TB
    Cystic Fibrosis Upper lobes Bilateral, young patient, sweat chloride >60
    ABPA Central/proximal (upper-mid) High attenuation mucus plugs, IgE >1000
    Aspiration Lower lobes (RLL, posterior) Recurrent aspiration history
    Idiopathic Lower lobes Bilateral, no identifiable cause

    Comparison: Bronchiectasis vs COPD¶

    Feature Bronchiectasis COPD
    Pathology Bronchial dilatation Emphysema + chronic bronchitis
    Sputum Copious, purulent Minimal-moderate, mucoid
    Hemoptysis Common (50-70%) Rare
    Crackles Coarse, localized Fine, diffuse (if present)
    HRCT Signet ring sign Emphysema, air trapping
    Reversibility Partial Minimal

    ABPA (Allergic Bronchopulmonary Aspergillosis) - Key Points¶

    Diagnostic Criteria (All must be present): 1. Asthma or CF 2. Total IgE >1000 IU/mL 3. Elevated Aspergillus IgE and/or IgG 4. Central bronchiectasis on HRCT

    Additional Features: - Eosinophilia (blood >500/μL) - Mucus plugs (high attenuation on HRCT) - Fleeting pulmonary infiltrates

    Treatment: - Prednisone 0.5 mg/kg/day × 2 weeks, taper over 3-6 months - Itraconazole 200 mg BID (reduces steroid requirement)

    Cystic Fibrosis - Diagnostic Tests¶

    Test Finding Significance
    Sweat Chloride >60 mEq/L DIAGNOSTIC (most commonly used)
    CFTR Gene Mutation Positive (F508del most common) Confirmatory
    Nasal Potential Difference Abnormal Alternative diagnostic test

    CF Features: - Bronchiectasis (upper lobes) - Pancreatic insufficiency (steatorrhea) - Infertility (males: absent vas deferens) - Nasal polyps, chronic sinusitis - Meconium ileus (newborns)

    Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia - Diagnostic Tests¶

    Test Finding
    Nasal Nitric Oxide Very low (<77 nL/min) - SCREENING TEST
    Ciliary Biopsy (EM) Absent dynein arms (outer or inner) - GOLD STANDARD
    High-Speed Video Microscopy Absent/abnormal ciliary beat pattern

    Common Mistakes to Avoid¶

    1. Normal CXR rules out bronchiectasis ❌
    2. CXR sensitivity only 50-60%

    3. HRCT is GOLD STANDARD

    4. All bronchiectasis patients need steroids ❌

    5. ICS only if concomitant asthma/COPD

    6. NOT routine

    7. Short antibiotic courses (5-7 days) for exacerbations ❌

    8. Need 14 days minimum (14-21 days for P. aeruginosa)

    9. DNase (Dornase alfa) is beneficial ❌

    10. ONLY in CF, may worsen non-CF bronchiectasis

    11. Long-term azithromycin is safe in all patients ❌

    12. Contraindicated if NTM colonization (risk of resistance)

    13. Need baseline ECG (QTc prolongation risk)

    14. Surgery is first-line treatment ❌

    15. Medical therapy is mainstay
    16. Surgery only for localized disease with failed medical therapy or massive hemoptysis

    PRACTICE MCQs¶

    Question 1¶

    A 35-year-old man presents with chronic productive cough with copious foul-smelling sputum, recurrent respiratory infections, and hemoptysis. On examination, situs inversus totalis is noted. He also has chronic sinusitis and infertility. What is the most likely diagnosis?

    A. Cystic Fibrosis B. Kartagener Syndrome C. Allergic Bronchopulmonary Aspergillosis D. Idiopathic Bronchiectasis

    Answer: B. Kartagener Syndrome

    Explanation: - Diagnosis: Kartagener Syndrome (Primary Ciliary Dyskinesia)

    Classic Triad: 1. Bronchiectasis (chronic productive cough, recurrent infections, hemoptysis) 2. Situs inversus (present in this patient) 3. Chronic sinusitis

    Additional Features: - Infertility (immotile sperm due to defective ciliary function)

    Pathophysiology: - Defective ciliary motility → Impaired mucociliary clearance → Bronchiectasis, sinusitis - Ciliary defect also affects sperm motility

    Diagnosis: - Nasal Nitric Oxide: Very low (<77 nL/min) - SCREENING TEST - Ciliary biopsy (Electron microscopy): Absent dynein arms - GOLD STANDARD

    Why NOT Other Options?: - CF: No situs inversus, would have pancreatic insufficiency, sweat chloride >60 - ABPA: No situs inversus, associated with asthma, IgE >1000 - Idiopathic: Diagnosis of exclusion, situs inversus points to specific etiology

    Key Point: Bronchiectasis + Situs inversus + Sinusitis + Infertility = Kartagener Syndrome

    Question 2¶

    A 28-year-old woman with bronchial asthma presents with recurrent episodes of pulmonary infiltrates and brownish mucus plugs in sputum. CXR shows fleeting infiltrates. HRCT shows central bronchiectasis with high attenuation mucus plugs. Lab tests show total IgE 1500 IU/mL and peripheral eosinophilia. What is the most appropriate treatment?

    A. Long-term azithromycin B. Prednisone and itraconazole C. Inhaled tobramycin D. Pirfenidone

    Answer: B. Prednisone and itraconazole

    Explanation: - Diagnosis: Allergic Bronchopulmonary Aspergillosis (ABPA)

    Diagnostic Criteria (All present): 1. Asthma or CF ✓ 2. Total IgE >1000 IU/mL ✓ (1500 IU/mL) 3. Central bronchiectasis on HRCT ✓ 4. Elevated Aspergillus-specific IgE/IgG (presumed positive)

    Additional Supportive Features: - Eosinophilia (blood >500/μL) - High attenuation mucus plugs on HRCT (pathognomonic) - Fleeting pulmonary infiltrates - Brownish mucus plugs in sputum

    Treatment: - Prednisone 0.5 mg/kg/day × 2 weeks, then taper over 3-6 months - Itraconazole 200 mg BID (antifungal) - Reduces steroid dose required - Treats fungal burden

    Why NOT Other Options?: - Azithromycin: Used for bacterial exacerbations in bronchiectasis, not ABPA - Inhaled Tobramycin: For P. aeruginosa colonization - Pirfenidone: Antifibrotic for IPF

    Key Point: ABPA = Asthma + IgE >1000 + Central bronchiectasis + Eosinophilia → Treatment = Prednisone + Itraconazole

    Question 3¶

    HRCT chest of a patient with chronic productive cough shows dilated bronchi with broncho-arterial ratio >1.5. What is this finding called?

    A. Tree-in-bud pattern B. Signet ring sign C. Reverse halo sign D. Mosaic attenuation

    Answer: B. Signet ring sign

    Explanation: - Signet Ring Sign: MOST SPECIFIC HRCT sign of bronchiectasis

    Description: - Dilated bronchus (ring) adjacent to pulmonary artery (signet stone) - Broncho-arterial ratio >1.5 (normally <1) - Appears as a ring with a dot (like a signet ring)

    Other HRCT Signs in Bronchiectasis: - Lack of bronchial tapering (bronchi visible in peripheral lung) - Tram-track appearance (parallel thickened bronchial walls) - Bronchial wall thickening

    Why NOT Other Options?: - Tree-in-bud: Mucus plugging in small airways (seen in bronchiectasis exacerbations, but not diagnostic) - Reverse halo sign (Atoll sign): Organizing pneumonia - Mosaic attenuation: Air trapping (seen in small airway disease, HP, bronchiectasis, but not specific)

    Key Point: Signet Ring Sign = Dilated bronchus > adjacent artery = BRONCHIECTASIS

    Question 4¶

    A 45-year-old patient with bronchiectasis colonized with Pseudomonas aeruginosa has 4 exacerbations requiring antibiotics in the past year despite good adherence to airway clearance techniques. What is the most appropriate next step in management?

    A. Add long-term oral ciprofloxacin B. Add long-term azithromycin C. Add inhaled corticosteroids D. Refer for surgery

    Answer: B. Add long-term azithromycin

    Explanation: - Indication for Chronic Suppressive Therapy: ≥3 exacerbations per year despite optimal airway clearance

    Long-term Azithromycin: - Dose: 250-500 mg 3x/week or daily - Mechanism: Anti-inflammatory, anti-biofilm, immunomodulatory (NOT just antibacterial) - Benefit: Reduces exacerbations by 40-50% - Monitoring: - Baseline ECG (QTc prolongation risk) - LFTs - Audiometry (hearing loss risk) - Rule out NTM colonization (contraindication)

    Before Starting: - CRITICAL: Must check for Nontuberculous Mycobacteria (NTM) - If NTM present → DO NOT give macrolide monotherapy (risk of resistance)

    Why NOT Other Options?: - Oral Ciprofloxacin (long-term): Risk of resistance, tendon rupture, not standard chronic suppressive therapy - Inhaled Tobramycin would be better for chronic P. aeruginosa suppression (but azithromycin is still first choice for frequent exacerbations) - ICS: Only if concomitant asthma/COPD, not routine - Surgery: For localized disease with failed medical therapy; this patient has diffuse disease with P. aeruginosa colonization (poor surgical candidate)

    Key Point: ≥3 exacerbations/year despite ACT → Add long-term Azithromycin (after ruling out NTM)

    Question 5¶

    A 50-year-old man with bronchiectasis presents with massive hemoptysis (300 mL in 6 hours). He is hemodynamically stable. What is the most appropriate management?

    A. Emergency lobectomy B. Bronchial artery embolization C. High-dose tranexamic acid D. Observation and antibiotics

    Answer: B. Bronchial artery embolization

    Explanation: - Massive Hemoptysis: >200-300 mL blood in 24 hours

    Pathophysiology: - Erosion of hypertrophied bronchial arteries (systemic circulation) - High pressure → Risk of exsanguination

    Management Approach:

    1. Resuscitation:
    2. Large-bore IV access
    3. Fluid resuscitation
    4. Blood transfusion (if needed)

    5. Reverse coagulopathy

    6. Positioning: Bleeding side down (if known)

    7. Prevents aspiration to healthy lung

    8. Bronchoscopy:

    9. Localize bleeding site

    10. Temporize bleeding (cold saline, topical vasoconstrictors)

    11. Definitive Treatment:

    12. Bronchial Artery Embolization (BAE): TREATMENT OF CHOICE
      • Success rate: 70-90%
      • Minimally invasive
      • Can be repeated if recurrence
    13. Surgery (Lobectomy): If BAE fails or recurrent bleeding
      • Reserved for localized disease with adequate pulmonary reserve

    Why NOT Other Options?: - Emergency lobectomy: High morbidity/mortality, reserve for BAE failure or recurrent hemoptysis - Tranexamic acid: May be used as adjunct, but NOT definitive treatment for massive hemoptysis - Observation: Inappropriate for massive hemoptysis (life-threatening)

    Key Point: Massive hemoptysis in bronchiectasis → Bronchial Artery Embolization (BAE) is treatment of choice

    Question 6¶

    A 30-year-old patient with cystic fibrosis and bronchiectasis presents with acute exacerbation. Which of the following should NOT be used in this patient?

    A. Inhaled DNase (Dornase alfa) B. Hypertonic saline C. Azithromycin D. Postural drainage

    Answer: Trick question - All options are appropriate for CF, but let me rephrase:

    CORRECTED Question 6: A 30-year-old patient with non-CF bronchiectasis presents with thick secretions. Which of the following mucoactive agents is contraindicated?

    A. Inhaled DNase (Dornase alfa) B. Hypertonic saline C. Mannitol D. N-acetylcysteine

    Answer: A. Inhaled DNase (Dornase alfa)

    Explanation: - DNase (Dornase alfa): Recombinant human DNase enzyme that breaks down extracellular DNA in sputum

    Indication: - ONLY Cystic Fibrosis - CF sputum has high DNA content from neutrophil breakdown - Improves lung function in CF

    Contraindication: - Non-CF Bronchiectasis - May worsen lung function - Increases exacerbation risk - Mechanism unclear (possibly disrupts innate immunity)

    Other Mucoactive Agents (Safe in non-CF bronchiectasis): - Hypertonic Saline (3-7%): ↑ Mucus hydration, improves clearance - Mannitol: Osmotic agent, improves clearance - N-acetylcysteine: Mucolytic (limited evidence, rarely used)

    Key Point: DNase is beneficial in CF but harmful in non-CF bronchiectasis

    Question 7¶

    What is the most common organism colonizing patients with bronchiectasis and associated with worse prognosis?

    A. Haemophilus influenzae B. Pseudomonas aeruginosa C. Staphylococcus aureus D. Moraxella catarrhalis

    Answer: B. Pseudomonas aeruginosa

    Explanation: - Pseudomonas aeruginosa: 30-40% of bronchiectasis patients

    Associated with WORSE Prognosis: - ↑ Exacerbation frequency - ↑ Hospitalization - ↓ FEV₁ (faster lung function decline) - ↑ Mortality - More severe disease (BSI score)

    Characteristics: - Forms biofilms (difficult to eradicate) - Resistant to multiple antibiotics - Chronic colonization common

    Treatment: - Acute exacerbation: Ciprofloxacin 750 mg BID × 14-21 days (or IV anti-pseudomonal) - Eradication (first isolation): Ciprofloxacin PO + Inhaled Tobramycin - Chronic suppression: Inhaled Tobramycin (28 days on/28 off cycles)

    Other Organisms: - H. influenzae: 30-40% (EQUALLY COMMON but NOT associated with worse prognosis) - S. aureus: 10-20% - M. catarrhalis: 5-10%

    Key Point: P. aeruginosa colonization = Worse prognosis in bronchiectasis

    Question 8¶

    A patient with bronchiectasis is started on long-term azithromycin. Which of the following should be checked before starting therapy?

    A. Chest X-ray B. Sputum for NTM culture C. Serum IgE levels D. Sweat chloride test

    Answer: B. Sputum for NTM culture

    Explanation: - Before Starting Long-term Azithromycin: Must rule out Nontuberculous Mycobacteria (NTM) colonization

    Rationale: - Macrolide monotherapy in NTM → Risk of drug resistance - NTM treatment requires multi-drug regimen (Azithromycin + Ethambutol + Rifampin ± Amikacin) - If NTM present → Do NOT give azithromycin alone

    NTM in Bronchiectasis: - Frequency: 10-15% - Common species: M. avium complex (MAC), M. abscessus - Associated with: Older age, female, thin body habitus, severe bronchiectasis

    Other Monitoring Before/During Azithromycin: - ECG: Baseline (QTc prolongation risk) - LFTs: Baseline and periodic - Audiometry: Baseline (hearing loss risk)

    Why NOT Other Options?: - CXR: Useful but not specific requirement before azithromycin - Serum IgE: For ABPA diagnosis, not related to azithromycin - Sweat chloride: For CF diagnosis, not related to azithromycin

    Key Point: ALWAYS check for NTM before starting long-term macrolide therapy in bronchiectasis

    Question 9¶

    Which HRCT distribution pattern is most suggestive of post-tuberculous bronchiectasis?

    A. Central/proximal airways (upper-mid zones) B. Upper lobes C. Lower lobes (bilateral) D. Right middle lobe and lingula

    Answer: B. Upper lobes

    Explanation: - Tuberculosis: Most common cause of bronchiectasis worldwide (endemic areas)

    HRCT Distribution: - Upper lobe predominance - Unilateral or asymmetric (unlike CF which is bilateral) - Associated findings: - Cavitation - Fibrosis - Volume loss (upper lobe retraction) - Calcified lymph nodes

    Other Distribution Patterns:

    Distribution Cause
    Upper lobes TB, CF, Sarcoidosis (late fibrosis)
    Central/proximal (upper-mid) ABPA (high attenuation mucus plugs)
    Lower lobes (bilateral) Idiopathic, Aspiration, Post-infectious (non-TB)
    RML + Lingula Lady Windermere Syndrome (NTM in elderly women)

    Key Point: Upper lobe bronchiectasis with cavitation/fibrosis → Think Post-TB

    Question 10¶

    What is the minimum duration of antibiotic therapy for an acute exacerbation of bronchiectasis?

    A. 5-7 days B. 10 days C. 14 days D. 21 days

    Answer: C. 14 days

    Explanation: - Acute Exacerbation: Minimum 14 days antibiotic duration

    Rationale: - Thicker mucus and structural damage in bronchiectasis - Harder to eradicate bacteria - Shorter courses → Incomplete eradication → Relapse

    Duration Guidelines: - Mild-Moderate (non-P. aeruginosa): 14 days - Severe or P. aeruginosa infection: 14-21 days

    Antibiotics: - Non-P. aeruginosa: Amoxicillin-clavulanate, Doxycycline, Azithromycin - P. aeruginosa: Ciprofloxacin 750 mg BID (or IV anti-pseudomonal)

    Comparison with Other Respiratory Infections: - CAP (uncomplicated): 5-7 days - COPD exacerbation: 5-7 days - Bronchiectasis exacerbation: 14-21 days (LONGER)

    Key Point: Bronchiectasis exacerbations require LONGER antibiotic courses (14 days minimum) compared to typical pneumonia/COPD exacerbations

    Question 11¶

    A 60-year-old woman with recurrent respiratory infections has bronchiectasis on HRCT. Serum immunoglobulin testing shows IgG 400 mg/dL (normal: 700-1600), IgA 50 mg/dL (normal: 70-400), IgM 30 mg/dL (normal: 40-230). What is the most likely diagnosis and treatment?

    A. Selective IgA deficiency; no specific treatment B. Common Variable Immunodeficiency; IV immunoglobulin C. X-linked agammaglobulinemia; stem cell transplant D. HIV/AIDS; antiretroviral therapy

    Answer: B. Common Variable Immunodeficiency; IV immunoglobulin

    Explanation: - Diagnosis: Common Variable Immunodeficiency (CVID)

    Diagnostic Criteria: - Low IgG (<400 mg/dL) ✓ - Low IgA and/or IgM ✓ - Onset typically in 2nd-4th decade (unlike XLA which is in infancy) - Recurrent sinopulmonary infections - Exclusion of other causes

    Clinical Features: - Recurrent respiratory infections (sinusitis, bronchitis, pneumonia) - Bronchiectasis (chronic damage from recurrent infections) - GI infections, autoimmune disorders - Increased risk of lymphoma

    Treatment: - IV Immunoglobulin (IVIG): 0.4-0.6 g/kg every 3-4 weeks - Prevents infections - May slow bronchiectasis progression - Subcutaneous Ig (SCIG): Alternative - Antibiotics: Aggressive treatment of infections - Airway clearance: For bronchiectasis management

    Why NOT Other Options?: - Selective IgA deficiency: Only IgA low, IgG and IgM normal; most are asymptomatic - X-linked agammaglobulinemia (XLA): Males only, presents in infancy (<1 year), all Ig very low (<100 mg/dL) - HIV/AIDS: Would show low CD4, positive HIV test, different clinical picture

    Key Point: CVID = Low IgG + Low IgA/IgM + Recurrent infections → Treatment = IVIG replacement

    Question 12¶

    Which of the following is the MOST IMPORTANT component of non-pharmacological management of bronchiectasis?

    A. Dietary modification B. Airway clearance techniques C. Annual influenza vaccination D. Avoidance of air pollution

    Answer: B. Airway clearance techniques

    Explanation: - Airway Clearance Techniques (ACT): CORNERSTONE of bronchiectasis management

    Rationale: - Breaks the vicious cycle: - Removes mucus → Reduces bacterial load → Reduces inflammation → Prevents further damage

    Evidence: - Improves QOL - Reduces exacerbation frequency - Improves lung function - Reduces hospital admissions

    Recommendation: ≥2 sessions per day (each 10-30 minutes)

    Techniques: - Active Cycle of Breathing - Autogenic Drainage - Postural Drainage - Oscillating PEP devices (Flutter, Acapella) - High-Frequency Chest Wall Oscillation (HFCWO vest)

    Adjuncts: - Nebulized Hypertonic Saline (3-7%): Before ACT - Give after bronchodilator (to prevent bronchospasm)

    Why NOT Other Options?: - Dietary modification: No specific role in bronchiectasis (unless associated condition like CF) - Influenza vaccination: Important for prevention, but NOT the MOST important - Avoidance of air pollution: Helpful, but NOT the MOST important

    Key Point: Airway Clearance Techniques are the MOST IMPORTANT non-pharmacological intervention in bronchiectasis

    SUMMARY¶

    Bronchiectasis is characterized by: - Permanent abnormal bronchial dilatation with chronic inflammation - Most common cause: TB (worldwide), CF (genetic) - Cardinal symptom: Chronic productive cough with copious sputum - Gold standard diagnosis: HRCT (Signet Ring Sign) - Most common organism: Pseudomonas aeruginosa (worse prognosis)

    Management: 1. Airway Clearance Techniques: CORNERSTONE (≥2 sessions/day) 2. Antibiotics: 14 days for exacerbations; long-term azithromycin if ≥3 exacerbations/year (rule out NTM first) 3. Treat underlying cause: ABPA (steroids + itraconazole), CVID (IVIG), CF (CFTR modulators) 4. Massive hemoptysis: Bronchial Artery Embolization (BAE)

    Key Syndromes: - Kartagener: Bronchiectasis + Situs inversus + Sinusitis + Infertility - ABPA: Asthma + IgE >1000 + Central bronchiectasis + Eosinophilia - CVID: Low IgG + IgA/IgM + Recurrent infections → IVIG

    Prognosis: Variable; worse with P. aeruginosa, frequent exacerbations, low FEV₁
