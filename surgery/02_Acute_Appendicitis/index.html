    ACUTE APPENDICITIS - NEET PG Study Material¶

    Definition¶

    Acute inflammation of the vermiform appendix - Most common surgical emergency of the abdomen.

    Anatomy¶

    Vermiform Appendix: - Location: Arises from posteromedial wall of cecum, 2 cm below ileocecal valve - Length: 6-9 cm (average) - Blood supply: Appendicular artery (branch of ileocolic artery - end artery) - Base: Taeniae coli converge at appendix base (McBurney's point)

    Positions of Appendix¶

    Position Frequency Clinical Significance
    Retrocecal 65% Pain in flank, no peritonism initially
    Pelvic 30% Urinary symptoms, rectal tenderness
    Subcecal 2% Mimics ureteric colic
    Pre-ileal 1% Rare
    Post-ileal 1% Rare

    NEET PG: Retrocecal appendix = Most common position (65%)

    Epidemiology¶

    • Age: 10-30 years (peak incidence)
    • Gender: Male > Female (slight)
    • Lifetime risk: ~7-8%

    Etiology & Pathogenesis¶

    Causes of Obstruction¶

    1. Fecalith (most common - 50%)
    2. Lymphoid hyperplasia (children, viral infections)
    3. Foreign body (seeds, pins)
    4. Tumors: Carcinoid, adenocarcinoma
    5. Parasites: Worms (Ascaris, Enterobius)

    Pathophysiology¶

    Luminal obstruction
      ↓
Continued mucus secretion
      ↓
↑ Intraluminal pressure
      ↓
Venous obstruction → Edema, bacterial overgrowth
      ↓
Arterial compromise (end artery)
      ↓
Ischemia → Necrosis
      ↓
Gangrene → Perforation
      ↓
Peritonitis / Appendicular abscess

    NEET PG: Appendicular artery is an end artery → Thrombosis leads to gangrene

    Clinical Features¶

    Symptoms¶

    1. Pain (100%): - Classic sequence: Periumbilical pain (visceral) → Shifts to RIF after 4-6 hours (parietal) - Character: Initially vague, colicky → Becomes constant, sharp - Migration: Periumbilical → RIF (McBurney's point)

    2. Anorexia (almost always present): - Pain before anorexia (unlike gastroenteritis where anorexia comes first)

    3. Nausea/Vomiting (75%): - Usually after onset of pain (unlike gastroenteritis)

    4. Low-grade fever (38°C): - Mild pyrexia (high fever suggests perforation)

    5. Altered bowel habits: - Constipation (common) - Diarrhea (pelvic appendix)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Classic triad: Periumbilical pain → RIF pain + Anorexia + Vomiting - Pain precedes vomiting (reverse in gastroenteritis) - Anorexia almost always present

    Signs¶

    1. Tenderness: - McBurney's point: Junction of lateral ⅓ and medial ⅔ of line joining ASIS to umbilicus - Maximum tenderness at McBurney's point

    2. Guarding & Rigidity: - Voluntary guarding: Early - Involuntary rigidity: Peritonitis

    3. Rebound Tenderness (Blumberg's sign): - Pain on sudden release of pressure - Indicates peritoneal irritation

    4. Rovsing's Sign: - Palpation of LIF → Pain in RIF - Suggests peritoneal irritation

    5. Psoas Sign: - Pain on extension of right hip (patient lying on left side) - Positive in retrocecal appendicitis (inflamed appendix overlies psoas)

    6. Obturator Sign: - Pain on internal rotation of flexed right thigh - Positive in pelvic appendicitis

    7. Dunphy's Sign: - Increased pain with coughing

    8. Rectal Examination: - Tenderness on right side (pelvic appendix) - Boggy mass (pelvic abscess)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - McBurney's point tenderness: Most consistent finding - Psoas sign: Retrocecal appendix - Obturator sign: Pelvic appendix - Rovsing's sign: Peritoneal irritation

    Special Situations¶

    1. Retrocecal Appendicitis¶

    • Pain: Flank/back pain
    • Signs: Psoas sign positive
    • Peritonism: Minimal (retroperitoneal)
    • Urinalysis: May show sterile pyuria

    2. Pelvic Appendicitis¶

    • Symptoms: Urinary frequency, diarrhea
    • Signs: Obturator sign positive
    • Rectal exam: Tenderness
    • Peritonism: Minimal abdominal

    3. Appendicitis in Pregnancy¶

    • Pain: May be in RUQ (displaced cecum)
    • Diagnosis: Difficult (leukocytosis normal)
    • Imaging: MRI preferred (avoid radiation)
    • Treatment: Urgent surgery (perforation risk high)

    4. Appendicitis in Elderly¶

    • Presentation: Atypical, minimal symptoms
    • Complications: Higher rate (gangrene, perforation)
    • Mortality: Higher

    5. Appendicitis in Children¶

    • Presentation: Rapid progression
    • Perforation: Earlier (thin wall)
    • Etiology: Lymphoid hyperplasia common

    NEET PG: In pregnancy, appendicitis pain may be in RUQ due to displaced cecum

    Differential Diagnosis¶

    Surgical¶

    • Acute cholecystitis (RUQ pain, Murphy's positive)
    • Perforated peptic ulcer (sudden onset, board-like rigidity)
    • Acute pancreatitis (epigastric pain, ↑ amylase)
    • Intestinal obstruction
    • Mesenteric adenitis (children, viral URTI)
    • Meckel's diverticulitis

    Urological¶

    • Ureteric colic (colicky pain, hematuria, no fever)
    • UTI/Pyelonephritis (dysuria, ↑ WBC in urine)
    • Testicular torsion (males)

    Gynecological (Females)¶

    • Ectopic pregnancy (β-hCG positive, missed period)
    • PID (bilateral pain, vaginal discharge)
    • Ovarian torsion (sudden onset, ultrasound findings)
    • Ruptured ovarian cyst
    • Mittelschmerz (mid-cycle ovulation pain)

    Medical¶

    • Gastroenteritis (vomiting before pain, diarrhea)
    • Yersinia ileitis (mimics appendicitis)
    • Crohn's disease
    • Diabetic ketoacidosis (abdominal pain, ↑ glucose)

    NEET PG: Gastroenteritis = Vomiting before pain (reverse of appendicitis)

    Investigations¶

    Laboratory¶

    1. Complete Blood Count: - Leukocytosis: 10,000-20,000/μL (mild to moderate) - Neutrophilia: >75% - Normal WBC: Does NOT rule out appendicitis

    2. Urinalysis: - Usually normal - Mild pyuria/hematuria possible (if appendix overlies ureter)

    3. Inflammatory markers: - CRP: Elevated (>10 mg/L)

    4. β-hCG (females of childbearing age): - Rule out ectopic pregnancy

    NEET PG: Normal WBC does NOT exclude appendicitis (10-20% have normal WBC)

    Imaging¶

    1. Ultrasound (1st line in children, pregnant women):

    Findings: - Non-compressible appendix (diameter >6 mm) - Target sign (cross-section) - Appendicolith (echogenic shadow) - Free fluid in RIF

    Sensitivity: 70-80%

    Advantages: No radiation, bedside, identifies gynecological causes

    NEET PG: USG appendix diameter >6 mm = Abnormal

    2. CT Scan (Gold standard in adults):

    Findings: - Enlarged appendix (>6 mm) - Appendiceal wall thickening - Periappendiceal fat stranding - Appendicolith - Free fluid, abscess

    Sensitivity: 95%

    Indication: Atypical presentation, obesity, diagnostic uncertainty

    NEET PG: CT is most sensitive for diagnosing appendicitis (95%)

    3. MRI: - Indication: Pregnancy (avoid radiation) - Sensitivity: Similar to CT

    Scoring Systems¶

    Alvarado Score (MANTRELS)¶

    Feature Score
    **M**igration of pain to RIF 1
    **A**norexia 1
    **N**ausea/Vomiting 1
    **T**enderness in RIF 2
    **R**ebound tenderness 1
    **E**levated temperature (>37.5°C) 1
    **L**eukocytosis (>10,000) 2
    **S**hift to left (>75% neutrophils) 1

    Total: 10 points

    Interpretation: - 0-4: Low probability (discharge, outpatient) - 5-6: Equivocal (observe, repeat exam, imaging) - 7-10: High probability (surgery)

    NEET PG: Alvarado score ≥7 → High probability of appendicitis

    Complications¶

    Early¶

    1. Perforation (15-20%):
    2. Risk increases after 24-36 hours
    3. Signs: High fever, tachycardia, diffuse peritonitis

    4. Treatment: Urgent surgery

    5. Gangrenous appendicitis:

    6. Ischemic necrosis

    7. High risk of perforation

    8. Appendicular abscess:

    9. Walled-off perforation
    10. Mass in RIF

    11. Treatment: Conservative (antibiotics) → Interval appendectomy (6-8 weeks)

    12. Generalized peritonitis:

    13. Perforated appendix
    14. Treatment: Urgent surgery, lavage, antibiotics

    NEET PG: Perforation risk increases significantly after 24-36 hours

    Late¶

    1. Appendicular mass:
    2. Omentum wraps around inflamed appendix
    3. Ochsner-Sherren regimen: Conservative management (NBM, IV fluids, antibiotics, close monitoring)

    4. Interval appendectomy: 6-8 weeks later

    5. Portal pylephlebitis:

    6. Septic thrombophlebitis of portal vein (rare, serious)

    7. Wound infection: Common post-op complication

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Appendicular mass: Ochsner-Sherren regimen → Interval appendectomy at 6-8 weeks - Perforation: Emergency surgery - Abscess: Conservative → Interval appendectomy

    Treatment¶

    Non-Operative Management¶

    Indications (Selected cases): 1. Appendicular mass (abscess): - Ochsner-Sherren regimen: - NPO (nil per oral) - IV fluids - IV antibiotics (broad-spectrum) - Nasogastric suction (if ileus) - Close monitoring (vitals, abdominal exam) - Interval appendectomy: 6-8 weeks

    1. Early uncomplicated appendicitis (controversial):
    2. Antibiotics alone (some studies show success)
    3. Recurrence risk: 20-30%

    NEET PG: Appendicular mass = Conservative initially (Ochsner-Sherren) + Interval appendectomy

    Operative Management - DEFINITIVE TREATMENT¶

    Indication: Acute appendicitis (uncomplicated or complicated)

    Timing: Urgent surgery (within 24 hours of diagnosis)

    Procedures:

    1. Laparoscopic Appendectomy (Preferred): - Advantages: - Diagnostic + therapeutic - Less pain, shorter hospital stay - Better cosmesis - Lower wound infection - Disadvantages: - Higher cost - Longer operative time

    2. Open Appendectomy: - Incision: Grid-iron (McBurney), Lanz, or midline - Indications: Peritonitis, abscess, pregnancy (2nd/3rd trimester)

    Grid-iron incision (McBurney): - Location: McBurney's point - Direction: Perpendicular to line ASIS → umbilicus - Advantages: Muscle-splitting (less nerve injury)

    Lanz incision: - Location: Same as grid-iron - Direction: Transverse (cosmetically better)

    NEET PG: Laparoscopic appendectomy preferred (diagnostic + therapeutic, less pain, faster recovery)

    Antibiotics¶

    Preoperative: - Uncomplicated: Single-dose prophylaxis (Cefazolin + Metronidazole) - Complicated (gangrenous, perforated): Broad-spectrum (continue post-op)

    Postoperative: - Uncomplicated: No antibiotics needed - Complicated: 5-7 days (Piperacillin-Tazobactam OR Ceftriaxone + Metronidazole)

    Post-operative Care¶

    Uncomplicated: - Oral fluids: 6-8 hours post-op - Discharge: 24-48 hours - Return to activity: 2-4 weeks

    Complicated (perforation, abscess): - NPO until bowel function returns - IV antibiotics: 5-7 days - Longer hospital stay

    Negative Appendectomy Rate¶

    Definition: Normal appendix on histopathology

    Acceptable rate: 10-15%

    Reason: Better to operate on 10-15% normal appendices than miss a case (due to morbidity of perforation)

    NEET PG: Negative appendectomy rate of 10-15% is acceptable (avoid missing diagnosis)

    Special Mention: Carcinoid Tumor¶

    Most common tumor of appendix (50% of all appendiceal tumors)

    Location: Tip of appendix (most common)

    Presentation: Incidental finding during appendectomy for presumed appendicitis

    Size: - <1 cm: Simple appendectomy sufficient - 1-2 cm: Consider wider resection - >2 cm: Right hemicolectomy (risk of metastasis)

    NEET PG: Carcinoid = Most common appendiceal tumor (tip of appendix)

    Practice MCQs¶

    1. Most common position of appendix? Answer: Retrocecal (65%)

    2. Blood supply of appendix? Answer: Appendicular artery (branch of ileocolic artery - end artery)

    3. Most common cause of appendiceal obstruction? Answer: Fecalith (50%)

    4. Classic pain sequence in appendicitis? Answer: Periumbilical pain → Shifts to RIF after 4-6 hours

    5. Most consistent sign of appendicitis? Answer: Tenderness at McBurney's point

    6. Investigation of choice in adults? Answer: CT abdomen (most sensitive - 95%)

    7. Alvarado score ≥7 indicates? Answer: High probability of appendicitis (surgery indicated)

    8. Management of appendicular mass? Answer: Conservative (Ochsner-Sherren) → Interval appendectomy at 6-8 weeks

    Memory Aids¶

    Alvarado Score (MANTRELS)¶

    • **M**igration to RIF (1)
    • **A**norexia (1)
    • **N**ausea/Vomiting (1)
    • **T**enderness RIF (2)
    • **R**ebound tenderness (1)
    • **E**levated temperature (1)
    • **L**eukocytosis (2)
    • **S**hift to left (1)

    Positions of Appendix¶

    "**R**etrocecal **P**osition **S**till **P**lenty **P**opular" - **R**etrocecal (65%) - **P**elvic (30%) - **S**ubcecal (2%) - **P**re-ileal (1%) - **P**ost-ileal (1%)

    Signs¶

    "PROM" - **P**soas sign (retrocecal) - **R**ovsing's sign (peritonitis) - **O**bturator sign (pelvic) - **M**cBurney's point tenderness (all)

    Revision Checklist¶

    • Know anatomy of appendix (positions, blood supply)
    • Understand pathophysiology of appendicitis
    • Learn classic clinical presentation
    • Memorize all clinical signs (McBurney's, Psoas, Obturator, Rovsing's)
    • Know differential diagnosis
    • Learn Alvarado scoring system
    • Understand imaging modalities (USG vs CT)
    • Know management of appendicular mass
    • Learn surgical techniques (laparoscopic vs open)
    • Practice 40+ MCQs on appendicitis
