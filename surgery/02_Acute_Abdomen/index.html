    ACUTE ABDOMEN - NEET PG Study Material¶

    Definition¶

    Acute abdomen: Sudden onset severe abdominal pain requiring urgent evaluation and often surgical intervention

    Key principle: "Never let the sun set or rise on an acute abdomen without a diagnosis"

    CAUSES - By Quadrant¶

    Right Upper Quadrant (RUQ)¶

    • Acute cholecystitis
    • Acute cholangitis
    • Biliary colic
    • Hepatic abscess
    • Perforated duodenal ulcer
    • Acute hepatitis
    • Right lower lobe pneumonia
    • Budd-Chiari syndrome

    Right Lower Quadrant (RLQ)¶

    • Acute appendicitis (most common)
    • Meckel's diverticulitis
    • Ileitis (Crohn's)
    • Ruptured ovarian cyst
    • Ectopic pregnancy
    • Ovarian torsion
    • PID (Pelvic Inflammatory Disease)
    • Psoas abscess

    Left Upper Quadrant (LUQ)¶

    • Splenic rupture
    • Splenic infarction
    • Gastric ulcer perforation
    • Acute pancreatitis

    Left Lower Quadrant (LLQ)¶

    • Diverticulitis
    • Sigmoid volvulus
    • Ruptured ovarian cyst
    • Ectopic pregnancy
    • Ovarian torsion
    • PID

    Periumbilical/Generalized¶

    • Small bowel obstruction
    • Perforated peptic ulcer
    • Acute pancreatitis
    • Mesenteric ischemia
    • Ruptured AAA
    • Peritonitis

    NEET PG: Acute appendicitis = Most common surgical cause of acute abdomen

    CLINICAL EVALUATION¶

    History (SOCRATES)¶

    **S**ite: Location **O**nset: Sudden vs gradual **C**haracter: Colicky, sharp, dull, burning **R**adiation: Referred pain **A**ssociations: Nausea, vomiting, fever **T**ime course: Duration, progression **E**xacerbating/relieving factors **S**everity: Pain scale

    Important Questions¶

    • Last menstrual period (females - r/o pregnancy)
    • Past medical/surgical history
    • Medications (anticoagulants, steroids)
    • Trauma

    Physical Examination¶

    Inspection¶

    • Position: Lying still (peritonitis) vs writhing (colic)
    • Distension: Obstruction, ascites
    • Visible peristalsis: Obstruction
    • Scars: Previous surgery (adhesions)

    Palpation¶

    • Tenderness: Localized vs generalized
    • Guarding: Voluntary (pain) vs Involuntary (peritonitis)
    • Rigidity: Board-like abdomen (perforation)
    • Rebound tenderness (Blumberg's sign): Peritonitis
    • Mass: Tumor, abscess, AAA

    Percussion¶

    • Tympany: Gas (obstruction, ileus)
    • Dullness: Fluid (ascites), mass
    • Shifting dullness: Ascites
    • Obliteration of liver dullness: Pneumoperitoneum (perforation)

    Auscultation¶

    • High-pitched tinkling sounds: Obstruction
    • Absent bowel sounds: Ileus, peritonitis
    • Bruit: AAA

    NEET PG: Absent bowel sounds + rigidity = Peritonitis

    Special Signs - HIGH YIELD¶

    Sign Technique Positive in
    Murphy's sign RUQ palpation during deep inspiration → Arrest Acute cholecystitis
    Rovsing's sign LLQ palpation → RLQ pain Acute appendicitis
    McBurney's point Tenderness ⅓ distance from ASIS to umbilicus Acute appendicitis
    Psoas sign Pain on hip extension (right) Retrocecal appendicitis, psoas abscess
    Obturator sign Pain on internal rotation of flexed hip Pelvic appendicitis
    Cullen's sign Periumbilical ecchymosis Acute pancreatitis, ruptured ectopic
    Grey Turner's sign Flank ecchymosis Acute pancreatitis, AAA rupture
    Courvoisier's sign Palpable, non-tender gallbladder + jaundice Pancreatic head cancer (NOT stones)
    Kehr's sign Left shoulder pain (referred) Splenic rupture
    Carnett's sign ↑ Pain when lifting head (tensing abdominal muscles) Abdominal wall pain (NOT visceral)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Murphy's sign: Cholecystitis - Rovsing's sign: Appendicitis - Cullen's + Grey Turner's: Hemorrhagic pancreatitis - Courvoisier's law: Palpable GB + jaundice = Malignancy (NOT stones)

    INVESTIGATIONS¶

    Laboratory¶

    Mandatory: - CBC: Leukocytosis (infection), anemia (bleeding) - Serum amylase/lipase: Pancreatitis (lipase more specific) - LFTs: Cholangitis, hepatitis - Serum electrolytes: Dehydration - Urine pregnancy test: All women of childbearing age - Urinalysis: UTI, stones

    Others: - Serum lactate: ↑ in mesenteric ischemia - Coagulation profile: Before surgery - Blood group & cross-match: If bleeding suspected - ABG: Metabolic acidosis (ischemia, sepsis)

    NEET PG: β-hCG (pregnancy test) mandatory in all females with acute abdomen

    Imaging¶

    X-ray Abdomen (Erect + Supine)¶

    Erect CXR: - Free gas under diaphragm (pneumoperitoneum): Perforation - Rigler's sign: Both sides of bowel wall visible - Football sign: Large pneumoperitoneum (oval lucency)

    Erect AXR: - Air-fluid levels: Obstruction - Dilated bowel loops: Obstruction - Sentinel loop: Localized ileus (pancreatitis, appendicitis) - Psoas shadow loss: Retroperitoneal pathology - Calcifications: Gallstones, renal stones, aortic calcification

    Supine AXR: - Dilated bowel: Obstruction - Thumbprinting: Ischemic colitis, IBD - Gas in bowel wall (pneumatosis): Ischemia - Portal venous gas: Mesenteric ischemia (grave sign)

    NEET PG: - Pneumoperitoneum: Best seen on erect CXR (free gas under diaphragm) - Portal venous gas: Mesenteric ischemia (poor prognosis)

    Ultrasound (USG)¶

    First-line for: - RUQ pain: Gallstones, cholecystitis, cholangitis - Pelvic pain (females): Ectopic pregnancy, ovarian torsion, PID - AAA: Screening, rupture

    Advantages: Bedside, no radiation, cheap

    NEET PG: USG first-line for biliary pathology & gynecological causes

    CT Scan (CECT Abdomen)¶

    Gold standard for acute abdomen

    Indications: - Unclear diagnosis - Trauma - Suspected perforation (if X-ray negative) - AAA rupture - Mesenteric ischemia - Appendicitis (if diagnosis unclear) - Diverticulitis - Pancreatitis (complications)

    Findings: - Free air: Perforation - Stranding: Inflammation (appendicitis, diverticulitis, pancreatitis) - Abscess - Vascular abnormalities

    NEET PG: CT = Gold standard for acute abdomen evaluation

    ACUTE APPENDICITIS - HIGH YIELD¶

    Most common surgical emergency

    Pathophysiology¶

    1. Luminal obstruction (fecalith, lymphoid hyperplasia, worms)
    2. Bacterial overgrowth
    3. Inflammation
    4. Ischemia & necrosis
    5. Perforation (if untreated)

    Clinical Features¶

    Classic presentation: 1. Periumbilical pain (visceral - referred T10) 2. Anorexia, nausea, vomiting 3. Pain migrates to RLQ (McBurney's point) - somatic (parietal peritoneum) 4. Fever (low-grade initially)

    Timeline: Symptoms usually <48 hours

    Examination: - Tenderness at McBurney's point (⅓ from ASIS to umbilicus) - Rovsing's sign (+) - Rebound tenderness - Guarding - Psoas sign (+) if retrocecal - Obturator sign (+) if pelvic

    NEET PG: Pain migration (periumbilical → RLQ) = Classic for appendicitis

    Alvarado Score (MANTRELS)¶

    Feature Score
    **M**igration of pain to RLQ 1
    **A**norexia 1
    **N**ausea/vomiting 1
    **T**enderness in RLQ 2
    **R**ebound tenderness 1
    **E**levated temperature (>37.3°C) 1
    **L**eukocytosis (>10,000) 2
    **S**hift to left (neutrophils >75%) 1

    Total: 10 points

    Interpretation: - 1-4: Unlikely appendicitis - 5-6: Possible (observe or image) - 7-8: Probable (admit, consider surgery) - 9-10: Very likely (surgery)

    NEET PG: Alvarado score ≥7 → High probability of appendicitis

    Investigations¶

    Laboratory: - Leukocytosis (10,000-18,000) with left shift - Urine pregnancy test (females) - Urinalysis (r/o UTI)

    Imaging: - USG: Thickened, non-compressible appendix (>6 mm), target sign, free fluid - CT scan (if diagnosis unclear): Appendiceal thickening, periappendiceal stranding, appendicolith

    NEET PG: - Appendix diameter >6 mm = Abnormal - CT: Most accurate (95% sensitivity)

    Treatment¶

    Medical (initially): - NPO (nil per oral) - IV fluids - Analgesia - Antibiotics (if perforation suspected)

    Surgical: Appendectomy (open or laparoscopic) - Timing: Urgent (within 24 hours) - Laparoscopic: Preferred (less pain, faster recovery)

    Interval appendectomy: If appendiceal mass/abscess - Conservative management (antibiotics) - Appendectomy after 6-8 weeks

    NEET PG: Laparoscopic appendectomy preferred (diagnostic + therapeutic)

    Complications¶

    • Perforation (20-30%): ↑ If delayed >24-48 hours
    • Appendiceal abscess (periappendiceal)
    • Peritonitis (generalized)
    • Pylephlebitis: Septic thrombophlebitis of portal vein (rare, serious)

    NEET PG: Perforation risk ↑ if delay >24-48 hours

    ACUTE CHOLECYSTITIS - HIGH YIELD¶

    Pathophysiology¶

    90% due to gallstones (calculous cholecystitis)

    Sequence: 1. Stone impaction in cystic duct 2. Obstruction → Bile stasis 3. Chemical inflammation (bile salts) 4. Bacterial infection (E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterococcus) 5. Gangrene/perforation (if untreated)

    Clinical Features¶

    Biliary colic → Acute cholecystitis: - Severe RUQ pain (constant, >6 hours) - vs colic (<6 hours) - Fever (>38°C) - Nausea, vomiting - Positive Murphy's sign - Palpable GB (if distended) - Mild jaundice (10-20%)

    NEET PG: Murphy's sign = Inspiratory arrest on RUQ palpation

    Investigations¶

    Laboratory: - Leukocytosis (12,000-15,000) - Mild ↑ bilirubin (<4 mg/dl) - Mild ↑ ALP, GGT - Amylase/lipase (r/o pancreatitis)

    Imaging: - USG (first-line): - Gallstones - GB wall thickening (>3 mm) - Pericholecystic fluid - Sonographic Murphy's sign - HIDA scan (if USG equivocal): Non-filling of GB (cystic duct obstruction)

    NEET PG: USG first-line for cholecystitis (Murphy's sign + GB wall thickening)

    Tokyo Guidelines (Severity)¶

    Grade I (Mild): No organ dysfunction Grade II (Moderate): Leukocytosis >18,000, palpable mass, duration >72 hours Grade III (Severe): Organ dysfunction (hypotension, renal/hepatic/CNS dysfunction)

    Treatment¶

    Medical (initially): - NPO, IV fluids - Analgesia (NSAIDs, opioids) - Antibiotics: Broad-spectrum (ceftriaxone + metronidazole)

    Surgical: Laparoscopic cholecystectomy - Timing: - Early (<72 hours): Preferred (easier, less complications) - Delayed (6-8 weeks): If >72 hours or high-risk patient

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Laparoscopic cholecystectomy: Definitive treatment - Early surgery (<72 hours): Better outcomes - Calot's triangle: Cystic artery identified during surgery

    Complications¶

    • Gangrenous cholecystitis: Ischemia, necrosis
    • Perforation: Free (peritonitis) or localized (abscess)
    • Emphysematous cholecystitis: Gas in GB wall (diabetes, elderly)
    • Cholecystoenteric fistula: Fistula to duodenum
    • Gallstone ileus: Large stone → Small bowel obstruction (elderly)
    • Mirizzi syndrome: Impacted stone in cystic duct → Compress CBD → Jaundice

    NEET PG: - Emphysematous cholecystitis: Gas in GB (diabetes, Clostridium) - Gallstone ileus: Pneumobilia + SBO + ectopic gallstone

    PERFORATED PEPTIC ULCER¶

    Causes¶

    • Duodenal ulcer (more common - anterior wall)
    • Gastric ulcer (greater curvature, antrum)
    • NSAIDs, H. pylori, steroids

    Clinical Features¶

    Sudden onset severe epigastric pain ("Worst pain ever") - Generalized peritonitis - Board-like rigidity - Absent bowel sounds - Shallow breathing (pain) - Position: Lying still

    NEET PG: Board-like rigidity = Perforation

    Investigations¶

    X-ray: - Erect CXR: Pneumoperitoneum (free gas under diaphragm) - 75-80% - Rigler's sign: Gas on both sides of bowel wall

    CT scan: If X-ray negative (more sensitive)

    Treatment¶

    Resuscitation: - NPO, NG tube (decompress stomach) - IV fluids - Broad-spectrum antibiotics - PPI (IV)

    Surgical: Emergency laparotomy - Graham's patch repair (omental patch) - Definitive surgery: If gastric ulcer (concern for malignancy)

    NEET PG: Graham's patch (omental patch) for perforated duodenal ulcer

    ACUTE PANCREATITIS¶

    (Covered in detail in separate gastroenterology topics, key points below)

    Causes¶

    Mnemonic: I GET SMASHED - **I**diopathic - **G**allstones (most common) - **E**thanol (alcohol - 2nd most common) - **T**rauma - **S**teroids - **M**umps (& other infections) - **A**utoimmune - **S**corpion sting - **H**yperlipidemia, Hypercalcemia - **E**RCP - **D**rugs (azathioprine, valproate, thiazides)

    Practice MCQs¶

    1. Most common surgical cause of acute abdomen? Answer: Acute appendicitis

    2. Murphy's sign positive in? Answer: Acute cholecystitis

    3. Best imaging for acute abdomen? Answer: CT scan (CECT)

    4. Pneumoperitoneum best seen on? Answer: Erect chest X-ray

    5. Alvarado score ≥7 indicates? Answer: High probability of appendicitis

    6. Treatment of perforated peptic ulcer? Answer: Graham's patch (omental patch repair)

    7. Timing for cholecystectomy in acute cholecystitis? Answer: Early (<72 hours) preferred

    8. Portal venous gas indicates? Answer: Mesenteric ischemia

    Revision Checklist¶

    • Know causes by quadrant
    • Learn all special signs
    • Understand imaging findings
    • Know Alvarado score
    • Learn appendicitis management
    • Understand cholecystitis diagnosis & treatment
    • Know perforation signs
    • Memorize I GET SMASHED (pancreatitis)
    • Practice 40+ MCQs on acute abdomen
