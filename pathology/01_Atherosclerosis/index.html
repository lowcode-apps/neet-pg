    Atherosclerosis¶

    DEFINITION ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    Atherosclerosis: Progressive chronic disease characterized by intimal thickening and lipid accumulation in large and medium-sized arteries ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    Key Features: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Lipid deposition (cholesterol, LDL) in intima ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Fibrous cap formation (smooth muscle cells, collagen) ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Inflammation (macrophages, T cells) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Calcification (late stage) ⭐⭐⭐

    Arteries Affected: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Large elastic arteries: Aorta, carotid, iliac ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Medium muscular arteries: Coronary, cerebral, renal, mesenteric ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    EPIDEMIOLOGY ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    Prevalence: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Leading cause of death worldwide ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Responsible for 50% of all deaths in developed countries - Underlying cause of myocardial infarction, stroke, peripheral vascular disease ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    Age & Sex: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Begins in childhood (fatty streaks) ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Progresses with age - Males > Females (until menopause, then equal) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

    RISK FACTORS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    MODIFIABLE (Major) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    1. Hyperlipidemia: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Most important modifiable risk factor ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - ↑ LDL cholesterol ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (most atherogenic) - ↓ HDL cholesterol ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (protective; <40 mg/dL in men, <50 in women = risk) - ↑ Total cholesterol (>200 mg/dL) - ↑ Triglycerides (>150 mg/dL) - ↑ Lipoprotein(a) [Lp(a)] ⭐⭐⭐

    LDL Goals (NEET PG): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Very high risk (CAD, DM with target organ damage): <70 mg/dL ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - High risk (DM, CKD): <100 mg/dL ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Moderate risk: <130 mg/dL - Low risk: <160 mg/dL

    2. Hypertension: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Accelerates atherosclerosis ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Endothelial injury from increased shear stress - Both systolic and diastolic HTN are risk factors

    3. Smoking: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Doubles risk of MI ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Mechanisms: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Endothelial dysfunction - ↑ LDL oxidation - ↑ Platelet aggregation - ↑ Fibrinogen - ↓ HDL - Risk decreases after quitting (50% reduction in 1 year) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

    4. Diabetes Mellitus: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Doubles to quadruples CVD risk ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - DM is CAD-equivalent ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (treat as if patient already has heart disease) - Mechanisms: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Hyperglycemia → glycation of proteins - Insulin resistance → dyslipidemia - Endothelial dysfunction - Inflammation

    5. Obesity: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Central/abdominal obesity (waist circumference >102 cm men, >88 cm women) ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Associated with metabolic syndrome

    6. Sedentary Lifestyle: ⭐⭐⭐ - Lack of exercise increases risk - 30 min moderate exercise 5 days/week reduces risk

    7. Diet: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - High saturated fat, trans fat, cholesterol → ↑ LDL - High refined carbohydrates → ↑ triglycerides - Low fruits, vegetables, fiber → ↑ risk

    NON-MODIFIABLE ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    1. Age: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Men ≥45 years ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Women ≥55 years ⭐⭐⭐⭐

    2. Male Gender: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Men at higher risk until women reach menopause - Estrogen is protective (↑ HDL, ↓ LDL)

    3. Family History: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Premature CAD in first-degree relative: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Male <55 years - Female <65 years - Genetic factors (familial hypercholesterolemia)

    4. Genetics: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Familial hypercholesterolemia (FH) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - LDL receptor mutations ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Homozygous: LDL >500 mg/dL, MI in childhood ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Heterozygous: LDL 250-400 mg/dL, MI in 30s-40s ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Tendon xanthomas (Achilles, extensor tendons) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Corneal arcus (lipid deposits in cornea) ⭐⭐⭐

    EMERGING RISK FACTORS ⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    1. C-Reactive Protein (CRP): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Marker of inflammation ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - High-sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - >3 mg/L = high cardiovascular risk ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Independent predictor of MI, stroke

    2. Homocysteine: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - ↑ Homocysteine (>15 μmol/L) → endothelial injury - Causes: Folate/B12/B6 deficiency, genetic (MTHFR mutation) - Treatment: Folic acid, B12, B6 ⭐⭐⭐

    3. Lipoprotein(a): ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Lp(a) >30 mg/dL increases risk - Structural similarity to plasminogen → impairs fibrinolysis

    4. Fibrinogen: ⭐⭐⭐ - ↑ Fibrinogen → ↑ thrombosis

    5. Metabolic Syndrome: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - ≥3 of 5 criteria: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ 1. Waist circumference >102 cm (M), >88 cm (F) ⭐⭐⭐⭐ 2. Triglycerides ≥150 mg/dL ⭐⭐⭐⭐ 3. HDL <40 mg/dL (M), <50 mg/dL (F) ⭐⭐⭐⭐ 4. BP ≥130/85 mmHg ⭐⭐⭐⭐ 5. Fasting glucose ≥100 mg/dL ⭐⭐⭐⭐

    PATHOGENESIS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    RESPONSE-TO-INJURY HYPOTHESIS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    Current Paradigm: Atherosclerosis is a chronic inflammatory response to endothelial injury ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    STAGES OF ATHEROSCLEROSIS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    1. Endothelial Injury/Dysfunction: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Initiating event ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Causes: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Hemodynamic stress (hypertension, turbulent flow) - Oxidized LDL (oxLDL) - Toxins (cigarette smoke) - Hyperglycemia - Infections (CMV, Chlamydia pneumoniae - controversial) - Consequences: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - ↑ Permeability to lipoproteins ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - ↑ Leukocyte adhesion (upregulation of VCAM-1, ICAM-1) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - ↓ NO production (vasoconstrictor, prothrombotic) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

    2. Lipid Accumulation: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - LDL enters intima (especially at sites of endothelial injury) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - LDL oxidation by free radicals → oxLDL ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - oxLDL is atherogenic: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Chemotactic for monocytes - Toxic to endothelium - Taken up by macrophages (not regulated)

    3. Monocyte Adhesion & Migration: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Monocytes adhere to endothelium (via VCAM-1) ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Migrate into intima (via MCP-1) ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Differentiate into macrophages ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    4. Foam Cell Formation: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Macrophages engulf oxLDL via scavenger receptors (CD36, SR-A) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Unregulated uptake → lipid-laden macrophages = FOAM CELLS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Hallmark of early atherosclerosis ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    5. Fatty Streak Formation: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Earliest lesion (visible by age 10) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Yellow linear elevation on intimal surface - Composed of: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Foam cells (lipid-laden macrophages) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - T lymphocytes - Clinically silent (does not obstruct flow) ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Reversible at this stage ⭐⭐⭐⭐

    6. Smooth Muscle Cell (SMC) Migration & Proliferation: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Growth factors (PDGF, FGF) from macrophages/platelets ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - SMCs migrate from media → intima ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - SMCs proliferate and synthesize ECM (collagen, elastin, proteoglycans) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    7. Fibrous Plaque (Atheroma) Formation: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Advanced lesion (by 3rd-4th decade) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Components: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Fibrous cap: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Smooth muscle cells + dense collagen - Covers the plaque - Lipid core (necrotic center): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Cholesterol esters, foam cells (dead) - Cell debris, calcium - Shoulder region: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Edge of plaque - Active inflammation (macrophages, T cells) - Site of rupture ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    Structure: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ 

        Lumen
      ↓
  [Fibrous Cap] ← SMCs + Collagen
  [Lipid Core]  ← Cholesterol, Foam Cells, Necrosis
  [Shoulder]    ← Inflammation (Macrophages, T cells)
      ↓
    Media

    8. Complicated Plaque: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Advanced lesions with superimposed changes: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Plaque rupture/fissuring ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Thrombosis ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Hemorrhage into plaque ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Calcification ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Aneurysm formation ⭐⭐⭐

    PLAQUE STABILITY ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    STABLE vs UNSTABLE PLAQUES ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    Stable (Fibrotic) Plaque: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Thick fibrous cap ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Small lipid core ⭐⭐⭐ - Dense collagen - Chronic, stable angina ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Gradual narrowing → predictable symptoms

    Unstable (Vulnerable) Plaque: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Thin fibrous cap (<65 μm) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Large lipid core (>40% of plaque) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Active inflammation (macrophages, T cells at shoulder) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - ↓ SMCs, ↓ collagen ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Prone to rupture ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Acute coronary syndromes (unstable angina, MI) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    Plaque Rupture: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Triggers acute thrombosis ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Exposure of subendothelial collagen → platelet adhesion/activation → thrombus - Mechanism of acute MI, sudden cardiac death ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - NOT always related to degree of stenosis ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - 70% of MIs occur from plaques causing <50% stenosis ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    CONSEQUENCES OF ATHEROSCLEROSIS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    1. Chronic Ischemia: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Gradual luminal narrowing ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Critical stenosis: >70% occlusion → symptoms at rest ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Stable angina (coronary), intermittent claudication (peripheral)

    2. Acute Plaque Change: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Plaque rupture → thrombosis ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Acute MI, unstable angina, sudden death ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Stroke (carotid), mesenteric ischemia

    3. Thromboembolism: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Thrombus forms on plaque → embolizes distally - Stroke (from carotid/aortic plaques)

    4. Aneurysm Formation: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Weakening of arterial wall (medial atrophy, loss of elastic tissue) - Abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    SITES OF ATHEROSCLEROSIS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    Predilection for Branching Points & Curvatures ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Areas of turbulent flow, low shear stress

    Common Sites: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ 1. Abdominal aorta (most common) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ 2. Coronary arteries (LAD > RCA > LCx) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ 3. Carotid bifurcation ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ 4. Circle of Willis ⭐⭐⭐⭐ 5. Iliac, femoral, popliteal arteries ⭐⭐⭐⭐

    CLINICAL MANIFESTATIONS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    Depends on Artery Involved: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    Coronary Arteries: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Stable angina ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Acute coronary syndrome (unstable angina, MI) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Sudden cardiac death ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    Cerebral Arteries: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - TIA (transient ischemic attack) ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Ischemic stroke ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    Peripheral Arteries: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Intermittent claudication ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Rest pain, gangrene ⭐⭐⭐⭐

    Renal Arteries: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Renovascular hypertension ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Ischemic nephropathy ⭐⭐⭐

    Mesenteric Arteries: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Chronic mesenteric ischemia ("intestinal angina") ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Acute mesenteric ischemia ⭐⭐⭐⭐

    Aorta: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Abdominal aortic aneurysm (AAA) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    DIAGNOSIS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    Non-invasive: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Lipid profile ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, TG) - hs-CRP ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (inflammation marker) - Ankle-brachial index (ABI) ⭐⭐⭐⭐ (<0.9 = peripheral artery disease) - Carotid ultrasound (carotid IMT - intima-media thickness) ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Coronary calcium score (CT) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Agatston score >400 = high risk - Stress testing (exercise ECG, stress echo, nuclear) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

    Invasive: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Coronary angiography ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (gold standard for coronary artery disease) - Intravascular ultrasound (IVUS) ⭐⭐⭐ - Optical coherence tomography (OCT) ⭐⭐⭐

    PREVENTION & TREATMENT ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    PRIMARY PREVENTION ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    Lifestyle Modification: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Smoking cessation ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Diet: Low saturated fat, trans fat, cholesterol; high fruits, vegetables, whole grains, fish ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Exercise: 30 min moderate activity 5 days/week ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Weight loss (if overweight/obese) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

    Pharmacologic: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    1. Statins (HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - First-line for LDL reduction ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - ↓ LDL 30-50% ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Pleiotropic effects: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Stabilize plaques - Anti-inflammatory (↓ CRP) - Improve endothelial function - Examples: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Atorvastatin (most potent) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Rosuvastatin ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Simvastatin, Pravastatin - Side effects: Myalgias, ↑ LFTs, rhabdomyolysis (rare) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

    2. Aspirin: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Antiplatelet (irreversible COX-1 inhibition) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - 75-100 mg daily ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Primary prevention: Controversial (benefit vs bleeding risk) - Secondary prevention: All patients with CAD/CVD ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    3. Antihypertensives: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Target BP <140/90 (general), <130/80 (DM, CKD) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - ACE inhibitors/ARBs preferred (especially if DM, CKD) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    4. Diabetes Control: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Target HbA1c <7% ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    5. Other Lipid-Lowering Agents: ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Ezetimibe (cholesterol absorption inhibitor) ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Add to statin if LDL not at goal - PCSK9 inhibitors (evolocumab, alirocumab) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Monoclonal antibodies - Dramatically ↓ LDL (50-60% additional reduction) - Very high-risk patients, familial hypercholesterolemia - Expensive - Fibrates (gemfibrozil, fenofibrate) ⭐⭐⭐ - ↓ Triglycerides, ↑ HDL ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Used if TG >500 mg/dL (pancreatitis risk) - Niacin (nicotinic acid) ⭐⭐⭐ - ↓ LDL, ↑ HDL, ↓ TG - Side effect: Flushing (prostaglandin-mediated)

    SECONDARY PREVENTION ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    After MI, Stroke, or Established CAD/CVD: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    Aggressive Risk Factor Management: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - High-intensity statin ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ (atorvastatin 40-80 mg, rosuvastatin 20-40 mg) - Aspirin 75-100 mg daily ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - ACE inhibitor (all CAD patients, especially if LV dysfunction) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Beta-blocker (post-MI, heart failure) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Blood pressure control ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Diabetes control ⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Smoking cessation ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ - Lifestyle modification ⭐⭐⭐⭐

    HIGH-YIELD POINTS FOR NEET PG ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    1. Atherosclerosis is a chronic inflammatory disease ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
    2. Fatty streak = earliest lesion (foam cells + T cells), reversible ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
    3. Foam cells = lipid-laden macrophages (oxLDL via scavenger receptors) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
    4. Unstable plaque = thin cap + large lipid core + inflammation → rupture → MI ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
    5. 70% of MIs from plaques <50% stenosis ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
    6. LDL is atherogenic, HDL is protective ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
    7. Diabetes = CAD equivalent (treat aggressively) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
    8. Familial hypercholesterolemia = LDL receptor defect, tendon xanthomas ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
    9. Statins = first-line for LDL reduction, pleiotropic effects ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
    10. Metabolic syndrome = ≥3 of 5 criteria (waist, TG, HDL, BP, glucose) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

    MNEMONICS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    Major Modifiable Risk Factors: "**H**igh **L**ipids **H**igh **S**ugar **D**amage **S**mokers" - **H**ypertension - **L**ipidemia - **H**igh sugar (Diabetes) - **D**iet/obesity - **S**moking

    Metabolic Syndrome (5 criteria): "**W**aist **T**riglycerides **H**DL **B**P **G**lucose" - **W**aist >102/88 cm - **T**G ≥150 - **H**DL <40/50 - **B**P ≥130/85 - **G**lucose ≥100

    Unstable Plaque Features: "**T**hin cap, **L**arge core, **I**nflammation"

    NEET PG MCQS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐¶

    Q1. Earliest microscopic change in atherosclerosis? A) Foam cell formation B) Smooth muscle proliferation C) Calcification D) Thrombosis

    Answer: A - Foam cells (lipid-laden macrophages) form fatty streaks - the earliest visible lesion

    Q2. Characteristic feature of unstable atherosclerotic plaque? A) Thick fibrous cap B) Small lipid core C) Thin fibrous cap D) Dense calcification

    Answer: C - Unstable plaque has thin fibrous cap (<65 μm) + large lipid core + inflammation → prone to rupture

    Q3. LDL goal in patient with diabetes mellitus and prior MI? A) <160 mg/dL B) <130 mg/dL C) <100 mg/dL D) <70 mg/dL

    Answer: D - Very high-risk patient (DM + CAD) → LDL goal <70 mg/dL

    Q4. Patient with recurrent MI at age 30, tendon xanthomas, corneal arcus. Most likely diagnosis? A) Metabolic syndrome B) Familial hypercholesterolemia C) Type 2 DM D) Hypothyroidism

    Answer: B - Familial hypercholesterolemia (FH) - premature CAD + tendon xanthomas + corneal arcus + very high LDL

    Q5. Mechanism of foam cell formation in atherosclerosis? A) LDL uptake via LDL receptors B) Oxidized LDL uptake via scavenger receptors C) HDL uptake D) VLDL uptake

    Answer: B - Macrophages take up oxidized LDL (oxLDL) via scavenger receptors (CD36, SR-A) - unregulated → foam cells
