    INFLAMMATION - NEET PG Study Material¶

    Definition¶

    Inflammation: Complex biological response to harmful stimuli (pathogens, damaged cells, irritants)

    Cardinal Signs (Celsus + Virchow): 1. Rubor (Redness) - Vasodilation 2. Tumor (Swelling) - Exudation 3. Calor (Heat) - ↑ Blood flow 4. Dolor (Pain) - Mediators (bradykinin, PGE2) 5. Functio laesa (Loss of function) - Combined effect

    NEET PG: 5 cardinal signs of inflammation

    ACUTE INFLAMMATION¶

    Duration: Minutes to days Hallmark: Neutrophils (PMNs) Vascular changes + Cellular infiltration

    Vascular Changes - SEQUENCE¶

    1. Transient Vasoconstriction¶

    Duration: Few seconds Mechanism: Neurogenic

    2. Vasodilation¶

    Duration: Sustained Mediators: Histamine, nitric oxide, prostaglandins Result: ↑ Blood flow → Redness & heat

    3. Increased Vascular Permeability¶

    Mechanism: Endothelial contraction, retraction, direct injury Result: Exudation of fluid → Edema & swelling

    Stages of increased permeability: 1. Immediate transient (5-30 min): Histamine, bradykinin (most common) 2. Immediate sustained (hours): Severe injury 3. Delayed prolonged (2-12 hours): Sunburn, x-ray, bacterial toxins

    NEET PG: Histamine causes immediate transient increase in permeability

    Types of Exudate¶

    Serous Exudate¶

    Composition: Low protein (<3 g/dl), few cells Examples: Skin blister, pleural effusion (viral pleuritis)

    Fibrinous Exudate¶

    Composition: High fibrin content Examples: Fibrinous pericarditis, lobar pneumonia (pneumococcus) Organization: Scarring (adhesions)

    NEET PG: Fibrinous exudate → Organization → Adhesions

    Purulent Exudate (Suppurative)¶

    Composition: Pus (neutrophils + necrotic debris + bacteria) Examples: Abscess, empyema, pyogenic infections Organisms: Staph aureus, Strep pyogenes, E. coli

    Abscess: Localized collection of pus Empyema: Pus in body cavity (pleural, peritoneal)

    Hemorrhagic Exudate¶

    Composition: RBCs + other inflammatory cells Examples: Necrotizing infections (plague, anthrax), viral hemorrhagic fevers

    Cellular Events - HIGH YIELD¶

    Leukocyte Extravasation (5 Steps)¶

    1. Margination¶

    Definition: Leukocytes move to periphery of blood vessel Mechanism: Slowing of blood flow (vasodilation, ↑ viscosity)

    2. Rolling¶

    Molecules: Selectins (E-selectin, P-selectin on endothelium; L-selectin on leukocytes) Ligands: Sialyl-Lewis X (on leukocytes) Result: Leukocytes roll along endothelium

    NEET PG: Selectins mediate rolling

    3. Adhesion (Firm attachment)¶

    Molecules: - Integrins on leukocytes (LFA-1, Mac-1, VLA-4) - ICAM-1, VCAM-1 on endothelium

    Upregulated by: Cytokines (TNF, IL-1), bacterial products (LPS)

    NEET PG: Integrins (LFA-1, Mac-1) mediate firm adhesion

    4. Transmigration (Diapedesis)¶

    Definition: Migration through endothelium (between cells) Molecules: PECAM-1 (CD31) at intercellular junctions

    5. Chemotaxis¶

    Definition: Directional migration toward chemical gradient

    Chemotactic Agents: - Bacterial products: fMLP (N-formyl-methionyl peptides), LPS - Complement: C5a (most potent) - Cytokines: IL-8, chemokines - Lipids: LTB4 (leukotriene B4)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - C5a: Most potent chemotactic agent - LTB4: Lipid chemotactic agent - IL-8: Chemokine for neutrophils

    Leukocyte Activation & Phagocytosis¶

    Recognition & Attachment¶

    Mechanisms: 1. Opsonization (coating of particle): - IgG (Fc receptor) - C3b (complement receptor CR1) 2. Direct binding: Mannose receptors, scavenger receptors

    NEET PG: Opsonization enhances phagocytosis (IgG & C3b = main opsonins)

    Engulfment¶

    Process: Pseudopods surround particle → Phagosome formation

    Killing & Degradation¶

    A. Oxygen-Dependent (Respiratory Burst)

    NADPH oxidase system: 

    NADPH + 2O2 → 2O2- (superoxide) + NADP+ + H+
         ↓ (Superoxide dismutase)
        H2O2 (hydrogen peroxide)
         ↓ (Myeloperoxidase + Cl-)
        HOCl (hypochlorous acid) - MOST POTENT

    NEET PG: - NADPH oxidase: Generates superoxide (respiratory burst) - Myeloperoxidase (MPO): H2O2 + Cl- → HOCl (bleach) - HOCl: Most potent bactericidal agent

    Chronic Granulomatous Disease (CGD): - Defect: NADPH oxidase (X-linked or autosomal recessive) - Features: Recurrent catalase-positive bacterial/fungal infections (Staph, Serratia, Aspergillus, Nocardia) - Test: NBT test (nitroblue tetrazolium) - negative

    NEET PG: CGD = Defective NADPH oxidase, recurrent Staph infections, negative NBT test

    B. Oxygen-Independent - Lysozyme: Degrades bacterial cell wall - Lactoferrin: Binds iron - Defensins: Antimicrobial peptides - Proteases: Elastase, cathepsins

    Leukocyte Adhesion Deficiency (LAD)¶

    LAD-1: - Defect: CD18 (β2 integrin) deficiency - Affected molecules: LFA-1, Mac-1, CR3 - Features: Recurrent bacterial infections, delayed umbilical cord separation, leukocytosis (no margination), poor wound healing - Diagnosis: Flow cytometry (absent CD18)

    LAD-2: - Defect: Sialyl-Lewis X (selectin ligand) - Features: Similar to LAD-1 + mental retardation

    NEET PG High-Yield: - LAD-1: Defective β2 integrin (CD18) - Features: Delayed cord separation, leukocytosis, recurrent infections - No pus formation (neutrophils can't migrate)

    Chemical Mediators of Inflammation - HIGH YIELD¶

    Cell-Derived Mediators¶

    A. Vasoactive Amines¶

    1. Histamine Source: Mast cells, basophils, platelets Stored: Preformed in granules Release: IgE (Type I hypersensitivity), C3a, C5a, trauma, cold Actions: - Vasodilation (H1 & H2 receptors) - ↑ Vascular permeability (H1 - venules) - Bronchoconstriction (H1) Duration: Short-lived (degraded by histaminase)

    2. Serotonin (5-HT) Source: Platelets, mast cells (rodents) Actions: Similar to histamine

    NEET PG: Histamine = Immediate transient permeability (first few minutes)

    B. Arachidonic Acid Metabolites¶

    Source: Cell membrane phospholipids (via phospholipase A2)

    Two pathways:

    1. Cyclooxygenase (COX) Pathway → Prostaglandins & Thromboxanes¶

    COX-1: Constitutive (gastric mucosa, platelets, kidneys) COX-2: Inducible (inflammatory cells)

    Products: - PGE2, PGI2 (Prostacyclin): - Vasodilation - Potentiate edema (with histamine) - Pain & fever (PGE2) - TXA2 (Thromboxane A2): - Vasoconstriction - Platelet aggregation

    NEET PG: - NSAIDs: Inhibit COX → ↓ PGE2 (anti-inflammatory, antipyretic, analgesic) - Aspirin: Irreversibly inhibits COX (antiplatelet effect - TXA2) - COX-2 selective: Less GI side effects (celecoxib)

    2. Lipoxygenase Pathway → Leukotrienes¶

    5-LOX (in leukocytes): - LTB4: Chemotaxis & leukocyte adhesion (most potent) - LTC4, LTD4, LTE4 (slow-reacting substance of anaphylaxis): - Bronchoconstriction - Vasoconstriction - ↑ Vascular permeability

    NEET PG High-Yield: - LTB4: Neutrophil chemotaxis - LTC4, LTD4, LTE4: Bronchoconstriction (asthma) - Zileuton: 5-LOX inhibitor - Montelukast: Leukotriene receptor antagonist (asthma)

    C. Cytokines¶

    1. TNF (Tumor Necrosis Factor) & IL-1 Source: Activated macrophages Actions: - Endothelial activation (↑ selectins, integrins) - Fever (act on hypothalamus) - Acute phase proteins (liver) - Systemic effects (shock in high doses)

    2. IL-6 Actions: Acute phase protein synthesis (CRP, fibrinogen)

    3. Chemokines IL-8 (CXCL8): Neutrophil chemotaxis MCP-1: Monocyte chemotaxis

    NEET PG: TNF & IL-1 = Major pro-inflammatory cytokines

    D. Nitric Oxide (NO)¶

    Source: Endothelial cells, macrophages Enzyme: Nitric oxide synthase (NOS) - eNOS: Constitutive (endothelium) - iNOS: Inducible (macrophages, inflammation)

    Actions: - Vasodilation (smooth muscle relaxation) - Antimicrobial (in macrophages) - Inhibits platelet aggregation

    NEET PG: NO = Endothelium-derived relaxing factor (EDRF)

    Plasma-Derived Mediators¶

    A. Complement System - HIGH YIELD¶

    Activation pathways: 1. Classical: Antigen-antibody complexes (IgG, IgM) 2. Alternative: Bacterial cell walls (LPS, endotoxin) - NO antibody 3. Lectin: Mannose-binding lectin (MBL)

    All converge at: C3 convertase → C3a + C3b

    Functions: - C3a, C5a: Anaphylatoxins (histamine release, chemotaxis, vasodilation) - C5a: Most potent (chemotaxis >> C3a) - C3b: Opsonization (enhance phagocytosis) - C5b-9: Membrane attack complex (MAC) (cell lysis)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - C5a: Most potent anaphylatoxin & chemotactic agent - C3b: Main opsonin - MAC (C5b-9): Lysis of Gram-negative bacteria, Neisseria

    Complement deficiencies: - C1, C2, C4: SLE-like syndrome - C3: Recurrent pyogenic infections - C5-C9: Recurrent Neisseria infections - C1 inhibitor: Hereditary angioedema

    NEET PG: C5-C9 deficiency = Recurrent Neisseria meningitidis infections

    B. Kinin System¶

    Hageman factor (Factor XII) activation → Prekallikrein → Kallikrein → High molecular weight kininogen (HMWK) → Bradykinin

    Bradykinin: - ↑ Vascular permeability - Vasodilation - Pain (most potent pain mediator) - Bronchoconstriction

    Degraded by: ACE (angiotensin-converting enzyme)

    NEET PG: Bradykinin = Pain mediator, degraded by ACE

    C. Coagulation & Fibrinolytic Systems¶

    Factor XII (Hageman factor) activates: - Coagulation cascade → Thrombin - Kinin system → Bradykinin - Fibrinolytic system → Plasmin

    Thrombin: Converts fibrinogen → Fibrin (fibrinous exudate)

    NEET PG: Factor XII links inflammation, coagulation, kinin, fibrinolytic systems

    Outcomes of Acute Inflammation¶

    1. Complete resolution: Removal of stimulus, no tissue damage
    2. Healing by scarring (fibrosis): Substantial tissue destruction
    3. Abscess formation: Purulent inflammation (pyogenic bacteria)
    4. Progression to chronic inflammation: Persistent stimulus

    NEET PG: Resolution = Best outcome (restitutio ad integrum)

    CHRONIC INFLAMMATION¶

    Duration: Weeks to years Hallmark: Mononuclear cells (lymphocytes, macrophages, plasma cells) Tissue destruction + Repair (fibrosis, angiogenesis)

    Causes¶

    1. Persistent infections: TB, syphilis, fungi, viruses
    2. Prolonged toxic exposure: Silica (silicosis), asbestos (asbestosis)
    3. Autoimmune diseases: RA, SLE, IBD
    4. Unknown: Sarcoidosis, Crohn's disease

    NEET PG: Chronic inflammation can start de novo (NOT always from acute)

    Cellular Players¶

    1. Macrophages (Most Important)¶

    Derived from: Blood monocytes Activated by: IFN-γ (from T cells), bacterial products, cytokines

    Functions: - Phagocytosis - Antigen presentation (MHC II) - Cytokine secretion: IL-1, IL-6, TNF, IL-12 - Growth factors: PDGF, FGF, TGF-β (fibrosis)

    Products cause: - Tissue injury (ROS, proteases) - Fibrosis (TGF-β, PDGF)

    Activated macrophages → Epithelioid cells (in granulomas)

    NEET PG: Macrophage = Central cell in chronic inflammation

    2. Lymphocytes¶

    T cells: Cell-mediated immunity, activate macrophages (IFN-γ) B cells: Antibody production → Plasma cells

    3. Plasma Cells¶

    Function: Antibody secretion Seen in: Chronic inflammation, autoimmune diseases

    4. Eosinophils¶

    Seen in: Parasitic infections, allergic reactions, tumors (Hodgkin lymphoma) Products: Major basic protein (MBP), eosinophil cationic protein

    5. Mast Cells¶

    Products: Histamine, heparin, tryptase, cytokines

    NEET PG: Mast cells = Type I hypersensitivity (IgE-mediated)

    GRANULOMATOUS INFLAMMATION - HIGH YIELD¶

    Definition: Chronic inflammation with epithelioid cell granulomas

    Granuloma: Focal collection of activated macrophages (epithelioid cells) ± giant cells

    Types¶

    1. Foreign Body Granuloma¶

    Cause: Inert foreign material (sutures, talc, silica) Giant cells: Foreign body giant cells (haphazard nuclei)

    2. Immune Granuloma¶

    Cause: T cell-mediated immunity (poorly degradable antigens) Examples: TB, sarcoidosis, leprosy, Crohn's disease, fungal infections

    Structure: - Center: Epithelioid cells (activated macrophages with abundant pink cytoplasm) - Periphery: Lymphocytes, fibroblasts - Giant cells: Langhans giant cells (horseshoe-shaped nuclei at periphery) - Caseous necrosis: TB (caseating granuloma)

    NEET PG High-Yield: - Caseating granuloma: TB, histoplasmosis - Non-caseating granuloma: Sarcoidosis, Crohn's, leprosy, cat-scratch disease - Langhans giant cells: TB, sarcoidosis (nuclei at periphery) - Foreign body giant cells: Nuclei randomly distributed

    Specific Granulomatous Diseases¶

    Disease Type Features
    Tuberculosis Caseating Langhans cells, central caseous necrosis, acid-fast bacilli
    Sarcoidosis Non-caseating Schaumann bodies, asteroid bodies, epithelioid granulomas
    Leprosy Non-caseating Foamy macrophages (lepra cells in lepromatous), no giant cells
    Cat-scratch disease Necrotizing Stellate abscess, Bartonella henselae
    Crohn's disease Non-caseating Transmural inflammation, fissures

    NEET PG: - Schaumann bodies: Sarcoidosis (lamellated calcified structures) - Asteroid bodies: Sarcoidosis (star-shaped inclusions in giant cells)

    Systemic Effects of Inflammation¶

    1. Fever¶

    Pyrogens: - Exogenous: Bacterial LPS (endotoxin) - Endogenous: IL-1, IL-6, TNF

    Mechanism: Act on hypothalamus → ↑ PGE2 → ↑ Temp set point

    NEET PG: NSAIDs reduce fever by inhibiting PGE2 synthesis

    2. Acute Phase Response¶

    Mediators: IL-6, IL-1, TNF Liver produces: - Positive acute phase proteins (↑): CRP, fibrinogen, serum amyloid A (SAA), haptoglobin, ferritin - Negative acute phase proteins (↓): Albumin, transferrin

    CRP (C-reactive protein): - Opsonin (binds C-polysaccharide of pneumococcus) - Marker of inflammation - Normal <1 mg/dL

    ESR (Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate): - ↑ in inflammation (due to ↑ fibrinogen)

    NEET PG: - CRP: Best acute phase reactant (rises within 6 hours) - ESR: Slow to rise, non-specific

    3. Leukocytosis¶

    Neutrophilia: Bacterial infections Eosinophilia: Parasites, allergies Lymphocytosis: Viral infections, chronic inflammation

    Left shift: ↑ Band forms (immature neutrophils) in blood → Indicates acute bacterial infection

    NEET PG: Left shift = Acute bacterial infection

    Practice MCQs¶

    1. Hallmark cell of acute inflammation? Answer: Neutrophils

    2. Most potent chemotactic agent? Answer: C5a

    3. Mediator of rolling in leukocyte extravasation? Answer: Selectins

    4. Mediator of firm adhesion? Answer: Integrins (LFA-1, Mac-1)

    5. Defect in Chronic Granulomatous Disease? Answer: NADPH oxidase

    6. Hallmark of chronic inflammation? Answer: Mononuclear cells (macrophages, lymphocytes, plasma cells)

    7. Caseating granuloma seen in? Answer: Tuberculosis

    8. Best acute phase reactant? Answer: CRP
