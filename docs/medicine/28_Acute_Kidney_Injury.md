## Definition and Classification

### Definition

**Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)**: Rapid decline in kidney function

**KDIGO Criteria (2012)** - AKI if ANY of the following:
1. **Serum creatinine ↑ ≥0.3 mg/dL within 48 hours** OR
2. **Serum creatinine ↑ ≥1.5× baseline within 7 days** OR
3. **Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hr for 6 hours**

### Staging (KDIGO)

| Stage | **Serum Creatinine** | **Urine Output** |
|-------|---------------------|------------------|
| **1** | 1.5-1.9× baseline OR ↑ ≥0.3 mg/dL | <0.5 mL/kg/hr × 6-12 hrs |
| **2** | 2.0-2.9× baseline | <0.5 mL/kg/hr × ≥12 hrs |
| **3** | ≥3× baseline OR ↑ to ≥4 mg/dL OR Initiation of RRT | <0.3 mL/kg/hr × ≥24 hrs OR Anuria × 12 hrs |

**Higher stage** = Worse prognosis

## Etiology

### Anatomic Classification

**Mnemonic: "Pre-Renal-Post"**

**1. Prerenal (60-70%)** - Decreased renal perfusion
**2. Intrinsic Renal (25-40%)** - Kidney parenchymal damage
**3. Postrenal (5-10%)** - Urinary tract obstruction

---

### Prerenal AKI

**Definition**: ↓ Renal perfusion → ↓ GFR (kidneys structurally NORMAL)

**Causes**:

**Hypovolemia** (True volume depletion):
- **Hemorrhage**: GI bleeding, trauma, surgery
- **GI losses**: Vomiting, diarrhea, NG suction
- **Renal losses**: Diuretics, osmotic diuresis (DKA, hyperglycemia), diabetes insipidus
- **Third-spacing**: Pancreatitis, burns, cirrhosis (ascites), nephrotic syndrome
- **Insensible losses**: Fever, sweating

**Decreased Effective Arterial Blood Volume**:
- **Heart failure** (↓ cardiac output)
- **Cirrhosis** (splanchnic vasodilation, ↓ effective volume)
- **Sepsis** (vasodilation)

**Renal Vasoconstriction**:
- **NSAIDs**: Inhibit prostaglandin → afferent arteriole constriction
- **ACE inhibitors / ARBs**: Efferent arteriole dilation → ↓ GFR (especially if renal artery stenosis, volume depletion)
- **Hepatorenal syndrome**
- **Contrast-induced nephropathy**

**Large Vessel Disease**:
- **Renal artery stenosis** (bilateral or solitary kidney)
- **Renal artery thrombosis/embolism**
- **Abdominal aortic aneurysm**

---

### Intrinsic Renal AKI

**Definition**: Direct kidney parenchymal injury

**Classification by Anatomic Site**:

#### **A. Tubular (Most Common Intrinsic)**

**Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN)** - 80-90% of intrinsic AKI:

**Ischemic ATN** (Most common):
- **Prolonged prerenal state** → tubular cell death
- **Causes**: Shock (septic, cardiogenic, hypovolemic), surgery, severe hypotension

**Toxic (Nephrotoxic) ATN**:
- **Exogenous**:
  - **Aminoglycosides** (gentamicin, tobramycin)
  - **Amphotericin B**
  - **Radiocontrast** (iodinated contrast)
  - **Cisplatin** (chemotherapy)
  - **Tenofovir** (antiretroviral)
  - **Heavy metals**: Lead, mercury
  - **Ethylene glycol**, methanol
- **Endogenous**:
  - **Myoglobin** (rhabdomyolysis)
  - **Hemoglobin** (hemolysis)
  - **Light chains** (multiple myeloma - "cast nephropathy")
  - **Uric acid** (tumor lysis syndrome)

---

#### **B. Interstitial**

**Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN)**:
- **Inflammation of renal interstitium**

**Causes**:
- **Drugs** (75% of cases):
  - **Antibiotics**: Penicillins (methicillin), cephalosporins, fluoroquinolones, rifampin, sulfonamides
  - **PPIs** (omeprazole, pantoprazole) - increasing cause
  - **NSAIDs**
  - **Allopurinol**
  - **5-ASA**
- **Infections**: Pyelonephritis, viral (CMV, EBV), Legionella
- **Autoimmune**: SLE, Sjögren's, sarcoidosis, IgG4-related disease
- **Idiopathic**

**Classic Triad** (only 10-30% have all 3):
- **Fever**
- **Rash**
- **Eosinophilia**

---

#### **C. Glomerular**

**Rapidly Progressive Glomerulonephritis (RPGN)**:
- **Crescentic GN** on biopsy
- **Rapid loss** of kidney function (days to weeks)

**Causes** (by immunofluorescence pattern):
1. **Anti-GBM disease** (Goodpasture's): Linear IF
2. **Immune complex GN**: Granular IF (post-infectious, lupus nephritis, IgA nephropathy)
3. **Pauci-immune GN**: Negative IF (ANCA-associated vasculitis)

---

#### **D. Vascular**

**Large Vessel**:
- **Renal artery thrombosis/embolism**: Atrial fibrillation, endocarditis
- **Renal vein thrombosis**: Nephrotic syndrome, hypercoagulable state
- **Aortic dissection**

**Small Vessel**:
- **Thrombotic Microangiopathy (TMA)**:
  - **HUS** (Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome): Shiga toxin (E. coli O157:H7), atypical (complement-mediated)
  - **TTP** (Thrombotic Thrombocytopenic Purpura): ADAMTS13 deficiency
- **Malignant hypertension**
- **Scleroderma renal crisis**
- **Atheroembolic disease** (cholesterol emboli - post-catheterization)

---

### Postrenal AKI

**Definition**: Urinary tract obstruction

**Causes**:

**Bladder Outlet**:
- **BPH** (benign prostatic hyperplasia) - most common
- **Prostate cancer**
- **Bladder cancer**
- **Neurogenic bladder**
- **Urethral stricture**

**Bilateral Ureteral Obstruction** (or single kidney):
- **Retroperitoneal fibrosis**
- **Pelvic malignancy** (cervical, ovarian, colorectal)
- **Bilateral stones** (rare)
- **Papillary necrosis**: Diabetes, sickle cell, NSAIDs

**Intrarenal Obstruction**:
- **Crystals**: Uric acid (tumor lysis), oxalate (ethylene glycol), sulfonamides, acyclovir, methotrexate
- **Light chains** (myeloma cast nephropathy)

---

## Pathophysiology

### Prerenal AKI

**Mechanism**:
```
↓ Renal perfusion
      ↓
↓ GFR (reversible if perfusion restored)
      ↓
Tubular function INTACT
      ↓
Avid Na⁺ and water reabsorption (trying to restore volume)
      ↓
↑ BUN (reabsorbed), ↑ Creatinine (less reabsorbed)
      ↓
BUN:Cr ratio >20:1
```

**Key**: **Kidneys are structurally normal** → responds to volume/perfusion restoration

---

### Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN)

**Ischemic ATN**:
```
Prolonged ischemia
      ↓
Tubular cell injury/death
      ↓
Loss of brush border, cell sloughing
      ↓
Tubular obstruction (cellular debris)
      ↓
Backleak of filtrate
      ↓
↓ GFR
```

**Phases**:
1. **Initiation** (hours to days): Injury occurs
2. **Maintenance** (1-2 weeks): Established AKI, oliguria
3. **Recovery** (days to weeks): Tubular repair, polyuria (unable to concentrate urine)

**Nephrotoxic ATN**:
- **Direct tubular cell toxicity**
- **Proximal tubule** most vulnerable (high metabolic demand)

---

### Acute Interstitial Nephritis

**Mechanism**:
- **Type IV hypersensitivity** (cell-mediated)
- **Interstitial inflammation** (lymphocytes, eosinophils)
- **Tubular dysfunction**

---

### Postrenal AKI

**Mechanism**:
```
Urinary obstruction
      ↓
↑ Intratubular pressure
      ↓
↓ GFR
      ↓
If prolonged: Tubular damage, fibrosis
```

**Reversibility**: Depends on duration
- **<1 week**: Fully reversible
- **>12 weeks**: Irreversible damage

---

## Clinical Features and Diagnosis

### Clinical Presentation

**Symptoms** (Often asymptomatic - discovered on labs):
- **Oliguria** (<400 mL/day) or **anuria** (<100 mL/day)
- **Nausea, vomiting** (uremia)
- **Confusion** (uremia, electrolytes)
- **Edema** (fluid overload)
- **Dyspnea** (pulmonary edema)

**Signs**:
- **Volume status**: Hypovolemia (prerenal) vs overload (oliguric ATN)
- **Rash** (AIN, vasculitis)
- **Palpable bladder** (obstruction)

---

### Diagnostic Approach

#### **Step 1: Confirm AKI**

**Serum Creatinine**:
- **↑ ≥0.3 mg/dL in 48 hours** OR **≥1.5× baseline**
- **Limitation**: Lags behind actual GFR (takes time to equilibrate)

**Urine Output**:
- **<0.5 mL/kg/hr × 6 hours**

**Baseline Creatinine**:
- Use **lowest creatinine** in past 3 months (if available)
- If unknown: **Estimate** from MDRD equation assuming GFR 75 mL/min

---

#### **Step 2: Differentiate Prerenal vs Intrinsic vs Postrenal**

**History**:
- **Prerenal**: Vomiting, diarrhea, bleeding, poor oral intake, diuretics, heart failure, cirrhosis
- **Intrinsic**: Hypotension, nephrotoxins (drugs, contrast, rhabdomyolysis), systemic disease (vasculitis, lupus)
- **Postrenal**: BPH, pelvic cancer, difficulty urinating

**Physical Exam**:
- **Volume status**: JVP, skin turgor, mucous membranes, edema
- **Bladder**: Palpable (obstruction), percussion
- **Skin**: Livedo reticularis (atheroemboli, vasculitis)

---

**Urinalysis and Microscopy** (CRITICAL):

| Finding | Diagnosis |
|---------|-----------|
| **Hyaline casts** | Prerenal, normal |
| **Muddy brown/granular casts** | **ATN** |
| **RBC casts** | **Glomerulonephritis** |
| **WBC casts** | **Pyelonephritis, AIN** |
| **Eosinophils** | **AIN** (urine eosinophils - low sensitivity) |
| **Crystals** | Uric acid, oxalate, drug crystals |
| **Proteinuria (>3.5 g/day)** | Nephrotic syndrome |
| **Hematuria + proteinuria** | Glomerulonephritis |

**Muddy brown casts** = **Pathognomonic for ATN**

---

**Urine Indices**:

| Index | **Prerenal** | **ATN (Intrinsic)** |
|-------|--------------|---------------------|
| **FENa** (Fractional Excretion of Sodium) | **<1%** | **>2%** |
| **FEUrea** | <35% | >50% |
| **Urine Na⁺** | <20 mEq/L | >40 mEq/L |
| **Urine Osmolality** | >500 mOsm/kg | <350 mOsm/kg |
| **Urine Specific Gravity** | >1.020 | <1.010 |
| **BUN:Cr Ratio** | **>20:1** | **<20:1** |

**FENa Formula**:
```
FENa = (Urine Na × Plasma Cr) / (Plasma Na × Urine Cr) × 100

<1% = Prerenal
>2% = ATN
1-2% = Indeterminate
```

**Interpretation**:
- **Prerenal**: Kidneys intact → avid Na⁺ reabsorption → low urine Na⁺, low FENa
- **ATN**: Tubular dysfunction → can't reabsorb Na⁺ → high urine Na⁺, high FENa

**Limitations of FENa**:
- **False low FENa** (<1%) in ATN if:
  - **Diuretics** (use **FEUrea** instead - not affected by diuretics)
  - Chronic kidney disease
  - Contrast nephropathy, rhabdomyolysis
- **False high FENa** (>1%) in prerenal if:
  - **Diuretics**
  - Adrenal insufficiency

**FEUrea**:
- **Use if on diuretics** (not affected)
- <35% = Prerenal
- >50% = ATN

---

**Imaging**:

**Renal Ultrasound**:
- **First-line** for suspected obstruction
- **Findings**:
  - **Hydronephrosis** (postrenal)
  - Kidney size (small kidneys = chronic disease)
  - Renal artery Doppler (if vascular suspected)

**CT Abdomen/Pelvis** (without contrast):
- **Stones**, masses, obstruction
- **Avoid IV contrast** (nephrotoxic in AKI)

---

#### **Step 3: Specific Diagnosis**

**Labs**:
- **CBC**: Anemia (hemolysis, chronic disease), eosinophilia (AIN), schistocytes (TMA)
- **CMP**: Electrolytes (hyperkalemia, acidosis), calcium, phosphate
- **CK**: Rhabdomyolysis (CK >5000 U/L)
- **LDH, haptoglobin**: Hemolysis
- **Serum protein electrophoresis (SPEP), urine protein electrophoresis (UPEP)**: Myeloma
- **Complement (C3, C4)**: Low in post-infectious GN, lupus
- **ANA, anti-dsDNA**: SLE
- **ANCA**: Vasculitis (MPO, PR3)
- **Anti-GBM**: Goodpasture's

**Kidney Biopsy**:
- **Indications**:
  - **Unclear etiology** (especially if not responding)
  - **Suspected glomerulonephritis** (guide treatment)
  - **Suspected AIN** (if no improvement after stopping drug)

---

## Management

### General Principles

**1. Identify and Treat Underlying Cause**:
- **Prerenal**: Restore volume/perfusion
- **Intrinsic**: Stop nephrotoxins, treat infection, immunosuppression (if GN/AIN)
- **Postrenal**: Relieve obstruction

**2. Supportive Care**:
- **Fluid management**
- **Avoid nephrotoxins**
- **Adjust medications** for GFR
- **Treat complications**

**3. Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT)** if indicated

---

### Prerenal AKI

**Restore Renal Perfusion**:

**Hypovolemia**:
- **IV Fluids**:
  - **Isotonic crystalloid** (0.9% saline, Ringer's lactate)
  - **Bolus**: 500-1000 mL, reassess
  - **Goal**: Restore euvolemia (urine output >0.5 mL/kg/hr, ↓ creatinine)

**Heart Failure**:
- **Optimize cardiac output**: Diuretics (if volume overloaded), inotropes (if cardiogenic shock)

**Sepsis**:
- **Early aggressive fluids** (30 mL/kg), vasopressors (norepinephrine)

**Discontinue Offending Agents**:
- **NSAIDs**, **ACE inhibitors/ARBs** (if volume depleted)
- **Diuretics** (temporarily)

**Monitor Response**:
- **Creatinine** should ↓ within 24-48 hours if prerenal

---

### Intrinsic AKI

#### **Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN)**

**Supportive Care** (NO specific treatment):

**1. Identify and Remove Cause**:
- **Stop nephrotoxins**: Aminoglycosides, NSAIDs, contrast
- **Treat shock** (ischemic ATN)

**2. Fluid Management**:
- **Euvolemia**: Balance fluid intake and output
  - **Oliguria**: Restrict fluids (avoid overload)
  - **Polyuria** (recovery phase): Replace losses

**3. Avoid Further Nephrotoxins**:
- **Adjust drug doses** for GFR (antibiotics, etc.)

**4. Monitor and Treat Complications**:
- Hyperkalemia, acidosis, uremia, volume overload

**5. Nutrition**:
- **Adequate calories** (prevent catabolism)
- **Protein**: 0.8-1 g/kg/day (if not on dialysis)

**Recovery**:
- **ATN usually recovers** in 7-21 days (if cause removed)
- **Polyuric phase**: Tubules can't concentrate urine yet → large urine output (replace losses)

---

#### **Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN)**

**1. Stop Offending Drug** (MOST IMPORTANT):
- **Recovery may take weeks** even after stopping

**2. Corticosteroids**:
- **Controversial** (no RCTs)
- **Consider if**:
  - No improvement 3-7 days after stopping drug
  - Severe AKI
  - Biopsy-proven AIN
- **Prednisone 1 mg/kg/day** × 2-4 weeks, taper

**3. Supportive Care**

---

#### **Glomerulonephritis**

**Immunosuppression** (depends on etiology):
- **Anti-GBM**: Plasmapheresis + cyclophosphamide + corticosteroids
- **ANCA-associated**: Cyclophosphamide + corticosteroids, rituximab
- **Lupus nephritis**: Cyclophosphamide or mycophenolate + corticosteroids
- **Post-infectious**: Supportive (usually self-limited)

---

#### **Rhabdomyolysis**

**1. Aggressive IV Fluids**:
- **Goal**: Urine output 200-300 mL/hr
- **Isotonic saline**: 1-2 L/hr initially

**2. Alkalization** (controversial):
- **Sodium bicarbonate** in IV fluids (if pH <6.5)
- **Goal**: Urine pH >6.5 (prevent myoglobin precipitation)

**3. Monitor**:
- **CK**, **potassium** (↑ from muscle breakdown), **calcium** (↓ then ↑), **phosphate** (↑)

**4. Avoid**:
- **Loop diuretics**: Don't prevent AKI, may worsen

---

### Postrenal AKI

**Relieve Obstruction** (URGENT):

**Bladder Outlet Obstruction**:
- **Foley catheter** (first-line)
- **Suprapubic catheter** (if urethral stricture)

**Bilateral Ureteral Obstruction**:
- **Percutaneous nephrostomy** OR
- **Ureteral stents** (retrograde)

**Post-Obstructive Diuresis**:
- After relief of **prolonged obstruction** (days to weeks):
  - **Massive diuresis** (impaired concentrating ability)
  - **Replace losses** (0.45% saline at 50-75% of urine output)
  - **Monitor electrolytes** (hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia)

---

### Renal Replacement Therapy (RRT)

**Indications** (Mnemonic: "AEIOU"):
- **A**: Acidosis (severe metabolic acidosis, pH <7.1 refractory to medical management)
- **E**: Electrolyte abnormalities (hyperkalemia >6.5 mEq/L with ECG changes, refractory)
- **I**: Intoxications (dialyzable toxins: methanol, ethylene glycol, lithium, salicylates)
- **O**: Overload (volume overload, pulmonary edema refractory to diuretics)
- **U**: Uremia (pericarditis, encephalopathy, bleeding)

**Additional**:
- **Oligo-anuria** (if prolonged, unresponsive)
- **Tumor lysis syndrome** (with hyperkalemia, hyperphosphatemia, AKI)

---

**Modalities**:

**Intermittent Hemodialysis (IHD)**:
- **3-4 hours**, 3× per week
- **Rapid correction** of electrolytes, volume
- **Unstable patients**: May cause hypotension

**Continuous Renal Replacement Therapy (CRRT)**:
- **24 hours/day**, slower, continuous
- **Hemodynamically unstable**: Better tolerated (less hypotension)
- **ICU setting**

**Peritoneal Dialysis (PD)**:
- **Rarely used** for AKI (less efficient)

---

### Complications of AKI

**Electrolyte Abnormalities**:
- **Hyperkalemia**: Life-threatening (arrhythmias)
  - **Treatment**: Calcium gluconate (membrane stabilization), insulin + glucose, sodium bicarbonate, diuretics, dialysis
- **Hyperphosphatemia**: Binds calcium → hypocalcemia
- **Hypocalcemia**: Tetany, seizures
- **Metabolic acidosis**: Anion gap (uremia)

**Volume Overload**:
- **Pulmonary edema**, **hypertension**
- **Treatment**: Diuretics (if responsive), dialysis

**Uremia**:
- **Pericarditis**, **encephalopathy**, **bleeding** (platelet dysfunction)
- **Treatment**: Dialysis

**Anemia**:
- **Decreased erythropoietin**, **blood loss**

**Infection**:
- **Increased risk** (uremia impairs immunity)

## Specific Conditions

### Contrast-Induced Nephropathy (CIN)

**Definition**:
- **AKI within 48-72 hours** after IV iodinated contrast
- **Cr ↑ ≥0.5 mg/dL or ≥25%** from baseline

**Risk Factors**:
- **Pre-existing CKD** (eGFR <60) - **MOST IMPORTANT**
- **Diabetes mellitus**
- **Volume depletion**
- **Heart failure**
- **High contrast volume**
- **NSAIDs**, **diuretics**

**Pathophysiology**:
- **Renal vasoconstriction** + **Direct tubular toxicity**

**Prevention**:

**1. IV Fluids** (MOST EFFECTIVE):
- **Isotonic saline** (0.9% NaCl) or **sodium bicarbonate**:
  - **1 mL/kg/hr** × 12 hours **before** and **12 hours after** procedure
  - **If urgent**: 3 mL/kg/hr × 1 hour before, 1 mL/kg/hr × 6 hours after

**2. Use Low-Osmolar or Iso-Osmolar Contrast**:
- **Minimize volume**

**3. Avoid Nephrotoxins**:
- **Hold NSAIDs**, **metformin** (lactic acidosis risk if AKI develops)

**4. N-Acetylcysteine (NAC)**:
- **Controversial** (mixed evidence)
- **600-1200 mg PO BID** × 2 days (day before, day of procedure)
- **Low cost, minimal harm** → often given

**5. Avoid if Possible**:
- **Consider alternative imaging** (non-contrast CT, MRI without gadolinium, ultrasound)

**Management** (if CIN develops):
- **Supportive care** (usually self-limited, recovers in 7-10 days)
- **Avoid repeat contrast** until recovery

---

### Rhabdomyolysis

**Definition**:
- **Skeletal muscle breakdown** → release of intracellular contents (myoglobin, CK, K⁺, phosphate)

**Causes**:
- **Trauma/Crush injury** (most common)
- **Prolonged immobilization** (fall, coma, seizure)
- **Drugs**: Statins, fibrates, cocaine, amphetamines
- **Strenuous exercise** (especially heat stroke)
- **Infections**: Influenza, viral myositis
- **Electrolyte abnormalities**: Hypokalemia, hypophosphatemia
- **Toxins**: Alcohol, carbon monoxide

**Clinical Features**:
- **Muscle pain, weakness, swelling**
- **Dark urine** ("tea-colored" or "cola-colored" - myoglobinuria)
- **AKI**

**Diagnosis**:
- **CK >1000 U/L** (often >5000-10,000)
  - **5× ULN** diagnostic
  - **Peak** at 24-72 hours
- **Myoglobinuria**: Urine dipstick **positive for blood** (detects heme), but **NO RBCs** on microscopy
- **Hyperkalemia** (muscle breakdown)
- **Hyperphosphatemia**
- **Hypocalcemia** (early - calcium precipitates in damaged muscle)
- **Hypercalcemia** (late - recovery phase, calcium released)
- **Elevated AST, ALT** (muscle contains transaminases)

**Complications**:
- **AKI** (30-50%):
  - **Myoglobin** precipitates in tubules (especially if acidic urine)
  - **Direct tubular toxicity**
  - **Renal vasoconstriction**
- **Hyperkalemia** (life-threatening)
- **Compartment syndrome**
- **DIC**

**Management**:

**1. Aggressive IV Fluids** (MOST IMPORTANT):
- **Goal**: Urine output **200-300 mL/hr**
- **Isotonic saline**: 1-2 L/hr initially, then titrate
- **Early, aggressive hydration** prevents AKI

**2. Urine Alkalinization**:
- **Sodium bicarbonate** in IV fluids
- **Goal**: Urine pH >6.5 (prevents myoglobin precipitation)
- **Controversial** (no strong RCT evidence)

**3. Treat Hyperkalemia**:
- **Calcium gluconate** (if ECG changes)
- **Insulin + glucose**
- **Dialysis** (if severe, refractory)

**4. Monitor**:
- **CK** (daily until declining)
- **Electrolytes** (K⁺, Ca²⁺, phosphate)
- **Creatinine**
- **Urine output**

**5. Avoid**:
- **Loop diuretics**: Don't prevent AKI, may worsen (increase urine concentration)

**6. Treat Underlying Cause**:
- **Fasciotomy** (if compartment syndrome)

**Prognosis**:
- **AKI usually recovers** if treated early with fluids

---

### Tumor Lysis Syndrome (TLS)

**Definition**:
- **Massive cell lysis** (usually after chemotherapy) → release of intracellular contents

**High-Risk Malignancies**:
- **Burkitt lymphoma** (highest risk)
- **Acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL)**
- **High-grade lymphomas**
- **High tumor burden**

**Timing**:
- **12-72 hours** after chemotherapy initiation

**Laboratory TLS** (≥2 of following within 3 days before or 7 days after chemotherapy):
- **Hyperuricemia** (uric acid >8 mg/dL or ↑ 25%)
- **Hyperkalemia** (K⁺ >6 mEq/L or ↑ 25%)
- **Hyperphosphatemia** (phosphate >4.5 mg/dL or ↑ 25%)
- **Hypocalcemia** (calcium <7 mg/dL or ↓ 25%)

**Clinical TLS**:
- **Laboratory TLS** + **≥1** of:
  - **Acute kidney injury**
  - **Cardiac arrhythmia/sudden death**
  - **Seizure**

**Pathophysiology**:
```
Massive cell lysis
      ↓
Release of K⁺, phosphate, nucleic acids (purines)
      ↓
Purines → Uric acid (via xanthine oxidase)
      ↓
Uric acid precipitation in renal tubules
      ↓
Obstructive nephropathy → AKI
```

**Prevention** (High-risk patients):

**1. Aggressive IV Hydration**:
- **2-3 L/m² per day** (maintain urine output >100 mL/m²/hr)

**2. Allopurinol** (Xanthine Oxidase Inhibitor):
- **Prevents NEW uric acid formation** (doesn't lower existing uric acid)
- **300-600 mg daily**, start **24-48 hours before chemotherapy**
- **Continue** × 3-7 days after chemotherapy

**OR**

**3. Rasburicase** (Recombinant Urate Oxidase):
- **Converts uric acid → allantoin** (more soluble, easily excreted)
- **Rapidly lowers uric acid** (within hours)
- **Indications**: High-risk TLS, established TLS, allopurinol contraindicated
- **Dosing**: 0.2 mg/kg IV single dose (may repeat)
- **Contraindication**: **G6PD deficiency** (causes hemolysis)

**4. Avoid Urine Alkalinization**:
- **Old practice** (no longer recommended)
- **Risks**: Calcium phosphate precipitation (worsens kidney injury)

**Treatment** (if TLS develops):
- **Aggressive fluids**
- **Rasburicase** (if uric acid >8 mg/dL)
- **Treat hyperkalemia** (calcium, insulin/glucose, dialysis)
- **Dialysis** (if refractory hyperkalemia, severe AKI)

---

### Cardiorenal Syndrome

**Definition**:
- **Interplay** between heart and kidney dysfunction

**Types**:

**Type 1 (Acute Cardiorenal)**:
- **Acute heart failure** → **AKI**
- **Mechanism**: ↓ Cardiac output → ↓ renal perfusion, venous congestion

**Type 2 (Chronic Cardiorenal)**:
- **Chronic heart failure** → **CKD**

**Type 3 (Acute Renocardiac)**:
- **AKI** → **Acute cardiac dysfunction** (volume overload, uremia)

**Type 4 (Chronic Renocardiac)**:
- **CKD** → **Cardiac disease** (LVH, heart failure)

**Type 5 (Secondary)**:
- **Systemic disease** (sepsis, cirrhosis) → heart AND kidney dysfunction

---

## High-Yield Points

### AKI Definition (KDIGO Criteria)

**ANY of**:
- **Cr ↑ ≥0.3 mg/dL in 48 hours** OR
- **Cr ↑ ≥1.5× baseline in 7 days** OR
- **Urine output <0.5 mL/kg/hr × 6 hours**

### Prerenal vs ATN Differentiation

| Parameter | **Prerenal** | **ATN** |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| **BUN:Cr** | **>20:1** | **<20:1** |
| **FENa** | **<1%** | **>2%** |
| **Urine Na⁺** | <20 mEq/L | >40 mEq/L |
| **Urine Osmolality** | >500 | <350 |
| **Urine Sediment** | Hyaline casts | **Muddy brown casts** |
| **Response to Fluids** | **Improves** | No improvement |

**FENa <1% in ATN**: Diuretics, contrast nephropathy, rhabdomyolysis (use **FEUrea** instead)

### Urinalysis Patterns

- **Muddy brown/granular casts**: **ATN**
- **RBC casts**: **Glomerulonephritis**
- **WBC casts**: **Pyelonephritis, AIN**
- **Eosinophils**: **AIN** (low sensitivity)

### AIN Classic Triad (10-30% have all):
- **Fever** + **Rash** + **Eosinophilia**

**Common Drugs**:
- Antibiotics (penicillins, cephalosporins, fluoroquinolones)
- **PPIs** (increasing cause)
- NSAIDs, allopurinol

### RRT Indications: "AEIOU"
- **A**cidosis (pH <7.1, refractory)
- **E**lectrolytes (K⁺ >6.5 with ECG changes)
- **I**ntoxications (methanol, ethylene glycol, lithium)
- **O**verload (pulmonary edema, refractory)
- **U**remia (pericarditis, encephalopathy, bleeding)

### Contrast-Induced Nephropathy

**Prevention**:
- **IV fluids** (isotonic saline 1 mL/kg/hr × 12 hrs before and after) - **MOST EFFECTIVE**
- **Minimize contrast volume**
- **NAC** (controversial, low harm)

### Rhabdomyolysis

**Diagnosis**:
- **CK >5000 U/L** (often >10,000)
- **Myoglobinuria**: Urine dipstick **positive for blood**, **NO RBCs** on microscopy

**Management**:
- **Aggressive IV fluids** (goal UOP 200-300 mL/hr) - **MOST IMPORTANT**
- **Alkalinize urine** (controversial)
- **Treat hyperkalemia**
- **Avoid loop diuretics**

### Tumor Lysis Syndrome

**Prevention** (high-risk):
- **IV hydration** + **Allopurinol** (start 24-48 hrs before chemo)
- **Rasburicase** (if high-risk or established TLS)
  - **Contraindicated in G6PD deficiency**

### Question 1
A 70-year-old man with hypertension presents with fatigue. Labs: Cr 2.8 mg/dL (baseline 1.0), BUN 56 mg/dL. Urinalysis: Specific gravity 1.025, Na⁺ 15 mEq/L, no casts. FENa 0.8%. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?

A) Acute tubular necrosis
B) Prerenal azotemia
C) Acute interstitial nephritis
D) Postrenal obstruction

**Answer**: **B) Prerenal azotemia**

**Explanation**:
- **Prerenal AKI**:
  - **BUN:Cr ratio**: 56/2.8 = **20:1** (>20:1 suggests prerenal) ✓
  - **FENa <1%** (0.8%) ✓
  - **Urine Na⁺ <20 mEq/L** (15) ✓
  - **High specific gravity** (>1.020) ✓
  - **No casts** (hyaline casts may be seen in prerenal)

- **Kidneys intact** → avid Na⁺ reabsorption → concentrated urine

- **ATN**:
  - FENa **>2%**
  - Urine Na⁺ **>40 mEq/L**
  - **Muddy brown casts**
  - BUN:Cr <20:1

A) ATN; supportive care
B) AIN; stop penicillin
C) AIN; stop penicillin + corticosteroids
D) Pyelonephritis; continue penicillin + add gentamicin

**Explanation**:
- **Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN)**:

  - **Classic triad** (30% have all):
    - **Fever** ✓
    - **Rash** ✓
    - **Eosinophilia** ✓
  - **Urinalysis**: **WBC casts**, eosinophils ✓

  - **Stop offending drug** (penicillin) - **MOST IMPORTANT**
  - **Corticosteroids** (prednisone 1 mg/kg/day):

### Question 3
A 28-year-old man is brought to the ER after being found down for several hours. Labs: CK 45,000 U/L, K⁺ 6.8 mEq/L, Cr 3.5 mg/dL, phosphate 8 mg/dL. Urine dipstick is positive for blood, but microscopy shows no RBCs. What is the MOST appropriate initial management?

A) Aggressive IV fluids
B) Loop diuretics
C) Immediate hemodialysis
D) Sodium bicarbonate only

**Answer**: **A) Aggressive IV fluids**

**Explanation**:
- **Rhabdomyolysis**:
  - **CK >5000 U/L** (45,000) ✓
  - **Hyperkalemia** (muscle breakdown) ✓
  - **Hyperphosphatemia** ✓
  - **Urine positive for blood (heme), NO RBCs** = **Myoglobinuria** ✓
  - **AKI** ✓

- **Management**:

**1. Aggressive IV Fluids** (MOST IMPORTANT):
- **Isotonic saline 1-2 L/hr** initially
- **Goal UOP 200-300 mL/hr**
- **Prevents AKI**

**2. Treat Hyperkalemia** (K⁺ 6.8):
- **Calcium gluconate** (membrane stabilization - especially if ECG changes)
- **Insulin + glucose**
- **May need dialysis** if refractory

**3. Urine Alkalinization** (controversial):
- **Sodium bicarbonate** (goal pH >6.5)

**4. Monitor**: CK, K⁺, Ca²⁺, phosphate, Cr

**Avoid**:
- **Loop diuretics**: Don't prevent AKI, may worsen (↑ urine concentration)

- **Dialysis**: May be needed if severe, refractory hyperkalemia or AKI, but **fluids are first-line**

### Question 4
A 65-year-old man with CKD (baseline Cr 2.0 mg/dL) is scheduled for contrast CT. What is the MOST effective measure to prevent contrast-induced nephropathy?

A) N-Acetylcysteine 600 mg PO BID × 2 days
B) IV isotonic saline 1 mL/kg/hr × 12 hours before and after
C) Hold ACE inhibitor 24 hours before
D) Sodium bicarbonate 150 mEq/L at 3 mL/kg/hr × 1 hour before

**Answer**: **B) IV isotonic saline 1 mL/kg/hr × 12 hours before and after**

**Explanation**:
- **Contrast-Induced Nephropathy (CIN) Prevention**:

**MOST EFFECTIVE**: **IV Fluids**
- **Isotonic saline (0.9% NaCl)** OR **Sodium bicarbonate**:
  - **1 mL/kg/hr × 12 hours before** and **12 hours after** procedure
  - **If urgent**: 3 mL/kg/hr × 1 hour before, 1 mL/kg/hr × 6 hours after

**Other measures**:
- **Minimize contrast volume**, use low-osmolar/iso-osmolar contrast
- **N-Acetylcysteine** (NAC): **Controversial** (mixed evidence)
  - 600-1200 mg PO BID × 2 days
  - Low cost, minimal harm → often given
- **Hold nephrotoxins**: NSAIDs, metformin

- **ACE inhibitor**: Holding is reasonable but **less important than fluids**

- **Risk factors**: **CKD** (baseline Cr 2.0) ✓, diabetes, volume depletion

### Question 5
A 60-year-old man with newly diagnosed Burkitt lymphoma is started on chemotherapy. On day 2, labs show: K⁺ 6.5 mEq/L, uric acid 12 mg/dL, phosphate 7 mg/dL, calcium 6.8 mg/dL, Cr 3.2 mg/dL. What is the diagnosis and MOST appropriate immediate management?

A) Tumor lysis syndrome; aggressive IV fluids + allopurinol
B) Tumor lysis syndrome; aggressive IV fluids + rasburicase + treat hyperkalemia
C) Rhabdomyolysis; aggressive IV fluids + bicarbonate
D) AKI from chemotherapy; hold chemotherapy

**Answer**: **B) Tumor lysis syndrome; aggressive IV fluids + rasburicase + treat hyperkalemia**

**Explanation**:
- **Tumor Lysis Syndrome (TLS)**:
  - **High-risk malignancy**: Burkitt lymphoma (highest risk) ✓
  - **Timing**: Day 2 after chemotherapy ✓
  - **Laboratory TLS** (≥2 of):
    - **Hyperuricemia** (12 mg/dL) ✓
    - **Hyperkalemia** (6.5 mEq/L) ✓
    - **Hyperphosphatemia** (7 mg/dL) ✓
    - **Hypocalcemia** (6.8 mg/dL) ✓
  - **Clinical TLS**: Laboratory TLS + **AKI** ✓

- **Management**:

**1. Aggressive IV Fluids**:
- 2-3 L/m²/day

**2. Rasburicase** (Recombinant Urate Oxidase):
- **Rapidly lowers uric acid** (within hours)
- **Indicated** for established TLS (uric acid 12 mg/dL)
- **0.2 mg/kg IV** single dose
- **Contraindicated**: G6PD deficiency (hemolysis)

**3. Treat Hyperkalemia** (K⁺ 6.5):
- **Calcium gluconate** (if ECG changes)
- **Insulin + glucose**
- **May need dialysis**

**4. Dialysis** (if refractory hyperkalemia, severe AKI)

**Allopurinol**:
- **Prevents NEW uric acid formation**
- **Does NOT lower existing uric acid**
- Used for **prevention** (start before chemo), **NOT acute treatment**

### Question 6
A 72-year-old man presents with anuria for 24 hours. Bladder scan shows 800 mL. Creatinine is 4.5 mg/dL (baseline 1.2). What is the MOST appropriate next step?

A) IV furosemide
B) Foley catheter insertion
C) Emergent hemodialysis
D) Renal ultrasound

**Answer**: **B) Foley catheter insertion**

**Explanation**:
- **Postrenal AKI** (Bladder Outlet Obstruction):
  - **Anuria** (no urine output) ✓
  - **Bladder scan 800 mL** (distended bladder) ✓
  - **AKI** (Cr 4.5, baseline 1.2) ✓

- **Most likely**: **BPH** (72-year-old man)

- **Management**:

**1. Relieve Obstruction** (URGENT):
- **Foley catheter** (first-line for bladder outlet obstruction)
- **Suprapubic catheter** (if urethral stricture, can't pass Foley)

**2. Monitor for Post-Obstructive Diuresis**:
- After relief of **prolonged obstruction** → massive diuresis (impaired concentration)
- **Replace losses** (0.45% saline at 50-75% of UOP)
- **Monitor electrolytes** (K⁺, Mg²⁺)

**3. Renal function should improve** after obstruction relieved

- **Renal ultrasound**: Would confirm hydronephrosis, but **don't delay catheter** (clinical diagnosis clear)

- **Dialysis**: May be needed if severe metabolic derangements, but **relieve obstruction first**

### Question 8
A 45-year-old man develops AKI 48 hours after cardiac catheterization. Creatinine rises from 1.2 to 2.8 mg/dL. He has livedo reticularis on lower extremities and eosinophilia. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?

A) Contrast-induced nephropathy
B) Atheroembolic disease (cholesterol emboli)
C) Acute interstitial nephritis
D) Acute tubular necrosis

**Answer**: **B) Atheroembolic disease (cholesterol emboli)**

**Explanation**:
- **Atheroembolic Disease** (Cholesterol Emboli):
  - **Post-catheterization** (manipulation of atherosclerotic aorta) ✓
  - **Timing**: Days to weeks after procedure ✓
  - **Livedo reticularis** (blue/purple net-like pattern on skin) ✓
  - **Eosinophilia** ✓
  - **AKI** ✓

**Other findings**:
- **Blue toe syndrome** (digital ischemia)
- **Hypocomplementemia** (low C3, C4)
- **Elevated ESR**

**Diagnosis**:
- **Skin or kidney biopsy**: Cholesterol clefts (needle-shaped spaces in arterioles)

**Management**:
- **Supportive** (no specific treatment)
- **Avoid further vascular procedures**
- **Statin** (stabilize plaques)

**Prognosis**: Often progressive (may lead to CKD)

- **Contrast-induced nephropathy**:
  - **AKI within 48-72 hours** (similar timing)
  - **NO livedo reticularis**, NO eosinophilia
  - **Usually recovers** in 7-10 days

### Question 9
A 68-year-old woman with history of heart failure is admitted with acute decompensated heart failure. Despite aggressive diuresis, creatinine rises from 1.5 to 3.2 mg/dL. She is euvolemic. What type of cardiorenal syndrome is this?

A) Type 1 (Acute cardiorenal)
B) Type 2 (Chronic cardiorenal)
C) Type 3 (Acute renocardiac)
D) Type 4 (Chronic renocardiac)

**Answer**: **A) Type 1 (Acute cardiorenal)**

**Explanation**:
- **Cardiorenal Syndrome Classification**:

**Type 1 (Acute Cardiorenal)**:
- **Acute heart failure** → **AKI** ✓
- **This patient**: Acute decompensated HF → Cr rise (AKI)

**Type 2 (Chronic Cardiorenal)**:
- Chronic heart failure → CKD

**Type 3 (Acute Renocardiac)**:
- AKI → Acute cardiac dysfunction

**Type 4 (Chronic Renocardiac)**:

- **Mechanism** (Type 1):

  - **Venous congestion** → ↑ renal venous pressure
  - **Diuretics**: May worsen prerenal state if over-diuresed

### Question 10
A patient with AKI develops peaked T waves on ECG. Potassium is 7.2 mEq/L. What is the MOST appropriate FIRST step in management?

A) Insulin + glucose
B) Calcium gluconate
C) Sodium bicarbonate
D) Emergent hemodialysis

**Answer**: **B) Calcium gluconate**

**Explanation**:
- **Severe Hyperkalemia** (K⁺ 7.2 mEq/L) with **ECG changes** (peaked T waves):

**Treatment Steps**:

**1. Calcium Gluconate** (FIRST - if ECG changes):
- **10-20 mL of 10% calcium gluconate IV** over 2-3 minutes
- **Mechanism**: **Membrane stabilization** (protects myocardium)
- **Does NOT lower K⁺**
- **Onset**: Immediate (within minutes)
- **Duration**: 30-60 minutes

**2. Shift K⁺ Intracellularly**:
- **Insulin + Glucose**:
  - 10 units regular insulin + 25 g dextrose (D50W 50 mL) IV
  - Onset: 15-30 minutes
  - Duration: 4-6 hours

- **Sodium Bicarbonate** (if metabolic acidosis):
  - 50-100 mEq IV
  - Onset: 30-60 minutes

- **Albuterol** (nebulized):
  - 10-20 mg
  - Onset: 30 minutes

**3. Remove K⁺ from Body**:
- **Diuretics** (furosemide) - if functioning kidneys
- **Sodium polystyrene sulfonate** (Kayexalate) - GI exchange resin (slow, limited efficacy)
- **Patiromer** (oral K⁺ binder)
- **Hemodialysis** (definitive, if severe/refractory)

**Sequence**: **Calcium first** (if ECG changes) → Shift K⁺ → Remove K⁺

## Summary

**Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)**:
- **Definition**: Cr ↑ ≥0.3 mg/dL in 48 hrs OR ≥1.5× baseline in 7 days OR UOP <0.5 mL/kg/hr × 6 hrs

**Classification**:
- **Prerenal** (60-70%): BUN:Cr >20:1, FENa <1%, responds to fluids
- **Intrinsic** (25-40%): ATN (muddy brown casts, FENa >2%), AIN (fever/rash/eosinophilia), GN (RBC casts)
- **Postrenal** (5-10%): Obstruction (hydronephrosis on ultrasound)

**Management**:
- **Prerenal**: IV fluids
- **ATN**: Supportive (remove nephrotoxins, monitor, treat complications)
- **AIN**: Stop drug, consider steroids
- **Postrenal**: Relieve obstruction (Foley catheter, nephrostomy)
- **RRT**: AEIOU (acidosis, electrolytes, intoxications, overload, uremia)

**Specific Conditions**:
- **Contrast-induced nephropathy**: IV fluids (most effective prevention)
- **Rhabdomyolysis**: Aggressive fluids (goal UOP 200-300 mL/hr), avoid diuretics
- **Tumor lysis syndrome**: Fluids + rasburicase (if established TLS)
