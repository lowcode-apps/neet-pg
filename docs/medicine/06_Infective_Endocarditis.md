## Definition & Overview

### Definition
**Infective Endocarditis (IE)** is an infection of the endocardial surface of the heart, primarily affecting heart valves, characterized by formation of **vegetations** (infected platelet-fibrin thrombi).

### Classification

#### **By Onset:**
```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│         CLASSIFICATION BY ONSET                          │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Acute IE:                                                │
│   • Rapid onset (<6 weeks)                               │
│   • Virulent organisms (S. aureus)                       │
│   • Normal valves                                        │
│   • High mortality                                       │
│                                                          │
│ Subacute IE:                                             │
│   • Insidious onset (weeks to months)                    │
│   • Less virulent organisms (S. viridans)                │
│   • Abnormal valves                                      │
│   • Better prognosis                                     │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

#### **By Valve Type:**
- **Native Valve Endocarditis (NVE):** Most common
- **Prosthetic Valve Endocarditis (PVE):**
  - **Early PVE:** <12 months post-surgery (nosocomial organisms)
  - **Late PVE:** >12 months post-surgery (similar to NVE)
- **IV Drug User Endocarditis (IVDU-IE):** Affects **tricuspid valve** (right-sided)

#### **By Culture:**
- **Culture-positive:** Organism identified (85%)
- **Culture-negative:** No organism identified (10-15%)

## Etiology & Risk Factors

### Predisposing Cardiac Conditions

**High Risk - "PIVR"**
```
P - Prosthetic heart valves (Highest risk)
I - IV drug abuse
V - Valvular heart disease:
    • Rheumatic heart disease (Most common in India)
    • Mitral valve prolapse with regurgitation
    • Bicuspid aortic valve
    • Degenerative valve disease
R - Repaired/Congenital heart defects:
    • VSD, PDA, Coarctation
    • Previous IE (Very high risk)
```

**Valves Affected (NVE):**
- **Mitral valve:** 40% (Most common in developing countries - RHD)
- **Aortic valve:** 35% (Most common in developed countries)
- **Tricuspid valve:** 10-15% (IVDU)
- **Pulmonic valve:** <5% (Rare)

**Right-sided IE (Tricuspid/Pulmonary):**
- **IV drug users** (Most common cause)
- Pacemaker/Central line infections
- Congenital heart disease (ASD, VSD)

### Risk Factors

**"IVDU CHAMP"**
```
I - IV drug use (S. aureus, Candida)
V - Valvular disease (RHD, MVP)
D - Diabetes mellitus
U - Urinary tract procedures

C - Cardiac devices (Pacemaker, ICD)
H - Hemodialysis, Healthcare exposure
A - Alcoholism, Advanced age
M - Mitral valve prolapse
P - Prosthetic valves, Prior IE
```

## Pathophysiology

### Mechanism of IE Development

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│         PATHOGENESIS OF IE                             │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                        │
│ 1. ENDOTHELIAL DAMAGE                                  │
│    • Turbulent flow (valve disease)                    │
│    • High-pressure jets (VSD, AR)                      │
│    • Foreign material (prosthetic valve)               │
│              ↓                                         │
│ 2. STERILE VEGETATION (NBTE)                           │
│    • Platelet-fibrin thrombus                          │
│    • On damaged endothelium                            │
│              ↓                                         │
│ 3. BACTEREMIA                                          │
│    • Dental procedure, GI/GU procedure                 │
│    • IV drug use                                       │
│    • Spontaneous (dental caries, UTI)                  │
│              ↓                                         │
│ 4. BACTERIAL ADHERENCE                                 │
│    • Bacteria adhere to NBTE                           │
│    • Facilitated by surface adhesins                   │
│              ↓                                         │
│ 5. VEGETATION FORMATION                                │
│    • Bacterial proliferation                           │
│    • More platelet-fibrin deposition                   │
│    • Biofilm formation                                 │
│              ↓                                         │
│ 6. COMPLICATIONS                                       │
│    • Valve destruction                                 │
│    • Emboli                                            │
│    • Immune complex deposition                         │
│                                                        │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Vegetations:**
- Friable masses of platelets, fibrin, bacteria
- Located on **atrial side of AV valves** or **ventricular side of semilunar valves**
- Size: mm to cm (HACEK → large vegetations)

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms

**Classic Triad (Only 10-15%):**
1. **Fever** (85%) - Most common symptom
2. **New heart murmur** (85%)
3. **Embolic phenomena** (30%)

**General Symptoms:**
- **Fever:** Low-grade (subacute) or high-grade (acute)
- **Chills, rigors**
- **Fatigue, malaise**
- **Weight loss** (subacute IE)
- **Night sweats**
- **Arthralgia, myalgia**

**Cardiac Symptoms:**
- **Dyspnea** (Heart failure - most common cause of death)
- **Chest pain** (MI from coronary embolism)
- **Palpitations**

**Embolic Symptoms:**
- **Stroke** (CNS emboli - most common neurological)
- **Renal infarct:** Flank pain, hematuria
- **Splenic infarct:** LUQ pain
- **Pulmonary emboli:** Right-sided IE (dyspnea, hemoptysis)

### Signs

**Cardiovascular:**
- **New or changing murmur** (80-85%)
  - Mitral regurgitation: Holosystolic, apex → axilla
  - Aortic regurgitation: Early diastolic, left sternal border
- **Heart failure signs** (50-60%)
- **Tachycardia**

**Peripheral Stigmata (Rare now - <10%):**

**"OLD JROS" - Classic Signs**
```
O - Osler nodes (Painful, red nodules on fingers/toes)
L - Libman-Sacks endocarditis (SLE - sterile)

D - Dark-field microscopy (for spirochetes - not IE)

J - Janeway lesions (Painless, hemorrhagic macules on palms/soles)
R - Roth spots (Retinal hemorrhages with white center)
O - Osler nodes (Already mentioned)
S - Splinter hemorrhages (Linear hemorrhages under nails)
```

**Better Mnemonic - "FROM JANE"**
```
F - Fever (Most common)
R - Roth spots (Retinal)
O - Osler nodes (Painful - fingers/toes)
M - Murmur (New/changed)

J - Janeway lesions (Painless - palms/soles)
A - Anemia
N - Nail findings (Splinter hemorrhages)
E - Embolic phenomena
```

**Key Differences:**
- **Osler nodes:** **PAINFUL**, **tips** of fingers/toes, **immune complex** deposition
- **Janeway lesions:** **PAINLESS**, **palms/soles**, **septic emboli**
- **Roth spots:** Retinal hemorrhages with **pale center** (cotton-wool spots)
- **Splinter hemorrhages:** Linear, under nails

**Splenomegaly:** 30-40% (subacute IE)

**Clubbing:** Rare, subacute IE

## Investigations

### Microbiological

**1. Blood Cultures - MOST IMPORTANT**

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- **3 sets** of blood cultures from **different sites**
- **Before antibiotics** (if stable)
- Interval: **1 hour apart** (or 30 min if unstable)
- **Aerobic and anaerobic** bottles
- **Prolonged incubation:** 7-21 days (HACEK, Brucella)

**Culture-Positive:**
- **≥2 positive** cultures with **same organism** → Major criterion
- Continuously positive cultures (12 hours apart) → Major criterion

**Typical Organisms** (Major criterion):
- S. viridans, S. bovis, HACEK
- S. aureus, Enterococcus (community-acquired, no other focus)

**2. Serology** (if culture-negative):
- **Coxiella burnetii** (Q fever) - IgG Phase I >1:800
- **Bartonella**
- Brucella, Legionella

**3. PCR/Molecular testing** (valve tissue)

### Cardiac Imaging

**1. Echocardiography - MANDATORY**

**Transthoracic Echocardiography (TTE):**
- **First-line** imaging
- **Sensitivity:** 60-70% (NVE), 40-50% (PVE)
- **Findings:**
  - **Vegetation:** Mobile, oscillating mass on valve
  - **Valve perforation**
  - **Abscess:** Thickened area with echolucency
  - **Dehiscence** (prosthetic valve)
  - **New regurgitation**

**Transesophageal Echocardiography (TEE):**
- **More sensitive:** 90-95% (NVE), 85-90% (PVE)
- **Indications:**
  - **Prosthetic valves**
  - **Negative TTE with high clinical suspicion**
  - **Complications:** Abscess, perforation, fistula
  - **Pre-operative assessment**
- **Better for:**
  - Small vegetations (<5 mm)
  - Perivalvular complications
  - Aortic valve

**Vegetation Characteristics:**
- Size >10 mm → ↑ Embolic risk
- Mobile → ↑ Embolic risk
- Anterior mitral leaflet → ↑ Embolic risk

**2. Cardiac CT/MRI:**
- Alternative if TEE contraindicated or non-diagnostic
- Assess for abscess, fistula

### Other Investigations

**Hematology:**
- **CBC:**
  - **Anemia** (normocytic, normochromic - anemia of chronic disease)
  - **Leukocytosis** (acute IE)
  - Normal WBC (subacute IE)
- **ESR/CRP:** Elevated (non-specific)

**Immunology:**
- **Rheumatoid factor:** Positive (50% - subacute IE)
- **Complement (C3, C4):** Low (immune complex)
- **Cryoglobulins:** May be positive

**Renal:**
- **Urinalysis:** **Microscopic hematuria, proteinuria** (glomerulonephritis)
- **Creatinine:** Elevated (renal failure - immune complex GN or septic emboli)

**ECG:**
- **PR prolongation/AV block:** Suggests **perivalvular abscess** (aortic root extension)
- Conduction abnormalities

**Imaging (for emboli):**
- **CT brain:** Stroke, mycotic aneurysm
- **CT chest:** Pulmonary emboli (right-sided IE)
- **CT abdomen:** Splenic/renal infarct

## Diagnosis - Duke Criteria

### Modified Duke Criteria (Most Widely Used)

**Definite IE:**
- **2 Major criteria**, OR
- **1 Major + 3 Minor criteria**, OR
- **5 Minor criteria**

**Possible IE:**
- **1 Major + 1 Minor**, OR
- **3 Minor criteria**

**Rejected:**
- Alternative diagnosis, OR
- Resolution with <4 days antibiotics, OR
- Does not meet "Possible" criteria

---

### MAJOR CRITERIA

**1. Positive Blood Cultures**
- **Typical organism** in ≥2 separate cultures:
  - S. viridans, S. bovis, HACEK group
  - S. aureus, Enterococcus (community-acquired, no other focus)

- **Persistently positive cultures:**
  - ≥2 positive cultures >12 hours apart, OR
  - All of 3, or majority of ≥4 cultures positive

- **Single positive culture** for **Coxiella burnetii** or **IgG Phase I >1:800**

**2. Evidence of Endocardial Involvement (Echocardiography)**
- **Vegetation:** Oscillating intracardiac mass on valve/supporting structures
- **Abscess:** Perivalvular abscess
- **Dehiscence:** New partial dehiscence of prosthetic valve
- **New valvular regurgitation** (not pre-existing)

---

### MINOR CRITERIA

**1. Predisposing Condition:**
- Predisposing heart condition (valve disease, prosthetic valve)
- **IV drug use**

**2. Fever:**
- Temperature **>38°C** (100.4°F)

**3. Vascular Phenomena:**
- **Major arterial emboli**
- **Septic pulmonary infarcts** (right-sided IE)
- **Mycotic aneurysm**
- **Intracranial hemorrhage**
- **Janeway lesions**
- **Conjunctival hemorrhages**

**4. Immunologic Phenomena:**
- **Glomerulonephritis**
- **Osler nodes**
- **Roth spots**
- **Rheumatoid factor** positive

**5. Microbiological Evidence:**
- Positive blood culture **not meeting major criterion**, OR
- Serological evidence of infection with organism consistent with IE

## Management

### General Principles

**"ABCDE"**
```
A - Admit (hospitalize all cases)
B - Blood cultures (x3, before antibiotics)
C - Cardiology/Infectious disease consult
D - Determine organism (culture-directed therapy)
E - Echocardiography (TTE ± TEE)
```

### Antimicrobial Therapy

#### **Empiric Therapy** (Before culture results)

**Native Valve, Community-Acquired:**
- **Ampicillin-Sulbactam** 12 g/day IV + **Gentamicin** 3 mg/kg/day IV
- OR **Vancomycin** 15 mg/kg IV q12h + **Gentamicin** 3 mg/kg/day
- OR **Ceftriaxone** 2g IV daily + **Gentamicin**

**Prosthetic Valve (or Nosocomial/Healthcare-associated):**
- **Vancomycin** 15 mg/kg IV q12h + **Gentamicin** 3 mg/kg/day + **Rifampin** 300 mg PO q8h
- (Covers MRSA, Coagulase-negative Staph, Enterococcus)

**IV Drug User (Right-sided):**
- **Vancomycin** + **Gentamicin** (covers MRSA)
- If MSSA confirmed → **Nafcillin** or **Oxacillin**

#### **Targeted Therapy** (Culture-directed)

```
┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│         ORGANISM-SPECIFIC THERAPY                             │
├───────────────────────────┬───────────────────────────────────┤
│ ORGANISM                  │  TREATMENT (Duration)             │
├───────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────┤
│ Viridans Strep (PCN-S)    │ Penicillin G 12-18 MU/day (4 wks)│
│ (MIC <0.12 µg/ml)         │ + Gentamicin 3 mg/kg (2 wks)      │
│                           │ OR Ceftriaxone 2g daily (4 wks)   │
├───────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────┤
│ Viridans Strep (PCN-R)    │ Penicillin G 24 MU/day (4-6 wks)  │
│ (MIC >0.12 µg/ml)         │ + Gentamicin 3 mg/kg (4-6 wks)    │
│                           │ OR Vancomycin (if PCN allergy)    │
├───────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────┤
│ MSSA (Staph aureus)       │ Nafcillin/Oxacillin 12 g/day      │
│ Native valve              │ (4-6 weeks)                       │
│                           │ + Gentamicin 3 mg/kg (3-5 days)   │
├───────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────┤
│ MRSA                      │ Vancomycin 15 mg/kg q12h (6 wks)  │
│ OR Prosthetic valve MSSA  │ + Rifampin 300 mg q8h (PVE)       │
│                           │ + Gentamicin 3 mg/kg (2 wks-PVE)  │
├───────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────┤
│ Enterococcus (PCN-S)      │ Ampicillin 12 g/day (4-6 wks)     │
│                           │ + Gentamicin 3 mg/kg (4-6 wks)    │
│                           │ OR Vancomycin (if PCN allergy)    │
├───────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────┤
│ Enterococcus (VRE)        │ Linezolid 600 mg q12h (8 wks)     │
│ OR High-level gent resist │ OR Daptomycin 10-12 mg/kg         │
├───────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────┤
│ HACEK group               │ Ceftriaxone 2g IV daily (4 wks)   │
│                           │ OR Ampicillin-Sulbactam           │
├───────────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────┤
│ Fungal (Candida)          │ Amphotericin B (6-10 wks)         │
│                           │ + Valve replacement (MANDATORY)   │
│                           │ Then Fluconazole lifelong         │
└───────────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Duration:**
- **Native valve:** 4-6 weeks
- **Prosthetic valve:** 6 weeks minimum
- **Right-sided (IVDU, uncomplicated):** 2 weeks (if sensitive S. aureus)

**Monitoring:**
- **Repeat blood cultures** at 48-72 hours (should be negative)
- **Weekly blood cultures** until negative
- **Gentamicin levels** (trough <1 µg/ml, peak 3-4 µg/ml)
- **Vancomycin levels** (trough 15-20 µg/ml)

### Surgical Management

**Indications for Surgery - "SHAVE"**

```
S - Severe heart failure (Acute regurgitation, refractory)
H - Heart block (Perivalvular abscess)
A - Abscess (Perivalvular, myocardial)
V - Vegetation (Large >10 mm, mobile, recurrent emboli)
E - Emboli (Recurrent despite antibiotics)

Additional:
F - Fungal IE
P - Prosthetic valve dehiscence
R - Resistant organisms (S. aureus PVE, multi-drug resistant)
```

#### **Detailed Indications:**

**1. Heart Failure (Most common indication):**
- **Acute severe AR/MR** with pulmonary edema
- Refractory heart failure despite medical therapy
- **Fistula** formation (valve-to-chamber)
- **Chordal rupture** (flail leaflet)

**2. Uncontrolled Infection:**
- **Persistent bacteremia** >5-7 days despite appropriate antibiotics
- **Perivalvular abscess**, pseudoaneurysm, fistula
- **Fungal IE** (Candida, Aspergillus)
- **Difficult-to-treat organisms:** S. lugdunensis, Multi-drug resistant
- **Prosthetic valve endocarditis** with S. aureus or fungal

**3. Prevention of Embolism:**
- **Large vegetation >10 mm** (especially anterior mitral leaflet)
- **Recurrent emboli** despite appropriate therapy
- **Mobile vegetation >10 mm** + other indications

**4. Conduction Abnormalities:**
- **New AV block, bundle branch block** → Suggests abscess

**Timing:**
- **Emergent** (<24 hours): Severe refractory heart failure
- **Urgent** (few days): Perivalvular abscess, heart block
- **Elective** (1-2 weeks): After completing antibiotics (if stable)

**Contraindications to Surgery:**
- **Recent large stroke with hemorrhagic transformation** (delay 3-4 weeks)
- Severe neurological damage
- Terminal malignancy

## Complications

### Cardiac (Most Common)

**1. Heart Failure** (50-60%)
- **Most common cause of death** in IE
- Mechanism: Acute severe valve regurgitation (destruction)
- Mortality: 20-40%

**2. Perivalvular Abscess** (10-15%)
- More common: Aortic valve, Prosthetic valves
- Clue: **New AV block** (extension to septum)
- Diagnosis: **TEE**
- Treatment: **Surgery** (drainage + valve replacement)

**3. Myocardial Abscess**
- Extension from valve
- High mortality

**4. Myocarditis, Pericarditis**
- Rare

**5. Valve Perforation**
- Leads to acute regurgitation

### Embolic (20-50%)

**Left-Sided IE:**
- **CNS (65%):**
  - **Ischemic stroke** (Most common neurological)
  - **Hemorrhagic stroke** (mycotic aneurysm rupture)
  - **Brain abscess**
  - **Meningitis**
- **Spleen** (44%): Splenic infarct/abscess
- **Kidney** (30%): Renal infarct
- **Coronary:** MI (rare)
- **Liver, bowel**

**Right-Sided IE:**
- **Pulmonary emboli** (multiple, septic)
- Lung abscesses
- Empyema

**Mycotic Aneurysm:**
- **Not fungal** (misnomer - "mycotic" = fungus-like appearance)
- Infected arterial aneurysm
- Sites: **Middle cerebral artery** (most common), Aorta, Visceral arteries
- Complication: **Rupture** → Intracerebral hemorrhage (20% mortality)
- Imaging: CT/MR angiography
- Treatment: Antibiotics ± Endovascular/surgical repair

### Renal (10-30%)

**1. Glomerulonephritis** (Immune complex)
- **Diffuse proliferative GN** (most common)
- Hematuria, proteinuria, ↑ Creatinine
- Treatment: Antibiotics (resolves with IE treatment)

**2. Renal Infarction** (Emboli)

**3. Interstitial Nephritis** (Drug-induced)

### Neurological (20-40%)

**1. Stroke** (ischemic > hemorrhagic)
**2. Intracerebral hemorrhage** (mycotic aneurysm rupture)
**3. Brain abscess**
**4. Meningitis**
**5. Seizures**
**6. Encephalopathy**

**Thrombolysis in IE-related stroke:**
- **Generally contraindicated** (risk of hemorrhagic transformation from mycotic aneurysm)

### Splenic

**1. Splenic Infarction/Abscess**
- LUQ pain, splenomegaly
- May require splenectomy

### Sepsis/Septic Shock
- More common with **S. aureus** IE

## Prophylaxis

### Current Guidelines (AHA 2007, ESC 2015)

**MAJOR CHANGE:** Prophylaxis **ONLY** for **HIGH-RISK** patients undergoing **HIGH-RISK** procedures

**Previously:** Given liberally (discontinued due to lack of evidence, risk of antibiotic resistance)

### High-Risk Cardiac Conditions (Prophylaxis Indicated)

**"PPHC" - Only 4 Indications**
```
P - Prosthetic cardiac valve
P - Previous infective endocarditis
H - Heart transplant with valvulopathy
C - Congenital heart disease:
    • Unrepaired cyanotic CHD
    • Repaired CHD with residual defect
    • Repaired CHD <6 months post-repair
```

**NO longer indicated for:**
- Mitral valve prolapse
- Bicuspid aortic valve
- Acquired valve disease (AS, AR, MS, MR)
- Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy
- ASD, VSD, PDA (repaired >6 months)

### High-Risk Procedures (Prophylaxis Indicated)

**1. Dental Procedures:**
- **ONLY** if manipulation of **gingival tissue** or **periapical region of teeth** or **perforation of oral mucosa**
- **Examples:** Tooth extraction, periodontal procedures, dental implants

**NOT required for:**
- Routine cleaning, fluoride treatment
- Local anesthetic injections
- X-rays
- Orthodontic appliance adjustment
- Shedding of deciduous teeth

**2. Respiratory Tract:**
- **NO longer recommended** (Previously: Bronchoscopy with biopsy)

**3. GI/GU Procedures:**
- **NO longer routinely recommended**
- **May consider** (not routine) if: High-risk cardiac + GU procedure with active infection

│         IE PROPHYLAXIS REGIMENS (Dental)                   │

## NEET PG High-Yield Points

### 🔥 MUST KNOW

1. **Most common organism overall:** **S. aureus** (acute IE, IVDU)

2. **Most common organism in subacute IE:** **S. viridans** (dental)

3. **IVDU IE:**
   - Organism: **S. aureus** (most common)
   - Valve: **Tricuspid** (right-sided)
   - Complication: **Septic pulmonary emboli**

4. **Prosthetic valve IE (Early <12 months):** **S. epidermidis** (Coagulase-negative Staph)

5. **S. bovis (S. gallolyticus) IE:** Associated with **colon cancer** → Screen with colonoscopy

6. **Culture-negative IE:**
   - Most common cause: **Prior antibiotics**
   - Most common organism: **Coxiella burnetii** (Q fever)
   - **HACEK** group: Fastidious gram-negative, prolonged culture

7. **Duke Criteria:**
   - **2 Major** OR **1 Major + 3 Minor** OR **5 Minor** = Definite IE
   - **Major:** (1) Positive blood cultures (typical organism x2), (2) Echo positive
   - **Minor:** (1) Predisposing, (2) Fever, (3) Vascular, (4) Immunologic, (5) Micro evidence

8. **Osler nodes:** **PAINFUL**, fingers/toes (immune complex)

9. **Janeway lesions:** **PAINLESS**, palms/soles (septic emboli)

10. **Roth spots:** Retinal hemorrhages with **white center**

11. **Most common complication:** **Heart failure** (most common cause of death)

12. **New AV block in IE:** Suggests **perivalvular abscess**

13. **Most common embolic site:** **CNS** (stroke)

14. **Right-sided IE emboli:** **Pulmonary** (septic emboli)

15. **Mycotic aneurysm:** Infected aneurysm (not fungal), **middle cerebral artery**

16. **Renal involvement:** **Immune complex glomerulonephritis** (diffuse proliferative GN)

17. **Fungal IE:** **Candida** (IVDU, immunocompromised), **Surgery mandatory**

18. **Surgical indications - "SHAVE":**
    - **S**evere heart failure
    - **H**eart block (abscess)
    - **A**bscess
    - **V**egetation (large >10 mm, recurrent emboli)
    - **E**mboli (recurrent)

19. **Prophylaxis indications (HIGH-RISK only):**
    - **P**rosthetic valve
    - **P**revious IE
    - **H**eart transplant valvulopathy
    - **C**yanotic CHD (unrepaired or repaired <6 months)

20. **Prophylaxis NOT indicated:** MVP, Bicuspid AV, AS/AR, VSD/ASD (repaired >6 months)

21. **Prophylactic drug:** **Amoxicillin 2g PO** (1 hour before dental procedure)

22. **Empiric therapy (native valve):** **Ampicillin-Sulbactam + Gentamicin** OR **Vancomycin + Gentamicin**

23. **MSSA treatment:** **Nafcillin/Oxacillin** (4-6 weeks)

24. **MRSA treatment:** **Vancomycin** (6 weeks)

25. **Enterococcus treatment:** **Ampicillin + Gentamicin** (both for 4-6 weeks - synergy)

### Clinical Scenarios

**Scenario 1:** IVDU with fever, multiple pulmonary nodules → **Right-sided (tricuspid) IE with septic pulmonary emboli**

**Scenario 2:** Dental extraction, 2 weeks later fever + new murmur → **Subacute IE (S. viridans)**

**Scenario 3:** IE with S. bovis bacteremia → **Screen for colon cancer**

**Scenario 4:** IE with new PR prolongation/AV block → **Perivalvular abscess** → Need **surgery**

**Scenario 5:** IE with painless hemorrhagic lesions on palms → **Janeway lesions** (septic emboli)

**Scenario 6:** IE with painful nodules on fingertips → **Osler nodes** (immune complex)

**Scenario 7:** IE with hematuria, proteinuria, RBC casts → **Immune complex glomerulonephritis**

**Scenario 8:** IE with large anterior mitral vegetation (15 mm) → High embolic risk → **Consider surgery**

**Scenario 9:** Prosthetic valve patient undergoing dental extraction → **Amoxicillin 2g PO prophylaxis**

**Scenario 10:** Culture-negative IE with IgG Phase I antibody >1:800 → **Coxiella burnetii (Q fever)**

### Question 1
A 30-year-old IV drug user presents with fever and multiple bilateral pulmonary nodules on chest X-ray. Blood cultures grow S. aureus. Which valve is most likely affected?

**A)** Mitral
**B)** Aortic
**C)** Tricuspid
**D)** Pulmonic

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: C) Tricuspid**

**Explanation:**
- **IVDU IE** most commonly affects **right-sided valves**
- **Tricuspid valve** (90% of IVDU IE)
- **Organism:** S. aureus (most common in IVDU)
- **Pulmonary nodules:** Septic pulmonary emboli from right-sided IE
- **Other features:** Dyspnea, hemoptysis, pleuritic chest pain

**Left-sided valves** (Mitral, Aortic) affected in non-IVDU IE
</details>

### Question 2
A patient with IE has painless erythematous macules on palms and soles. What is the most likely diagnosis of these lesions?

**A)** Osler nodes
**B)** Janeway lesions
**C)** Roth spots
**D)** Splinter hemorrhages

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: B) Janeway lesions**

**Explanation:**
- **Janeway lesions:** **PAINLESS**, hemorrhagic macules on **palms/soles**
- Mechanism: **Septic emboli**
- More common in **acute IE** (S. aureus)

**Differentials:**
- **Osler nodes:** **PAINFUL**, nodules on **finger/toe tips** (immune complex)
- **Roth spots:** **Retinal** hemorrhages with white center
- **Splinter hemorrhages:** Linear, under **nails**

**Mnemonic:**
- **J**aneway = **J**ust doesn't hurt (Painless)
- **O**sler = **O**uch! (Painful)
</details>

### Question 3
A patient with IE develops new first-degree AV block. What complication should be suspected?

**A)** Large vegetation
**B)** Perivalvular abscess
**C)** Myocarditis
**D)** Septic pulmonary emboli

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: B) Perivalvular abscess**

**Explanation:**
- **New conduction abnormalities** (AV block, BBB) in IE → Suspect **perivalvular abscess**
- **Mechanism:** Abscess extends into **interventricular septum** (conduction system)
- **Most common:** Aortic valve IE (proximity to septum)

**Diagnosis:**
- **TEE** (more sensitive than TTE for abscess)

**Management:**
- **Surgical drainage + valve replacement** (URGENT)
- High mortality if untreated

**Other surgical indications:**
- Heart failure, fungal IE, persistent bacteremia, large mobile vegetations
</details>

### Question 4
A patient with S. bovis endocarditis should be screened for which malignancy?

**A)** Pancreatic cancer
**B)** Colon cancer
**C)** Renal cell carcinoma
**D)** Hepatocellular carcinoma

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: B) Colon cancer**

**Explanation:**
- **S. bovis** (now reclassified as **S. gallolyticus**) IE has **strong association** with **colon cancer**
- **Mechanism:** S. bovis colonizes colorectal tumors/polyps → Transient bacteremia
- **Up to 60%** of S. bovis IE patients have colonic neoplasia

**Management:**
- **Colonoscopy** mandatory in all S. bovis IE cases
- Also consider other GI pathology (diverticulosis, inflammatory bowel disease)

**Other GI-associated organisms:**
- **Enterococcus:** GI/GU procedures
- **Clostridium septicum:** Colon cancer (septicemia, not IE typically)
</details>

### Question 5
Which of the following patients requires IE prophylaxis before dental extraction?

**A)** Mitral valve prolapse without regurgitation
**B)** Bicuspid aortic valve
**C)** Previous infective endocarditis
**D)** Repaired VSD (2 years ago, no residual defect)

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: C) Previous infective endocarditis**

**Explanation:**
- **AHA 2007 Guidelines:** Prophylaxis ONLY for **HIGH-RISK** patients

**HIGH-RISK (Prophylaxis indicated) - "PPHC":**
1. **P**rosthetic cardiac valve
2. **P**revious infective endocarditis ✓
3. **H**eart transplant with valvulopathy
4. **C**yanotic congenital heart disease (unrepaired or repaired <6 months with residual defect)

**NO longer indicated for:**
- MVP (with or without regurgitation)
- Bicuspid aortic valve
- Acquired valve disease (AS, AR, MS, MR)
- **Repaired CHD >6 months without residual defect** (VSD, ASD, PDA)

**Regimen:** **Amoxicillin 2g PO** (30-60 min before procedure)
</details>

### Question 6
A patient with culture-negative IE has IgG Phase I antibody titer of 1:1024 against Coxiella burnetii. This is a major criterion for which classification system?

**A)** Jones criteria
**B)** Duke criteria
**C)** SIRS criteria
**D)** Framingham criteria

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: B) Duke criteria**

**Explanation:**
- **Modified Duke Criteria** used for IE diagnosis

**Major Criterion:**
- **Single positive blood culture for Coxiella burnetii** OR
- **IgG Phase I antibody titer >1:800**

**Coxiella burnetii (Q fever):**
- **Most common cause** of culture-negative IE (intracellular organism)
- Diagnosis: **Serology** (not blood culture)
- **Chronic Q fever** → IE
- Treatment: **Doxycycline + Hydroxychloroquine** (18 months minimum)

**Other Criteria:**
- **Jones:** Rheumatic fever
- **SIRS:** Sepsis
- **Framingham:** Heart failure
</details>

### Question 7
A patient with mitral valve IE has microscopic hematuria and RBC casts. What is the most likely renal complication?

**A)** Renal infarction
**B)** Acute tubular necrosis
**C)** Immune complex glomerulonephritis
**D)** Interstitial nephritis

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: C) Immune complex glomerulonephritis**

**Explanation:**
- **RBC casts** are pathognomonic of **glomerulonephritis**
- IE-associated GN: **Immune complex-mediated** (Type III hypersensitivity)

**Pathology:**
- **Diffuse proliferative GN** (most common)
- Less common: Focal proliferative, Membranoproliferative, Crescentic

**Presentation:**
- Microscopic hematuria
- Proteinuria (usually non-nephrotic)
- ↑ Creatinine (variable)
- Hypocomplementemia (low C3, C4)

**Management:**
- **Treat IE** (antibiotics) → GN usually resolves
- No need for immunosuppression (unlike primary GN)

**Differentials:**
- **Renal infarction:** Flank pain, hematuria (no RBC casts)
- **ATN:** Muddy brown casts
- **Interstitial nephritis:** WBC casts, eosinophiluria
</details>

### Question 8
What is the most appropriate empiric antibiotic regimen for suspected native valve IE in a community-acquired case?

**A)** Vancomycin alone
**B)** Ceftriaxone + Gentamicin
**C)** Ampicillin-Sulbactam + Gentamicin
**D)** Nafcillin + Rifampin

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: C) Ampicillin-Sulbactam + Gentamicin**

**Explanation:**
- **Empiric therapy** (before culture results) must cover **most likely organisms**

**Native Valve, Community-Acquired:**
- **Cover:** S. viridans, S. aureus (MSSA), Enterococcus
- **Regimen:** **Ampicillin-Sulbactam 12 g/day IV + Gentamicin 3 mg/kg/day**
  - Ampicillin-Sulbactam: Covers Strep, MSSA, Enterococcus
  - Gentamicin: Synergy for Strep and Enterococcus

**Alternatives:**
- **Vancomycin + Gentamicin** (if PCN allergy or risk of MRSA)
- **Ceftriaxone + Gentamicin** (alternative)

**Prosthetic Valve or Nosocomial:**
- **Vancomycin + Gentamicin + Rifampin** (cover MRSA, Coag-neg Staph)

**Targeted therapy** after culture results
</details>
