## DEFINITION & CLASSIFICATION

### Definition
**Interstitial Lung Diseases (ILDs)** are a heterogeneous group of disorders characterized by:
- Inflammation and/or fibrosis of the **pulmonary interstitium**
- **Restrictive pattern** on pulmonary function tests
- Impaired **gas exchange**
- Progressive **dyspnea** and **dry cough**

### Classification

```
INTERSTITIAL LUNG DISEASES
│
├─ KNOWN CAUSE (35%)
│  ├─ Drugs (Amiodarone, Bleomycin, Methotrexate, Nitrofurantoin)
│  ├─ Environmental/Occupational (Asbestosis, Silicosis, Coal worker's)
│  ├─ Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis (Farmer's lung, Bird fancier's)
│  └─ Connective Tissue Disease (RA, SLE, SSc, PM/DM, Sjögren's)
│
├─ IDIOPATHIC INTERSTITIAL PNEUMONIAS (35%)
│  ├─ Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis (IPF) - MOST COMMON
│  ├─ Non-Specific Interstitial Pneumonia (NSIP)
│  ├─ Cryptogenic Organizing Pneumonia (COP)
│  ├─ Acute Interstitial Pneumonia (AIP)
│  ├─ Respiratory Bronchiolitis-ILD (RB-ILD)
│  └─ Desquamative Interstitial Pneumonia (DIP)
│
└─ GRANULOMATOUS ILD (20%)
   ├─ Sarcoidosis - MOST COMMON GRANULOMATOUS ILD
   ├─ Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis (Chronic)
   └─ Berylliosis
```

### Mnemonic: "GRODE" for ILD Classification
- **G** - Granulomatous (Sarcoidosis, HP)
- **R** - Rheumatologic (CTD-ILD)
- **O** - Occupational/Environmental (Pneumoconiosis)
- **D** - Drug-induced
- **E** - idiopathic (IPF, NSIP, COP)

## EPIDEMIOLOGY

### Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis (IPF)
- **Most common idiopathic ILD** (~30% of all ILDs)
- Age: **>60 years** (rare <50 years)
- Sex: **Male > Female** (2:1)
- Risk factors: **Smoking**, genetic factors (TERT, TERC mutations), GERD

### Sarcoidosis
- **Most common ILD overall** in young adults
- Age: **20-40 years** (bimodal: 20-40 and 50-60)
- Sex: **Female > Male**
- Race: **African Americans** (10-17x higher), Scandinavians
- Löfgren syndrome: Acute sarcoidosis with **excellent prognosis**

### Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis
- Occurs at **any age**
- Related to **occupational/environmental exposure**
- Farmer's lung (moldy hay), Bird fancier's lung (avian proteins)

## PATHOPHYSIOLOGY

### Common Pathway

```
INITIAL INJURY (Drugs/Environment/Autoimmune/Unknown)
           ↓
    ALVEOLAR INJURY
           ↓
    INFLAMMATION (Alveolitis)
    • Lymphocytes, Macrophages
    • Cytokine release (TGF-β, PDGF)
           ↓
    FIBROBLAST PROLIFERATION
           ↓
    COLLAGEN DEPOSITION
           ↓
    INTERSTITIAL FIBROSIS
           ↓
    ARCHITECTURAL DISTORTION
    • Honeycombing
    • Loss of alveolar-capillary units
           ↓
    RESTRICTIVE LUNG DISEASE
    • ↓ Lung volumes
    • ↓ Compliance
    • ↓ Diffusion capacity
           ↓
    PULMONARY HYPERTENSION (Late)
           ↓
    COR PULMONALE
```

### Key Pathologic Patterns

| Pattern | Characteristic Feature | Associated Disease |
|---------|------------------------|-------------------|
| **UIP** (Usual Interstitial Pneumonia) | **Honeycombing**, subpleural/basal, **heterogeneous**, fibroblast foci | **IPF** (definite), CTD-ILD |
| **NSIP** (Non-Specific Interstitial Pneumonia) | **Homogeneous**, temporally uniform, cellular or fibrotic | CTD-ILD, **Idiopathic NSIP** |
| **OP** (Organizing Pneumonia) | **Masson bodies** (plugs in alveoli/bronchioles), patchy | **COP**, Secondary OP |
| **DAD** (Diffuse Alveolar Damage) | **Hyaline membranes**, acute onset | **AIP**, ARDS |
| **RB** (Respiratory Bronchiolitis) | **Smoker's macrophages** in bronchioles | **RB-ILD** |
| **DIP** (Desquamative Interstitial Pneumonia) | Diffuse **alveolar macrophage accumulation** | **DIP** (smoking-related) |
| **Granulomatous** | **Non-caseating granulomas** | **Sarcoidosis**, HP |

## CLINICAL FEATURES

### Common Symptoms (Across All ILDs)

1. **Progressive Dyspnea on Exertion** (MOST COMMON)
   - Insidious onset over months-years
   - Progressive breathlessness

2. **Dry Cough** (Non-productive)
   - Persistent, irritating
   - May be the presenting symptom

3. **Constitutional Symptoms**
   - Fatigue, weight loss
   - Low-grade fever (HP, Sarcoidosis)

4. **Chest Discomfort**
   - Vague chest tightness

### Physical Examination

1. **Respiratory Examination**
   - **Inspiratory Bibasilar Crackles** ("Velcro crackles")
     - **Most sensitive sign** (90% of IPF)
     - Fine, dry crackles at lung bases
   - **Reduced chest expansion**

2. **Digital Clubbing**
   - Present in **50-70% of IPF** (HIGHLY SPECIFIC)
   - Rare in Sarcoidosis (helps differentiate)

3. **Signs of Pulmonary Hypertension** (Advanced disease)
   - Loud P2, RV heave, JVP elevation
   - Pedal edema, ascites

4. **Extrapulmonary Manifestations** (Disease-specific)
   - **Sarcoidosis**: Skin lesions, uveitis, lymphadenopathy
   - **CTD-ILD**: Joint deformities, skin changes
   - **HP**: None typically

## DIAGNOSIS

### Initial Evaluation

#### 1. Pulmonary Function Tests (PFTs)

**Classic Finding: RESTRICTIVE PATTERN with ↓ DLCO**

| Parameter | Finding | Explanation |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **FVC** | ↓ Decreased | Loss of lung volume |
| **FEV₁** | ↓ Decreased | Proportional to FVC |
| **FEV₁/FVC** | **Normal or ↑** | ≥0.70 (RESTRICTIVE) |
| **TLC** | ↓ Decreased | ↓ Lung compliance |
| **RV** | ↓ Decreased | Cannot fully expand |
| **DLCO** | **↓↓ Markedly Decreased** | Loss of alveolar-capillary surface |
| **A-a gradient** | ↑ Increased | Impaired gas exchange |

**Key Point**: ↓ DLCO is the **most sensitive early marker** of ILD.

#### 2. Chest X-Ray

**Classic Findings**:
- **Bilateral reticular opacities** (interstitial pattern)
- **Lower lobe predominance** (IPF, NSIP, CTD-ILD)
- **Upper lobe predominance** (Sarcoidosis, HP, Silicosis)
- **Honeycombing** (Advanced IPF)
- **Reduced lung volumes**

**Limitations**: Normal CXR in **10-15% of early ILD**

#### 3. High-Resolution CT (HRCT) Chest

**GOLD STANDARD for ILD diagnosis and characterization**

```
HRCT PATTERNS IN ILD
│
├─ UIP PATTERN (IPF)
│  • Subpleural, basal predominance
│  • Honeycombing (definite UIP)
│  • Reticular opacities
│  • Traction bronchiectasis
│  • Minimal ground-glass opacities
│  • Heterogeneous (patchy) distribution
│
├─ NSIP PATTERN
│  • Subpleural sparing (unlike UIP)
│  • Ground-glass opacities > reticular
│  • Homogeneous distribution
│  • Lower lobe predominance
│  • Minimal honeycombing
│
├─ ORGANIZING PNEUMONIA (OP)
│  • Patchy consolidation
│  • Peribronchovascular distribution
│  • Ground-glass opacities
│  • Subpleural/peribronchial
│  • "Reverse halo sign" (Atoll sign)
│
├─ HYPERSENSITIVITY PNEUMONITIS
│  • Ground-glass opacities (Acute/Subacute)
│  • Centrilobular nodules
│  • Mosaic attenuation (Air trapping)
│  • Upper/mid lobe predominance
│  • Fibrosis (Chronic HP)
│
└─ SARCOIDOSIS
   • Bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy (BHL)
   • Perilymphatic nodules
   • Upper lobe predominance
   • Bronchocentric distribution
   • Fibrosis (Stage IV)
```

#### 4. Laboratory Tests

| Test | Purpose | Finding |
|------|---------|---------|
| **CBC** | Anemia, eosinophilia | Eosinophilia (Drug-induced ILD, CEP) |
| **ESR/CRP** | Inflammation | ↑ in Sarcoidosis, CTD-ILD |
| **ANA** | Connective tissue disease | Positive in SLE, SSc, Sjögren's |
| **RF** | Rheumatoid arthritis | Positive in RA-ILD |
| **Anti-Scl-70** | Systemic sclerosis | SSc-ILD |
| **Anti-Jo-1** | Myositis | PM/DM-ILD |
| **ACE level** | Sarcoidosis | ↑ in 60% (not specific) |
| **Serum calcium** | Sarcoidosis | Hypercalcemia (10%) |
| **Serum precipitins** | Hypersensitivity pneumonitis | Positive (exposure history) |

#### 5. Arterial Blood Gas (ABG)

**Early**: Hypoxemia on exertion
**Advanced**: Hypoxemia at rest
**A-a gradient**: ↑ Increased (V/Q mismatch, diffusion limitation)
**Pattern**: Type 1 Respiratory Failure (↓ PaO₂, Normal/↓ PaCO₂)

#### 6. 6-Minute Walk Test (6MWT)

- Assess **exercise capacity** and **desaturation**
- **>4% drop in SpO₂** or **<250 meters** distance → Poor prognosis
- Serial monitoring for disease progression

#### 7. Bronchoscopy with BAL/Biopsy

**Indications**:
- Uncertain diagnosis after HRCT
- Rule out infection/malignancy
- Specific diagnoses (Sarcoidosis, HP, Eosinophilic pneumonia)

| Disease | BAL Findings |
|---------|--------------|
| **Sarcoidosis** | ↑ Lymphocytes, **CD4/CD8 ratio >3.5** |
| **Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis** | ↑↑ Lymphocytes (>50%), **CD4/CD8 ratio <1** |
| **Eosinophilic Pneumonia** | ↑↑ Eosinophils (>25%) |
| **Pulmonary Alveolar Proteinosis** | **Milky fluid**, PAS-positive material |
| **Hemorrhage** | Hemosiderin-laden macrophages |
| **IPF** | Neutrophils, eosinophils (non-specific) |

**Transbronchial Biopsy**:
- **Good yield**: Sarcoidosis (granulomas in 80%), Organizing pneumonia
- **Low yield**: IPF, NSIP (need surgical biopsy)

#### 8. Surgical Lung Biopsy

**Indications**:
- Uncertain diagnosis after non-invasive tests
- **IPF diagnosis** (if HRCT not typical)
- Young patients requiring definitive diagnosis

**Methods**:
- **Video-Assisted Thoracoscopic Surgery (VATS)** - Preferred
- Open lung biopsy (if VATS not feasible)

**Sites**: Multiple lobes (heterogeneity in ILD), avoid lingula/middle lobe

## IDIOPATHIC PULMONARY FIBROSIS (IPF)

### Definition
**Most common** and **most lethal** idiopathic interstitial pneumonia
Histopathologic pattern: **UIP (Usual Interstitial Pneumonia)**

### Clinical Features

**Age**: **>60 years** (rare <50)
**Sex**: Male > Female (2:1)
**Risk Factors**: Smoking, GERD, family history, viral infections

**Symptoms**:
- **Progressive dyspnea** over 6-24 months
- **Dry cough** (persistent)
- **Fatigue**, weight loss

**Signs**:
- **Bibasilar inspiratory crackles** (Velcro crackles) - 90%
- **Clubbing** (50-70%)
- Cyanosis, pulmonary hypertension (advanced)

### Diagnosis

#### HRCT Criteria for UIP Pattern

| Feature | Definite UIP | Probable UIP | Indeterminate | Alternative Dx |
|---------|--------------|--------------|---------------|----------------|
| **Honeycombing** | Present | Absent | Absent | Absent |
| **Reticular opacities** | Subpleural/basal | Subpleural/basal | Subpleural/basal | Variable |
| **Distribution** | Heterogeneous | Heterogeneous | Heterogeneous | Diffuse |
| **Ground-glass** | Minimal | Minimal | Extensive | Predominant |
| **Consolidation** | Absent | Absent | May be present | Present |
| **Nodules/cysts** | Absent | Absent | May be present | Present |

**IPF Diagnosis Algorithm**:

```
HRCT → Definite UIP pattern + Clinical context
        ↓
        IPF DIAGNOSIS (No biopsy needed)

HRCT → Probable UIP/Indeterminate
        ↓
        SURGICAL LUNG BIOPSY (VATS)
        ↓
        UIP histology → IPF
```

#### Histopathology (UIP Pattern)

**Key Features**:
1. **Heterogeneous** involvement (patchy)
2. **Temporal heterogeneity** (old and new fibrosis)
3. **Fibroblast foci** (active fibrosis)
4. **Honeycombing** (end-stage)
5. **Subpleural and paraseptal distribution**
6. **Minimal inflammation**

### Prognosis

**Median Survival**: **2.5-3.5 years** from diagnosis
**Poor Prognostic Factors**:
- Age >70 years
- Male sex
- Severe dyspnea (NYHA III/IV)
- ↓↓ FVC (<50%), ↓↓ DLCO (<35%)
- ↓ 6MWT distance (<250 m) or desaturation
- Pulmonary hypertension
- Acute exacerbation

**GAP Index** (Gender, Age, Physiology):

| Variable | Points |
|----------|--------|
| **Gender** (Male) | 1 |
| **Age** (>60 years) | 1 |
| **FVC** 50-75% predicted | 1 |
| **FVC** <50% predicted | 2 |
| **DLCO** 36-55% predicted | 1 |
| **DLCO** ≤35% predicted | 2 |

- **0-3 points**: Stage I (Mortality 6% at 1 year)
- **4-5 points**: Stage II (Mortality 16% at 1 year)
- **6-8 points**: Stage III (Mortality 39% at 1 year)

### Treatment

#### Antifibrotic Therapy

**Approved Drugs** (Slow disease progression):

1. **Pirfenidone**
   - Mechanism: Anti-fibrotic, anti-inflammatory
   - Dose: 801 mg TID (with food)
   - Side effects: **Photosensitivity**, GI upset, elevated LFTs
   - Monitoring: LFTs every month × 6, then q3 months
   - **Sun protection mandatory**

2. **Nintedanib**
   - Mechanism: Tyrosine kinase inhibitor (PDGFR, FGFR, VEGFR)
   - Dose: 150 mg BID
   - Side effects: **Diarrhea** (60%), nausea, elevated LFTs
   - Monitoring: LFTs, pregnancy test (teratogenic)

**Evidence**: Both drugs ↓ FVC decline by ~50%, no mortality benefit proven

#### Supportive Care

- **Oxygen therapy**: For hypoxemia (SpO₂ <88%)
- **Pulmonary rehabilitation**: Improves QOL
- **GERD treatment**: PPIs (↓ microaspiration)
- **Vaccination**: Influenza, pneumococcal
- **Smoking cessation**: Mandatory

#### Lung Transplantation

**Indications**:
- Progressive disease despite antifibrotics
- FVC <80% or DLCO <40%
- Significant hypoxemia
- 6MWT <250 m or desaturation

**Timing**: **Early referral** (GAP Stage II or III)

**Contraindications**:
- Age >65-70 years (relative)
- Severe comorbidities
- Active malignancy

### Acute Exacerbation of IPF

**Definition**: Acute worsening of dyspnea (<1 month) without identifiable cause

**Diagnostic Criteria**:
1. Previous/concurrent IPF diagnosis
2. Acute worsening (<1 month)
3. HRCT: New bilateral ground-glass/consolidation
4. Not explained by infection, PE, heart failure, pneumothorax

**Management**:
- **High-dose corticosteroids**: Methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg IV × 3 days
- **Supportive care**: Oxygen, mechanical ventilation
- **Empiric antibiotics**: If infection cannot be excluded
- Consider **lung transplant** evaluation

**Prognosis**: In-hospital mortality **50-80%**

## HYPERSENSITIVITY PNEUMONITIS

### Definition
Immunologically mediated lung disease from **repeated inhalation of organic antigens** in sensitized individuals.

Also called: **Extrinsic Allergic Alveolitis**

### Common Causes

| Disease | Antigen Source | Antigen |
|---------|---------------|---------|
| **Farmer's Lung** | Moldy hay, grain | Thermophilic actinomycetes |
| **Bird Fancier's Lung** | Avian droppings, feathers | Avian proteins |
| **Humidifier Lung** | Contaminated water | Bacteria, fungi, amoebae |
| **Hot Tub Lung** | Hot tub water | *Mycobacterium avium* complex |
| **Cheese Worker's Lung** | Cheese mold | *Penicillium casei* |
| **Mushroom Worker's Lung** | Mushroom compost | Thermophilic actinomycetes |
| **Bagassosis** | Moldy sugarcane | Thermophilic actinomycetes |
| **Malt Worker's Lung** | Moldy barley | *Aspergillus clavatus* |

### Pathophysiology

**Type III (Immune Complex) + Type IV (Cell-Mediated) Hypersensitivity**

```
ANTIGEN INHALATION (in sensitized individual)
           ↓
    IMMUNE RESPONSE
    • Type III: Immune complex formation (4-8 hours)
    • Type IV: T-cell mediated (48-72 hours)
           ↓
    ACUTE HP: Alveolitis, PMNs
           ↓
    SUBACUTE HP: Granulomas, Lymphocytes
           ↓
    CHRONIC HP: Fibrosis, Honeycombing
```

### Clinical Presentation

#### Acute HP

**Timing**: **4-8 hours after exposure**

**Symptoms**:
- Sudden onset **dyspnea, cough**
- **Fever, chills** (flu-like illness)
- Chest tightness

**Signs**:
- Tachypnea, crackles
- Fever

**Recovery**: Symptoms resolve in **24-48 hours** if exposure stopped

**Key Point**: **Recurrent acute episodes** with re-exposure

#### Subacute HP

**Timing**: Days to weeks of exposure

**Symptoms**:
- Progressive dyspnea, cough
- Fatigue, weight loss
- Low-grade fever

**Signs**:
- Bibasilar crackles
- No clubbing

#### Chronic HP

**Timing**: Months to years of **low-level exposure**

**Symptoms**:
- Insidious dyspnea, cough
- Weight loss, fatigue
- **No fever** (unlike acute)

**Signs**:
- Crackles
- Clubbing (rare, <10%)
- Pulmonary hypertension (advanced)

**Key Point**: Mimics **IPF** (difficult to differentiate)

### Diagnosis

#### 1. History

**CRITICAL**: Detailed **exposure history**
- Occupation, hobbies (birds, farming)
- Home environment (mold, humidifiers, hot tubs)
- Temporal relationship (symptoms improve away from exposure)

#### 2. HRCT Chest

| Stage | HRCT Findings |
|-------|---------------|
| **Acute** | Diffuse **ground-glass opacities**, poorly defined centrilobular nodules |
| **Subacute** | **Centrilobular nodules**, ground-glass, **mosaic attenuation** (air trapping), upper/mid lobe |
| **Chronic** | **Fibrosis** (UIP-like pattern), traction bronchiectasis, honeycombing, upper lobe |

**Mosaic Attenuation on Expiratory CT**: **Highly suggestive** of HP (air trapping)

#### 3. Pulmonary Function Tests

- **Restrictive** pattern (↓ FVC, ↓ TLC)
- **↓↓ DLCO** (prominent)
- **Air trapping** (↑ RV/TLC ratio)
- **Mixed obstructive-restrictive** in chronic HP

#### 4. Serum Precipitins (IgG antibodies)

- Test for specific antigens (avian, thermophilic actinomycetes)
- **Positive**: Indicates **exposure**, NOT necessarily disease
- **Negative**: Does not rule out HP (sensitivity 70-90%)

#### 5. Bronchoalveolar Lavage (BAL)

**Most helpful test**:
- **Lymphocytosis** (>50% in subacute, >30% in chronic)
- **CD4/CD8 ratio <1** (CD8+ predominance) - **Opposite of Sarcoidosis**
- ↑ Mast cells

#### 6. Lung Biopsy

**Histology**:
- **Poorly-formed non-caseating granulomas** (centrilobular)
- **Chronic bronchiolitis**
- **Lymphocytic infiltration**
- Fibrosis (chronic HP)

### Diagnostic Criteria (Clinical Prediction Rule)

**Major Criteria** (need all 4):
1. Exposure to known antigen
2. Positive serum precipitins or BAL lymphocytosis
3. Compatible HRCT findings
4. Compatible histology (if biopsy done)

**Minor Criteria**:
- Recurrent symptoms with re-exposure
- Inspiratory crackles
- Weight loss
- Restrictive PFTs with ↓ DLCO

### Treatment

#### Antigen Avoidance

**MOST IMPORTANT**: Eliminate exposure
- Occupational changes
- Remove birds, contaminated equipment
- Environmental modifications (dehumidifiers, air filters)

**Prognosis**: Acute/Subacute HP → **Complete recovery** if antigen avoided early

#### Corticosteroids

**Indications**:
- Moderate-to-severe acute/subacute HP
- Chronic HP with significant inflammation

**Regimen**:
- **Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day** × 4-8 weeks
- Taper over 3-6 months

**Efficacy**: Improves symptoms/PFTs in acute/subacute, limited benefit in chronic fibrotic HP

#### Immunosuppressants

For chronic fibrotic HP not responding to steroids:
- **Mycophenolate mofetil** (1-3 g/day)
- **Azathioprine** (2 mg/kg/day)

#### Antifibrotics

**Nintedanib**: FDA-approved for **progressive fibrosing ILD** (includes chronic HP)

#### Lung Transplantation

For end-stage chronic HP

## SARCOIDOSIS

### Definition
**Multisystem granulomatous disease** of unknown etiology
Characterized by **non-caseating granulomas**

### Epidemiology

- **Peak age**: 20-40 years (bimodal: also 50-60)
- **Female > Male**
- **Race**: African Americans (10-17x), Scandinavians
- **Organ involvement**: Lungs (90%), Lymph nodes (90%), Skin (25%), Eyes (25%), Liver, Heart

### Pathophysiology

```
UNKNOWN TRIGGER (Infection? Environmental?)
           ↓
    CD4+ T-CELL ACTIVATION
           ↓
    TH1 CYTOKINE RELEASE (IFN-γ, IL-2, TNF-α)
           ↓
    MACROPHAGE ACTIVATION
           ↓
    NON-CASEATING GRANULOMA FORMATION
    • Epithelioid cells
    • Multinucleated giant cells (Langhans)
    • CD4+ T-cells at periphery
           ↓
    OUTCOMES
    • Spontaneous resolution (60-70%)
    • Persistent disease (20-30%)
    • Fibrosis (10-20%)
```

### Clinical Features

#### Pulmonary Sarcoidosis (90%)

**Symptoms**:
- **Asymptomatic** (30-50%) - Incidental CXR finding
- Dry cough, dyspnea
- Chest discomfort
- Wheezing (rare)

**Signs**:
- Crackles (uncommon, unlike IPF)
- **Clubbing is RARE** (<5%) - Helps differentiate from IPF

#### Extrapulmonary Sarcoidosis

| Organ | Manifestations | Investigations |
|-------|----------------|----------------|
| **Lymph Nodes** | Bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy (BHL), mediastinal LAD | CXR/CT |
| **Skin** (25%) | **Erythema nodosum** (acute), **Lupus pernio** (chronic, violaceous plaques on nose/cheeks) | Biopsy |
| **Eyes** (25%) | **Uveitis** (anterior > posterior), lacrimal gland enlargement | Slit-lamp exam |
| **Cardiac** (5%) | **Arrhythmias** (VT, heart block), cardiomyopathy, sudden death | ECG, echo, cardiac MRI |
| **Nervous** (5%) | **Cranial nerve palsy** (CN VII - Bell's palsy), meningitis, seizures | MRI, CSF |
| **Liver** | Hepatomegaly, cholestasis, cirrhosis (rare) | LFTs, biopsy |
| **Kidney** | Hypercalcemia, hypercalciuria, nephrolithiasis, nephrocalcinosis | Serum Ca²⁺, PTH |
| **Bone** | Osteolytic lesions (phalanges), dactylitis | X-ray |
| **Spleen** | Splenomegaly | Exam, imaging |

#### Classic Syndromes

**1. Löfgren Syndrome** (Acute Sarcoidosis)
- **Triad**: Erythema nodosum + BHL + Arthralgias (ankles)
- **Excellent prognosis**: 80-90% spontaneous remission
- Treatment: NSAIDs, rarely steroids

**2. Heerfordt Syndrome** (Uveoparotid Fever)
- **Tetrad**: Uveitis + Parotitis + Fever + Facial nerve palsy (CN VII)

**3. Mikulicz Syndrome**
- Bilateral enlargement of lacrimal and parotid glands

### Diagnosis

#### Staging (CXR)

| Stage | CXR Findings | Frequency | Spontaneous Resolution |
|-------|--------------|-----------|------------------------|
| **0** | Normal CXR | 5% | N/A |
| **I** | **BHL alone** (no parenchymal involvement) | 50% | **60-80%** |
| **II** | BHL + Parenchymal infiltrates | 30% | 50-60% |
| **III** | Parenchymal infiltrates **without BHL** | 10% | 30% |
| **IV** | **Pulmonary fibrosis** (honeycombing, traction bronchiectasis) | 5% | **0%** (irreversible) |

**Key Point**: **BHL is BILATERAL and SYMMETRIC** (if asymmetric → suspect malignancy)

#### HRCT Findings

- **Perilymphatic nodules** (along bronchovascular bundles, subpleural, interlobular septa)
- **Upper lobe predominance** (unlike IPF)
- BHL with calcification (eggshell pattern in late stages)
- Fibrosis: Bronchocentric, upper lobe, traction bronchiectasis

#### Laboratory Tests

| Test | Finding | Utility |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Serum ACE** | Elevated in 60% | Monitor disease activity (not diagnostic) |
| **Serum calcium** | Hypercalcemia (10%) | Granulomas produce calcitriol (1,25-OH Vit D) |
| **24-hr urine calcium** | Hypercalciuria (40%) | More sensitive than serum calcium |
| **Serum lysozyme** | Elevated | Alternative to ACE |
| **CBC** | Lymphopenia, anemia | Non-specific |
| **LFTs** | ↑ ALP, ↑ GGT | Liver/bone involvement |

**ACE Level**:
- Elevated in 60% of **active** sarcoidosis
- **NOT specific** (also elevated in TB, silicosis, Gaucher's)
- Useful for **monitoring treatment response**

#### Bronchoalveolar Lavage (BAL)

- **Lymphocytosis** (>15%)
- **CD4/CD8 ratio >3.5** (CD4+ predominance) - **Opposite of HP**

#### Tissue Biopsy

**Gold Standard**: **Non-caseating granulomas** (epithelioid cells, Langhans giant cells, no necrosis)

**Biopsy sites** (in order of preference):
1. **Skin lesions** (if present) - Easiest
2. **Transbronchial lung biopsy** (TBLB) - Yield 80-90% in Stage II/III
3. **Endobronchial ultrasound-guided biopsy** (EBUS) - Mediastinal lymph nodes
4. **Mediastinoscopy** - If bronchoscopy non-diagnostic
5. **Kveim test** (historical, no longer used)

**Important**: Must exclude **infection** (AFB, fungal stains)

### Indications for Treatment

**NOT all patients require treatment** (many have spontaneous remission)

**Treat if**:
1. **Symptomatic disease** (dyspnea, cough)
2. **Organ-threatening disease**:
   - Cardiac sarcoidosis (arrhythmias)
   - Neurologic sarcoidosis
   - Ocular sarcoidosis (posterior uveitis, threat to vision)
   - Hypercalcemia
3. **Progressive pulmonary disease** (↓ PFTs, worsening CXR)
4. **Stage II/III with symptoms**

**Do NOT treat**:
- Asymptomatic Stage I (BHL alone) - High rate of spontaneous resolution
- Asymptomatic Stage II with stable PFTs

### Treatment

#### Corticosteroids (First-Line)

**Regimen**:
- **Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day** (20-40 mg/day) × 4-6 weeks
- Taper to 10-20 mg/day over 6 months
- **Total duration: 12-24 months** (longer than HP)

**Response**: Improves symptoms, CXR, PFTs in 60-80%

**Monitoring**:
- PFTs every 3-6 months
- CXR/HRCT
- Serum ACE (if initially elevated)

#### Steroid-Sparing Agents (Second-Line)

**Indications**: Steroid-refractory, intolerable side effects, need for prolonged therapy

1. **Methotrexate** (10-25 mg/week)
   - Most commonly used
   - Monitor: CBC, LFTs, avoid alcohol

2. **Azathioprine** (2-3 mg/kg/day)
   - Check TPMT level before starting

3. **Mycophenolate mofetil** (1-3 g/day)

4. **Leflunomide** (10-20 mg/day)

#### Biologics (Third-Line)

**Infliximab** (Anti-TNF-α):
- Refractory sarcoidosis
- Dose: 3-5 mg/kg at 0, 2, 6 weeks, then q8 weeks
- **FDA-approved** for pulmonary, cutaneous, extrapulmonary sarcoidosis

#### Organ-Specific Therapy

| Organ | Treatment |
|-------|-----------|
| **Cardiac** | **High-dose steroids** (40-60 mg/day), AICD for VT/VF, pacemaker for heart block |
| **Neurologic** | **High-dose steroids**, MTX/AZA, infliximab |
| **Ocular** | **Topical steroids** (anterior uveitis), systemic steroids (posterior uveitis) |
| **Hypercalcemia** | Steroids, avoid Vitamin D and sun exposure, hydration |
| **Skin** | Topical/intralesional steroids, antimalarials (hydroxychloroquine) |

### Prognosis

- **60-70%**: Spontaneous remission within 2 years
- **20-30%**: Persistent disease requiring treatment
- **10-20%**: Progressive fibrosis
- **Mortality**: 1-5% (cardiac, neurologic, respiratory failure)

**Favorable Prognosis**:
- Löfgren syndrome
- Stage I disease
- Asymptomatic
- Age <40 years

**Poor Prognosis**:
- Stage III/IV
- Lupus pernio
- Cardiac/neurologic involvement
- African American race
- Age >40 years at onset

## CONNECTIVE TISSUE DISEASE-ILD

### Overview

**ILD occurs in 10-40% of patients with CTD**
**Most common in Systemic Sclerosis (SSc-ILD)**

### Specific CTD-ILD

| CTD | ILD Frequency | Pattern | Key Features |
|-----|---------------|---------|--------------|
| **Systemic Sclerosis (SSc)** | **70-90%** | **NSIP > UIP** | **Most common CTD-ILD**, Anti-Scl-70 Ab, subpleural sparing on HRCT |
| **Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)** | 10-30% | **UIP > NSIP** | Male smokers, RA-ILD more common than RA-arthritis in some, Anti-CCP Ab |
| **Polymyositis/Dermatomyositis** | 40-65% | **NSIP, OP** | Anti-Jo-1 (Anti-synthetase syndrome), **Rapidly progressive** |
| **Sjögren's Syndrome** | 10-20% | NSIP, LIP | Xerostomia, keratoconjunctivitis sicca, Anti-Ro/La Ab |
| **SLE** | 3-10% | **Acute lupus pneumonitis**, NSIP | Pleuritis more common than ILD, Anti-dsDNA Ab |
| **Mixed CTD** | 20-40% | NSIP | Anti-U1 RNP Ab |

### Clinical Approach

**Suspect CTD-ILD if**:
- ILD in a young patient (<50 years)
- ILD + arthritis/skin rash/Raynaud's/myositis
- Positive ANA/RF/Anti-CCP
- HRCT shows **NSIP pattern** (more common than UIP in CTD-ILD)

### Specific Features

#### Systemic Sclerosis-ILD (SSc-ILD)

**Most common cause of death in SSc**

**Risk Factors**:
- **Diffuse cutaneous SSc** (dcSSc)
- **Anti-Scl-70 (Anti-topoisomerase I)** antibody
- African American race

**HRCT**: NSIP > UIP, **subpleural sparing** (unlike IPF)

**Treatment**:
- **Mycophenolate mofetil** (1-3 g/day) - **First-line**
- **Cyclophosphamide** (alternative)
- **Nintedanib** (FDA-approved for SSc-ILD)
- Avoid steroids alone (↑ renal crisis risk)

#### RA-ILD

**Epidemiology**: Male smokers, Anti-CCP positive

**Patterns**: UIP > NSIP, Organizing pneumonia, Follicular bronchiolitis

**Drug-Induced ILD**: Methotrexate (hypersensitivity), anti-TNF agents

**Treatment**:
- Steroids + steroid-sparing agents (AZA, MMF)
- Avoid MTX if drug-induced
- **Nintedanib** for progressive fibrosing ILD

#### Anti-Synthetase Syndrome

**Associated with**: Polymyositis/Dermatomyositis

**Antibodies**: Anti-Jo-1 (most common), Anti-PL-7, Anti-PL-12

**Clinical Features** - "Mechanic's Hands, Myositis, ILD":
- ILD (often presenting feature)
- Myositis (proximal muscle weakness)
- Arthritis (non-erosive)
- **Mechanic's hands** (hyperkeratosis, fissures)
- Raynaud's phenomenon
- Fever

**Pattern**: NSIP, Organizing pneumonia, **rapidly progressive**

**Treatment**:
- **High-dose steroids** + Immunosuppressants (AZA, MMF, CYC)
- Rituximab (refractory cases)

### Treatment Approach to CTD-ILD

1. **Treat underlying CTD**
2. **Immunosuppression** (more effective than in IPF):
   - Steroids + MMF/AZA/CYC
3. **Antifibrotics** (if progressive fibrosing ILD):
   - Nintedanib (FDA-approved for SSc-ILD and progressive fibrosing ILD)
4. **Lung transplant** (end-stage)

## DRUG-INDUCED ILD

### Common Drugs

| Drug Class | Specific Drugs | Pattern |
|------------|----------------|---------|
| **Chemotherapy** | **Bleomycin**, Methotrexate, Cyclophosphamide, Gemcitabine | Pulmonary fibrosis, HP-like |
| **Antibiotics** | **Nitrofurantoin** (most common), Sulfasalazine | Acute/Chronic pneumonitis |
| **Cardiovascular** | **Amiodarone**, Statins, Beta-blockers | Chronic interstitial pneumonitis, ARDS |
| **Biologics** | Anti-TNF (Infliximab, Etanercept), Rituximab, Checkpoint inhibitors (Nivolumab, Pembrolizumab) | NSIP, OP, HP-like |
| **Antimalarial** | Hydroxychloroquine | Rare |
| **Illicit** | Cocaine, Heroin | Acute lung injury, eosinophilic pneumonia |

### Mnemonic: "BANANA" for Common Drug-Induced ILD
- **B** - Bleomycin
- **A** - Amiodarone
- **N** - Nitrofurantoin
- **A** - Anti-TNF agents
- **N** - Nivolumab/Checkpoint inhibitors
- **A** - Azathioprine (rare)

### Specific Drugs

#### Amiodarone

**Frequency**: 5-15% of chronic users

**Risk Factors**:
- **Cumulative dose >400 g** (or >400 mg/day × 2 months)
- Pre-existing lung disease
- Thoracic surgery (↑ oxygen toxicity)

**Presentation**:
- Insidious dyspnea, cough
- Months-years after starting

**HRCT**: Diffuse ground-glass, consolidation, **high attenuation** (iodine content)

**Diagnosis**: High suspicion + compatible imaging + exclusion of other causes

**Treatment**:
- **STOP amiodarone** (long half-life: 40-60 days)
- **Steroids** (Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day × 4-6 weeks, then taper)
- Recovery in most, but can be fatal

#### Bleomycin

**Frequency**: 10% (dose-dependent)

**Risk Factors**:
- **Cumulative dose >450 units**
- Age >70 years
- Renal impairment
- Oxygen therapy, radiation

**Presentation**:
- Acute pneumonitis → Chronic fibrosis
- Dyspnea, dry cough during/after chemotherapy

**HRCT**: NSIP pattern, basal predominant

**Management**:
- **STOP bleomycin**
- **Steroids** (benefit unclear)
- Avoid supplemental oxygen (↑ toxicity)

**Prevention**: Limit cumulative dose, monitor PFTs (↓ DLCO early sign)

#### Nitrofurantoin

**Most common antibiotic causing ILD**

**Forms**:
1. **Acute** (within days-weeks):
   - Fever, dyspnea, cough
   - **Peripheral eosinophilia** (30%)
   - CXR: Bilateral infiltrates, pleural effusion
   - **Rapid improvement** after stopping drug

2. **Chronic** (months-years):
   - Insidious dyspnea
   - CXR/HRCT: Fibrosis (NSIP/UIP pattern)
   - May not improve after stopping

**Treatment**: STOP drug, steroids (acute form)

#### Checkpoint Inhibitors (Immune Checkpoint Inhibitor-Pneumonitis)

**Drugs**: Nivolumab, Pembrolizumab, Atezolizumab (Anti-PD-1/PD-L1)

**Frequency**: 3-5%

**Presentation**:
- Dyspnea, cough
- Median onset: 2-3 months (range: 1 week - 2 years)

**HRCT**: Organizing pneumonia, NSIP, hypersensitivity pneumonitis-like

**Management**:
- **Grade 1** (Asymptomatic): Continue drug, monitor
- **Grade 2** (Symptomatic): Hold drug, **Steroids** (0.5-1 mg/kg/day)
- **Grade 3-4** (Severe): **STOP drug permanently**, High-dose steroids (1-2 mg/kg/day)
- **Infliximab** if refractory (paradoxical, but anti-TNF helps)

### General Approach to Drug-Induced ILD

1. **High index of suspicion** (temporal relationship)
2. **Exclude other causes** (infection, CHF, PE)
3. **STOP offending drug**
4. **Steroids** (if severe or not improving)
5. **Re-challenge NOT recommended** (risk of recurrence)

**Resource**: www.pneumotox.com (comprehensive database)

## MANAGEMENT

### General Principles

| Strategy | Details |
|----------|---------|
| **Treat underlying cause** | Antigen avoidance (HP), immunosuppression (CTD-ILD), stop drug (Drug-ILD) |
| **Antifibrotics** | IPF, progressive fibrosing ILD |
| **Immunosuppression** | Sarcoidosis, HP, CTD-ILD, inflammatory ILDs |
| **Supportive care** | Oxygen, pulmonary rehabilitation, vaccinations |
| **Lung transplantation** | End-stage ILD |

### Oxygen Therapy

**Indications**:
- Resting SpO₂ ≤88% OR
- Exercise desaturation ≤88% OR
- PaO₂ ≤55 mmHg

**Goal**: SpO₂ ≥90% (PaO₂ ≥60 mmHg)

**Evidence**: Improves survival in IPF patients with hypoxemia

### Pulmonary Rehabilitation

**Components**:
- Exercise training (aerobic + strength)
- Breathing techniques
- Nutritional counseling
- Psychosocial support

**Benefits**:
- Improves 6MWT distance
- Reduces dyspnea
- Improves quality of life
- **No survival benefit**

### Vaccinations

**Recommended**:
- **Influenza** (annual)
- **Pneumococcal** (PCV20 or PCV15 + PPSV23)
- **COVID-19**

### Monitoring

**Schedule**: Every 3-6 months (more frequent if progressive)

| Test | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| **PFTs** | Assess progression (↓ FVC ≥10% or ↓ DLCO ≥15% → progressive) |
| **6MWT** | Functional capacity, desaturation |
| **CXR/HRCT** | Structural changes (annual HRCT) |
| **Pulse oximetry** | Hypoxemia |

### Lung Transplantation

**Indications** (General):
- Progressive disease despite optimal therapy
- Severe functional limitation (NYHA III-IV)
- Hypoxemia
- 6MWT <250 m

**Disease-Specific**:
- **IPF**: Refer early (median survival 3 years)
- **Sarcoidosis**: Stage IV, pulmonary hypertension
- **CTD-ILD**: Progressive despite immunosuppression

**Post-Transplant Survival**:
- Median survival: **5-6 years**
- IPF has better post-transplant outcomes than other ILDs

## COMPLICATIONS

### 1. Respiratory Failure

**Type I** (Hypoxemic):
- Common in advanced ILD
- Due to V/Q mismatch, diffusion limitation

**Type II** (Hypercapnic):
- Rare in ILD (unless severe end-stage)

**Management**: Oxygen, mechanical ventilation (prognosis poor if intubated)

### 2. Pulmonary Hypertension (PH)

**Frequency**: 30-40% of advanced ILD

**Pathophysiology**:
- Hypoxic vasoconstriction
- Vascular remodeling
- Destruction of pulmonary capillary bed

**Diagnosis**:
- **Echocardiography**: RVSP >40 mmHg (screening)
- **Right heart catheterization**: mPAP ≥20 mmHg (diagnostic)

**Classification**: WHO Group 3 PH (due to lung disease)

**Treatment**:
- **Oxygen therapy** (most important)
- Treat underlying ILD
- PH-specific therapy: **Generally NOT recommended** (INCREASE trial showed harm with Bosentan in IPF)
- Exception: Severe PH (mPAP >35 mmHg) → Consider PH therapies in specialized centers

**Prognosis**: ↓↓ Survival (median 1-2 years)

### 3. Lung Cancer

**Risk**: Increased in IPF, Sarcoidosis (Stage IV), Asbestosis

**IPF**: 5-year lung cancer risk ~15%

**Screening**: Not routinely recommended, but low-dose CT may be considered in high-risk patients

### 4. Infection

**Risk factors**: Immunosuppression, structural lung disease

**Common**:
- Bacterial pneumonia
- Opportunistic (PCP, Aspergillus) - especially on chronic steroids

**Prevention**:
- PCP prophylaxis (TMP-SMX) if on high-dose steroids (>20 mg/day × >1 month) + other immunosuppressant

### 5. Pneumothorax

**Risk**: Cystic lung diseases, advanced ILD with honeycombing

**Management**: Chest tube drainage, VATS pleurodesis

### 6. Acute Exacerbation

**Definition**: Acute worsening of ILD without identifiable cause

**Occurs in**: IPF (most common), other ILDs

**Management**: High-dose steroids, supportive care

**Mortality**: 50-80%

## NEET PG HIGH-YIELD POINTS

### Must-Know Facts

1. **ILD = Restrictive Pattern with ↓↓ DLCO**
   - FEV₁/FVC ≥0.70 (normal or ↑)
   - ↓ TLC, ↓ FVC
   - **DLCO is the most sensitive early marker**

2. **HRCT is the GOLD STANDARD** for ILD diagnosis

3. **IPF**:
   - Most common and most lethal idiopathic ILD
   - UIP pattern on HRCT: **Honeycombing + subpleural/basal distribution**
   - **Clubbing in 50-70%** (highly specific)
   - Treatment: **Pirfenidone or Nintedanib** (antifibrotics)
   - Median survival: **2.5-3.5 years**

4. **Sarcoidosis**:
   - Most common ILD in young adults
   - **Non-caseating granulomas**
   - **BHL** (bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy) - **Stage I**
   - **Clubbing is RARE** (<5%) - Differentiates from IPF
   - CD4/CD8 ratio **>3.5** (opposite of HP)
   - **Löfgren syndrome**: Erythema nodosum + BHL + Arthralgias → **Excellent prognosis**
   - Treatment: **Steroids** (not all patients need treatment)

5. **Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis**:
   - **Acute**: 4-8 hours after exposure, fever, dyspnea → resolves in 24-48 hours
   - **Chronic**: Mimics IPF
   - **Mosaic attenuation** (air trapping) on HRCT - highly suggestive
   - CD4/CD8 ratio **<1** (opposite of Sarcoidosis)
   - Treatment: **Antigen avoidance** (most important)

6. **CTD-ILD**:
   - **SSc-ILD**: Most common CTD-ILD (70-90%), NSIP > UIP, Anti-Scl-70
   - **RA-ILD**: Male smokers, UIP > NSIP
   - **Anti-Synthetase Syndrome**: Anti-Jo-1, Mechanic's hands, rapidly progressive
   - **NSIP pattern** more common in CTD-ILD than IPF

7. **Drug-Induced ILD**:
   - **"BANANA"**: Bleomycin, Amiodarone, Nitrofurantoin, Anti-TNF, Nivolumab, Azathioprine
   - **Amiodarone**: High attenuation on HRCT (iodine), cumulative dose >400 g
   - **Nitrofurantoin**: Most common antibiotic, acute (eosinophilia) vs chronic
   - Treatment: **STOP drug** + Steroids

### Comparison Tables

#### IPF vs Sarcoidosis vs HP

| Feature | IPF | Sarcoidosis | HP |
|---------|-----|-------------|-----|
| **Age** | >60 years | 20-40 years | Any age |
| **Clubbing** | 50-70% | Rare (<5%) | Rare (<10%) |
| **HRCT pattern** | UIP (honeycombing, basal) | Perilymphatic nodules, upper lobe | Mosaic attenuation, centrilobular nodules |
| **BAL CD4/CD8** | <1 (non-specific) | **>3.5** | **<1** |
| **Prognosis** | Poor (2.5-3.5 yr) | Good (60-70% remit) | Good if antigen avoided |
| **Treatment** | Antifibrotics | Steroids (if indicated) | Antigen avoidance |

#### UIP vs NSIP vs OP

| Feature | UIP | NSIP | OP |
|---------|-----|------|-----|
| **Distribution** | Heterogeneous, subpleural/basal | Homogeneous, subpleural sparing | Patchy, peribronchovascular |
| **Honeycombing** | Present (definite UIP) | Minimal/Absent | Absent |
| **Ground-glass** | Minimal | Prominent | Prominent |
| **Prognosis** | Poor | Intermediate | Good |
| **Steroid response** | Poor | Good | Excellent |

### High-Yield Drug Side Effects

| Drug | Side Effect | Monitoring |
|------|-------------|------------|
| **Pirfenidone** | **Photosensitivity**, GI upset, ↑ LFTs | LFTs monthly × 6, then q3mo; **Sun protection** |
| **Nintedanib** | **Diarrhea** (60%), nausea, ↑ LFTs | LFTs, pregnancy test (teratogenic) |
| **Prednisone** | Hyperglycemia, HTN, osteoporosis, infections | Blood glucose, BP, DEXA, PCP prophylaxis |

### Diagnostic Clues

1. **Bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy** → **Sarcoidosis** (if symmetric) or Lymphoma (if asymmetric)

2. **Mosaic attenuation on expiratory HRCT** → **Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis**

3. **High attenuation on HRCT** → **Amiodarone** pulmonary toxicity

4. **Honeycombing + clubbing + age >60** → **IPF**

5. **Erythema nodosum + BHL + arthralgias** → **Löfgren syndrome** (acute sarcoidosis)

6. **Mechanic's hands + myositis + ILD** → **Anti-Synthetase Syndrome** (Anti-Jo-1)

7. **Subpleural sparing on HRCT** → **NSIP** (common in SSc-ILD)

8. **Reverse halo sign** (Atoll sign) → **Organizing Pneumonia**

9. **Peripheral eosinophilia + acute pneumonitis** → **Drug-induced ILD** (Nitrofurantoin) or Eosinophilic pneumonia

10. **Upper lobe fibrosis** → **SAFTB**: Sarcoidosis, Ankylosing spondylitis, Fungal (ABPA), Tuberculosis, Berylliosis

### Formulae and Scores

**GAP Index** (Gender, Age, Physiology) - IPF Prognosis:
- Gender (Male): 1 point
- Age >60: 1 point
- FVC 50-75%: 1 point; <50%: 2 points
- DLCO 36-55%: 1 point; ≤35%: 2 points

**Stages**:
- 0-3: Stage I (6% 1-year mortality)
- 4-5: Stage II (16% 1-year mortality)
- 6-8: Stage III (39% 1-year mortality)

### Mnemonics

1. **"GRODE"** - ILD Classification:
   - **G**ranulomatous, **R**heumatologic, **O**ccupational, **D**rug-induced, id**E**opathic

2. **"BANANA"** - Drug-Induced ILD:
   - **B**leomycin, **A**miodarone, **N**itrofurantoin, **A**nti-TNF, **N**ivolumab, **A**zathioprine

3. **"SAFTB"** - Upper Lobe Fibrosis:
   - **S**arcoidosis, **A**nkylosing spondylitis, **F**ungal (ABPA), **T**uberculosis, **B**erylliosis

4. **Löfgren Syndrome** - "EBA":
   - **E**rythema nodosum, **B**HL, **A**rthralgias

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **All ILD patients need treatment** ❌
   - Sarcoidosis Stage I (asymptomatic BHL) → Monitor only

2. **All ILDs are steroid-responsive** ❌
   - IPF: Steroids NOT recommended (may be harmful)
   - Use antifibrotics instead

3. **Clubbing is common in Sarcoidosis** ❌
   - Clubbing is RARE in Sarcoidosis (<5%)
   - Common in IPF (50-70%)

4. **DLCO is normal in early ILD** ❌
   - ↓ DLCO is the **most sensitive early marker**

5. **UIP pattern always means IPF** ❌
   - UIP can occur in CTD-ILD (RA, SSc), chronic HP, drug-induced ILD
   - Need clinical correlation

6. **Positive serum precipitins = Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis** ❌
   - Indicates exposure, NOT necessarily disease
   - Need compatible clinical + HRCT + BAL

7. **Elevated ACE is diagnostic of Sarcoidosis** ❌
   - Elevated in only 60%, NOT specific
   - Biopsy (non-caseating granulomas) is gold standard

## PRACTICE MCQs

### Question 1
A 65-year-old male smoker presents with progressive dyspnea and dry cough over 18 months. Examination reveals bibasilar inspiratory crackles and clubbing. HRCT chest shows subpleural honeycombing with basal predominance. PFTs show FVC 55%, FEV₁/FVC 0.78, DLCO 35%. What is the most appropriate treatment?

A. Prednisone 40 mg/day
B. Azathioprine
C. Pirfenidone
D. Methotrexate

**Answer**: **C. Pirfenidone**

**Explanation**:
- **Diagnosis**: **Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis (IPF)**
  - Age >60, male, smoker, progressive dyspnea
  - **Clubbing** (50-70% in IPF)
  - **Bibasilar crackles**
  - HRCT: **Honeycombing + subpleural/basal distribution** = **Definite UIP pattern**
  - PFTs: Restrictive (FEV₁/FVC >0.70), ↓↓ DLCO

- **Treatment**:
  - **Pirfenidone or Nintedanib** (antifibrotics) - **First-line**
  - **Prednisone is NOT recommended** in IPF (may be harmful, no proven benefit)
  - Azathioprine, Methotrexate: No role in IPF

- **Key Point**: UIP pattern on HRCT (honeycombing + subpleural/basal) in appropriate clinical setting → Diagnose IPF without biopsy → Start antifibrotic therapy

### Question 2
A 32-year-old African American woman presents with incidental bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy on chest X-ray done for routine health checkup. She is asymptomatic. PFTs are normal. What is the most appropriate next step?

A. Start prednisone 40 mg/day
B. Bronchoscopy with transbronchial biopsy
C. Monitor with serial CXR and PFTs
D. Mediastinoscopy

**Answer**: **C. Monitor with serial CXR and PFTs**

**Explanation**:
- **Diagnosis**: **Sarcoidosis Stage I** (BHL alone, no parenchymal involvement)
  - Young African American woman (high-risk demographic)
  - Asymptomatic, incidental finding
  - BHL on CXR

- **Management**:
  - **Stage I Sarcoidosis with no symptoms** → **Monitor only**
  - **60-80% spontaneous remission** without treatment
  - No need for biopsy if classic presentation (can confirm if needed, but not mandatory)
  - **NO steroids** for asymptomatic Stage I

- **When to treat Sarcoidosis**:
  - Symptomatic disease
  - Organ-threatening (cardiac, neuro, ocular)
  - Progressive pulmonary disease (worsening PFTs, symptoms)

- **Key Point**: Not all Sarcoidosis patients need treatment. Asymptomatic Stage I → Observe.

### Question 3
A 45-year-old farmer presents with fever, dyspnea, and cough that started 6 hours after working with moldy hay. Symptoms resolve after 2 days. This has happened 3 times in the past month, always after working in the barn. HRCT shows ground-glass opacities and centrilobular nodules. BAL shows 65% lymphocytes with CD4/CD8 ratio of 0.5. What is the diagnosis?

A. Sarcoidosis
B. Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis
C. Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis
D. Eosinophilic Pneumonia

**Answer**: **C. Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis**

**Explanation**:
- **Diagnosis**: **Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis (Farmer's Lung)**
  - **Acute HP**: Symptoms **4-8 hours after exposure** (moldy hay)
  - **Recurrent episodes** with re-exposure
  - **Symptoms resolve** when away from exposure (24-48 hours)
  - **Farmer** → Exposed to thermophilic actinomycetes in moldy hay

- **HRCT**: Ground-glass opacities, centrilobular nodules (typical of acute/subacute HP)

- **BAL**:
  - **Lymphocytosis** (>50% in subacute, >30% in acute)
  - **CD4/CD8 ratio <1** (CD8+ predominance) - **KEY FINDING**

- **Differentiating from Sarcoidosis**:
  - Sarcoidosis: CD4/CD8 ratio **>3.5** (CD4+ predominance)
  - HP: CD4/CD8 ratio **<1** (CD8+ predominance)

- **Treatment**: **Antigen avoidance** (most important), steroids if severe

- **Key Point**: Acute HP = Recurrent flu-like illness 4-8 hours after specific exposure + BAL CD4/CD8 <1

### Question 4
A 70-year-old man on chronic amiodarone therapy (400 mg/day for 3 years) for atrial fibrillation presents with progressive dyspnea over 6 months. HRCT chest shows bilateral ground-glass opacities with areas of high attenuation. What is the most appropriate next step?

A. Continue amiodarone, add prednisone
B. Stop amiodarone, start prednisone
C. Increase amiodarone dose
D. Switch to sotalol

**Answer**: **B. Stop amiodarone, start prednisone**

**Explanation**:
- **Diagnosis**: **Amiodarone Pulmonary Toxicity**
  - Chronic amiodarone (400 mg/day × 3 years) → High cumulative dose
  - Progressive dyspnea
  - HRCT: **High attenuation** (pathognomonic due to iodine content in amiodarone)

- **Treatment**:
  1. **STOP amiodarone** (MOST IMPORTANT)
     - Note: Amiodarone has long half-life (40-60 days), so toxicity may persist
  2. **Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day** × 4-6 weeks, then taper
  3. Switch to alternative antiarrhythmic (if needed)

- **Option A is WRONG**: Continuing amiodarone will worsen toxicity despite steroids

- **Key Point**: High attenuation on HRCT = Amiodarone toxicity. Management = STOP drug + Steroids.

### Question 5
A 55-year-old woman with systemic sclerosis (diffuse cutaneous type) and positive Anti-Scl-70 antibody presents with progressive dyspnea. HRCT shows ground-glass opacities with subpleural sparing. PFTs show FVC 60%, DLCO 45%. What is the most appropriate treatment?

A. Prednisone alone
B. Pirfenidone
C. Mycophenolate mofetil
D. Observation

**Answer**: **C. Mycophenolate mofetil**

**Explanation**:
- **Diagnosis**: **Systemic Sclerosis-Interstitial Lung Disease (SSc-ILD)**
  - Diffuse cutaneous SSc (high risk for ILD)
  - **Anti-Scl-70 (Anti-topoisomerase I)** antibody (risk factor for ILD)
  - HRCT: Ground-glass with **subpleural sparing** (typical of NSIP pattern in SSc-ILD)
  - Significant functional impairment (FVC 60%, DLCO 45%)

- **Treatment**:
  - **Mycophenolate mofetil (1-3 g/day)** - **First-line for SSc-ILD**
  - Alternative: Cyclophosphamide
  - **Nintedanib**: FDA-approved for SSc-ILD (can add if progressive)

- **Why NOT Prednisone alone?**
  - Steroids alone are **NOT recommended** in SSc-ILD
  - ↑ Risk of **scleroderma renal crisis**
  - Need immunosuppressant (MMF/CYC)

- **Why NOT Pirfenidone?**
  - Pirfenidone is for **IPF**, not CTD-ILD
  - Nintedanib is the antifibrotic approved for SSc-ILD (not Pirfenidone)

- **Key Point**: SSc-ILD → Mycophenolate mofetil (First-line). Nintedanib can be added for progressive fibrosing ILD.

### Question 6
A 28-year-old woman presents with erythema nodosum on shins, bilateral ankle arthritis, and dyspnea. CXR shows bilateral hilar lymphadenopathy. What is the most appropriate management?

A. Prednisone 40 mg/day
B. NSAIDs and observation
C. Methotrexate
D. Infliximab

**Answer**: **B. NSAIDs and observation**

**Explanation**:
- **Diagnosis**: **Löfgren Syndrome** (Acute Sarcoidosis)
  - **Triad**: Erythema nodosum + BHL + Arthralgias (bilateral ankles)
  - Young woman

- **Prognosis**: **Excellent** (80-90% spontaneous remission)

- **Management**:
  - **NSAIDs** for symptomatic relief (arthritis, erythema nodosum)
  - **Observation** (most resolve without treatment)
  - **Steroids rarely needed** (only if severe symptoms)

- **Why NOT Prednisone?**
  - Löfgren syndrome has excellent prognosis without treatment
  - Reserve steroids for severe/refractory cases

- **Key Point**: Löfgren Syndrome = Excellent prognosis → NSAIDs + Observation (NOT steroids initially)

### Question 7
Which of the following PFT findings is most characteristic of Interstitial Lung Disease?

A. ↓ FEV₁/FVC ratio (<0.70)
B. ↑ Total Lung Capacity (TLC)
C. ↓ Diffusing Capacity (DLCO)
D. ↑ Residual Volume (RV)

**Answer**: **C. ↓ Diffusing Capacity (DLCO)**

**Explanation**:
- **ILD = Restrictive pattern with ↓↓ DLCO**

**PFT Findings in ILD**:
| Parameter | Finding |
|-----------|---------|
| FVC | ↓ Decreased |
| FEV₁ | ↓ Decreased |
| FEV₁/FVC | **Normal or ↑** (≥0.70) - RESTRICTIVE |
| TLC | ↓ Decreased |
| RV | ↓ Decreased |
| **DLCO** | **↓↓ Markedly Decreased** |

- **↓ DLCO**:
  - **Most sensitive early marker** of ILD
  - Reflects loss of alveolar-capillary surface area
  - May be decreased even when lung volumes are normal (early disease)

- **Option A (↓ FEV₁/FVC <0.70)**: Obstructive disease (COPD, Asthma)
- **Option B (↑ TLC)**: Obstructive disease (COPD with hyperinflation)
- **Option D (↑ RV)**: Obstructive disease (air trapping)

- **Key Point**: ↓ DLCO is the hallmark of ILD and the most sensitive early marker.

### Question 8
A 60-year-old woman on chronic methotrexate for rheumatoid arthritis develops acute dyspnea, fever, and dry cough over 1 week. CXR shows bilateral infiltrates. BAL shows 40% eosinophils. What is the most appropriate management?

A. Increase methotrexate dose
B. Add folic acid
C. Stop methotrexate, start prednisone
D. Start antibiotics

**Answer**: **C. Stop methotrexate, start prednisone**

**Explanation**:
- **Diagnosis**: **Methotrexate-Induced Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis**
  - Acute onset (days-weeks) - Unlike MTX-induced fibrosis (months-years)
  - **Peripheral eosinophilia** (40% on BAL)
  - Bilateral infiltrates

- **MTX Pulmonary Toxicity**:
  1. **Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis** (Acute):
     - Fever, dyspnea, cough
     - Eosinophilia (blood/BAL)
     - **NOT dose-related**
  2. **Pulmonary Fibrosis** (Chronic):
     - Insidious, months-years
     - Less eosinophilia

- **Management**:
  1. **STOP methotrexate** (MOST IMPORTANT)
  2. **Prednisone 0.5-1 mg/kg/day**
  3. Recovery in most cases

- **Why NOT continue MTX?**
  - MTX is the offending agent → Must stop
  - Re-challenge NOT recommended

- **Why NOT just antibiotics?**
  - Clinical picture suggests drug-induced HP (eosinophilia is key)
  - Rule out infection, but drug toxicity more likely

- **Key Point**: Drug-induced ILD → STOP drug + Steroids. Eosinophilia in BAL suggests drug-induced or eosinophilic pneumonia.

### Question 9
Which HRCT finding is most specific for Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis?

A. Perilymphatic nodules
B. Honeycombing with subpleural and basal distribution
C. Mosaic attenuation
D. Ground-glass opacities

**Answer**: **B. Honeycombing with subpleural and basal distribution**

**Explanation**:
- **Definite UIP pattern** (specific for IPF in appropriate clinical context):
  1. **Honeycombing** (Most specific feature)
  2. **Subpleural and basal distribution**
  3. **Heterogeneous** (patchy) involvement
  4. **Reticular opacities**
  5. **Minimal ground-glass** opacities

- **Honeycombing**:
  - **Clustered cystic airspaces** (3-10 mm) with thick walls
  - Subpleural location
  - Represents **end-stage fibrosis** (irreversible)
  - **Definite UIP pattern** → Can diagnose IPF without biopsy

- **Option A (Perilymphatic nodules)**: Sarcoidosis
- **Option C (Mosaic attenuation)**: Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis (air trapping)
- **Option D (Ground-glass opacities)**: Non-specific, seen in many ILDs (NSIP, OP, HP, acute processes)

- **Key Point**: Honeycombing + subpleural/basal distribution = UIP pattern = IPF (if no other cause identified)

### Question 10
A 50-year-old bird breeder presents with chronic dyspnea over 2 years. He has a budgie at home for 10 years. HRCT shows upper lobe fibrosis and mosaic attenuation. BAL shows 40% lymphocytes with CD4/CD8 ratio of 0.6. What is the most important treatment?

A. Prednisone
B. Remove the bird from home
C. Pirfenidone
D. Azathioprine

**Answer**: **B. Remove the bird from home**

**Explanation**:
- **Diagnosis**: **Chronic Hypersensitivity Pneumonitis** (Bird Fancier's Lung)
  - Bird breeder (budgie at home)
  - Chronic dyspnea (insidious onset over years)
  - HRCT: **Fibrosis** (chronic HP) + **Mosaic attenuation** (air trapping - highly suggestive of HP)
  - BAL: Lymphocytosis with **CD4/CD8 <1**

- **Treatment**:
  1. **Antigen avoidance** = **MOST IMPORTANT**
     - **Remove the bird** (source of avian proteins)
     - Without antigen avoidance, disease will progress despite medications
  2. Prednisone (if significant inflammation/symptoms)
  3. Antifibrotics (if progressive fibrosis despite avoidance)

- **Why antigen avoidance is priority?**
  - **Acute/Subacute HP**: Complete recovery if antigen avoided early
  - **Chronic HP**: Progression slows/stops if antigen avoided
  - Steroids/immunosuppressants are adjuncts, NOT substitutes for antigen avoidance

- **Key Point**: HP Management = Antigen avoidance FIRST, then pharmacotherapy. Without removing the antigen, all other treatments will fail.

### Question 11
A 65-year-old man with IPF on pirfenidone (FVC 65%, DLCO 50%) develops acute worsening of dyspnea over 5 days. He is afebrile. HRCT shows new bilateral ground-glass opacities superimposed on his baseline UIP pattern. Blood cultures are negative. What is the most likely diagnosis?

A. Bacterial pneumonia
B. Pulmonary embolism
C. Acute exacerbation of IPF
D. Congestive heart failure

**Answer**: **C. Acute exacerbation of IPF**

**Explanation**:
- **Acute Exacerbation of IPF**: Acute worsening of dyspnea (<1 month) without identifiable cause

**Diagnostic Criteria**:
1. Previous/concurrent IPF diagnosis ✓
2. Acute worsening (<1 month) ✓ (5 days)
3. HRCT: **New bilateral ground-glass/consolidation** ✓
4. **Not explained** by infection, PE, heart failure, pneumothorax
   - Afebrile, negative blood cultures → Less likely infection
   - No mention of CHF features

- **Management**:
  - **High-dose steroids** (Methylprednisolone 500-1000 mg IV × 3 days)
  - Supportive care (oxygen, mechanical ventilation if needed)
  - Empiric antibiotics (if infection cannot be excluded)
  - **Lung transplant evaluation**

- **Prognosis**: **In-hospital mortality 50-80%** (very poor)

- **Key Point**: IPF patient with acute worsening + new ground-glass on HRCT + no infection/PE/CHF → Acute exacerbation of IPF

### Question 12
Which of the following is an indication for lung transplantation in ILD?

A. Sarcoidosis Stage I
B. IPF with FVC 90% predicted
C. Hypersensitivity pneumonitis with ongoing antigen exposure
D. Progressive ILD with 6-minute walk distance <250 meters

**Answer**: **D. Progressive ILD with 6-minute walk distance <250 meters**

**Explanation**:
- **Lung Transplant Indications in ILD** (General):
  - Progressive disease despite optimal therapy
  - Severe functional limitation (NYHA III-IV)
  - Hypoxemia (SpO₂ <88% or PaO₂ <55 mmHg)
  - **6MWT <250 meters** or desaturation >4%
  - Poor prognostic factors

- **Disease-Specific**:
  - **IPF**: Early referral (GAP Stage II/III, FVC <80%, DLCO <40%)
  - **Sarcoidosis**: Stage IV fibrosis, pulmonary hypertension
  - **CTD-ILD**: Progressive despite immunosuppression

- **Option A (Sarcoidosis Stage I)**: Excellent prognosis (60-80% spontaneous remission) → NO transplant
- **Option B (IPF with FVC 90%)**: Mild disease → Continue medical therapy, NOT ready for transplant
- **Option C (HP with ongoing exposure)**: Must stop antigen exposure first before considering transplant
- **Option D**: ✓ **6MWT <250 m = Poor functional capacity** → Indication for transplant

- **Key Point**: 6MWT <250 meters or desaturation is a poor prognostic marker and indication for lung transplant evaluation.

## SUMMARY

**Interstitial Lung Diseases** are a diverse group of disorders characterized by:
- Inflammation and/or fibrosis of the **pulmonary interstitium**
- **Restrictive PFTs** with **↓↓ DLCO** (most sensitive marker)
- Progressive dyspnea and dry cough
- **HRCT is the gold standard** for diagnosis

**Key ILDs**:
1. **IPF**: Most common and lethal idiopathic ILD, UIP pattern, honeycombing, clubbing, antifibrotics (Pirfenidone/Nintedanib)
2. **Sarcoidosis**: Most common ILD in young adults, non-caseating granulomas, BHL, Löfgren syndrome (excellent prognosis)
3. **HP**: Antigen exposure, acute (4-8 hrs) vs chronic, mosaic attenuation, CD4/CD8 <1, antigen avoidance
4. **CTD-ILD**: SSc-ILD most common, NSIP pattern, immunosuppression
5. **Drug-ILD**: BANANA (Bleomycin, Amiodarone, Nitrofurantoin, Anti-TNF, Nivolumab)

**Management**:
- **IPF**: Antifibrotics (NOT steroids)
- **Sarcoidosis**: Steroids if indicated (NOT all patients)
- **HP**: Antigen avoidance (MOST important)
- **CTD-ILD**: Immunosuppression (MMF, CYC)
- **All**: Supportive care, oxygen, pulmonary rehabilitation, lung transplant (end-stage)

**Prognosis**: Varies widely from excellent (Löfgren syndrome) to poor (IPF: 2.5-3.5 years)
