## Definition & Overview

### Definition
**Chronic inflammatory disorder of the airways** characterized by **reversible airflow obstruction**, **bronchial hyperresponsiveness**, and **recurrent episodes** of wheezing, breathlessness, chest tightness, and cough.

### Epidemiology
- **Most common chronic disease in children**
- Prevalence: 5-10% worldwide
- Often begins in childhood
- **Atopy** (personal/family history of allergies) - Strong risk factor

### Key Features
- **Reversible** airflow obstruction (vs COPD - partially reversible)
- **Variable** symptoms (day-to-day, seasonal)
- **Triggered** by specific stimuli

---

## Pathophysiology

### Mechanism

```
Genetic Predisposition + Environmental Triggers
              ↓
    Airway Inflammation
      (Eosinophilic)
              ↓
    ┌─────────────┬────────────────┐
    ↓             ↓                ↓
Bronchoconstriction  Airway Edema   Mucus Plugging
    ↓             ↓                ↓
      ↓           ↓                ↓
        AIRFLOW OBSTRUCTION
              ↓
   Bronchial Hyperresponsiveness
              ↓
        ASTHMA SYMPTOMS
```

### Inflammatory Cells

**Type 2 (T2) Inflammation:**
- **Eosinophils** (Most important)
- **Mast cells** (Release histamine, leukotrienes)
- **Th2 lymphocytes** (IL-4, IL-5, IL-13)
- **IgE-mediated** (Allergic asthma)

**Inflammatory Mediators:**
- **Histamine, Leukotrienes** (LTC4, LTD4, LTE4) → Bronchoconstriction
- **IL-5** → Eosinophil activation
- **IL-4, IL-13** → IgE production

### Airway Remodeling (Chronic Asthma)

**Long-term inflammation** → Structural changes:
- **Basement membrane thickening**
- **Smooth muscle hypertrophy**
- **Goblet cell hyperplasia** → ↑ Mucus
- **Subepithelial fibrosis**

→ **Irreversible airflow obstruction** (if untreated)

---

### Triggers

**"ASTHMA" Mnemonic:**
```
A - Allergens (Pollen, Dust mites, Animal dander)
S - Smoke (Tobacco, Air pollution)
T - Temperature (Cold air)
H - Hypersensitivity (Aspirin, NSAIDs, Beta-blockers)
M - Mold, Medications
A - Activity (Exercise-induced)

Additional:
- Respiratory infections (Viral - RSV, Rhinovirus)
- Emotional stress
- GERD (Gastroesophageal reflux)
- Occupational exposures
```

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms

**Classic Triad:**
1. **Wheezing** (Expiratory >> Inspiratory)
2. **Dyspnea** (Breathlessness)
3. **Cough** (Often nocturnal, dry or productive)

**Additional:**
- **Chest tightness**
- **Symptoms worse at night/early morning**
- **Variable, episodic**
- **Triggered** by specific stimuli

**Cough-Variant Asthma:**
- **Chronic cough** as ONLY symptom
- No wheezing or dyspnea
- Responds to bronchodilators

### Physical Examination

**Stable Asthma:**
- Often **normal** between exacerbations

**During Exacerbation:**
- **Tachypnea, Tachycardia**
- **Prolonged expiratory phase**
- **Wheeze** (High-pitched, polyphonic, expiratory)
  - **Bilateral**
  - **Intensity NOT related to severity** (Severe: Silent chest - no air movement)
- **Use of accessory muscles**
- **Hyperinflation** (Barrel chest if chronic)
- **Pulsus paradoxus** (>10 mmHg - severe exacerbation)

## Diagnosis

### Clinical Diagnosis

**Asthma Suspected if:**
- **Recurrent respiratory symptoms** (Wheezing, dyspnea, cough, chest tightness)
- **Variable** (Day-to-day, seasonal)
- **Triggered** by specific stimuli
- **Worse at night/early morning**
- **Personal/Family history of atopy**

**Diagnosis Confirmed by:**
- **Reversible airflow obstruction** (Spirometry)

---

### Spirometry - GOLD STANDARD

**Obstructive Pattern:**
- **FEV₁/FVC ratio <0.70** (or <70%)
  - FEV₁: Forced Expiratory Volume in 1 second
  - FVC: Forced Vital Capacity
- **↓ FEV₁** (Severity)

**Reversibility Testing (Bronchodilator Response):**

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      BRONCHODILATOR REVERSIBILITY                  │
├────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ Post-bronchodilator improvement:                  │
│ • FEV₁ increase ≥12% AND ≥200 ml from baseline     │
│                                                    │
│ Confirms REVERSIBLE airflow obstruction            │
│ → Diagnostic for Asthma                            │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**How to Perform:**
1. Baseline spirometry
2. Administer bronchodilator (Salbutamol 400 mcg inhaled)
3. Repeat spirometry after 15-20 minutes
4. Calculate improvement in FEV₁

**Interpretation:**
- **↑ FEV₁ ≥12% and ≥200 ml** → Positive (Asthma)
- **No significant change** → Negative (Does NOT rule out asthma - may need further testing)

---

### Peak Expiratory Flow Rate (PEFR)

**Portable device** for monitoring

**Uses:**
- **Home monitoring** (Diurnal variation)
- **Assess severity** of exacerbation
- **Guide treatment** adjustments

**Diurnal Variation:**
- **>20% variation** between morning and evening → Suggests asthma
- **PEFR lowest in early morning**, highest in afternoon

**Normal PEFR:**
- Depends on age, height, sex
- **Personal best** is most useful reference

---

### Other Investigations

**Chest X-ray:**
- Usually **normal** (during stable period)
- **Hyperinflation** (Chronic asthma)
- **Rule out:** Pneumonia, Pneumothorax, Foreign body
- **During acute exacerbation:** To rule out complications

**CBC:**
- **Eosinophilia** (if allergic asthma)

**Serum IgE:**
- **Elevated** (Allergic asthma)

**Allergy Testing:**
- **Skin prick test** or **Specific IgE** (RAST)
- Identify triggers

**Methacholine Challenge Test:**
- If spirometry normal but asthma suspected
- **Positive:** PC20 <8 mg/ml (Provocative concentration causing 20% ↓ FEV₁)
- **Confirms bronchial hyperresponsiveness**

**Fractional Exhaled Nitric Oxide (FeNO):**
- **↑ in eosinophilic inflammation**
- **>50 ppb:** Suggests asthma
- **Useful:** Difficult-to-diagnose cases, Guide steroid therapy

## Management

### Goals
1. **Control symptoms**
2. **Prevent exacerbations**
3. **Maintain normal lung function**
4. **Minimize side effects**

### General Measures

**Avoid Triggers:**
- Allergens (Dust mites, Animal dander)
- Smoke (Tobacco, Air pollution)
- Cold air, Exercise (pre-medicate)
- NSAIDs, Beta-blockers (if sensitive)

**Environmental Control:**
- **Dust mite-proof** bedding
- **Remove carpets**, soft toys
- **HEPA filters**

**Patient Education:**
- Proper inhaler technique
- PEFR monitoring
- Action plan for exacerbations

---

### Stepwise Pharmacological Management (GINA Guidelines)

**"Step Up" if not controlled, "Step Down" if controlled ≥3 months**

```
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│         STEPWISE ASTHMA MANAGEMENT                         │
├──────────┬─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ STEP 1   │ As-needed low-dose ICS-Formoterol               │
│          │ OR As-needed SABA (Not preferred)               │
├──────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ STEP 2   │ Daily low-dose ICS                              │
│          │ + As-needed SABA                                │
│          │ OR As-needed low-dose ICS-Formoterol            │
├──────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ STEP 3   │ Low-dose ICS-LABA (Maintenance)                 │
│          │ + As-needed SABA                                │
│          │ OR Low-dose ICS-Formoterol (Maintenance + Relief│
├──────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ STEP 4   │ Medium/High-dose ICS-LABA                       │
│          │ + As-needed SABA                                │
│          │ Consider add: Tiotropium, Leukotriene modifier  │
├──────────┼─────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ STEP 5   │ High-dose ICS-LABA                              │
│          │ + Add-on: Anti-IgE (Omalizumab), Anti-IL5       │
│          │   (Mepolizumab), Anti-IL4R (Dupilumab)          │
│          │ OR Low-dose OCS (if needed)                     │
└──────────┴─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**ICS:** Inhaled Corticosteroid
**LABA:** Long-Acting Beta-2 Agonist
**SABA:** Short-Acting Beta-2 Agonist
**OCS:** Oral Corticosteroid

### Medications

#### **1. Inhaled Corticosteroids (ICS) - CONTROLLER (Mainstay)**

**Examples:**
- **Bud esonide, Fluticasone, Ciclesonide**

**Mechanism:** ↓ Airway inflammation (↓ Eosinophils, ↓ Cytokines)

**Indications:**
- **All persistent asthma** (Mild, Moderate, Severe)
- **First-line controller**

**Doses:**
- **Low:** Budesonide 200-400 mcg/day, Fluticasone 100-250 mcg/day
- **Medium:** Budesonide 400-800 mcg/day, Fluticasone 250-500 mcg/day
- **High:** Budesonide >800 mcg/day, Fluticasone >500 mcg/day

**Side Effects:**
- **Local:** Oral candidiasis (Thrush), Dysphonia (Hoarseness)
  - **Prevention:** Rinse mouth after use, Use spacer
- **Systemic:** Rare at low-moderate doses (Growth suppression, Adrenal suppression, Osteoporosis at high doses)

**Key Point:** **Start ICS early** (Even mild persistent asthma) - Prevents airway remodeling

---

#### **2. Long-Acting Beta-2 Agonists (LABA) - CONTROLLER**

**Examples:**
- **Formoterol, Salmeterol, Vilanterol**

**Mechanism:** Bronchodilation (12-24 hours)

**NEVER use as monotherapy** (↑ Asthma-related deaths)
**ALWAYS use with ICS** (Combination inhaler)

**Combination Inhalers:**
- **Budesonide/Formoterol** (Symbicort)
- **Fluticasone/Salmeterol** (Seretide)
- **Fluticasone/Vilanterol** (Relvar)

**Formoterol:**
- **Fast-acting** (Onset 5 minutes)
- Can be used for **Maintenance And Reliever Therapy (MART)**
  - Single inhaler for both daily maintenance and as-needed relief

---

#### **3. Short-Acting Beta-2 Agonists (SABA) - RELIEVER**

**Examples:**
- **Salbutamol (Albuterol), Terbutaline, Levosalbutamol**

**Mechanism:** Rapid bronchodilation (Onset 5 minutes, Duration 4-6 hours)

**Indications:**
- **As-needed relief** for acute symptoms
- **Pre-exercise** (Exercise-induced asthma)

**Overuse (>2 canisters/month or >2 doses/week):**
- Suggests **poor control** → Need to step up controller

**Side Effects:**
- **Tremor, Tachycardia, Hypokalemia** (High doses)

---

#### **4. Leukotriene Receptor Antagonists (LTRA) - CONTROLLER**

**Examples:**
- **Montelukast** (Most common - 10 mg OD)
- Zafirlukast

**Mechanism:** Block LTD4 receptors → ↓ Bronchoconstriction, ↓ Inflammation

**Indications:**
- **Add-on** to ICS (Step 3-4)
- **Aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease** (AERD)
- **Exercise-induced asthma**
- **Allergic rhinitis** coexisting

**Advantages:**
- **Oral** (Good for patients with poor inhaler technique)

**Side Effects:**
- Generally well tolerated
- **Neuropsychiatric effects** (Rare - Mood changes, Suicidality) - FDA warning

---

#### **5. Anticholinergics (Muscarinic Antagonists)**

**Long-Acting:**
- **Tiotropium** (Spiriva)

**Mechanism:** Block M3 muscarinic receptors → ↓ Bronchoconstriction

**Indications:**
- **Add-on** to ICS-LABA (Step 4-5) if uncontrolled

**Benefits:**
- ↓ Exacerbations
- ↑ Lung function

---

#### **6. Biologics (Monoclonal Antibodies) - SEVERE ASTHMA**

**Indications:**
- **Severe eosinophilic** or **allergic asthma**
- **Uncontrolled** on Step 4-5
- **Frequent exacerbations** despite high-dose ICS-LABA

**Options:**

**Anti-IgE:**
- **Omalizumab** (Xolair)
- **Indication:** Allergic asthma (High IgE, Positive skin tests)
- **Dose:** Based on IgE level and weight
- **SC injection every 2-4 weeks**

**Anti-IL-5:**
- **Mepolizumab** (Nucala), **Benralizumab** (Fasenra)
- **Indication:** Severe eosinophilic asthma (Eosinophils >300/μl)

**Anti-IL-4 Receptor:**
- **Dupilumab** (Dupixent)
- **Indication:** Type 2 asthma (Eosinophilic or FeNO >25 ppb)

---

#### **7. Oral Corticosteroids (OCS)**

**Indications:**
- **Acute exacerbations** (Short course: 5-7 days)
- **Severe asthma** uncontrolled on all other therapies (Maintenance - LAST RESORT)

**Dose:**
- **Acute:** Prednisolone 40-50 mg daily × 5-7 days
- **Maintenance:** Lowest possible dose (Side effects)

**Side Effects (Long-term):**
- Osteoporosis, Diabetes, HTN, Weight gain, Cataracts, Adrenal suppression, Immunosuppression

**Steroid-Sparing Agents:**
- Biologics (Omalizumab, Mepolizumab, etc.)
- Methotrexate, Azathioprine (Rarely used)

---

### Inhaler Devices

**Metered-Dose Inhaler (MDI):**
- Most common
- **Requires coordination** (Actuation + Inhalation)
- **Use with spacer** (↑ Lung deposition, ↓ Oropharyngeal deposition)

**Dry Powder Inhaler (DPI):**
- **Breath-actuated** (Easier for some patients)
- Requires adequate inspiratory flow

**Nebulizer:**
- Converts liquid to mist
- **For acute severe exacerbations**, Young children, Elderly

**Proper Technique:**
1. Shake (MDI)
2. Exhale fully
3. Seal lips around mouthpiece
4. **Actuate and inhale slowly and deeply** (MDI)
5. Hold breath for **10 seconds**
6. Exhale slowly
7. Rinse mouth (if ICS)

## Acute Exacerbation

### Definition
**Acute or subacute worsening** of symptoms beyond normal day-to-day variation

### Triggers
- **Viral infections** (Most common - RSV, Rhinovirus)
- Allergen exposure
- Air pollution
- Poor medication adherence
- Weather changes

### Clinical Features

**Symptoms:**
- **↑ Dyspnea, Wheezing, Cough, Chest tightness**
- **↓ Response to SABA**

**Signs:**
- **Tachypnea** (RR >30/min)
- **Tachycardia** (HR >120/min)
- **Prolonged expiration**
- **Wheeze** (May be **absent** in severe - "Silent chest")
- **Use of accessory muscles**
- **Pulsus paradoxus** (>10 mmHg)
- **Cyanosis** (Severe)
- **Altered consciousness** (Life-threatening)

---

### Severity Assessment

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│      SEVERITY OF ACUTE ASTHMA EXACERBATION               │
├─────────────┬────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ MILD-       │ • Talks in sentences                       │
│ MODERATE    │ • Prefers sitting to lying                 │
│             │ • Not agitated                             │
│             │ • RR <30/min                               │
│             │ • HR <120/min                              │
│             │ • PEFR >50% predicted/best                 │
│             │ • SpO₂ >90%                                │
├─────────────┼────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ SEVERE      │ • Talks in words                           │
│             │ • Sits hunched forward                     │
│             │ • Agitated                                 │
│             │ • RR >30/min                               │
│             │ • HR >120/min                              │
│             │ • PEFR 33-50% predicted/best               │
│             │ • SpO₂ 90-95%                              │
├─────────────┼────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│ LIFE-       │ • Cannot speak                             │
│ THREATENING │ • Exhausted, Confused, Drowsy              │
│             │ • Silent chest (No wheeze - no air movement│
│             │ • Cyanosis                                 │
│             │ • Bradycardia, Hypotension                 │
│             │ • PEFR <33% predicted/best                 │
│             │ • SpO₂ <90%                                │
│             │ • PaO₂ <60 mmHg, PaCO₂ >45 mmHg (or rising)│
└─────────────┴────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Life-Threatening Features:**
- **Silent chest** (No wheeze - very little air movement)
- **Cyanosis**
- **Bradycardia** (<60/min) - Sign of impending arrest
- **Hypotension**
- **Exhaustion, Confusion**
- **PaCO₂ >45 mmHg** or **Rising** (Respiratory failure)

**Normal/Low PaCO₂ expected** (Hyperventilation)
**Rising PaCO₂** = Respiratory muscle fatigue → Impending respiratory arrest

---

### Management of Acute Exacerbation

**"ASTHMA" Management:**
```
A - Assess severity (PEFR, SpO₂, ABG)
S - Supplemental oxygen (Target SpO₂ 93-95%)
T - Treatment (Bronchodilators, Steroids)
H - Hospital admission if severe
M - Monitor response (PEFR, Clinical)
A - Adjust therapy based on response
```

---

#### **Immediate Treatment (All Severities):**

**1. Oxygen:**
- **Target:** SpO₂ 93-95% (Adults), 94-98% (Children)
- **High-flow** if needed

**2. Short-Acting Beta-2 Agonist (SABA):**
- **Salbutamol:**
  - **MDI + Spacer:** 4-8 puffs (100 mcg each) every 20 min × 1 hour
  - **Nebulizer:** 2.5-5 mg every 20 min × 1 hour (Or continuous)
- **Response:** ↑ PEFR, ↓ Symptoms

**3. Ipratropium Bromide** (Anticholinergic):
- **Add to SABA** if **severe or poor response**
- **Nebulizer:** 0.5 mg every 20 min × 1 hour

**4. Systemic Corticosteroids:**
- **Prednisolone 40-50 mg PO** (Or IV Hydrocortisone 100 mg if unable to take oral)
- **Within 1 hour** of presentation
- **Continue 5-7 days** (No need to taper if <2 weeks)

---

#### **Additional Treatment (Severe/Life-Threatening):**

**5. Magnesium Sulfate:**
- **Indication:** Severe exacerbation not responding to initial treatment
- **Dose:** **2 g IV over 20 minutes** (Single dose)
- **Mechanism:** Bronchodilation (Ca²⁺ channel blocker)
- **Effective:** ↓ Hospital admissions

**6. IV Beta-2 Agonist:**
- If **nebulized not effective** or **life-threatening**
- **Salbutamol 5 mcg/kg IV** bolus, then infusion

**7. Heliox:**
- **Helium-Oxygen mixture** (80:20 or 70:30)
- ↓ Airway resistance (Helium less dense than air)
- **Alternative** if not responding

---

#### **Life-Threatening Asthma:**

**8. ICU Admission:**
- **Intubation and Mechanical Ventilation** (if respiratory arrest/failure)
- **Anesthetic agents:** Ketamine, Halothane (Bronchodilators)

**Indications for Intubation:**
- **Respiratory arrest**
- **Severe hypoxemia** despite O₂
- **Exhaustion, Altered consciousness**
- **PaCO₂ >50 mmHg and rising**

---

### Monitoring

- **PEFR:** Every 15-30 min initially
- **SpO₂:** Continuous
- **ABG:** If SpO₂ <92% or severe/life-threatening
- **Clinical assessment:** RR, HR, Work of breathing

**Response to Treatment:**
- **Good:** PEFR >60-80% predicted within 1-2 hours → Consider discharge
- **Incomplete:** PEFR 40-60% → Continue treatment, Admit
- **Poor:** PEFR <40%, Worsening → ICU

---

### Discharge Criteria

**Patient can be discharged if:**
- **PEFR >60-80%** predicted or personal best
- **Sustained improvement** ≥1 hour after last treatment
- **Adequate inhaler technique**
- **Stable on discharge medications**

**Discharge Medications:**
- **Continue SABA** as needed
- **ICS +/- LABA** (Controller - Start or increase dose)
- **Prednisolone 40-50 mg daily × 5-7 days** (Oral steroids)

**Follow-up:**
- Within **2-7 days**
- Review medications, Inhaler technique, Action plan

## Special Types

### 1. Exercise-Induced Bronchoconstriction (EIB)

**Definition:** Transient airflow obstruction triggered by exercise

**Mechanism:**
- **Airway cooling and drying** during exercise (Hyperventilation)
- Triggers mast cell degranulation → Bronchoconstriction

**Clinical:**
- **Wheezing, Dyspnea, Cough** during or **5-15 min after** exercise
- **Resolves spontaneously** within 30-60 min
- **Refractory period:** 2-3 hours (Less responsive if exercise repeated)

**Diagnosis:**
- **Exercise challenge test:** ↓ FEV₁ ≥10% post-exercise

**Management:**
- **Pre-treatment:** SABA 15 min before exercise (Salbutamol 2-4 puffs)
- **Long-term:** ICS (If frequent EIB)
- **LTRA:** Montelukast (Effective alternative)
- **Warm-up** before exercise

---

### 2. Aspirin-Exacerbated Respiratory Disease (AERD)

**Samter's Triad:**
1. **Asthma**
2. **Chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyps**
3. **Aspirin/NSAID sensitivity**

**Mechanism:**
- COX-1 inhibition → ↑ Leukotriene production → Bronchoconstriction

**Clinical:**
- **Bronchospasm** within **30 min - 3 hours** after aspirin/NSAID
- **Severe exacerbation** possible

**Management:**
- **Avoid aspirin, NSAIDs**
- **Safe alternatives:** Paracetamol, Selective COX-2 inhibitors (Celecoxib - Usually safe)
- **LTRA:** Montelukast (Beneficial)
- **Aspirin desensitization:** If aspirin needed (e.g., Cardiac disease)

---

### 3. Occupational Asthma

**Definition:** Asthma caused by exposure to workplace sensitizers

**Common Agents:**
- **High molecular weight:** Flour (Baker's asthma), Animal proteins, Latex
- **Low molecular weight:** Isocyanates (Paints), Wood dust, Epoxy resins

**Diagnosis:**
- **Work-related symptoms:** Improve on weekends/vacations
- **Serial PEFR monitoring:** At work vs away from work

**Management:**
- **Avoid exposure** (Primary)
- **Change job** if avoidance not possible
- Pharmacotherapy (ICS, Bronchodilators)

## NEET PG High-Yield Points

### 🔥 MUST KNOW

1. **Asthma Definition:** Reversible airflow obstruction + Bronchial hyperresponsiveness + Inflammation

2. **Key Feature:** **Reversibility** (vs COPD - partially reversible)

3. **Diagnosis:**
   - **FEV₁/FVC <0.70** (Obstructive)
   - **Bronchodilator reversibility:** FEV₁ ↑ ≥12% AND ≥200 ml post-SABA

4. **Most Common Chronic Disease in Children**

5. **ICS (Inhaled Corticosteroids):**
   - **Mainstay of treatment** (Controller)
   - **All persistent asthma** (Mild, Moderate, Severe)
   - **Side effects:** Oral candidiasis, Dysphonia (Rinse mouth, Use spacer)

6. **LABA:** **NEVER as monotherapy** (Always with ICS)

7. **SABA:** As-needed reliever (Overuse >2 canisters/month → Poor control)

8. **Stepwise Management:**
   - **Step 1:** Intermittent - As-needed SABA
   - **Step 2:** Mild persistent - Daily low-dose ICS
   - **Step 3:** Moderate - ICS-LABA
   - **Step 4-5:** Severe - High-dose ICS-LABA + Add-ons (Tiotropium, LTRA, Biologics)

9. **Acute Exacerbation Management:**
   - **Oxygen:** Target SpO₂ 93-95%
   - **SABA:** Salbutamol nebulizer 2.5-5 mg every 20 min
   - **Ipratropium:** Add if severe
   - **Systemic steroids:** Prednisolone 40-50 mg (Within 1 hour)
   - **Magnesium sulfate 2 g IV:** If severe, not responding

10. **Life-Threatening Features:**
    - **Silent chest** (No wheeze - no air movement)
    - **Cyanosis, Bradycardia**
    - **Exhaustion, Confusion**
    - **PaCO₂ >45 mmHg or rising**

11. **Exercise-Induced Asthma:**
    - **Pre-treatment:** SABA 15 min before exercise
    - **Long-term:** LTRA (Montelukast)

12. **Aspirin-Exacerbated Respiratory Disease (AERD):**
    - **Samter's Triad:** Asthma + Nasal polyps + Aspirin sensitivity
    - **Avoid:** Aspirin, NSAIDs
    - **Safe:** Paracetamol, Selective COX-2 inhibitors

13. **Biologics:**
    - **Omalizumab:** Anti-IgE (Allergic asthma)
    - **Mepolizumab, Benralizumab:** Anti-IL-5 (Eosinophilic asthma)
    - **Dupilumab:** Anti-IL-4R (Type 2 asthma)

### Clinical Scenarios

**Scenario 1:** Recurrent wheezing, worse at night, FEV₁/FVC <0.70, FEV₁ ↑ 15% post-SABA → **Asthma**

**Scenario 2:** Mild persistent asthma (Symptoms 3x/week) → Start **Daily low-dose ICS**

**Scenario 3:** Severe asthma exacerbation, SpO₂ 88%, PEFR 30% → **Oxygen + Salbutamol nebulizer + Ipratropium + Prednisolone 40 mg + Magnesium sulfate 2 g IV**

**Scenario 4:** Asthma patient with silent chest, bradycardia, PaCO₂ 50 mmHg → **Life-threatening** → **ICU, Consider intubation**

**Scenario 5:** Wheezing 10 min after exercise, resolves in 30 min → **Exercise-induced asthma** → Pre-treat with **SABA**

**Scenario 6:** Asthma + Nasal polyps + Bronchospasm after aspirin → **AERD (Samter's Triad)**

- [ ] Asthma: Reversible airflow obstruction
- [ ] Diagnosis: FEV₁/FVC <0.70, Reversibility ≥12% and ≥200 ml

## Practice Questions

### Question 1
A 25-year-old presents with recurrent episodes of wheezing and dyspnea, worse at night. Spirometry shows FEV₁/FVC 0.65. Post-bronchodilator FEV₁ increases by 18% and 250 ml. What is the diagnosis?

**A)** COPD
**B)** Asthma
**C)** Bronchiectasis
**D)** Interstitial lung disease

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: B) Asthma**

**Explanation:**
- **Obstructive pattern:** FEV₁/FVC <0.70
- **Significant reversibility:** FEV₁ ↑ 18% AND 250 ml (Both criteria met: ≥12% AND ≥200 ml)
- **Confirms REVERSIBLE airflow obstruction** → **Asthma**

**Why not COPD:**
- COPD has **partial reversibility** (<12% or <200 ml)
- Usually in older patients with smoking history
</details>

### Question 2
Which medication should NEVER be used as monotherapy in asthma?

**A)** Inhaled corticosteroid (ICS)
**B)** Long-acting beta-2 agonist (LABA)
**C)** Short-acting beta-2 agonist (SABA)
**D)** Leukotriene receptor antagonist (LTRA)

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: B) Long-acting beta-2 agonist (LABA)**

**Explanation:**
- **LABA as monotherapy** is associated with **increased asthma-related deaths** (SMART trial)
- **ALWAYS use LABA with ICS** (Combination inhaler)

**Mechanism:**
- LABA provides bronchodilation but does NOT treat underlying inflammation
- Masks symptoms while inflammation worsens

**All other options can be used as monotherapy:**
- ICS: Mainstay controller
- SABA: As-needed reliever
- LTRA: Alternative controller
</details>

### Question 3
A patient with severe asthma exacerbation is not responding to nebulized salbutamol and ipratropium. SpO₂ is 89%, PEFR 35%. What is the next most appropriate medication?

**A)** Oral prednisolone
**B)** IV magnesium sulfate 2 g
**C)** Oral montelukast
**D)** IV aminophylline

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**Answer: B) IV magnesium sulfate 2 g**

**Explanation:**
- **Severe exacerbation** not responding to initial bronchodilators
- **Magnesium sulfate 2 g IV** over 20 min is indicated
- **Mechanism:** Bronchodilation (Ca²⁺ channel blocker)
- **Evidence:** ↓ Hospital admissions in severe asthma

**Note:**
- **Oral prednisolone** should have already been given (Within 1 hour of presentation)
- If not, give it NOW as well
- Montelukast: Not for acute management
- Aminophylline: Second-line (Not preferred - narrow therapeutic window, side effects)
</details>
