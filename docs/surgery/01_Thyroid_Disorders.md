# THYROID DISORDERS - NEET PG Study Material

## Anatomy - Quick Review

**Location**: Anterior neck (C5-T1)
**Lobes**: Right & Left connected by **isthmus**
**Weight**: 15-20g
**Blood Supply**:
- **Superior thyroid artery** (1st branch of ECA)
- **Inferior thyroid artery** (from thyrocervical trunk)
- **Ima artery** (10%, directly from aorta)

**Venous Drainage**:
- Superior, middle, inferior thyroid veins

**Nerves** (SURGICAL IMPORTANCE):
1. **Recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN)**:
   - Close relation with inferior thyroid artery
   - Injury → Hoarseness, bilateral injury → Stridor
2. **External branch of superior laryngeal nerve**:
   - Close to superior thyroid vessels
   - Injury → Cannot increase pitch

**NEET PG**: RLN injury most common complication of thyroid surgery

---

## Thyroid Function Tests

| Test | Normal Range | Indication |
|------|--------------|------------|
| **TSH** | 0.4-4.0 mIU/L | Screening test |
| **Free T4** | 0.8-1.8 ng/dL | Hypothyroidism |
| **Free T3** | 2.3-4.2 pg/mL | Hyperthyroidism (T3 toxicosis) |
| **Anti-TPO** | Negative | Autoimmune thyroid disease |
| **Thyroglobulin** | <50 ng/mL | Tumor marker (thyroid cancer) |

**NEET PG**: TSH is best screening test (most sensitive)

---

## GOITER

**Definition**: Enlargement of thyroid gland

### Classification

#### By Function
- **Euthyroid goiter**: Normal thyroid function
- **Toxic goiter**: Hyperthyroid
- **Hypothyroid goiter**: Hypothyroid

#### By Anatomy
- **Diffuse**: Entire gland enlarged (Graves', iodine deficiency)
- **Nodular**:
  - **Solitary** (adenoma, cyst, cancer)
  - **Multinodular** (MNG)

---

## SIMPLE (NON-TOXIC) GOITER

### Endemic Goiter
**Cause**: **Iodine deficiency** (most common worldwide)
**Pathophysiology**: ↓ Iodine → ↓ T4 → ↑ TSH → Goiter
**Prevention**: Iodized salt

### Sporadic Goiter
**Causes**:
- Goitrogens (cabbage, cassava)
- Drugs (lithium, amiodarone)
- Enzyme defects

**Clinical**: Asymptomatic neck swelling, euthyroid

**NEET PG**: Iodine deficiency = Most common cause of goiter worldwide

---

## MULTINODULAR GOITER (MNG)

**Definition**: Multiple nodules in thyroid

**Pathophysiology**: Long-standing simple goiter → Nodule formation

**Clinical Features**:
- Neck swelling (cosmetic concern)
- **Compressive symptoms**:
  - Dysphagia (esophagus)
  - Dyspnea, stridor (trachea)
  - SVC obstruction
  - Hoarseness (RLN)
- **Pemberton's sign**: Raise arms above head → Facial flushing, distress (SVC compression)

**Retrosternal goiter**: Extension into mediastinum (>50% below sternal notch)

**Investigations**:
- **Thyroid function tests**: Usually euthyroid
- **USG neck**: Multiple nodules
- **FNAC**: Rule out malignancy
- **CXR/CT**: Retrosternal extension, tracheal deviation

**Treatment**:
- **Conservative**: If asymptomatic, small
- **Thyroxine suppression**: Limited role
- **Surgery (Total thyroidectomy)**:
  - Compressive symptoms
  - Retrosternal extension
  - Suspicion of malignancy
  - Cosmesis

**NEET PG**:
- **Pemberton's sign**: MNG with retrosternal extension
- **Malignancy risk**: 5% in MNG

---

## HYPERTHYROIDISM

**Definition**: ↑ Thyroid hormones

### Causes
- **Graves' disease** (70-80%) - Most common
- **Toxic multinodular goiter** (Plummer's disease)
- **Toxic adenoma**
- **Subacute thyroiditis** (transient)
- **Exogenous thyroid hormone**

---

## GRAVES' DISEASE

**Pathophysiology**: **Autoimmune** (TSH receptor antibodies → Stimulate thyroid)

**Clinical Features**:
1. **Hyperthyroidism**:
   - Weight loss despite ↑ appetite
   - Heat intolerance, sweating
   - Tremors, anxiety, irritability
   - Palpitations, tachycardia, AF
   - Diarrhea
   - Proximal muscle weakness
   - Menstrual irregularities

2. **Diffuse goiter**: Smooth, non-tender, bruit present

3. **Ophthalmopathy** (30-50%):
   - **Exophthalmos** (proptosis)
   - Lid lag, lid retraction
   - Diplopia
   - Corneal ulceration

4. **Pretibial myxedema** (dermopathy) (5%)

5. **Thyroid acropachy** (clubbing) - Rare

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- **Triad**: Hyperthyroidism + Ophthalmopathy + Pretibial myxedema
- Only Graves' causes **ophthalmopathy**
- **TSI (Thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulin)**: Pathognomonic

**Investigations**:
- **↓ TSH, ↑ Free T4/T3**
- **TSI antibodies**: Positive
- **Radioiodine uptake**: **Diffusely increased**
- **USG**: Diffuse enlargement, increased vascularity

**Treatment**:

**1. Medical** (1st line):
- **Antithyroid drugs (ATD)**:
  - **Carbimazole/Methimazole**: Preferred (once-daily)
  - **Propylthiouracil (PTU)**: 1st trimester pregnancy, thyroid storm
- **Mechanism**: Inhibit thyroid peroxidase (↓ T4/T3 synthesis)
- **Duration**: 12-18 months
- **Adverse effects**:
  - **Agranulocytosis** (check if fever, sore throat)
  - Hepatotoxicity (PTU > carbimazole)
  - Rash, arthralgia

**2. Beta-blockers** (Propranolol):
- Symptomatic relief
- Blocks peripheral conversion T4 → T3

**3. Radioiodine (I-131)**:
- **Indications**: Relapse after ATD, elderly, poor surgical candidate
- **Mechanism**: Destroys thyroid tissue
- **Contraindications**: Pregnancy, lactation, severe ophthalmopathy
- **Complication**: **Hypothyroidism** (60-80%)

**4. Surgery (Total thyroidectomy)**:
- **Indications**:
  - Large goiter (compressive symptoms)
  - Pregnancy (2nd trimester)
  - Suspicion of malignancy
  - Patient preference
- **Preparation**:
  - ATD until euthyroid
  - **Lugol's iodine** (10-14 days before surgery) - ↓ Vascularity
  - Beta-blocker
- **Complications**: RLN injury, hypoparathyroidism, hypothyroidism, thyroid storm

**NEET PG**:
- **PTU**: Preferred in pregnancy (1st trimester) & thyroid storm (blocks T4→T3 conversion)
- **Lugol's iodine**: Pre-op to ↓ vascularity (10-14 days)
- **I-131**: Contraindicated in pregnancy

---

## THYROID STORM (Thyrotoxic Crisis)

**Definition**: Life-threatening hyperthyroidism

**Triggers**:
- Surgery (inadequate preparation)
- Infection, trauma, DKA
- Withdrawal of ATD
- Radioiodine therapy

**Clinical Features** (Burch-Wartofsky criteria):
- **High fever** (>40°C)
- **Tachycardia** (>140 bpm), AF
- **CNS**: Agitation, confusion, seizures, coma
- **GI**: Diarrhea, vomiting, jaundice
- **Cardiovascular collapse**

**NEET PG**: Diagnosis clinical (no time for labs)

**Treatment** (ABCDE):
1. **Antithyroid drugs**: PTU 600mg loading → 200mg q6h (preferred - blocks conversion)
2. **Beta-blocker**: Propranolol 40-80mg q6h (or IV if severe)
3. **Corticosteroids**: Hydrocortisone 100mg q8h (blocks T4→T3, treats adrenal insufficiency)
4. **Dexamethasone**: Alternative to hydrocortisone
5. **Iodine**: Lugol's iodine 5 drops q6h (give **1 hour after ATD**)

**Additional**:
- Cooling blankets (avoid aspirin - ↑ free T4)
- IV fluids
- Treat precipitating cause

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- **Give iodine 1 hour AFTER ATD** (prevents iodine use for hormone synthesis)
- **PTU preferred** over carbimazole (blocks peripheral conversion)
- **Avoid aspirin** (displaces T4 from proteins)

**Mortality**: 20-30% (medical emergency)

---

## HYPOTHYROIDISM

### Causes

**Primary** (95%):
- **Hashimoto's thyroiditis** (most common in iodine-sufficient areas)
- Iodine deficiency
- Post-thyroidectomy
- Post-radioiodine
- Drugs (amiodarone, lithium)

**Secondary**: Pituitary failure

**Tertiary**: Hypothalamic failure

**NEET PG**: Hashimoto's = Most common cause of hypothyroidism in iodine-sufficient areas

---

## HASHIMOTO'S THYROIDITIS

**Pathophysiology**: **Autoimmune** (anti-TPO, anti-thyroglobulin antibodies)

**Clinical**:
- **Hypothyroidism**: Weight gain, cold intolerance, fatigue, constipation, bradycardia, dry skin
- **Goiter**: Firm, non-tender, diffuse

**Histology**:
- **Lymphocytic infiltration**
- **Hurthle cells** (oxyphilic cells)
- **Germinal centers**

**Associations**:
- Other autoimmune diseases (Type 1 DM, vitiligo, Addison's)
- Slightly ↑ risk of **thyroid lymphoma**

**NEET PG**:
- **Anti-TPO antibodies**: Hashimoto's
- **Hurthle cells**: Hashimoto's (also in Graves')

**Treatment**: **Levothyroxine** (lifelong)

---

## THYROID NODULES - HIGH YIELD

**Prevalence**: 4-7% by palpation, 50-60% on USG

**Malignancy risk**: 5-15%

### Evaluation

**History**:
- **High-risk features**:
  - Age <20 or >60 years
  - Male gender
  - Rapid growth
  - Hoarseness, dysphagia
  - Family history of thyroid cancer
  - Radiation exposure (childhood)

**Clinical**:
- **Suspicious features**:
  - Hard, fixed nodule
  - Cervical lymphadenopathy
  - Vocal cord palsy

**Investigations**:

**1. TSH**:
- **Low TSH**: Hyperfunctioning (likely benign) → **Thyroid scan**
- **Normal/High TSH**: → **USG + FNAC**

**2. Thyroid USG**:
- **High-risk features**: Hypoechoic, microcalcifications, irregular margins, taller than wide, ↑ vascularity

**3. FNAC** (Gold standard):
- **Bethesda classification**:
  - I: Non-diagnostic → Repeat
  - II: Benign → Follow-up
  - III/IV: Indeterminate → Molecular testing or surgery
  - V: Suspicious → Surgery
  - VI: Malignant → Surgery

**NEET PG**:
- **FNAC**: Most important investigation
- **Bethesda II** (Benign) → Observe
- **Bethesda VI** (Malignant) → Surgery

## THYROID CANCER

### Classification

| Type | Percentage | Prognosis | Features |
|------|------------|-----------|----------|
| **Papillary** | 80% | Excellent | Psammoma bodies, Orphan Annie nuclei |
| **Follicular** | 10-15% | Good | Hematogenous spread (bone, lung) |
| **Medullary** | 5% | Intermediate | Calcitonin, amyloid, MEN 2A/2B |
| **Anaplastic** | 1-2% | Poor (months) | Elderly, rapidly fatal |

---

### PAPILLARY THYROID CANCER (PTC)

**Most common thyroid cancer** (80%)

**Risk Factors**:
- **Radiation exposure** (childhood) - Strongest risk factor
- Young age
- Female

**Pathology**:
- **Psammoma bodies** (laminated calcifications)
- **Orphan Annie eye nuclei** (ground-glass, empty nuclei)
- **Nuclear grooves**
- **Intranuclear inclusions**

**Spread**: **Lymphatic** (cervical lymph nodes) >> Hematogenous

**Subtypes**:
- **Tall cell variant**: Aggressive
- **Follicular variant**: Good prognosis

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- **Most common thyroid cancer**
- **Psammoma bodies** = Papillary carcinoma
- **Lymphatic spread** (cervical LN)
- **Best prognosis**

**Treatment**:
- **Total thyroidectomy** + Central lymph node dissection (if LN+)
- **Radioiodine ablation** (I-131) post-op
- **TSH suppression** (levothyroxine to keep TSH low)

**Follow-up**: **Thyroglobulin** (tumor marker)

---

### FOLLICULAR THYROID CANCER (FTC)

**Second most common** (10-15%)

**Pathology**:
- **Cannot diagnose on FNAC** (requires capsular/vascular invasion)
- **Histology**: Capsular or vascular invasion

**Spread**: **Hematogenous** (bone, lung) >> Lymphatic

**NEET PG**:
- **Cannot differentiate from follicular adenoma on FNAC** (needs histology)
- **Hematogenous spread** (bone, lung)

**Treatment**: Same as PTC (total thyroidectomy + radioiodine)

---

### MEDULLARY THYROID CANCER (MTC)

**Arises from**: **Parafollicular C cells** (produce calcitonin)

**Types**:
- **Sporadic** (75%)
- **Familial** (25%):
  - **MEN 2A**: MTC + Pheochromocytoma + Hyperparathyroidism
  - **MEN 2B**: MTC + Pheochromocytoma + Mucosal neuromas + Marfanoid habitus
  - **Familial MTC** (FMTC)

**Pathology**:
- **Amyloid stroma** (from calcitonin)
- **RET proto-oncogene mutation**

**Tumor Markers**:
- **Calcitonin**: Diagnostic & follow-up (↑ with pentagastrin/Ca2+ stimulation)
- **CEA**: Prognosis

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- **MEN 2A & 2B**: Associated with MTC
- **Calcitonin**: Tumor marker
- **Amyloid deposition**: MTC
- **Screen for pheochromocytoma** before surgery (MEN 2)

**Treatment**:
- **Total thyroidectomy** + Central LN dissection
- **NO role for radioiodine** (C cells don't take up iodine)

**Prophylactic thyroidectomy**: Children with RET mutation (MEN 2)

---

### ANAPLASTIC THYROID CANCER

**Most aggressive thyroid cancer**

**Age**: Elderly (>60 years)

**Clinical**: Rapidly growing neck mass, dysphagia, hoarseness, stridor

**Pathology**: Undifferentiated, pleomorphic cells

**Prognosis**: **Median survival 6 months**

**NEET PG**: Rapidly fatal, no role for surgery (unless for airway)

**Treatment**: Palliative (radiation, chemotherapy, tracheostomy if airway obstruction)

---

## COMPLICATIONS OF THYROID SURGERY

### Immediate
1. **Hemorrhage**: Most dangerous (can cause airway obstruction)
   - **Management**: Open wound at bedside, re-explore

2. **Airway obstruction**: Hematoma, bilateral RLN injury, tracheomalacia

3. **Thyroid storm**: Inadequate preparation

### Early (1 week)
1. **Recurrent laryngeal nerve injury** (1-2%):
   - **Unilateral**: Hoarseness, weak voice
   - **Bilateral**: Stridor, respiratory distress (emergency tracheostomy)

2. **Superior laryngeal nerve (external branch) injury**:
   - Cannot increase pitch (singers)

3. **Hypoparathyroidism** (transient or permanent):
   - **Hypocalcemia**: Perioral numbness, tetany, Chvostek's sign, Trousseau's sign
   - **Treatment**: IV calcium gluconate (emergency), oral calcium + vitamin D

### Late
1. **Hypothyroidism**: All patients (on levothyroxine)
2. **Keloid/scar**: Cosmetic

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- **RLN injury**: Most common nerve injury
- **Bilateral RLN injury**: Stridor (emergency tracheostomy)
- **Hypoparathyroidism**: Check Ca2+ post-op
- **Hemorrhage**: Open wound at bedside if airway compromise
