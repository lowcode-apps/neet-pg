# GENERAL ANESTHETICS - NEET PG Study Material

## DEFINITION

**General anesthesia**: Reversible state of CNS depression producing:
1. **Loss of consciousness**
2. **Analgesia** (pain relief)
3. **Amnesia** (memory loss)
4. **Muscle relaxation**
5. **Loss of reflexes**

**NEET PG**: General anesthesia = Reversible CNS depression with loss of consciousness

---

## STAGES OF ANESTHESIA (Guedel's Classification)

### Stage I: Analgesia (Induction)

**Characteristics**:
- Conscious but drowsy
- Analgesia present
- **Amnesia may occur**

**Clinical**: From start of anesthetic to loss of consciousness

---

### Stage II: Excitement (Delirium)

**Characteristics**:
- **Loss of consciousness**
- **Irregular breathing**
- **Increased muscle tone**
- **Exaggerated reflexes**
- **Vomiting, laryngospasm may occur**
- **Dangerous stage**

**Goal**: **Pass through quickly** (use IV induction agents)

**NEET PG**: Stage II = Excitement, dangerous (irregular breathing, laryngospasm)

---

### Stage III: Surgical Anesthesia

**Divided into 4 planes**:

**Plane 1**:
- Regular respiration
- Roving eyeballs
- **Surgery can begin**

**Plane 2**:
- Eyes central & fixed
- Corneal & laryngeal reflexes lost

**Plane 3**:
- Intercostal paralysis (diaphragmatic breathing only)
- Pupil dilation begins

**Plane 4**:
- Diaphragmatic paralysis imminent
- **Dangerous** - near overdose

**NEET PG**: Stage III = Surgical anesthesia (planes 1-4)

---

### Stage IV: Medullary Paralysis (Overdose)

**Characteristics**:
- **Respiratory arrest**
- **Cardiovascular collapse**
- **Dilated pupils**
- **Death** if not treated

**Treatment**: Stop anesthetic, ventilate, circulatory support

**NEET PG**: Stage IV = Overdose, respiratory/cardiovascular collapse

---

## CLASSIFICATION

### A. Inhalational Anesthetics

**1. Gases**:
- Nitrous oxide (N₂O)

**2. Volatile liquids**:
- **Halogenated hydrocarbons**:
  - Halothane
  - Isoflurane
  - Sevoflurane
  - Desflurane
  - Enflurane (obsolete)
- **Ether** (obsolete)

### B. Intravenous Anesthetics

**1. Barbiturates**:
- Thiopental sodium

**2. Non-barbiturates**:
- **Propofol**
- **Etomidate**
- **Ketamine**
- Benzodiazepines (midazolam)

---

## PHARMACOKINETIC PRINCIPLES

### Minimum Alveolar Concentration (MAC)

**Definition**: **Alveolar concentration** at which **50% of patients** do not move in response to surgical incision

**Key points**:
- Measure of **potency** (↓ MAC = ↑ potency)
- Additive (0.5 MAC + 0.5 MAC = 1 MAC)
- **Inverse of potency**

**Factors INCREASING MAC** (resistant):
- Hyperthermia
- Hypernatremia
- Chronic alcoholism
- Drugs: Amphetamine, ephedrine, cocaine

**Factors DECREASING MAC** (sensitive):
- Hypothermia
- Hypoxia, severe anemia
- Pregnancy
- Elderly
- Drugs: Opioids, α2-agonists, acute alcohol

**NEET PG High-Yield**: MAC = Measure of potency, 50% no response to incision

---

### Blood:Gas Partition Coefficient

**Definition**: **Solubility** of anesthetic in blood

**Significance**:
- **↓ Solubility → ↑ Rate of induction & recovery** (faster)
- **↑ Solubility → ↓ Rate** (slower)

**Order** (fastest to slowest):
```
Desflurane (0.42) > Sevoflurane (0.65) > Isoflurane (1.4) > Halothane (2.3) > Ether (12)
```

**NEET PG**: Low blood:gas coefficient = Faster induction (desflurane fastest)

---

### Concentration Effect & Second Gas Effect

**Concentration effect**: ↑ Inspired concentration → ↑ Rate of rise of alveolar concentration
- Used with N₂O (delivered at high %)

**Second gas effect**: N₂O accelerates uptake of other volatile anesthetics

**NEET PG**: N₂O shows concentration & second gas effects

---

## INHALATIONAL ANESTHETICS

### NITROUS OXIDE (N₂O) - "Laughing Gas"

**Properties**:
- **Gas** (not volatile liquid)
- **Low potency** (MAC = 105% - cannot be used alone)
- **Very low blood:gas** (0.47) → **Rapid induction & recovery**
- Non-flammable (supports combustion)
- Sweet odor

**Mechanism**: NMDA receptor antagonist (like ketamine)

---

#### Clinical Uses

**1. Analgesia**:
- Dental procedures
- Labor analgesia
- Minor procedures

**2. General anesthesia** (in combination):
- With volatile anesthetics (↓ their requirement)
- With IV agents

**Concentration**: **50-70%** (with 30-50% O₂)

**NEET PG**: N₂O cannot be used alone (MAC >100%)

---

#### Adverse Effects

**1. Diffusion hypoxia**:
- At end of anesthesia
- N₂O diffuses out rapidly → Dilutes alveolar O₂
- Prevention: Give **100% O₂** for 5-10 min after stopping N₂O

**2. Expansion of closed gas spaces** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- N₂O more soluble than N₂
- Diffuses into closed spaces faster than N₂ comes out
- **Contraindicated in**:
  - **Pneumothorax**
  - **Bowel obstruction**
  - **Middle ear surgery**
  - **Intracranial air** (pneumocephalus)
  - **Eye surgery with intraocular gas**

**3. Bone marrow depression**:
- Inactivates **vitamin B12**
- Megaloblastic anemia (prolonged use)

**4. Teratogenic** (avoid in early pregnancy)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- N₂O contraindicated in pneumothorax, bowel obstruction (expands gas spaces)
- Diffusion hypoxia prevented by 100% O₂ after stopping N₂O

---

### HALOTHANE

**Properties**:
- **Non-pungent**, pleasant odor
- Potent (MAC = 0.75%)
- **Bronchodilator**
- Moderate blood:gas (2.3)

**Advantages**:
- **Smooth induction** (non-irritant)
- **Bronchodilation** (useful in asthma)
- Non-flammable

---

#### Cardiovascular Effects

- **Myocardial depression** (↓ contractility)
- **Hypotension**
- **Bradycardia** (vagal stimulation)
- **Sensitizes heart to catecholamines** → arrhythmias
- ↓ Baroreceptor reflex

**NEET PG**: Halothane sensitizes heart to catecholamines (arrhythmias)

---

#### Adverse Effects

**1. Hepatotoxicity** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **"Halothane hepatitis"**
- Two types:
  - **Type 1**: Mild, transient ↑ transaminases (25%)
  - **Type 2**: Severe, **fulminant hepatic necrosis** (1 in 10,000)
    - **Immune-mediated** (antibodies to trifluoroacetylated proteins)
    - **Higher risk**: Repeated exposure, obesity, middle-aged women
- **Most serious complication**

**2. Malignant hyperthermia**:
- Genetic (ryanodine receptor mutation)
- With succinylcholine
- Treatment: **Dantrolene**

**3. Uterine relaxation** (↑ postpartum bleeding)

**4. ↑ Intracranial pressure** (cerebral vasodilation)

**5. No analgesia**

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Halothane hepatitis = Most serious complication
- Halothane + catecholamines → arrhythmias

---

### ISOFLURANE

**Properties**:
- **Pungent** (irritant to airways)
- Potent (MAC = 1.2%)
- Low blood:gas (1.4) → faster than halothane

**Advantages**:
- **Minimal metabolism** (<0.2%) → **No hepatotoxicity**
- **Does NOT sensitize heart to catecholamines**
- **Cardiovascular stable** (maintains cardiac output)
- **Muscle relaxation** (good)

**Disadvantage**:
- Pungent → not for inhalational induction

**NEET PG**: Isoflurane = No hepatotoxicity, no arrhythmias

---

### SEVOFLURANE

**Properties**:
- **Non-pungent**, pleasant odor
- Potent (MAC = 2%)
- **Low blood:gas (0.65)** → **Rapid induction & recovery**

**Advantages**:
- **Ideal for inhalational induction** (especially children)
- Non-irritant
- Cardiovascular stable
- Minimal metabolism

**Disadvantage**:
- **Compound A** (with CO₂ absorbers - soda lime)
  - Potentially nephrotoxic
  - Low fresh gas flows → ↑ Compound A

**NEET PG High-Yield**: Sevoflurane = Best for inhalational induction (non-pungent, rapid)

---

### DESFLURANE

**Properties**:
- **Lowest blood:gas (0.42)** → **Fastest induction & recovery**
- Pungent
- MAC = 6%

**Advantages**:
- **Fastest emergence** (rapid recovery)
- Minimal metabolism

**Disadvantages**:
- **Pungent** (airway irritation, cough, laryngospasm)
- Requires special vaporizer (low boiling point)

**NEET PG**: Desflurane = Fastest recovery

---

## INTRAVENOUS ANESTHETICS

### THIOPENTAL SODIUM

**Classification**: **Ultra-short acting barbiturate**

**Mechanism**: **GABA-A receptor** potentiation

---

#### Pharmacokinetics

**Onset**: **Rapid** (10-20 seconds - "one arm-brain circulation time")

**Duration**: **Short** (5-10 minutes)
- **NOT due to metabolism**
- Due to **redistribution** (highly lipid soluble)
  - Brain → Muscle → Fat
  - Recovery when brain levels ↓

**Metabolism**: **Slow** (hepatic) - hours
- **Hangover effect** (residual sedation)

**NEET PG**: Thiopental short duration due to redistribution (NOT metabolism)

---

#### Uses

**1. Induction of general anesthesia** (rapid, smooth)

**2. Status epilepticus** (refractory)

**3. ↓ Intracranial pressure** (cerebral vasoconstriction)

**NEET PG**: Thiopental for rapid induction, status epilepticus

---

#### Adverse Effects

**1. Respiratory depression** (dose-dependent)

**2. Myocardial depression**:
- ↓ Contractility
- Hypotension
- **Avoid in shock, cardiovascular instability**

**3. Laryngospasm, bronchospasm**:
- **Contraindicated in asthma**

**4. Venous thrombosis, arterial injection**:
- **Intra-arterial injection** → **Severe pain, gangrene**
- Treatment: Leave needle in, inject vasodilators (papaverine, tolazoline)

**5. Porphyria** (absolute contraindication)

**6. No analgesia**

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Thiopental contraindicated in asthma, porphyria, shock
- Intra-arterial injection → gangrene

---

### PROPOFOL

**Mechanism**: GABA-A receptor potentiation

**Formulation**: **Lipid emulsion** (soybean oil, egg lecithin)

---

#### Pharmacokinetics

**Onset**: Rapid (30-45 seconds)

**Duration**: Short (3-10 minutes) - redistribution

**Metabolism**: Hepatic + extrahepatic (faster than thiopental)

**Recovery**: **Smooth, rapid** (no hangover)

---

#### Uses

**1. Induction of anesthesia** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Most commonly used IV induction agent**
- Smooth induction & recovery

**2. Maintenance** (continuous infusion - TIVA: Total IV anesthesia)

**3. Sedation** (ICU, procedures)

**4. Antiemetic** (↓ PONV - postoperative nausea/vomiting)

**NEET PG**: Propofol = Most common IV induction agent

---

#### Advantages over Thiopental

- **Faster recovery**, no hangover
- **Antiemetic** property
- **Less cardiovascular depression** (safer)
- Can be used for maintenance (infusion)

---

#### Adverse Effects

**1. Pain on injection**:
- **Most common complaint**
- Prevention: Lidocaine IV before/with propofol

**2. Hypotension**:
- Vasodilation
- Less than thiopental

**3. Respiratory depression**

**4. **Propofol infusion syndrome** (PRIS)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- Prolonged high-dose infusion
- **Metabolic acidosis, rhabdomyolysis, cardiac failure**
- Can be fatal

**5. Contraindications**:
- **Egg/soy allergy** (formulation)

**6. No analgesia**

**7. **Supports bacterial growth** (lipid emulsion)
- Use strict aseptic technique
- Discard after 6 hours

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Propofol pain on injection (give with lidocaine)
- Propofol infusion syndrome (prolonged high-dose)
- Contraindicated in egg/soy allergy

---

### ETOMIDATE

**Mechanism**: GABA-A receptor potentiation

---

#### Advantages

**Cardiovascular stability** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Minimal effect** on BP, HR
- **Drug of choice in**:
  - **Shock**
  - **Cardiovascular disease**
  - **Hypovolemia**

**Cerebral protective**: ↓ ICP, ↓ CMR (cerebral metabolic rate)

**NEET PG**: Etomidate = Most cardiovascularly stable (DOC in shock)

---

#### Adverse Effects

**1. Adrenal suppression**:
- **Inhibits 11β-hydroxylase**
- ↓ Cortisol synthesis
- Even single dose
- **Avoid prolonged infusion**

**2. Myoclonus** (involuntary movements)

**3. Pain on injection**

**4. PONV** (high incidence)

**5. No analgesia**

**NEET PG High-Yield**: Etomidate causes adrenal suppression

---

### KETAMINE - "Dissociative Anesthetic"

**Mechanism**: **NMDA receptor antagonist**

**Unique properties**:
- **Analgesia** (strong)
- **Dissociative state** (eyes open, but unresponsive)
- **Maintains airway reflexes**
- **Bronchodilation**

---

#### Cardiovascular Effects

**Sympathomimetic**:
- ↑ HR, ↑ BP, ↑ Cardiac output
- **Useful in shock, asthma**

**Mechanism**: ↑ Catecholamine release, blocks reuptake

**NEET PG**: Ketamine ↑ BP (vs other anesthetics ↓ BP)

---

#### Uses

**1. Induction** (especially in):
- **Shock, hypovolemia**
- **Asthma** (bronchodilator)
- **Pediatric** (IM route possible)
- **Mass casualties, disasters**

**2. Analgesia** (chronic pain, procedural)

**3. Sedation** (ICU)

**NEET PG**: Ketamine = DOC in shock with asthma

---

#### Adverse Effects

**1. Emergence reactions** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐:
- **Hallucinations, nightmares, delirium**
- During recovery
- **Prevention**: Benzodiazepines (midazolam)

**2. ↑ Intracranial pressure**:
- ↑ Cerebral blood flow
- **Contraindicated in head injury**

**3. ↑ Intraocular pressure**:
- Contraindicated in glaucoma

**4. Salivation** (↑ secretions)

**5. Tachycardia, hypertension**

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Ketamine emergence reactions (give benzodiazepines)
- Contraindicated in head injury, glaucoma (↑ ICP, ↑ IOP)

---

## BALANCED ANESTHESIA

**Definition**: Combination of multiple agents to achieve:
- Unconsciousness
- Analgesia
- Muscle relaxation
- Amnesia

**Typical regimen**:
1. **Premedication**: Benzodiazepine (anxiolysis, amnesia)
2. **Induction**: Propofol or thiopental (IV)
3. **Muscle relaxation**: Succinylcholine (intubation) → Vecuronium (maintenance)
4. **Maintenance**: Volatile anesthetic (sevoflurane/isoflurane) + N₂O + O₂
5. **Analgesia**: Opioid (fentanyl)

**Advantages**:
- ↓ Dose of each drug → ↓ toxicity
- Better control
- Faster recovery

**NEET PG**: Balanced anesthesia = Combination approach

---

## PREMEDICATION

**Goals**:
1. **Anxiolysis**
2. **Amnesia**
3. **Analgesia**
4. ↓ Secretions (antisialagogue)
5. ↓ Vagal reflexes
6. ↓ Gastric acidity
7. Antiemetic

**Drugs**:

**1. Benzodiazepines** (most common):
- Midazolam, diazepam
- Anxiolysis, amnesia, sedation

**2. Anticholinergics**:
- **Atropine**, glycopyrrolate
- ↓ Secretions, prevent bradycardia

**3. Opioids**:
- Morphine, fentanyl
- Analgesia, sedation

**4. H2-blockers / PPIs**:
- Ranitidine, omeprazole
- ↓ Gastric acid (aspiration risk)

**5. Antiemetics**:
- Metoclopramide, ondansetron

**NEET PG**: Midazolam most common premedication (anxiolysis, amnesia)

## HIGH-YIELD COMPARISONS

### Inhalational Anesthetics

| Drug | MAC | Blood:Gas | Key Feature |
|------|-----|-----------|-------------|
| **N₂O** | 105% | 0.47 | Cannot use alone, expands gas spaces |
| **Halothane** | 0.75% | 2.3 | Hepatotoxicity, arrhythmias |
| **Isoflurane** | 1.2% | 1.4 | No hepatotoxicity, no arrhythmias |
| **Sevoflurane** | 2% | 0.65 | Best for induction (non-pungent) |
| **Desflurane** | 6% | 0.42 | Fastest recovery |

---

### IV Anesthetics

| Drug | Onset | Key Use | Major Adverse Effect |
|------|-------|---------|----------------------|
| **Thiopental** | Fastest | Rapid induction, status epilepticus | Contraindicated in asthma, porphyria |
| **Propofol** | Fast | Most common induction agent | Pain on injection, PRIS |
| **Etomidate** | Fast | Shock, CV disease | Adrenal suppression |
| **Ketamine** | Fast | Shock + asthma, pediatric | Emergence reactions, ↑ ICP |

## Practice MCQs

1. **What does MAC represent?**
   Answer: Alveolar concentration at which 50% don't respond to incision

2. **Fastest induction & recovery inhalational agent?**
   Answer: Desflurane (lowest blood:gas)

3. **Most serious complication of halothane?**
   Answer: Hepatotoxicity (fulminant hepatic necrosis)

4. **Best agent for inhalational induction in children?**
   Answer: Sevoflurane (non-pungent, rapid)

5. **Why is N₂O contraindicated in pneumothorax?**
   Answer: Expands closed gas spaces

6. **How to prevent diffusion hypoxia?**
   Answer: Give 100% O₂ for 5-10 min after stopping N₂O

7. **Most common IV induction agent?**
   Answer: Propofol

8. **IV anesthetic of choice in shock?**
   Answer: Etomidate (cardiovascular stability)

9. **Treatment for ketamine emergence reactions?**
   Answer: Benzodiazepines (midazolam)

10. **Why is thiopental short-acting?**
    Answer: Redistribution (NOT metabolism)
