# Tuberculosis - Pathology

## DEFINITION ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Tuberculosis**: Chronic **granulomatous infection** caused by *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Hallmark**: **Caseating granulomas** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**See Microbiology Topic 03 for full clinical details** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

## PATHOGENESIS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

### PRIMARY TB (First Infection) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Ghon Focus**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Small peripheral subpleural lesion** (usually lower lobe) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Caseating granuloma** at site of initial infection ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Ghon Complex**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Ghon focus + draining hilar lymph node involvement** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Diagnostic of primary TB** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Ranke Complex**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Ghon complex that has healed** with **calcification** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Healed primary TB** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Outcome of Primary TB** (90-95%): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Contained** by cell-mediated immunity ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Latent TB** (dormant bacteria, no symptoms) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Positive TST/IGRA**, **normal CXR** (or calcified Ranke) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

### SECONDARY (REACTIVATION) TB ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Pathogenesis**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Reactivation of latent infection** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Waning immunity** (HIV, immunosuppression, aging, malnutrition) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Site**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Apical-posterior segments of upper lobes** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Superior segment of lower lobes** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- (High O₂ tension favors Mtb growth) ⭐⭐⭐

**Pathology**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Caseating granulomas** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Cavitation** (breakdown of caseous material → cavity) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Fibrosis** (healing with scarring) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

## GRANULOMA FORMATION ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Caseating Granuloma** (Tubercle): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Structure**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
1. **Central caseous necrosis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
   - Acellular, eosinophilic, "cheese-like"
   - **Contains MTB (viable bacteria)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
2. **Epithelioid histiocytes** (activated macrophages) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
3. **Langhans giant cells** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
   - Multi-nucleated (10-20 nuclei)
   - **Nuclei arranged peripherally in horseshoe/arc pattern** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
4. **Lymphocytic rim** (CD4+ T cells) ⭐⭐⭐⭐
5. **Fibrosis** (periphery, chronic stage) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Key Points**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Caseation** is hallmark of TB (vs non-caseating in sarcoid) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Langhans giant cells** are NOT specific to TB (also in sarcoid, fungal) ⭐⭐⭐
- **Caseum contains viable MTB** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

## PULMONARY TB - PATHOLOGIC FEATURES ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Gross**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Apical cavitary lesions** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Gray-white nodules** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Caseous material** (yellow, cheese-like) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Fibrosis, scarring** (chronic) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Microscopic**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Caseating granulomas** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Acid-fast bacilli** (Ziehl-Neelsen, auramine-rhodamine stain) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Langhans giant cells** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Spread**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Bronchogenic spread**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - Cavity → cough → airways → **"tree-in-bud" pattern** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - Can cause **endobronchial TB, tuberculous bronchopneumonia**
- **Lymphohematogenous spread**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Miliary TB** (hematogenous dissemination) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
  - **Extrapulmonary TB** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

## MILIARY TB ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Definition**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Hematogenous dissemination** of MTB ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Innumerable tiny (1-2 mm) granulomas** in multiple organs ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Pathology**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **"Millet seed"** appearance ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Bilateral, diffuse, 1-2 mm nodules** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Lungs, liver, spleen, bone marrow, meninges** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**High-risk**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Immunocompromised** (HIV, infants, elderly) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

## EXTRAPULMONARY TB ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

### LYMPH NODES (Tuberculous Lymphadenitis) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Most common extrapulmonary site** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Sites**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Cervical** (most common) - **"Scrofula"** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- Mediastinal, axillary

**Pathology**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Caseating granulomas** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Matted, enlarged nodes** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Cold abscess** (fluctuant, non-tender, no warmth) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Sinus tract formation** ⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

### CNS (Tuberculous Meningitis) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Most serious form of TB** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Pathology**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Thick, gelatinous exudate at base of brain** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Involves cranial nerves** (esp. CN II, III, VI) ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Granulomas** in meninges
- **Vasculitis** → stroke ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Tuberculoma**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Mass lesion** (caseating granuloma) in brain parenchyma
- **Ring-enhancing lesion** on CT/MRI ⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

### GASTROINTESTINAL TB ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Most common site**: **Ileocecal junction** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Pathology**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Transverse ulcers** (along Peyer's patches) ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Caseating granulomas** in bowel wall ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Strictures, obstruction** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Ascites** (peritoneal TB) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

### GENITOURINARY TB ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Kidney** (most common GU site): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Caseating granulomas** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Caseous necrosis of papillae** → **"sterile pyuria"** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Calcification** ("putty kidney") ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Hydronephrosis** (ureteral strictures) ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Genital**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Epididymis** (males) - beaded, firm ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Fallopian tubes** (females) - **infertility** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Endometrium** - irregular bleeding ⭐⭐⭐

---

### SKELETAL TB (Pott's Disease) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Spine** (most common): ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Pott's disease** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Lower thoracic/lumbar** spine ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Vertebral body destruction** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Gibbus deformity** (angular kyphosis) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Paravertebral/psoas abscess** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Spinal cord compression** → paraplegia ⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Other sites**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Hip, knee** (weight-bearing joints)
- **Tuberculousdactylitis** (digits - **"spina ventosa"**) ⭐⭐⭐

---

### SKIN TB (Lupus Vulgaris) ⭐⭐⭐

**Lupus Vulgaris**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Most common cutaneous TB**
- **Reddish-brown plaque** (face, neck)
- **"Apple jelly" nodules** on diascopy ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Caseating granulomas** ⭐⭐⭐

---

## SPECIAL FORMS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

### TUBERCULOUS PERICARDITIS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Pathology**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Fibrinous/hemorrhagic pericarditis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Granulomas** in pericardium
- **Thick, fibrotic pericardium** (chronic) → **constrictive pericarditis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

### TUBERCULOUS PLEURAL EFFUSION ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Most common cause of exudative pleural effusion in endemic areas** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Pathology**: ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Granulomas** in pleura (biopsy) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Exudative effusion** (high protein, LDH)
- **Lymphocyte-predominant** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Low glucose** ⭐⭐⭐⭐
- **Elevated ADA** (adenosine deaminase) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

## COMPARISON: CASEATING vs NON-CASEATING GRANULOMAS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

| Feature | **Caseating** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | **Non-caseating** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **Central necrosis** | **YES (caseum)** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | **NO** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ |
| **Causes** | **TB** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐<br>**Fungal** (Histoplasma) ⭐⭐⭐ | **Sarcoidosis** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐<br>Crohn's, Berylliosis ⭐⭐⭐ |
| **Langhans cells** | Yes | Yes (not specific) |
| **AFB stain** | **May be positive** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | **Negative** ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ |

## HIGH-YIELD PATHOLOGY PEARLS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

1. **Ghon complex** = Ghon focus + hilar LN (primary TB) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
2. **Ranke complex** = healed, calcified Ghon complex ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
3. **Secondary TB** = apical/upper lobes, cavitation ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
4. **Caseating granuloma** = hallmark, central necrosis ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
5. **Langhans giant cells** = horseshoe-shaped nuclei, NOT specific ⭐⭐⭐⭐
6. **Miliary TB** = hematogenous, millet seeds, 1-2 mm nodules ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
7. **Scrofula** = cervical tuberculous lymphadenitis, cold abscess ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
8. **Pott's disease** = spine TB, gibbus deformity, paraplegia ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
9. **Sterile pyuria** = GU TB (pus without bacteria on routine culture) ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐
10. **TB pleural effusion** = lymphocytes, low glucose, high ADA ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

---

## MNEMONICS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Ghon vs Ranke**: "**G**hon = **G**ranuloma, **R**anke = **R**eally healed (calcified)"

**Secondary TB Location**: "**A**pical **P**osterior **S**egments" (Upper lobes)

**Langhans Cells**: "**L**anghans = **L**ine of nuclei (horseshoe)"

**Pott's Disease**: "**P**ott's = **P**araplegia (spine compression)"

**Sterile Pyuria Causes**: "**T**B, **G**U malignancy, **C**hlamydia" (TGC)

## NEET PG MCQS ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

**Q1.** Healed primary TB with calcification. Name?
A) Ghon focus B) Ghon complex C) Ranke complex D) Assman focus

**Answer: C** - Ranke complex = healed, calcified Ghon complex

**Q2.** Pathognomonic histologic feature of TB?
A) Langhans cells B) Caseating granuloma C) Epithelioid cells D) Lymphocytes

**Answer: B** - Caseating granuloma (central caseous necrosis) is hallmark of TB

**Q3.** Patient with TB spine, angular kyphosis. Deformity name?
A) Gibbus B) Scoliosis C) Lordosis D) Kyphosis

**Answer: A** - Gibbus deformity (angular kyphosis from vertebral collapse in Pott's disease)

**Q4.** TB patient with 1-2 mm nodules in lungs, liver, spleen. Type?
A) Primary TB B) Secondary TB C) Miliary TB D) Endobronchial TB

**Answer: C** - Miliary TB (hematogenous dissemination, millet seed appearance)

**Q5.** Cervical lymphadenopathy, fluctuant, non-tender, no warmth. Most likely?
A) Acute lymphadenitis B) Tuberculous lymphadenitis C) Lymphoma D) Metastasis

**Answer: B** - Tuberculous lymphadenitis (Scrofula): Cold abscess (fluctuant, non-tender, no warmth)
