# CELL INJURY & ADAPTATION - NEET PG Study Material

## Overview
Cells respond to stress by:
1. **Adaptation** (reversible)
2. **Injury** (reversible or irreversible)
3. **Death** (irreversible)

---

## CELLULAR ADAPTATIONS

### 1. HYPERTROPHY
**Definition**: Increase in **cell size** → Increase in organ size
**Mechanism**: ↑ Protein synthesis, ↑ organelles
**Type**: **Physiological** or **Pathological**

**Examples**:

| Physiological | Pathological |
|---------------|--------------|
| Skeletal muscle (exercise) | Left ventricular hypertrophy (HTN, AS) |
| Uterus in pregnancy | Bladder wall (BPH) |
| | Right ventricular hypertrophy (COPD) |

**NEET PG**: Cardiac myocytes undergo only hypertrophy (cannot divide)

---

### 2. HYPERPLASIA
**Definition**: Increase in **cell number** → Increase in organ size
**Mechanism**: ↑ Cell division (mitosis)
**Requirement**: Cells must be capable of division

**Types**:

#### A. Physiological Hyperplasia
- **Hormonal**: Breast during pregnancy, endometrium in menstrual cycle
- **Compensatory**: Liver regeneration after partial hepatectomy

#### B. Pathological Hyperplasia
- **Endometrial hyperplasia** (excess estrogen)
- **Benign prostatic hyperplasia** (BPH)
- **Thyroid goiter** (TSH stimulation)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- Hyperplasia can be **precancerous** (e.g., endometrial hyperplasia → Endometrial carcinoma)
- **Viral warts**, **Molluscum**: Hyperplasia due to viral infection

---

### 3. ATROPHY
**Definition**: Decrease in cell size and number
**Mechanism**: ↓ Protein synthesis, ↑ protein degradation (ubiquitin-proteasome, autophagy)

**Causes** - "**D**isuse **D**enervation **D**ecreased blood **I**schemia **N**utrition **H**ormone **P**ressure **A**ging"

| Cause | Example |
|-------|---------|
| **Disuse** | Immobilized limb, bedridden patient |
| **Denervation** | Polio, nerve injury |
| **Decreased blood supply** | Atherosclerosis |
| **Inadequate nutrition** | Marasmus, kwashiorkor |
| **Loss of endocrine stimulation** | Post-menopause (endometrium, breast) |
| **Pressure** | Brain atrophy from expanding tumor |
| **Aging** | Brain, reproductive organs |

**Microscopy**: **Lipofuscin** (brown atrophy) - residual bodies in atrophic cells

**NEET PG**: Lipofuscin = "Wear and tear pigment"

---

### 4. METAPLASIA
**Definition**: Reversible change where one **differentiated cell type** is replaced by another
**Mechanism**: Reprogramming of stem cells
**Significance**: Adaptation to chronic stress, but **PRECANCEROUS**

**Types**:

#### Epithelial Metaplasia

| Location | From → To | Cause |
|----------|-----------|-------|
| **Bronchi** | Ciliated columnar → Squamous | Smoking |
| **Esophagus** (Barrett's) | Squamous → Columnar (intestinal type) | GERD |
| **Cervix** | Columnar → Squamous | Chronic irritation |
| **Bladder** | Transitional → Squamous | Chronic infection, stones |

#### Mesenchymal Metaplasia
- **Myositis ossificans**: Muscle → Bone (after trauma)

**NEET PG High-Yield**:
- **Barrett's esophagus**: Squamous → Columnar (intestinal metaplasia)
  - Risk factor for **adenocarcinoma** of esophagus
  - Marker: **Goblet cells** on biopsy
- **Bronchial metaplasia**: Predisposes to **squamous cell carcinoma**

---

### 5. DYSPLASIA
**Definition**: Disordered growth, loss of uniformity and orientation
**Features**:
- Loss of polarity
- Nuclear pleomorphism
- ↑ Mitotic figures
- Loss of cellular maturation

**Grades**: Mild → Moderate → Severe (Carcinoma in situ)

**Reversibility**: Can reverse if stimulus removed (e.g., smoking cessation)

**NEET PG**:
- **Dysplasia ≠ Cancer** (no invasion of basement membrane)
- **Carcinoma in situ**: Severe dysplasia but no invasion
- **Cervical dysplasia** (CIN): HPV-related, precancerous

---

## CELL INJURY

### Causes of Cell Injury
1. **Hypoxia**: Most common cause
2. **Physical agents**: Trauma, temperature, radiation, electric shock
3. **Chemical agents**: Toxins, drugs, poisons
4. **Infectious agents**: Bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites
5. **Immunological**: Autoimmune diseases, hypersensitivity
6. **Genetic**: Sickle cell, inborn errors of metabolism
7. **Nutritional**: Deficiency, excess

---

## REVERSIBLE vs IRREVERSIBLE INJURY

### Reversible Injury
**Features**:
- Reduced ATP
- Cell swelling
- Blebs on cell membrane
- Chromatin clumping
- **Mitochondria**: Swollen but intact

**Microscopy**:
- **Cellular swelling** (hydropic degeneration)
- **Fatty change** (steatosis) - in liver, heart, kidney

**NEET PG**: Reversible if cause removed

---

### Irreversible Injury → NECROSIS

**Point of No Return**:
- Severe mitochondrial damage (↓↓ ATP)
- Membrane damage (lysosomal rupture)
- Massive Ca2+ influx

**Features**:
- Nucleus: **Pyknosis** → **Karyorrhexis** → **Karyolysis**
- Cytoplasm: Eosinophilic (pink on H&E)
- Cell membrane: Ruptured
- Mitochondria: Disrupted

---

## TYPES OF NECROSIS - HIGH YIELD

### 1. COAGULATIVE NECROSIS
**Mechanism**: Protein denaturation > Enzyme digestion
**Architecture**: **Preserved** (ghost outline)
**Microscopy**: Anucleate cells with preserved outline
**Common sites**: All organs **EXCEPT brain**

**Example**: **Myocardial infarction**

**NEET PG**: Most common type of necrosis

---

### 2. LIQUEFACTIVE NECROSIS
**Mechanism**: Enzyme digestion > Protein denaturation
**Result**: Liquid, viscous mass
**Common sites**: **Brain** (lack of supporting stroma), **Abscess** (bacterial infection)

**Example**:
- **Brain infarction** → Cystic space
- **Bacterial abscess** → Pus

**NEET PG**: Brain infarction = Liquefactive necrosis

---

### 3. CASEOUS NECROSIS
**Appearance**: "Cheese-like" (caseous)
**Architecture**: Lost (amorphous)
**Microscopy**: Granular, amorphous debris
**Pathognomonic**: **Tuberculosis**

**NEET PG**: Caseous necrosis = TB until proven otherwise

---

### 4. FAT NECROSIS
**Types**:

#### A. Enzymatic Fat Necrosis
**Cause**: Acute pancreatitis (lipase release)
**Mechanism**: Lipase digests fat → Fatty acids + Glycerol
**Result**: **Chalky white deposits** (Ca2+ soaps)
**Microscopy**: Shadowy fat cells with basophilic Ca2+ deposits

**NEET PG**: Acute pancreatitis → ↑ Serum lipase → Fat necrosis

#### B. Traumatic Fat Necrosis
**Cause**: Trauma to adipose tissue (breast)
**Mechanism**: Physical disruption of fat cells
**Result**: Focal enzyme release by adipocytes

---

### 5. FIBRINOID NECROSIS
**Sites**: Blood vessel walls
**Causes**: Immune-mediated injury
  - Polyarteritis nodosa
  - Acute rheumatic fever
  - Malignant hypertension

**Microscopy**: Bright pink (eosinophilic), amorphous material (fibrin deposition)

**NEET PG**: Vasculitis, malignant hypertension

---

### 6. GANGRENOUS NECROSIS
**Definition**: Necrosis + putrefaction (bacterial overgrowth)

**Types**:

| Type | Underlying | Appearance | Example |
|------|------------|------------|---------|
| **Dry gangrene** | Coagulative | Dry, shriveled | Diabetic foot |
| **Wet gangrene** | Liquefactive | Wet, swollen | Gas gangrene (Clostridium) |
| **Gas gangrene** | Liquefactive | Crepitus, foul smell | C. perfringens infection |

**NEET PG**: Gas gangrene = Clostridium perfringens

---

## APOPTOSIS vs NECROSIS

| Feature | Apoptosis | Necrosis |
|---------|-----------|----------|
| **Cell size** | Reduced (shrinkage) | Increased (swelling) |
| **Nucleus** | Fragmentation (karyorrhexis) | Pyknosis → Karyolysis |
| **Membrane** | Intact | Disrupted |
| **Contents** | Retained | Released (inflammation) |
| **Inflammation** | **NO** | **YES** |
| **Outcome** | **Apoptotic bodies** → Phagocytosed | Enzymatic digestion |
| **Energy** | ATP-dependent | ATP-independent |
| **Physiologic** | Yes (normal process) | No (always pathologic) |

**NEET PG**: Key difference = Apoptosis has **NO inflammation**

---

## APOPTOSIS - Programmed Cell Death

### Physiological Apoptosis
1. **Embryogenesis**: Interdigital web removal, organ development
2. **Hormone-dependent involution**: Endometrium (menstruation), mammary gland (post-lactation)
3. **Cell turnover**: Intestinal epithelium
4. **Immune system**: Negative selection of T cells in thymus

### Pathological Apoptosis
1. **DNA damage**: Radiation, chemotherapy (p53-mediated)
2. **Viral infections**: Hepatitis, HIV
3. **Atrophy**: After duct obstruction (pancreas, kidney)

---

## Apoptosis Pathways

### 1. INTRINSIC PATHWAY (Mitochondrial)
**Trigger**: DNA damage, growth factor withdrawal, hypoxia
**Mechanism**:
- Bcl-2 family proteins (Bax, Bak → pro-apoptotic)
- Mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization
- **Cytochrome c** release
- Apoptosome formation (Cytochrome c + Apaf-1 + Caspase-9)
- **Executioner caspases** (3, 6, 7) activation

**Regulators**:
- **Pro-apoptotic**: Bax, Bak, Bad, Bid
- **Anti-apoptotic**: **Bcl-2**, Bcl-xL

**NEET PG**: Bcl-2 (anti-apoptotic) overexpressed in **follicular lymphoma** (t(14;18))

### 2. EXTRINSIC PATHWAY (Death Receptor)
**Trigger**: Fas ligand (FasL), TNF
**Mechanism**:
- FasL binds **Fas (CD95)** receptor
- **FADD** (Fas-associated death domain) recruitment
- **Caspase-8** activation
- Executioner caspases activation

**NEET PG**: Used by cytotoxic T cells (CTL) to kill infected/cancer cells

---

## Morphology of Apoptosis

**Light Microscopy**:
- Cell shrinkage
- Chromatin condensation (pyknosis)
- Nuclear fragmentation (karyorrhexis)
- **Apoptotic bodies** (membrane-bound fragments)

**Electron Microscopy**:
- Chromatin condensation at nuclear periphery
- Membrane blebs
- Intact organelles

**Detection**:
- **TUNEL assay**: Detects DNA fragmentation
- **Annexin V staining**: Detects phosphatidylserine on outer membrane

---

## INTRACELLULAR ACCUMULATIONS

### 1. LIPIDS

#### Steatosis (Fatty Change)
**Sites**: Liver, heart, kidney
**Causes**: Alcohol, obesity, diabetes, toxins, hypoxia
**Mechanism**:
- ↑ Fatty acid entry
- ↑ Fatty acid synthesis
- ↓ Fatty acid oxidation
- ↓ Lipoprotein secretion

**Microscopy**: Clear vacuoles in cytoplasm (H&E), red with Sudan stain

**NEET PG**:
- **Alcohol**: Most common cause of fatty liver
- Reversible if cause removed

#### Cholesterol
- **Atherosclerosis**: Foam cells in arterial intima
- **Xanthomas**: Cholesterol deposits in skin

---

### 2. PROTEINS

**Causes**:
- Reabsorption droplets (PCT in nephrotic syndrome)
- Russell bodies (immunoglobulins in plasma cells)
- **Mallory bodies** (alcoholic hepatitis - eosinophilic cytoplasmic inclusions)
- **Neurofibrillary tangles** (Alzheimer's - tau protein)

**NEET PG**: Mallory bodies = Alcoholic hepatitis

---

### 3. GLYCOGEN

**Examples**:
- **Diabetes**: Glycogen in renal tubules, liver
- **Glycogen storage diseases** (Von Gierke, Pompe)

---

### 4. PIGMENTS

#### Exogenous
- **Carbon** (anthracosis): Coal miners
- **Tattoos**: Tattoo pigment in macrophages

#### Endogenous

**A. Lipofuscin**
- "Wear and tear pigment"
- Brown, granular, autofluorescent
- Sites: Liver, heart, brain (aging)

**B. Melanin**
- Sites: Skin, hair, eyes
- Deficiency: Albinism
- Excess: Melanoma

**C. Hemosiderin**
- Iron storage form
- **Prussian blue** stain (blue color)
- Causes:
  - **Local**: Hemorrhage, bruises
  - **Systemic**: Hemochromatosis, transfusions

**NEET PG**:
- **Hemosiderin**: Prussian blue positive
- **Hemochromatosis**: "Bronze diabetes" (↑ iron deposition)

---

## PATHOLOGIC CALCIFICATION

### 1. DYSTROPHIC CALCIFICATION
**Definition**: Ca2+ deposition in **dead/dying tissue**
**Serum Ca2+**: **Normal**

**Causes**:
- Atherosclerotic plaques
- Old TB lesions (Ghon complex)
- Heart valves (calcific aortic stenosis)
- Fat necrosis (pancreatitis)

**Mechanism**: ↑ pH in necrotic tissue → Ca2+ precipitation

**NEET PG**: Normal serum Ca2+, calcification in abnormal tissue

---

### 2. METASTATIC CALCIFICATION
**Definition**: Ca2+ deposition in **normal tissue**
**Serum Ca2+**: **Elevated** (hypercalcemia)

**Causes of Hypercalcemia**:
- Hyperparathyroidism
- Vitamin D toxicity
- Sarcoidosis
- Malignancy (bone metastases, PTHrP)
- Renal failure

**Sites** (tissues with alkaline environment):
- **Kidneys** (nephrocalcinosis)
- Gastric mucosa
- Lungs
- Blood vessels

**NEET PG**: High serum Ca2+, calcification in normal tissue

## Practice MCQs

1. **Most common type of necrosis?**
   Answer: Coagulative necrosis

2. **Type of necrosis in brain infarction?**
   Answer: Liquefactive necrosis

3. **Hallmark of caseous necrosis?**
   Answer: Tuberculosis

4. **Barrett's esophagus is an example of?**
   Answer: Metaplasia (squamous → columnar)

5. **Wear and tear pigment?**
   Answer: Lipofuscin

6. **Apoptosis vs necrosis - key difference?**
   Answer: Apoptosis has NO inflammation

7. **Stain for hemosiderin?**
   Answer: Prussian blue

   Answer: Bcl-2
